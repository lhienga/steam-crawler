\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a great and satisfying game if you like puzzley resource management and min maxing with limited space. Slight spoiler alert - The later chapters/maps have unexpected mechanics, which in hindsight the game does telegraph, but expect to restart a few chapters. I was caught off guard multiple times and needed to restart to change my layout/strategy. Once things start to go sideways, it's easy to find yourself in an unwinnable situation without enough time to respond.

The science events often have negative outcomes that come with permanent stability impact, which IMO, is unbalanced when most positive outcomes reward a 10% trust bonus. Consistently getting your science crews killed early will cause you to lose in a later chapter due to low stability, which carries over each chapter.

These design choices are in line with the game's ideology, but I found them to be a little frustrating. The only other thing I really was annoyed by was not being able to demolish things when you're out of storage space. Working conditions are really important, avoid extra hours or overwork. This mechanic isn't explained but crew won't die during accidents under optimal conditions.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLong story short, dont get it unless you love the premise and genre. And even then you might be best advised to wait for a sale. I really really really wanted to love this game, im just a absolute sucker for the basic premise of being lost/wondering/orphaned in space. Its not a bad game, but it sure doesnt class in as a "good" one either, it is a wobbly experience and the game is a odd missmash which makes it hard to properly capture in a simple fashion.

Story, which should be the absolute peak of the experience in this kind of game, is a mess. There are tons of great concepts and ideas in there, the basic premise is absolutely perfect for a masterpiece of a game, but in the end its like the game wobbles through the story in stilts, possibly even missing a limb or two with ill-fitting ones crafted in place. It feels like there are entire diolague threads through the entire game that are missing, missing storylines and conversations, events and characters that should be there are just ripped out of the thread. It is a weird Frankenstein creation which makes me suspect that either the writers were either inexperienced both in medium and writing or that they simply werent listened to or ignored, possibly with someone else taking the original work and forcing it into place to rush a finished product.

As for construction they just consistently manage to turn what should be a enjoyable challenge, a "skill check", that could be varying degrees of difficult, into a bloody chore at every point and turn. Events and mechanics without resonable counterplay. A absolute grind. The grid and building system is just... wrong on every single turn and take. There is a phenomenal lack of consistency in building size and placement, it is impossible to reasonably plan out anything until you have everything unlocked and even at that point all the grids just fit together phenomenally bad with all of the shapes working together incredibly frustrating. Look at Frostpunk, it is a masterclass in HOW to do these things, grids adjusting and generally fitting together so you can plan things, and where they dont fit it generally fits by removing another row or through resonable pre-planning and foresight or limited adjustment. Whereas in ixion nothing ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ fits together, grids and blocks consistently fit badly as if it adds a "challenge". But it isnt, it is just a bloody grind having to rebuild everything over and over and having to build in a extraordinarilly visually displeasing way. It feels like playing D&D with a GM who sees it as his job to "defat" his party, no matter the cost or means, and when defeat isnt possible then just to frustrate them at every turn and waste time without a enjoyable challenge to it. In these kinds of games the player is supposed to be reasonably challenged not to make a mess of things but it should be a joy to see your settlements grow and flourish. Instead it is just a frustrating distraction from everything else, even from itself and worse, from the story, which is just a critical mistake in this kind of game.

Dont even get me started on accidents. I swear every single workplace in this game has a swimming pool, lined with banana peels, where all employees have to run in circles around repeatedly, dual wielding scissors, while under sustained sniper fire. Because there is no way in hell they would die and get wounded at this rate otherwise. And that is with optimal working conditions, all it seems to do is downgrade deaths to injuries. And while this is good, it shouldnt be that bad to begin with. Not to mention how easy it is to accidentally stray into a couple employees overwork and immediately get hit with several deaths.

Space weather just plain kills your ships. Boop they dead. No fixing. Even with unlockable upgrades. And its not just a rare thing. Ive lost my entire fleet of support ships atleast once over in total only partways through a single system. And it is with 2-3 ports worth of vessels. AND it is with all of the upgrades to prevent this unlocked. It isnt fun. It isnt challenging. There is no counterplay, you have to go into these areas. Either you design your game around having to replace these things constantly making them cheap with no personallity (bad for this kind of game), or you add actual counterplay options with upgrades and safeguards that keep them safe. It is not a challenge, it is just bad game design. It was so bad i actually disabled it, and i practically never disable these kinds of things in my playthroughs because i both enjoy a challenge and experiencing a game as it is supposed to be. But this system was just a crime against itself and the game.

I hope they go back to the drawing board on so many things, embrace the core ideas and learn from the greats in the genre. Homeworld, Battlestar Galactica, Frostpunk, Voyager (dont copy, i like the show but god you should learn from it and not copy the lizard tiny flippers, no matter how much you miss them), Stargate Universe etc.

In the end what i mostly feel is frustration and regret, because there was a absolute masterpiece of a game buried in there somewhere, but it was utterly lost in what came to be known as Ixion.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI almost skipped this one because of all this talk about difficulty and bad balancing. So let me give my own take on it.

It's best if you think of IXION not as a city builder, but as a survival game with a narrative focus that happens to be a city builder. This isn't the kind of experience where you chillingly watch your settlement turn from a simple village to a sprawling metropolis. You're in unknown space, on a rickety station that's literally falling apart; the question isn't if things will get even worse, but when.

As such, you're expected to plan ahead. Far ahead. You know your people will take another morale hit. You know the station will take more permanent damage; and so on. You also know you'll need the resources to grow, so you'll have to constantly be on the lookout for what you need, but also what you will need.

If that sounds harrowing - don't worry. You're being given plenty of resources (if you bother to look for them), many upgrades to turn the tide in your favour (if you bother to pay attention to the tech tree), and lots of science points to actually research those upgrades (if you bother to explore). Point is: you need to pay attention. This isn't a game that you can play with a podcast in the background (besides, the music is amazing).

Also, if you're the kind of person who feels horrible anxiety because their plan didn't turn out just right, and now impending doom is staring them in the face... skip this game. Unexpected stuff will happen, people will die. But if you keep your cool and use your head, you'll most definitely be fine. This is very important: occasionally, the game throws stuff at you which feel like everything is getting worse, but if you look at things calmly, you'll be able to navigate the crisis without issue.

And I think the game's worth playing, mainly for the story. The plot reminded me of Starcom: Nexus, except not as good - but it still kept me interested. And since story is the main element - the mission objectives, the expeditions, what have you - I wouldn't say the game has much replayability. That's fine in my book, but I know some people care about this more than others.

It's not just the story, though; the game's insanely fun to play, too. I started it on Saturday morning, and on Monday I was done, having played for 27 hours (note: I have a job and live with a partner). Haven't had this much of a blast playing something for quite some time. A friend of mine saw that and said something like "wow, those are some Stardew-level numbers".

One thing to mention: the game's logistic system requires manual attention and good understanding of how it works. If you've played Before We Leave you'll be familiar with it - although it's much more streamlined here. If you won't be able to understand and master this system you'll get insanely frustrated. So if having to occasionally adjust resource sliders sounds annoying to you, skip this one.

Some statistics to wrap things up: 85% of players finish the prologue, but only 65% beat the first chapter. Then the numbers start dropping even further, down to 35%... and then they slowly keep dropping until around ~20%, which is the percentage of people who beat the game. It's a decent number, if you ask me, but it also goes to show that many people bounce off when things get tough.

Personally, I beat the game on intended difficulty, without restarting/reloading (except one time where my ships flew into a death cloud), and generally without speeding up time - and that was 27 hours, more or less. I strongly recommend IXION, but it's not for everyone, that's for sure.
