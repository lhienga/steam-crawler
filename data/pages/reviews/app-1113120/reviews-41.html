\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTemped recommendation. If you want an unconventional city builder / management game, buy Frostpunk. Kinda frustrating that the same studio that gave us Mechanicus produced this. If you are craving a new city builder.... yeah, I could see this being fun if you're kinda hardcore about that sort of thing.


The Good Stuff- the visuals and sound work are all excellent. The initial premise is great if somewhat unoriginal.


The Bad: Just about everything else. The science is kind of hand-wavy, the central plot contrivance is a plot-hole and the game itself is designed to be tedious. If you've played Frostpunk, you've already played a better game. If you haven't, you should play frostpunk. If you have and just want another game LIKE frostpunk, yeah, I guess this game is tolerable.


39 Hour Edit:

Still temped recommendation. Sound track is frankly phenomenal. Two biggest problems is the game's tendency to blindside you and the game's tendency to mislead the players about it's actual core gameplay loop. It's less a city builder and more a puzzle / resource management game. Most of your day to day game time will be spent baby sitting resources, making sure people are fed and making sure your designated Space sector isn't getting flooded by all your docks.


And some mechanics just make no sense. For one thing you need to complete research and build a structure in every sector just to tell people to, say, eat less food. And I get that smaller rations makes the locals angry, but the rest make little sense. Why are people upset that they have to throw their trash away now instead of 'purging' it? In a post-extinction setting I understand some people being upset at the idea of salvaging all possible resources off gram-gram when she's dead but permanently for the duration of the practice? Why does the unhappiness penalty for staying on a map too long exist? It doesn't actually mean anything and you'll stay regardless until you have the resources you need because jumping to the next map is (almost) always the hardest part of the map. Game also actively encourages looking up a guide because maximizing the returns on blind choices is important and if you don't save scum you're just handicapping yourself because you're making blind choices.


And yeah, plot's dumb. The promise of humanity on the brink never really happens. A stable of characters are introduced never to show up again except in hand wave-y references in the text. The ending is dumb no matter which option you take. There is a dog, though, so that counts for something.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's basically Frostpunk IN SPAAACE. Recognizable grid gameplay, tight resources. About the same number of people -- too many to learn their names or care about them individually -- you get more by rescuing them, and they go on strike the instant the power goes out. Your city is a giant space station/mothership, full of homes, factories, and space farms. You command a fleet of smaller ships. As a space nerd, it is super cool to see them trundle across the solar system and watch them arrive at your docking bays. And, because your city is a space station, it can move too!

The story is great. It explores the topical hubris of tech billionaires, if you're self-aware enough to question what they tell you. The endings made me think a little. I won't forget them.

The soundtrack is a total banger. I will continue to listen to it.

The replayability is limited. Definitely go for the hidden-ish second ending. Maybe try again on the hardest difficulty if you're a masochist.

I'm glad I played.\t\t\t\t\t
