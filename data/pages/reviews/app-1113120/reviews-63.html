\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOverall I would give this a 7/10. It's definitely above average, but I feel like the gamplay loop gets a bit tiring by the end of the game and it feels like the game just forces you to sit on your ass and wait for objectives to be complete, especially in some of the later chapters that force you to do things like (Chapter 3 spoiler) open 2000 cryopods or (Chapter 5 spoiler) train all of your non-workers into colonists. I almost felt like just leaving the game on for a few hours and just walking away from the screen during parts like that, and they always come up at times when the stakes couldn't be lower, so it's just a chore.

I really enjoyed every other aspect of the game, however. I've seen people complain about the difficulty in their reviews, but from my experience there isn't any obstacle that can't be overcome by planning ahead and keeping in mind what the game has taught you so far in order to prepare for the future. The stability system is only a concern in the first few chapters, and by the end of the game the hull and food issues will be an afterthought. I do wish that there was more pressure at times, but I suppose that I could have just increased the difficulty on my playthrough.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAwesome city-building sim - with a great balance of tension brought on by managing resources and space in your ship/station the Tiqqun. I've really only gotten to the second chapter so far, but have started about a dozen playthroughs either by following a design thread that ended in the destruction of the station, or starting over just to try things out differently. Keep in mind that building arrangement is part of the game, and it does cost to move and rebuild things - which IMO after I started thinking about it like that the game got that much better because I was thinking about future arrangements and checking the tech tree for new things that I'd need to build. Rather than providing new "cities" or different locations to build in like other city games, it uses a progression system in which you unlock more of the station and gradually have to manage the stress it puts on the station itself. The story is a great mix of spec-fic and scifantasy as well, with interesting dialogues and missions. If you like the vibe of Homeworld this game is an AWESOME find if you like city builders - and I haven't even talked about the SOUNDTRACK. Guillaume David has made an absolutely amazing soundtrack - which TBH was the first thing that drew me to this. Play the demo if you can to try it out - it captures most of the basics and includes chapter 1 - and then if you dig it, get it! This is definitely a game the devs should be proud of, it's great work <3\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSuppose you expect this to be a thorough review of the main game. In that case, I'm afraid to disappoint other than saying the game is decently made with few bugs I have encountered. But since none I have seen made a good mention of the game's soundtrack, much of it should be recognised as a hidden gem alone to justify buying the soundtrack along with the game itself.

Most sci-fi soundtracks that accompany the stories that touch the same subjects as IXION go with the overly dramatic, the solemn, the grandiose. That can be fine, but it starts feeling like well-trodden ground when everyone's doing it. The safe route, if you will. We've heard it with Hans Zimmer, Max Richter, Ramin Djawadi, James Horner, and Michael Giacchino that sometimes self-plagiarises to evoke sci-fi plots.

However, Guillaume David, with IXION's soundtrack, showcases the singing the praises to the facet of humanity that not often receives aural attention: its industriousness and resilience, accompanied perfectly by how well the developers showcase the soundtrack syncing Vanir's Legacy in between the jump sequence cinematic .

More often than not, when those themes arise in media, they are accompanied by the epic sounds typical of a grand goal or dramatic undertones expected of a tragedy-testing our resolve. Instead, IXION's soundtrack appropriately musicalises the immense industriousness, resilience, ingenuity, and sacrifice involved in the day-to-day life of a space station of how the genre of the story is set up. Everything we define and take for granted and boring becomes heroic when set in outer space.
It's a wonderful soundtrack because it underlines the bravery of the mundane and the determination to push through the routine. Its mesmerising effects are tainted with the decorous nature with the juxtaposition of the techno-synth and choir singing in harmony- portraying the AI's purpose in the game and humanity's hope in harmony through the journey of the plot. It warms up what would typically be very distant and rote proceedings by infusing it with humanity's hopes and dreams. It's a beautiful mixture of electronic and organic, massive synths supporting ethereal choirs and ethnic drums. Humanity survives so far from Earth through sheer willpower.

Guillaume David did it again. We all know Mechanicus is great; it's been discussed at length. But IXION proves that David has a knack for striking the chord that perfectly encapsulates the media with the privilege of having one of his soundtracks accompanying it in the settle it belongs. It's refreshing, it's moving, it's absolutely magnificent. I would often compare Guillamue Kasedo to Mick Gordon for ID software. I'm certainly looking forward to more of his future works.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIxion is a fun space city builder. Compared to Frost Punk, I enjoy Frost Punk more since it's quicker and has other story options. Maybe that will come, who knows. I'll note some of what I've noticed/tips.

It's a struggle on normal difficulty and some quests are obnoxiously long. Like waking up 2000 people is a chore. I tried to keep my population small and manageable so this hit me like a brick house and I had to fast forward through a ♥♥♥♥ ton of cycles to get this finished up.

If you do something to early you'll either have to reload or get completed ♥♥♥♥♥♥. If I could tell you one thing before playing as a huge heads up it would be DO NOT ENGAGE THE PIRANESI until you are ready to leave the system and all other quests are done. I didn't do this and had to reset my game back over 100 cycles because I'm the type to explore pretty much everything ASAP. That can be a really big ♥♥♥♥ up.

Layout management is huge. I had to restart my first game because I was just haphazardly putting things down (which shame on me in a city builder) but unlike Frost Punk, things are much larger than you anticipate and you will need to maximize every inch you have, plus as you upgrade things, the sizing changes- to like weird af sizing. Almost the entirety of my chapter 3 was spent destroying and rebuilding things to fit better as I got more upgrades.

Save before you jump every time. This game will throw weird curve balls at you out of no where and if you aren't stacked up on resources or you pick the wrong option your run might be ruined. Much easier to start from the last chapter then the beginning or an auto save from god known when.

There is probably more but even with 30+ hours I haven't completed the story line. If you like city builders and space, I think it's wroth it and it has a very interesting story line. The art direction and graphics are pretty cool as well.\t\t\t\t\t
