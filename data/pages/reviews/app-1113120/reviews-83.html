\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR: This game blows. Save your money, buy Frontpunk instead.

I almost never write reviews, or comment on anything, ever. I think I've only ever made one other review in all my years on Steam, & that game blew too. I have no intention to seem eloquent, or succinct. Other reviewers have already done that, & quite well. I don't write angry reviews to be eloquent. You've got to really intend to ♥♥♥♥ up to raise my hackles enough to leave a comment. And oh boy, consider my hackles raised!

Ixion is superficially dolled up on the surface, with nice art & graphics. However, the game developers didn't make a survival game, or a city builder game, they made a buggy, grindy, difficulty simulator that feels more like one of those sham mobile games, & a broken one at that. It is the proverbial polished turd. To all the neck-beard incels ready to furiously type a preemptive reply before reading more than a few sentences of this review on their food encrusted RGB waifu keyboards, that the destruction of Earth, & subsequent rescue & survival of humanity is supposed to be hard. THAT IS NOT WHAT I'M TALKING ABOUT! I expected that to be hard, & in fact was one of the specific reasons I bought this game in the first place, but this game is just so aggressively awful in so many stupid ways.

Here is my list of PROS & CONS.
PRO: The game runs relatively smoothly.

PRO: Starvation. The solution to your problems!
I'll explain further down in the CONS section.

PRO: NEW modular difficulty settings that allows you to (limitedly) customize your gameplay experience on the fly, whilst in-game. Yay.

PRO: ???

PRO: Profit.


CON: Cryopods are a BS scam. The ONLY way to increase your population size is to retrieve people stored in cryopods scattered all over. You can find two types people in pods, workers & non-workers. You need workers to work your ships, factories, everything. Non-workers can be trained to become colonist ONLY. You may be asking yourself what's so bad about that? I've played the game all day, & have found & opened many cryopods. Of which contained 96 non-workers, & 4 workers. Non-workers are non-workers FOREVER, except as colonist (which are also useless). Why? So I have a handful of stressed, overworked, & angry workers barely holding the ship together, & dying while their at it, while hundreds of bums sit around eating ALL the food that should be going to actual productive members of humanity. You can't put them back into cryo, FOR SOME DUMBASS REASON, you can't open pods that only have workers. You are stuck with these worthless meat bags, & you don't even have the satisfaction of forcing them into the factories, or out an airlock.
There is a policy you can enact, to open worker pods first. Doesn't work. There is a new difficulty option to raise the ratio of workers to bums to 400%. Doesn't work. I opened all my pods. Reloaded my save, increased the ratio to max, & I got 4 workers, & 96 bums. Not only that, but the workers were the LAST thawed. None of the options for workers, ironically, work. WHY is there no option to train bums to be workers? Why is there NO option to ONLY open worker pods? Whose dumb idea was that? Whoever they are, I hope they get hangnails that catch on their clothes everyday for the rest of their life. Don't worry, you're just the last of humanity, feel free to just sit back & relax while a handful of useful people work themselves to death to provide EVERYTHING for you, while you do jack all.
I was expecting something more akin to the Battlestar Galactica reboot, with a dash of "Oops, you got some City Skylines in my Homeworld!", & what I got is the guy who hangs out in front of the gas station asking to borrow a dollar. At least the real guy standing in front of the gas station will do something useful with my dollar, & buy drugs, stimulating the economy.
That said, I have found a dystopian loophole to my bum problem, that I am fairly sure the devs did not intend to be an actual feature. I mentioned starvation as a PRO above. Let me explain. :) Expensive as it was, I opened up a new sector, & transferred all non-workers to it. I provided no homes, food, power, etc, & watched them all starve to death. This made everyone crazy upset, & nearly caused enough trust loss to destroy the last hope of humanity, but I figured out how to mitigate that by constantly transferring angry workers sector to sector, keeping them in limbo. I wouldn't mind playing an evil despot if that were an option, but it isn't. This game is on rails, with no option to turn your ship into little North Korea, like you can in Frostpunk, which is a MUCH better game by-the-way.

CON: Sectors. Why are sectors cut off from each other? It's all the same ship. For some reason I have to shuffle resources, & workers to different sectors by foot, until I pay to build an internal tram system. Who builds a ship this size, with no reactor, water reclamation, storage, or transportation system? Only video game developers can think of something as dumb as removing options, then charging extra for access to it later. The DLC experience as a mechanic INSIDE the game. WOW!
Commuting to work is a normal part of human life. But not in Ixion, which is run like Soviet Russia. One sector can starve while the one next door has a surplus of food, that if you don't micromanage, your people die. Or one sector can have three free workers, while the sector next door is literally bursting into flames because they need two people to stand around a supply depot.
It makes some sense to maybe have to set up a better ship wide logistics network, with a major shift in your mission going from exploration & colonization, to saving the human race. But why the terrible UI driven micromanagement? I admit that this is more of a minor gripe, but still one I did not enjoy being part of the game. My actual job is working with large data sets, & IS actual micromanagement, & my day job is more fun than this was! Bravo to the devs for finding a way to make my tedious job more fun than their game, bravo. Insert condescending slow clap here.

CON: The soundtrack in some synth garbage. Turned that off with a right quickness. That is not me hating on synth music, this is me hating on this soundtrack. I think they spent most of the budget for this game paying people to come up with the worst ideas for a survival city builder possible, & shining up this turd, & paid some college student to whip this soundtrack up last minute.

CON: Bugged missions soft locking you out of progress. You'll have glitched out objectives that can't be completed. This happened to me REPEATEDLY. Good luck if you forgot to make a save before the bug locked you up, because now you need to reload a much older save & re-experience all the "joy" of your idiotically suicidal crew, & the tedious micromanagement. Sometimes there are work arounds, but rarely.

CON: NEW modular difficulty settings that allows you to (limitedly) customize your game play experience on the fly, whilst in-game... Except some of them don't seem to do anything, even after a reload, waiting several cycles, or after progressing the story line. If you want to actually have that level of control, but actually have it work, download one of those "premium" trainers. Hey, you already burned your money buying this game, what's a few more dollars gonna hurt?
Personally, I'm going to wait for the next update, before I put the final nail in this coffin. In the meantime, I'm going back to games that don't make the vein on my forehead pop out. I suggest you buy Frostpunk instead. At least when it frustrates you, it's because it's actually a fun challenging experience, & not because of dumb wonky mechanics, broken ass features, or bugs.

This is already longer than I had intended, so I'll stop here.
I'd like to get my money back, but that is not realistic. I will never trust or buy another game from this dev now that I know they suck at it. May they forever stub their toes.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tUpdated review after further playing. I wanted to like this game, at first I was disappointed. It's not really a city builder in the usual sense, it's a base builder/micro-management game driven by a linear story. The standard difficulty settings are, IMO, totally out of whack, the game is either stupidly easy or ridiculously difficult, with the default settings falling into the latter category. You have to love micromanagement to play on the default setting. It's frustratingly hard to get the ship into a stable configuration and keep things balanced and running. You are ultimately doomed by the continuous consumption of materials that inevitably run out in your current system - iron is particularly the issue in the early game as it's constantly drained by the need to constantly repair the hull, which disintegrates at a rate that would make you think it's made of chocolate. Gradual attrition of crew to accidents and events can also eventually dooms you as there are only so many cryopods you can rescue to get more people - although they do become extremely plentiful later, making food and water more of a survival issue.

Eventually, driven into the settings by frustration, you realise you can just turn off whatever thing is currently killing you, at which point the game can become too easy to be interesting. Trapped in a loop with no iron available and the hull disintegrating? Just turn off hull decay. Fixed! Stuck in a feedback loop where strikes have crippled production causing starvation and unhappiness which leads to more strikes? Turn off strikes. Fixed! Feels like cheating, though. Changing difficulty settings ought to require starting over, and the standardised settings ought to be better balanced, in my opinion. With some experimentation though, I was able to choose settings that made gameplay feel more balanced.

Initially the game seems to crawl along. Most of the time you're either waiting for resources, or waiting for technology, waiting for something to be built, waiting for a ship to deliver something... But don't alt-tab out to do something else while you wait, because the game will auto-pause. Despite it's slow pace, if you dare to wait too long in one sector, the game punishes you by applying a permanent penalty,, that gets bigger as the game goes on. On the other hand, if you rush to beat its artificial time limit, you have no time to explore and gather the science, techs and resources you actually need. Later in the game, there's a lot more to do as you're managing a much bigger population and set of things to explore and mine.

The whole game has a kind of grim post-industrial depressing look and feel to it. Everything seems brown and grey. Although some of of the cut-scenes show detailed graphics, the main game itself isn't very inspiring in its appearance. No epic space views here, sadly. Unfortunately, so far as I've seen, the story is also kinda dull and predictable, at least as far as chapter 3 is a catalogue of doom and gloom. Presumably things ultimately improve! Event choices can be bizarrely random, you can't put any kind of thought into which option to pick - a sensible option might explode your science ship and kill everyone, while an obviously dumb choice might yield valuable resources and new techs. Says it all when game guides actually recommend saving before any choice so you can try each one, or using websites that list all the outcomes so you can avoid the stupid ones.

However, despite all its faults, this game has managed to keep me playing and, for the most part entertained, and as I've progressed, I feel I'm enjoying it a lot more than I did in those initial few hours, so on that score it's a win - and if you like this sort of game, maybe give it a try.\t\t\t\t\t
