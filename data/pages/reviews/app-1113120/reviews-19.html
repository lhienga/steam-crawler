\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you wanted a frostpunk esq game of 'saving the last bastion of humanity from a crisis and you are in charge' but in space... this is the game for you.
This is a survival management game with city building like game-play. But I will caution that this isnt a true city builder. You have to build in specific ways to be optimal or even just make it through the challenges this game throws at you.
The soundtrack and ambience of the game are very on point. The graphics are serviceable, until you zoom in really close, but that shouldn't surprise you in a city builder esq game. The gameplay, when things are going well can be relaxing and pleasant but things do quickly go south if you dont correct things quickly.
The only downside I hope the devs add in at some point, is there is no 'freeplay' mode in the game. There is just the campaign. That was something that added a lot of replay-ability in frostpunk that I think is sorely missing here.
I recommend this game but only if its on sale for like 30 dollars because of that. But for what you get it is a well made game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAN ACCIDENT HAS OCCURRED ON THE TIQQUN.

While the game has a compelling story and amazing visuals, its gameplay is unintuitive and easy to break -- and after the gameplay is thus broken, the game just becomes an exercise in tediousness until you're through with the story. Additionally, I wish more was available for somebody trying to follow the story and its many organizations and characters, such as a codex.

More detail follows, mostly me griping about the gameplay, which I'll split into sections.

CITY MANAGEMENT

Most of the gameplay is city management, which I'm experienced with. In IXION, the city management suffers badly from unintuitive surprise mechanics.

- City layout, for instance, involves no strategy except for keeping your stockpiles close to the buildings that supply and demand from them. Literally nothing else matters except for the fire station: you can put your housing anywhere, your stability buildings anywhere, your batteries anywhere, your mess halls almost anywhere. Being mindful of the shapes of buildings is important, but planning for that is an annoyance since you are never told the dimensions of a new building until you research it (so have fun trying to place down that exo-fighting dome).
- The accident mechanics are a massive existential threat until you understand that each sector needs two infirmaries (hint: if you're stretched for personnel and/or power you can shut one down until the other suffers an accident) and enough spare workers to step in if 90% of the workers in the sector's biggest building are suddenly injured and unable to work for a week. Then, the accident mechanics are trivial. It's that simple. You have to baby sectors a bit to make sure the working conditions are always "optimal" because as soon as they creep into "Extra Hours" you're going to be dealing with explosions and fires and dead workers all day every day.
- Cryopods are like loot boxes, 90% of the time you regret opening them. The in-game tutorials have a vague line about the cryo center opening worker and non-worker (aka useless) cryopods according to the ratio of people in the sector or something, but I seriously wish I could just mouse over my cryopod stocks and see how many were workers and how many were waste machines, since the cryo center workers seem to know.
- City management simply stops being a challenge halfway into Chapter 2, and it's easy to grasp why. Look at every single guide on the community hub and you'll see the same strategy repeated over and over: Rush Science. Frostpunk had an issue where the sole bottleneck you had to worry about was how quickly you could research new techs, and once you had that bottleneck under control the game was a cinch. IXION is very similar: once you've upgraded your tech lab and science ships, you no longer have to seriously worry yourself over which techs to prioritize because you can just research everything. Once you get upgraded housing you can stop worrying about homelessness and (mostly) stability; once you get the DLS Center you can stop worrying about stability entirely. Once you get upgraded input efficiency for your steel mills, you can stop worrying about hull repairs (hint: because of the steel mill's tiny input stockpile, input efficiency is crucial to keeping it running nonstop). Once you get crop farms, you can almost stop worrying about food until you get the mess hall upgrades (then food is never a problem again). Once you get waste processing set up, you never have to worry about any of your resources ever again except how much space they're taking up on your ship. Rush Science and your economy snowballs so hard, so fast, that by Chapter 3 the whole thing is kind of boring.
- Sector specialization bonuses are extremely important (a few of them anyway, industry specialization is useless) for keeping your economy running on all cylinders, but you won't know this until you actually know what the bonuses are, and you won't know that either until you either fulfill their thresholds or check the Steam Community Hub. Please list the sector specialization bonuses in game, devs.
- Please stop with the "surprise, that's a -1 to stability forever" landmine events. The DLS is supposed to be a cutting-edge system for telling me everything that my crew is thinking; an early warning would be nice. Granted, once everybody's in a domotic quarter (or at least "mostly" according to how the stability bonus works; bring on the cell housing) I don't have to care anyway.

SPACE EXPLORATION

Whenever you're not in F1 mode watching your crew roam in circles at the hanging edges of roads, you're in F3 mode poring over the star system map. Surprise mechanics are a bit more understandable here since space is a terrible environment, but there are still a number of issues that could use addressing.

- Chapter 2 introduces the player to something called Space Weather, large hazard blobs on the maps you visit. Chapter 2 has the most egregious instance of this, giving the player two static hazard blobs: one that almost instantly kills any probe and ship that blunders through it, and one that's just colder than the rest of space (somehow) and causes absolutely no issues to anything except the Tiqqun itself when navigating in it. The only hint you get that the two hazards are so different from each other is that when you are ordering ships or probes on the star map, the former hazard shows a red dotted line for the ships' navigation indicators and the latter hazard shows no such warnings. This can mislead the player into thinking that half the sector is a no-go zone. Later chapters have different types of space weather, whose effects are similarly uncommunicated; Chapter 4 in particular has a hazard blob that's completely harmless to ships and probes but sending the Tiqqun through it is a russian roulette situation. The game could benefit a lot from some indicator/text blob, somewhere, at all, hinting at each hazard blob's effects.
- The incredible dumbness of the mining, cargo, and science ships means they have to be babied fairly frequently, in order to make sure they don't sail right through some death blob or something more spoiler-y. It would be nice if the devs at least took a page out of Dwarf Fortress's book and had resource patches automatically set themselves to "avoid" if they suddenly found themselves in "you will die instantly if you go here" territory.
- The nature of expeditions encourages hardcore savescumming. That is all.

STORY

The story is why I kept playing this game after city management became a matter of "where am I going to put all these extra resources that are piling up." It's a great story, I'll give it that, and I only have a few quibbles.

- The game throws a lot of names, and organizations, and concepts, and projects at the player. Like, a lot. There's a huge amount to keep track of, and frustratingly, a great proportion of the lore is seen only in expedition flavor text that cannot be re-accessed after the expedition ends. I like the story a lot, and would deeply appreciate an in-game codex or something instead of having to resort to whatever wikia has sprung up for this game. Being able to piece together what the hell happened with the BMS and the Piranesi and so forth right in game would be amazing.
- The most crucial events in the story seem to depend on having enough resources hoarded up ahead of time. This is not an issue if you're a compulsive polymer hoarder (and if you looked at the tech tree for more than the thirty seconds necessary to understand that in order to win this game you need to Rush Science), but I can imagine it being a sticking point if you don't like to build stockpiles everywhere and/or you don't like having to reload to a previous save and wait to build up resources before you step on an event tripwire.
- The ending sequence and cinematic just felt empty and lifeless, after the amazing prologue.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA fun mess of a builder game.

It's a beautiful and frustrating little game plagued by a myriad of ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ mechanics. Quite fun if you save scum the every loving ♥♥♥♥ out of it. I would recommend a dozen builder games before this one. But if you've already beat all those, this one's not terrible.
