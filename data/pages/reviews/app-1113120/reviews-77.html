\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPros:
Nice Story
Nice Settings
Nice Music, and Thematic

Cons:
Poorly explained UI
Frustrating Resource Management System
Extremely unbalanced and poorly designed game

I would not recommend this game, as there are many similar style games (although not in this sort of setting), that simply are better, and do the gameplay loop better. Games like Frostpunk work, because there is a scarcity on resources for survival. Ixion doesn't work, because survival resources are abundant, but resources required to progress the story are scarce, and tech (unless you know to get the upgrade) is impossibly slow. This basically means, that you are either forced to wait in a chapter for 3 years afk while you grind out tech and resources, or you have to restart. (Now why does restarting fix this?)

Yes, welcome to the environmentalist overlords. The only way to overcome the scarcity of carbon and silicon that you need to progress the game, is (amazingly) Waste management. Unfortunately, waste manager is so overpowered with its infinite resource loop, that it trivializes the whole game.

Now, all of this is easily fixable.

1. Completely redesign the tech system from the ground up
2. Make resources abundant, but
3. Massively increase the negatives of idling, to enforce story progression, and to keep a sense of urgency. Now, instead of randomly running out of carbon to mine in the universe (like really, how is there no carbon to find in our solar system?????????), you will actively be having to make choices as to what you mine and where you mine it to avoid impending doom as you race against the clock.
4. Nerf Waste management
5. Remove softlocks from the game, and allow for resources to be pushed rather than pulled to your sectors

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's ok. It does not give the challenge of the design and balance perfection of Frostpunk and Against the Storm, but the concepts and mechanics or passable. A lot of the efficiency comes from tedious micro (like turning off storage to save energy and workers, or transferring people and goods between sectors) so the extend to which you can make the game challenging with strategic decisions is limited. Somebody who will do the micro will more efficient than somebody who doesn't. It's also too dependent on the story missions and knowing which rewards they give. So there is little motivation to replay it again on a harder difficulty.

The connection of base-layer and expedition-layer is one of the best of the genre though.

QoL issues:
-too long in system debuff should continue to grow to create a better tradeoff between happiness and resource looting
-can't see unemployed number in the HUD (have to compare 2 numbers in tooltip)
-can't see free houses
-can't assign multiple ships to deliver quest resources
-can't see how much to build to reach specialization level
-research queue should be longer than 1
-you should be able to queue research that gets unlocked by the research in front of the queue
-probe should be on the cursor when clicking on probe
-cargo ships keep flying to depleted points
-gargo bay resource overflow should be stored in any stockpile even if the sector does not request it
-launching a probe should not kick you out of probe launch mode, if you have another probe
-sector bugged out and workshop does not build anything anymore => fixed by demolish and rebuild
-delete key should delete a structure, shift+delete should do it without confirmation

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOverall, a fun game with an engaging story. Has some issues with the cringe-factor fluctuating, but it makes up for it in the end. Biggest annoyance is with the habit of hidden menus and expectations that you are going to find things (like upgrades), as well as things that should be on more than one screen but its hidden away in a sub menu somewhere (population information)

Bulwark are the kings of delivering an experience and the cinematic appeal. I got the experience with Mechanicus, and once again here. Just stellar. Its a joy to watch and play - and the game is basically a space screenshot generator.

The *philosophical roots* of the writing are very much in the front, and become more obvious the more you play. Much of which was making me cringe. To give credit where it is due thou - the game focuses on its delivery of the experience and there is actually a lot of depth with the intention of the player to pull back the layers a bit and think about the ideas (good or bad) being presented. Its key to realize that there is really only one distinct player choice in the game narrative-wise, and it has a lot of impact.

In summary, beautiful execution of setting, some annoyances with the hiding or obfuscation of mechanics and necessary information, interesting and clever writing with moments of semi-political cringe but intentions for the player to go deeper, and stellar voice acting that can get annoying when forced to replay scenarios.\t\t\t\t\t

Introduction
I want to first explain that I have absolutely no idea what other people are talking about in their reviews about this game being overly punishing or hard.

This is one of the best city builder games I've ever played, and I've played quite a lot. If you're into games like Anno or Frostpunk you'll love this game and probably not find it nearly as punishing as some of these reviewers.

Even if you're new to the genre, the difficulty options are good and there are easier ways to play, but I will also say that I recommended this game to someone who'd never even played a city builder before and is on track to finishing it first try like me, so I really don't understand the negative reviews around difficulty.

Gameplay
This game is, at its core, not a calming or relaxing city builder where you'll have a satisfying end result that you'll admire. The buildings fit awkwardly, the crew are getting tired of being on the station, everything seems to be going wrong constantly. That's the point. Your crappy little space station was never designed to undertake the journey you're about to take. That's why it's hard. You're trying to save the entirety of humanity where the odds are stacked against you in almost every way. If that doesn't sound like fun to you, then don't bother wasting your time becoming another player with 9 hours leaving a negative review about how "hard" it is to demolish a building. Like seriously, I finished the game while barely even using 2 sectors, with over 5800 population. The game is actually too easy towards the end if you play it right.

Negative Reviewer Problems (and how to fix them)

A lot of the things people complain about in other reviews are really easily fixable. I saw a guy complain that inter-sector transport is too slow. You can just build more stockpiles of the same resource, you get more carts. I had over 4000 people with only one sector producing food and it only took 3 small stockpiles to supply every sector in enough time.

Others complain that the alloy cost of maintaining the station is too much or that the steel mills can't supply enough. I don't even know what to say to this one other than.. have you tried building more than one steel mill? I only had 3 throughout the game and I usually had 1 turned off and sometimes even 2 turned off once recycling became a thing.

Then there's people complaining about stability loss for staying in a system or for various other things that happen in the game. Guys. There's literally a building (without upgrades) that gives you +2 stability (DLS center), a building that gives +1 stability and you're going to encounter a lot more stability upgrades over the course of the game. It's a natural thing to combat, just like the station maintenance. If these stability debuffs didn't exist you'd literally have no reason to continue improving the station to continuously combat the problem. What's the point of playing a city builder if there's no reason to build a city??
My point is, the debuff isn't the end of your game lmao. These reviewers seem to think that you HAVE to move on from the system or else your station will blow up. If you lose a game from a -1 stability loss then you're already playing the game badly and it shouldn't come to that. You build a single building or enact a policy and you're fine. It's honestly more lenient than you think.

People complaining that a lot of the people you wake up are unable to work and are "useless" - most of what I'm about to say overlaps with the paragraph above, but again guys, just build more houses. It's not hard. There's plenty of space if you know what you're doing, and moving things around really isn't hard either. I really can't imagine much of a game if one of the major problems wasn't that I had to build housing for the last remaining bastion in all of humanity's survival in the entire universe, like, can you imagine if the game didn't have non-workers? Would be too easy imo. Anyway, that's a thing you can change in the difficulty settings now, so that's not really a problem anymore.

There's a lot of other complaints mainly about resource management between sectors, but that isn't even hard either. You just move a freaking slider from the left to the right, and bam, you have transfer between sectors. I mean it really isn't rocket science.

The Problems
I love the game, but holy hell the developers do need to learn a thing or two about how city builders are supposed to work and also implement some quality of life stuff. Here's the main problems I'll admit to so far that aren't just a result of people being too bad to play the game:

- Cargo ships are stupid. They choose a destination and then go there and back even if the resource depletes during that time. They could be made to change their destination if a resource is going to deplete which would allow for much less micromanagement, but whatever, it's not really a problem once you have enough cargo ships.

- There's no GUI showing how many workers there are available in a sector, you have to manually calculate that in your head by taking the workers working and the total workers you have, which is really annoying when trying to remain in optimal conditions.

- A lot of the buildings are entirely useless and are terrible to use. The water treatment plant for example, 25 waste per cycle?? My finished Tiqqun had 5850 people and only produced a little more than 5 waste per cycle.. That is just ridiculous, one fusion station is enough to supply two algae farms (which is enough to supply 5850 people somehow). The game has terrible balancing in my opinion but for the complete opposite of what people are saying. It gets too easy once you research the relevant techs.

- Medium and Large stockpiles are stupid. They grant more space sure, but they DON'T INCREASE THE NUMBER OF THE CARTS TO TRANSFER RESOURCES. What?? The speed of constructing and deconstructing things is relevant to the amount of little carts you have transporting resources, but these stockpiles keep the same amount of carts but for more stuff, which just leads to more player frustration, it literally encourages you to not have as many carts. You're better off just sticking to small stockpiles, they're way better.

- Game has some pretty bad optimisation and FPS problems when it comes to certain solar systems and places your ship can go. On my system, changing the graphics settings didn't seem to affect this at all. I just had to play on 30fps for some entire sections of the game until it was suddenly 60fps again. Not a major problem, but I see some people literally quit the game just because of that, so.. L I guess.

The Good Things
In my opinion, the story is really good, I felt quite entranced by it and super immersed in the mission and why I was doing what I was doing. That being said, the last two chapters were pretty underwhelming. I don't want to spoil any of the story for anyone who's actually read this far who hasn't already played the game, but I was really expecting something harder or more engaging tbh, not just a reason to constantly move around the system.

I love the look and animations of everything. The game has a sweet atmosphere that the music helps set in too.

Conclusion
It's good in my opinion, but I really like hard games that challenge me. Definitely not for everyone I guess. Frostpunk is a very similar game without all the problems I mentioned above if you'd like to give that a go, but do understand that it is quite hard just as this is, but without any of the annoying problems of the developers not knowing how to make a good city builder I guess.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA (semi-)hard science fiction fan should absolutely give this a go. Echos of Cosmos and Interstellar will dance in your head. And a very competent "city builder" simulation, if a bit heavy of unnecessary micromanagement.


(Spoiler free, beyond the trailer, I hope)

Pluses:

+ Graphics/Sound: They nailed it. Equal parts haunting, desperate, and hopeful, depending on what's happening. You will cheer for the "Ixion", and simultaneously be horrified at the terrors it unleashes.

+ Story: (I have not completed it yet): So far, in Act II, it is equal part admonishing and hopeful. I hope it doesn't pull a bait-and-switch later.

+ Tutorial: Seated in story, the tutorial instructs you in-universe about what to do. Then says, "Go space explorer! Due to protocols, 'Houston' won't answer!"

+ Semi-hard Science Fiction: If you're into this sort of thing, it feels like something we'd experience in the near future. With all the technological advances and sociological warnings that contains.


Cons:

- Micromanagement: There are few things you have to do later, which distract from the grander mission. You become a Great Commander of the "Ixion", savior of humanity, to a logistics clerk moving packages between Amazon warehouses.

- UX: It's very polished, but still fails to give you everything you need. Five clicks are required. One part of your grand Space Arc is angry at you, but it requires multiple clicking to find out why, while time ticks down. (There is a pause). Housing concerns are in one screen, food in another inaccessible modal window.

End Note: More hopeful than Frostpunk, but with the same against-the-odds story. More purposeful and inspiring than other Depression Simulators.
