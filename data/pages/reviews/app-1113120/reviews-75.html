It feels cluncky overall, you can see as you play simple ways to improve the flow of the game, but you are stuck with what you bought. At the same time Ixion grips you tight precisely because everything really hard to manage and succeding in doing so is moderately satisfying. Here is the loop: you need a thing done but don't have the resources to make for it, the good part is you can build the building that will bring you said resources, but you ain't got energy to build said building, so you must either increase you energy production or shutdown other buildings, which you can't afford, at the same time you are not making enough food to feed your people, your running out fo alloys to repair your ship and since you had to shutdown a great deal of you infrastructure there is a massive gridlock going on. So that's that, you want to solve 3 things that pose inmediate danger to your station but resolving them is tremendously hard and complex, there is very little room to make things on the station better, but you must manage to not only survive, but thrive. Story time: it is really good... up to a certain point. The whole story becomes really odd by the end and it feels poorly resoluted, with a lot of answers missings, and a lot of completely unexplained misteries... Soundtrack: good! you have awe inspiring songs that don't really fit on the overall narrative, absolute bangers that perfectly suit what you are playing and some other that are just good enough, also really good voice acting. Performance: Not the kind of guy that can tell much about this, it runs poorly for me with a pretty high speck pc, but you are not here for the looks. So yes, I recommend this game, if you enjoy it you will end up like me, wondering how much more this could have been, but gratefull to have spent time on it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFirst of all, the evaluation is negative because it cannot be neutral. The game itself is very beautiful and imaginative, but I find the playing time it offers to be insufficient, because its replayability is severely limited.

I'm writing this after 62 hours of playtime. The first thing that comes to mind is that this game is a huge missed opportunity. it would be a gap-filling game, as there are quite a few sci-fi builder (or simcity like) games, but it lacks the option of free play, where the player can explore the universe in his own way. Instead, the game tells a story that could be good anyway, only a few important details are left out, this would be forgivable, but there are a few things in it that directly contradict the standard functioning of the human mind, for example, there is an enemy warship that needs to be stopped. We have a rocket engine and we have a nuclear reactor, but we don't mix them together to get a nuclear missile, but rather make a countermeasure agains missiles... Okay, countermeasure is importand, but it would be very cute to shoot back. Ok, let's move beyond that and accept that the crew is seriously idealistic. I really liked the story itself, but I'm still wondering what caused the moon to pop out or what happened in the time that passed, or how much time has passed. I would also be very interested in who built the evil raft that will chase us around, and in the end we will blast a pulsar in its face.

Overall, it's a very good little game and if you know the rules, it can be quite simple. The first 30 hours are fun, sometimes tense and exciting, sometimes depressing, as a story like this requires. Whether 30 hours is worth the price is up to everyone to decide for themselves.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI cautiously bought this game on sale having read various reviews mentioning harsh difficulty, logistical micromanagement, and ease in which players can find themselves in drawn out failure scenarios; what a mistake. This game easily ranks as one of the best I've ever played.

I purchased this game after the update addressing concerns regarding the difficulty, however I played on the default mode and barely glanced at the options to adjust the frequency of events/disasters.

Perhaps this was adjusted in the update as well, but I didn't find resource management a headache. You have to keep on top of it, and failure to do so can result in some grisly consequences, but that's part of the challenge. The player can research upgrades that makes moving resources much more efficient, however I found the most important factor was improving my own understanding through trial and error. Knowing which resources to prioritise based on your playstyle/objectives, knowing how to layout your station so resources are imported and products produced where you need them, knowing roughly how many factories you need to achieve the throughput of products your playstyle requires. Some approaches are more 'efficient' than others, but I didn't find the game rigid. I felt that there were other viable approaches that I didn't explore, but that I could on another run.

My first scenario wasn't a success. I was running out of steel, which was being used up to repair the hull at a rate that I couldn't build up enough to invest in a docking bay for an additional mining ship. At the time I was unaware that deconstructing buildings returns the full construction cost (I agree with this choice, and I think this makes more sense in a scavenging space scenario than a regular citybuilder), so perhaps I could have temporarily cannibalised a sector to get production back on it's feet. However, by this I already had a mental list of 'things I wish I could have done better', so I started again. I made it further on my second attempt before another growing problem presented itself, by this time my mental list had grown and I started again. This might sound frustrating, however at this point I wasn't trying to 'beat the game' - I had no idea what that would even look like! I was exploring the story, the tech tree, the best way of mining stuff and making stuff; and I was loving it!

The soundtrack is a masterpiece. The art style is great. The story is captivating; and I personally love how it's woven throughout the game in comments/notes waiting to be found. IXION feels like it was built around the story, rather than it being tacked on later in development. I haven't experienced a single bug (although I did purchase six months after release) and found the game to be very polished. If you're tempted, but uncertain, give it a go. I don't think you'll regret it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAfter a brilliant (and I mean polished, fun and challenging in the best possible way!) start, Ixion just kind of loses itself somewhere in the mid game. The kind of game where you replay the level before a couple of times so you might be strong enough to grind out a hard fight you know is coming.

Then, the economic model tips as your tech and economy mesh to increase your power exponentially and all of a sudden the stuff that would have been a major and that potentially game ending emergency doesn't matter all that much. Or at all. The challenge posed by the environment drops and then disappears entirely. The endgame is painfully boring. Worse still takes large blocks of time to go through, even at high speed. Putting hours and hours into a process like that kills the enjoyment of the experience and walking away before seeing the end because it's just too painful really leaves a sour taste in you mouth (at least it did for me).

To the dev - I really hope you find a way to keep that nail biting tension from the first couple systems and bring some life to the back end because Ixion really should be a contender. It's just not yet.\t\t\t\t\t
