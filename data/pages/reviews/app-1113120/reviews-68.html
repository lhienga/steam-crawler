My only major complaint is that research is VERY imbalanced. Some of the upgrades are completely game changing for the same cost as something borderline useless. For instance:

a fully upgraded Mess Hall cuts your food requirement by HALF on its own, versus a fully upgraded Drone Bay which does effectively nothing and costs more than twice as much research (just one un-upgraded drone bay will completely solve your late-game logistics problems)

So take your time, read through the research tree and plan carefully, and you'll do just fine.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat a great game, from someone who's played every citybuilder (and hates most of them by now because they're kinda all the same). Very refreshing, with lots of new mechanics, and you're constantly doing a balancing act as you try to tetris more buildings together in a way that gets you the bonuses you need.

After about 20 hours of my first playthrough, I'm already looking forward to playing through again on a harder difficulty

Similar to Frostpunk, but the fact that your resources are limited isn't an immediate threat; you have a lot of time to explore and build, without panic - until you do run out. There's a lot more flexibility than Frostpunk, there's not just one right way to build, and if you build the wrong thing, it's not the end of the world. But you do have to make compromises. There's always a risk that buildings can randomly break for a while, and you know what risks you're taking when you decide not to build a backup of that important building. When the inevitable happened, I never felt cheated; I should have known better. But if you act fast, you can usually fix it

Basically, it does a great job of giving you a threat that ramps up over time, without making it overwhelming. It felt like a few hours at a time of the usual chill citybuilding downtime, followed by a few minutes of panic as you realize you overbuilt, and now the sector's out of resources, and you haven't done the thing you need to do to move on yet

It also is (and/or has) a great story, very engaging, with lots of content and interesting exploration snippets

Unlike most games in the genre, you get to make a lot of choices as you build; the buildings you place in a sector determine its 'specialization', which gives it bonuses, but early on you're just struggling to place down buildings at all. You end up constantly rearranging and re-doing areas to try to min/max, and it's satisfying, because the building sizes and build areas are complex enough to make it challenging to fit everything together efficiently

There are also story choices, multiple endings, and if you decide to back out of a dangerous research situation, you lose whatever it could've given you forever - you could miss a lot from a bad choice

And weirdly, for a citybuilder, it had a lot of tension and excitement. Storage is relatively limited, and until endgame you're almost never full on anything, so when you notice something going low, it's too late - you need to panic and fix it, immediately, usually involving tearing down other things, or having sectors go without power as you scramble to move toward the resource you need, etc\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a gorgeous game with an intriguing story and lovely ideas that is unfortunately completely hamstrung by several horrible decisions. You play on a space station consisting of six sections, all right next to each other. Each is its own ecosystem and requires individual stockpiles for every good it will use, taking up the limiited floor space. Individual goods can be moved between sections, but only it seems, by setting request quotas, rather simply telling your transporters to move a set amount. Humans, meanwhile, CAN be moved piecemeal, and despite the huge bulkheads, transporters have no problems moving goods quickly between sectors. The main good, alloys becomes very scarce, very quickly, forcing you to economise hard, even as it's constantly drained by the need to maintain the station, and the only sources of the metal used to produce it are scattered across the systems and brought in so slowly that by the time a shipment enters the station, the last load has already been consumed, forcing a desperate hand to mouth life that was more frustrating than tense. Meanwhile, the forges that produce the materials you need require exact amounts to convert them to alloys or polymers, leaving them frozen if, inevitably, you end up with an odd amount from one of your slow moving shipments.

Despite all this, I wanted to like this game, but a tide of annoyances like these and Frostpunk-lite demands pushed me away, which is a shame.\t\t\t\t\t
