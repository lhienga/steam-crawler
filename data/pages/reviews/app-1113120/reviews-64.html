You have a few hours maybe TOTAL in the whole game where you will watch little dots go get goodies and bring them back, there is a fast forward up to 3x feature that i used sparingly because I personally always had some micromanaging I could do. Personally I used the Pause feature A LOT. I mean A LOT. There are just times where you need to take in a lot of info and you don't have a ton of time to fix things or figure it out so I paused the game during things such as opening a new Sector. I dont' think I really did much grind. I didn't min-max and get everything i could, when i felt prepped and stocked in a system I left stuff behind. You don't need EVERY material to get through the game. Just be smart. Realize that Iron is a time mechanic since you use it to constantly repair so you pay attention to how much is in the system and you're good. The ending was good, the late game had fun challenges. I didn't even need to open the last sector on my station so the game is not difficult\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe day I stopped playing IXION.

It started out fun and interesting. I've got a ship to run. It's huge and I can build it how I want. It seems weird that once you learn that using the drive doesn't just cause damage to the hull, but puts it into an increased rate of degradation, that we have to keep going. So I make sure I'm making enough material to keep it patched. I try to maximize my resource usage from our home system, but then the game tells me it wants me to move on by creating a mental problem for some of my people by staying in our home system too long.

We move on to the next star and start exploring. I open up the second section and start building. I'm planning on moving my food production to the new section and keeping manufacturing in Sector 1. Then I have a group of people come to me and complain about the noise in the industrial sector. It's constant, day and night, and they'll only be happy if I can shut the machines down for ten days, to give them some peace and quiet.

I'm puzzled. Do they realize that those machines are making the material that goes on the outside of our ship, to plug the holes, keep the air in, and keep us all alive? The leader of this group insisted, and I knew that morale was key to production, so I shut down the foundries. Two clocks started ticking. One was the time I needed to keep quiet to keep the people happy, the other was the degradation of the hull in our stationary space ship. Hull integrity kept dropping, so I turned the machines back on. I had some wiggle room with the noise concern, so I didn't fail that right away. As soon as I felt I could, I turned the machines off again. Would it be enough?

As the days crept by and hull integrity kept dropping, and my down time inched closer to its goal, I could tell we weren't going to make it. I pushed it to the last minute, hoping the quiet-loving folks would relent and let us turn on the factories to save our lives, but they held firm, they needed their quiet, and I was resigned that our whole ship would enjoy the quiet only a vacuum can bring as our hull failed.

I was surprised when the hull reached 0% and a one-time use emergency foam was deployed, stabilizing the structure, and giving me time to effect repairs. I was warned that this was a one-time use thing, and next time I wouldn't be so lucky.

It didn't matter. I'd learned that these people weren't interested in surviving. Perhaps the tragedy of the destruction of earth was too much for them, and a massive case of survivor syndrome had doomed our mission before it started. I'd hoped the last remnants of humanity would put up more of a fight, but I knew that no matter how much I wanted to keep them alive, they didn't want to survive. It was too hard, so I resigned command of the ship and volunteered at the ant farms. It wasn't long before the hull failed. I remember the klaxons, and a bit of surprise that it had happened so quickly. I wonder how long the ants will survive in their enclosure.\t\t\t\t\t
