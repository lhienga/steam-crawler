\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTo start off with a TL;DR for those who don't wish to read the paragraphs ahead on my thoughts of the game, buy the game expecting a visual and musical FEAST, but keep yourself cautious of the story and writing, primarily towards the very end of the game. You will likely be disappointed as I was. The Gameplay is great if you're a fan of Frostpunk, but if not you're in for a rough learning experience.

To properly begin the review, IXION is probably one of the most visually inspired and beautiful games of its genre, right alongside Frostpunk in sheer beauty of the environments you visit. If you can afford to with your station, try and visit every planet and locale you can to simply marvel at the sheer scale and beauty that the games artists and model artists have put into them.

The game's soundtrack is absolutely S+ tier, made by the outstanding composer Guillaume David, (Who has composed for other games such as Warhammer 40K Mechanicus). The soundtrack is filled with a wonderous combination of synth, chorus, traditional orchestra, and other wildly unique sounds and melodies to bring a matching atmosphere to the games premise.

The games premise itself is quite alluring as well, with the Tiqqun, (Your prototype FTL platform with a station attached to it), being the last hope for humanities survival after you accidentally throw yourself through time and space on your first FTL test, (Which also blew a hole through the moon, which severely affected the trout population on Earth). You must travel through various star systems in order to follow the remains of humanities last ditch effort to make it to a possibly habitable world far from the Solar System.
While most of the characters of this story are rather weakly written, one character that uniquely stands out is, of course, the Tiqqun itself. As you expand the station to house what could possibly be the last remnants of humanity in the universe, it grows in character and complexity, and you'll find yourself getting more and more attached to this lifeboat of our species.

The story itself, however, suffers towards the end of the game where unfortunately not many questions that have been asked throughout the games runtime are ever answered, and the pacing and quality of the final chapter feel...rushed. Its like they had run out of either budget or time for the final chapter and created something that dropped the ball with the rather unique and interesting story elements and mysteries that they had been alluding to. It is also unfortunately a bit buggy. Not game-breakingly so, but I happened to have accidentally "found" the alternative ending to the game before I was intended to, and as a result got shellshocked with the aftermath of a reveal I should have encountered earlier in the chapter.

So once again, play the game if you're a fan of other games within the genre like Frostpunk, or if you wish to witness a visual-auditory FEAST throughout a wonderful journey with a disappointing destination.

I have nothing but praise for the devs for what they have made here, and I'll remember everything but the final chapter fondly for the rest of my days, but I do wish that they would go back to the Final Chapter and fix it up, maybe even a revision so that the game can have the ending it truly deserves.\t\t\t\t\t
