\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFor its creativity and writing alone, I would recommend this game. The art, style, and themes they're playing with are absolutely worth the price of entry - if the primary cost to you is time. It is worth your time. It was worth the price in money to me, and I believe it would be worth that cost to you, random stranger, but that is a calculation that everyone has to make on their own.

For the review part of this: the gameplay itself is very good, though it falls short of some expectations that one would have or like to have of a game in the genre of panicked-infrastructure-management sims (Frostpunk, Factorio, Space Haven, & Anno - those are the ones I have also played). The comparisons to Frostpunk are especially sharp, though perhaps not favorable. I divide my issues into three.

Balance
Many reviews you will read about Ixion claim it is too hard. I disagree. It has its challenges at first, but they're often binary - "Did you build the right buildings? In the right places? Yes? Then you may move on. No? You explode." - and they're very vulnerable to save-scumming. Once you get past those hurdles, though, by chapter 3 you are positively cruising on the stockpile you've built up.

Especially research - gaze into the tech tree and you can find neat solutions to basically every resource shortfall you could possibly encounter. There's a small chain of techs that double the value of all food. All food! With a tech like that just buried in there, it becomes impossible to balance the game for a player with it versus one without. By the end of the game my biggest problem was docking bays getting clogged up with unspent resources. That is the wrong kind of pressure - I knew it was a problem that I wasn't supposed to have to deal with because they didn't have a button to vent all the garbage into space.

Until you had mastered its nonsense, Frostpunk knew how to push you. Every time you thought you were safe, it would ratchet up the intensity, or throw a curve ball at you. Best of all, it challenged you to willingly forgo the most advantageous options, putting a moral price on efficiency. Ixion hasn't worked out that magic yet.

Interface

When one plays games that demand you balance inputs and outputs on a razor’s edge of precision, one of the chief weapons in your arsenal is information and plans. Ixion has a lot of information – the most critical information – but it’s still missing some stuff.

One of the most glaring issues is the inability to plan your districts. Alright, sure, not every game can have Factorio’s blueprint system, but Anno at the very least had the ability to create ghost structures for free, and Ixion desperately needs it thanks to the time and difficulty of building and destroying things. The aggravation of redesigning a district pushes you to just load your game when you make a mistake or you don’t anticipate the footprint of a newly-unlocked structure.

Other interface weirdness:
You can’t name your ships. I had the Naglfar and the Atet flying next to the “Santa’s Sleigh.” This isn’t a big gripe, but I will gripe about it.

You can’t find out what astral phenomena are until you physically fly the Tiqqun through it and look at the in-ship interface to find a penalty description. Some storms are harmless. Others are beyond deadly. This is important to know without face-checking.

Once you finish an expedition, the record disappears. There’s no way to retrieve it, you have to just remember what it said. This becomes especially annoying when events happened in previous chapters that you can no longer remember. There’s a whole colorful cast of background characters, and I forgot who all of them were.

During expeditions, some choices invalidate other choices, while others allow you to loop. There is no indication which options loop back and which do not. Sometimes that’s because it’s a spoiler, but other times there’s no real justification for it.

There’s no information about astral bodies unless there’s an expedition there, and even then only once you deploy a science ship and investigate the point of interest. This feels like a writing oversight.

Save Scumming
At the end of the day, the game feels a lot like the “restart chapter” button in the exit menu was a critical design choice, and it leans on it heavily. It doesn’t want you to fix your worst problems, it wants you to fail and retry – districts are difficult to redesign, techs can’t be anticipated, expeditions barely even try to telegraph their effects, astral phenomena must be experienced to be known, and there’s also the regular genre items like resource shortfalls and unexpected shocks to the system that show you secretly lost an hour or two ago.. So, the game is very quick to tell you to just restart the chapter.

This isn’t even so much a flaw as it is a choice in the design. You try a chapter until you succeed. There is no ironman mode, because the game doesn’t really want you do that. This is not a game where you play through failure, it’s one where you reverse it. If you are going to play Ixion, you have to be comfortable with that.

Conclusion
Despite all that griping, I really enjoyed this game, and the review is a thumbs-up review. I think it’s worth it. As it stands, you will likely only play it once. It took me 45 or so hours, and I don’t regret them one bit. If you’ve been hungry for a game that’s like Frostpunk but with worse balance, cooler ideas, and different questions about morality and humanity, then Ixion is what you’re looking for.\t\t\t\t\t

- Aesthetically pleasing

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIXION is a city building game set on the interstellar colony ship, Tiqqun.

You must feed and protect the people under your care (or they'll come after you with pitchforks) and insure the hull integrity (or everyone dies) of the good ship, Tiqqun.

You are expected to help the crew to thrive and to grow. You also conduct research to gain further mastery of the technology you initially possess and develop new technologies. All of this as you face various challenges and move from star system to star system.

DESCRIPTION:
The ship Tiqqun is a right circular hollow cylinder separated into 6 sectors and as the game progresses you start from the 1st sector and move (hopefully) into the remaining 5. In each sector, you must construct buildings to feed and house your crew and to process raw ores into useful materials for buildings and to maintain the hull.

You build a LAB to provide for the advancement of technology, but your main source of research, initially, will be the science "gathered" by your SCIENCE ships. The gathered science fuels your research efforts.

There are several different ores to mine. To gain the materials necessary for buildings, maintenance, and external constructions, you must mine raw ores. You first mine the ore then transport it back to the Tiqqun for processing. You send MINING ships to separate useless rock from valuable ore. You use TRANSPORT ships to bring valuable ore (and a few other items) back to the Tiqqun.

You must have the appropriate buildings, in the Tiqqun, to process and store the gathered ores/items. Additionally, there are SCIENCE ships to gather science and to investigate points of interest (other than ore sites) in each star system you visit.

HOWEVER, to use ANY of your ships, you must construct and launch PROBES. From your stock of Polymers, you construct probes and use them to search areas within star systems for ores and science sites.

Polymers are also used to construct mining, transport, and science ships.

INITIAL PLAY:
As long as you keep hull integrity up and your people happy, that is to say FED, HOUSED, and NOT doing extra hours or being overworked, you will keep the peoples trust. You need people to have production capacity and to run farms for food and you need alloys to build housing and other things. You need to be constantly on the look out for new resources and opportunities to gather science and do research.

These things are absolutely crucial and if you do these things properly, you will have gone a long way in mastering the game.

Follow the list of goals in the upper left of the screen as you play for the first time. The only deviation I would make would be to research the Insect Farm as soon as I could. This is the first and, for a while, the only source of food you'll have. Try to research new abilities for the LAB as much as you can as well.

Watch your use of power and turn stuff off you don't need BEFORE you power up new constructions; blackouts reduce the peoples trust. DON'T LET IT GET TO ZERO!! You can, with the proper resources, build additional solar panels and other things for power .... and DON'T forget the batteries.

OPINION:
Some negative comments I've read revolve around the game being too hard. It is NOT too hard but it is unforgiving. You can enter what is termed the "death spiral" if you don't look after the peoples welfare. Unfed and un-housed people become angry people. Angry people work less, go on strike more and produce less food and material; you must plan ahead.

My first achievement was "YOU HAD ONE JOB,"a rarer achievement, but not a great achievement. I must've started and restarted the game 10 or 15 times.

DEVELOPERS:
(PLEASE note I'm a noob and some of the following may be in existence and I just don't know. Please
forgive my ignorance.)

I would like to see added/changed:

1) The ability to delete and re add individual fields for crops without having to delete the entire structure.
2) The "avoid" switches should move left and right and read 0 or 1 or on and off.
3) I think the Tiqqun should be able to move to any probed area (exhausted ore sites, for example) not just
planets, especially when ... avoiding ... certain things.
4) The ability to jettison some or all of individual stockpiles.

All in all, a GREAT, GREAT, GAME !!!

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTLDR: Great game worth every penny. Interesting enough plot with many simple yet pleasant game mechanics to keep you engaged for many hours. If you are a fan of survival city building along the lines of Frostpunk this is a must. The soundtrack for the whole game is stunning and creates the perfect atmosphere to this dystopian sci-fi masterpiece. There is at least two endings that I have seen and they are worth exploring.
PROS: Visually the game is stunning and on board buildings are supremely detailed. City building is easy to learn and does not punish demolishing buildings and redrawing the districts. System exploration is necessary and vital but never feels like a chore. In-depth descriptors for the quests you find in every system are unique and keep you engaged with the story as it unfolds. Music and ambient sounds are really good and help build up the immersion. The game does have several endings so the replay value is high both for the story's sake as well as achievements, overall efficiency and other quest outcomes. The story overall has several WOW moments that really make you sit up in your chair and I loved the thrill of sending out expeditions to figure out what is going on in the system. All of your resources are acquired through mining which you do not control directly but instead recon deposits first, then assign ships to different priorities and let them figure out the rest. The manufacturing is simple enough to figure out and set up, being able to recycle garbage and use that to make resources allows you to have more than one way to build up your industry.
CONS: Many day one issues have since been fixed but I still have some complaints. The tech tree is sizeable and the tech descriptions are obscure to a point where it is needlessly hard to figure out (especially on the first play-through) what is worth the precious tech points and what is not. Some tech is absolutely vital to your survival while others are nearly useless. The requirement to spend points to research story related tech has now been removed thankfully because budgeting research points WILL take up a lot of your time. My biggest gripe with this game is that similar to FP it also gives you societal and other challenges with "challenging decisions" but whatever you do it never leads to any serious or tangible change or consequences. It always sounds interesting and is fun to read and honestly it never gets preachy the way some dystopian sims do, but once you learn the lack of consequences the thrill is gone. Something that can be considered a bit of a spoiler is that right off the bat the game telegraphs to you how long it is going to be which is a very questionable design choice in a story that builds up suspense and feeling of hopelessness as well as it is done here. The sector specialisation mechanic is the most obscure part of the game and you will not learn it without looking at guides, they really need to change that which has to be my biggest problem with the whole game but you can comfortably finish the game never bothering with it at all.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIXION is an exceptional game that requires astute resource management, careful consideration of space for building, and strategic time management. If you possess expertise in building and resource management games, I challenge you to take on this game and complete it on your own. The satisfaction of achieving this difficult feat is immeasurable. Upon finishing your first playthrough, you'll likely have a strong desire to replay the game with the knowledge you've gained, planning your moves ahead of time. Planning is crucial in this game, and being aware of what lies ahead is vital.

For those new to building games, I recommend watching tutorials or gameplay videos on YouTube to familiarize yourself with the game mechanics. Without prior knowledge, even a small misstep can prove costly, making the game exceedingly challenging for beginners.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA great sci-fi tale, full with morsels of narrative, framed in a brutal city sim.

Normally, city builders, or city management sims, whichever you prefer, are rote stories about modern cities. There are also outliers, where you're in charge of a post-apocalyptic town, or a medieval village. This is fine, but by definition, they are limited into what the overarching narrative can offer.

NARRATIVE:

Ixion is certainly not a masterpiece of the narrative genre, but you can tell the storytellers were passionate about the subject, and the story beats are interesting, and very often, surprising. The premise of having the city you manage be a nomadic space station is ripe with narrative potential, and while the story itself is somewhat linear (there are choices you can make along the way that will determine some of the endings), the side stories are all very interesting and thought provoking, coloring a good story with an impressive variety of sci-fi tonalities.

MECHANICS:

This is a Kasedo game, from Mechanicus fame, so you already know you're in for some battering. While Mechanicus turned easier as you went along, despite a brutal start, Ixion never lets up. Keeping your population fed, content, healthy, and generally alive in the face of impossible odds, is a lofty task. While the game eschews certain genre conventions to avoid becoming overwhelming (fires aren't permanent and aren't too destructive, you can't permanently lose buildings, crime isn't a thing), the fact that the game is centered around managing a constant drain on every single resource you have, can be daunting at times.

However, the interface is clean, even if it obfuscates some things (like station events, where the PA will let you know one is awaiting input but won't let you know where, so you have to search for it on your station), and never hinders your management. The thing is that, while a lot of the logistics tasks are automated (stockpiles hoard regardless of distance and pathfinding is not an issue), less than you would expect is. Resources are manually assigned between sectors, and tech has to be researched two things at a time (if you have the science). Events have to be manually activated, and so on. So, there's a lot of stuff to do, for better or worse.

CONCLUSION:

The game is good. Great, even. It's good sci-fi, the mechanics are satisfactory, and the tension of going through the motions is undeniably there. The game has grown a lot since its release, and now difficulty is largely customizable, but I would still recommend the base experience for the challenge, if you're up for it.

Special mention to the music. We all loved Mechanicus' soundtrack, and Ixion's no slouch. Even if the tunes might seem a bit generic, the fact that I never got tired of the music after close to 35 hours of listening to it, is testament to its quality. Looking forward to more work by Guillaume David.\t\t\t\t\t
