\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSummary: It's ok. Not great but ok. I had fun, but it could have been better.

Longform: The game looks and sounds amazing, very reminiscent of The Expanse. Even the Tiqun is a dead ringer for the Nauvoo. And I LOVE the VHOLE/IXION FTL jump-effect to "self-similar space". It's mysterious, trippy, arcane, foreboding, and hopeful. Spice must flow! Later you encounter horror elements right out of Event Horizon, Doctor Who, and even Resident evil. There's even a Boss Fight at the end! I should be gushing about this.

Except the game itself just isn't that fun or engaging. The worst part is the random accidents. They happen far too frequently, even under optimum conditions. Sometimes you get major accidents with 20+ people being injured, or fatalities. But it seems completely random. As if that wasn't bad enough, once I had a Steel Mill active but idle (no iron) that suffered an accident. Why??? Were the workers so bored they tried to use the machines as an obstacle course??

Speaking of accidents, sometimes they are randomly fatal. See if this were Frostpunk, I'd expect the first death to be a major event. I'd expect your docking bay to be briefly shut down for a funeral, and an in-game scene of casks being jettisoned into space. Nope Ixion treats this as an afterthought. This is a big problem with most of the game's narrative also. You encounter strange trippy things, crew coming back from missions strangely altered. At one point a science vessel thought lost is miraculously recovered with everyone alive. But all of this is just an afterthought, game does nothing with this.

The tech tree is another problem. It's divided up into rings, and you have to research all the items in one ring, before the next is unlocked. You also can dig deeper into specific techs, like better crew housing. But the game doesn't make this readily apparent. There's only these tiny little circles under techs, that are easy to miss. I had to restart a game because of this. Also I feel the research system isn't well balanced. There are a lot of tech upgrades that could have been really useful at the beginning of the game. But by the time I had enough research points to unlock, they had outlived their usefulness.

In later stages, you get large hazard zones in space. But you can't order your ships to avoid them, or manually navigate. All you can do is click on individual objects/points of interest, and slide the "Ignore" tab. This can become very tedious if you have dozens of mining/resource sites in danger zones. Also there's the risk of your ships accidentally flying through danger zones. Manually herding them is another annoyance.

For all my misgivings, I did enjoy the game. But if you can't get a discount, or you aren't a hardcore sci-fi fan, this probably isn't for you.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIXION is a story-based space station simulator with strategy and survival aspects. As you progress through the story through events and choices made, the game can become more challenging, which is equally balanced by buildings and research that offer reprieve. The game offers fixed difficulty settings that can also be customized, even from chapter to chapter. There is an aspect of exploration that will serve as a source of income and research, as well as ship and probe management. As a hard mechanic, you will have to keep your hull in shape, which is also tied to your resource management. A second mechanic is the limit of power produced, either by solar cells or other options later on.

Graphics can be divided into two aspects: aesthetics and proportionality. Aesthetically, I found its art style very pleasing, but that aspect is subjective. I appreciated that it tries to stick to realistic and plausible design proportions without the usual exaggerations found in some other sci-fi games.

The soundtrack is, in my opinion, well-made and underlines the atmosphere of this game. The sound design itself is overall well-done.

The story itself, however, could be narrated better, especially when connected to some in-game events. Sometimes, you are either hit out of the blue with some devastating mechanic, or outcomes get so jumbled that it's unclear that you've missed something or did something in the wrong order. Luckily, the game offers some replay value, and I hope it gets expanded further in the future. Perhaps with a sequel?

Mechanically, I have only a few criticisms. The accident mechanic is not very well-thought-out, as you have little defense against it, and its only purpose is to artificially introduce temporary hurdles and delays. I realize that random events are part of the mechanics of this genre, but if the effect is simply the same with the occasional fire hazard, you don't feel like you have complete agency over your own management. It would be better if buildings had visible risks of hazards and wear and tear as well, to actually give some credible background to random negative breakdowns and outbreaks of fire. It would make the game a little bit more complex but a lot less annoying.

I have experienced only a few bugs in my game; only one forced me to reload an older save, but sometimes the game decided to hang up on loading. Otherwise, it ran smoothly.

If you like games like Frostpunk and Against the Storm, then this game might be right up your alley.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR: Beautiful world building and visuals. Amazing soundtrack. Bones of a good survival city builder. Atmosphere pulls you in, but the core game play loop is tedious, unforgiving, and for me, just feels like the illusion of choice. You will be constantly restarting. No automation for micro management systems. Everything feels completely manual. The constant degradation of the ships hull just feels like a big timer forcing me to play the game perfectly a single way before the time runs out(your hull degrades). What little rewarding feeling you get from progressing, is squashed when you realize you just have to do it all again.

I didn't read the reviews before I bought. Got the game on sale. I definitely should have, because a lot of them say exactly what I found out by playing this game.

The game isn't bad, but my god, "unrelenting" really is the most apt descriptor for this game.

The world of Ixion is beautiful. It sounds amazing. The atmosphere of a meticulously crafted sci-fi world is all here.

As a huge sci-fi nerd, I loved the idea of Ixion. I caught big Expanse vibes just seeing the Tiqqun and it reminded me of The Nauvoo/The Behemoth/Medina Station from that series.

Some of the game play here is on the right track. I like the building options, and the way the tech tree works, and the progression system. It is really cool sending your mining and transport ships out from Tiqqun to gather desperately needed resources, and any fans of Anno will recognize the limited, grid based building system, where you have to carefully manage which productions and service buildings need to go where.

The part where this game falls apart, is the outright tedium. You will be restarting, A LOT. Once things start to go down hill, there is almost no saving it, and for me this was all on the normal difficulty. You really have to be picture perfect with how you setup your buildings, how you spend your resources, what research projects and investments should be focused on, and all the while your hull is constantly deteriorating and needs upkeep. Having to be picture perfect with all of these aspects, it feels like I'm very limited in how I can actually play. It doesn't feel like city builder after a certain point. It feels like micro-management hell. Don't get me wrong, I love micro management games, but this is beyond that. Even just gathering too many resources can be detrimental because you need to have storage buildings to hold all of these resources, and if you don't build enough storage, your docks get backed up, but you can't just go building a ton of storage, because that costs valuable alloys(the key building material) and takes up space. So the answer is another tedious micro management of how many resources you need. Taking just what you need. This isn't a bad idea, but executed poorly, as there is no way to do it easily or in a fun way. You have to first manually count how many resources you think you need for that immediate moment, then find them by sending probes out, then going to your mining and transport ships and manually checking off which resources they should gather, and then remembering to un-check them once you get the amount you need. You will forget to do this often as you're juggling 100 other things like riots, and science/exploration choices, and building. Repeat this every time. There is no way to automate it and create an equilibrium.

Every sub-system in this game is like this. Completely manual.

When you open up new areas of the station to build in, your resources and population need to be manually sent into the next area. So if you build a big food production layout in area 2, you need to find out how much food area 2 needs, then subtract that from how much food you want to manually send over to area 1! But oh no! In Area 1 I have my cryopods, which is how you grow your population. So as I get more population in Area 1, the food demand goes up, so now I have to go back to area 2 and re-calculate the food debacle. Fast forward 10 minutes. I've opened up area 3. I build a refining/production hub in this sector. I must now transfer population from area 1 to area 3(again, manually). This throws the food calculations out of whack and I need to do literally everything over again to account for it. Doing something like this for almost every subsystem/mechanic of the game is how you will spend 90% of your time in Ixion

It's just simply not fun for me.

I never use guides, and on my 4th attempt I found myself googling one. I followed along with it a bit, but just got bored because it felt like I was playing the game on autopilot. It also wasn't fun.

All the while, perhaps my biggest gripe is the hull integrity. Tiqqun's hull ticks down slowly and sometimes rapidly based on where in the story you are and which choices you make. It's just a timer. That's how you have to think about it. There are ways you can slow it down and other modifiers that can help you avoid degradation, but in the end, its a timer. I hate games that give you a time limit. This is different from challenging. Challenging makes you feel good when you accomplish something. What little accomplishment you achieve in Ixion, is quickly squashed when you realize each chapter is just going to be more of what you just slogged through, and there is a constant clock ticking down reminding you that if you don't play it perfectly, you're going to lose. This combined with the aforementioned tedium of the actual game play makes me feel like I'm just wasting my time.\t\t\t\t\t
