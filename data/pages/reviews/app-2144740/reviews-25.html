\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHeartbreaking, but I'm chopping this up as a miss. I loved the first game, but something feels amiss with the second.

People have made the claim this is easier - and while that may be true in combat I found traversing the map between combat to be the majority of my deaths. These are typically sections of games that you breeze through with the right inputs. Something was chronically wrong with the spacing of travesal objects that I would often fall short, or jump over, things. The swinging poles are a great example; something felt so off (possibly aspect ratio/field of view issues?) where as I jumped towards them I felt like I was positioned correctly so that Jack would be able to grab it, but he falls instead. Out of shear paranoia I found myself dashing to each pole, to which felt like if I was viewing the connection in third person, I'd see Jack hit the pole with his shins. Something so very not right with the detection here.

Ghostunner implements a 'Hub' between missions (at least in the early stages of the game that I've played). When adding additional features to a sequel the big question is "does this add or remove value". In my experience, it removes. Ghostrunner 1 was a continuous series of back-to-back missions unfolding in a linear fashion. This leaned favourably to the game's pace, as while the combat is fast, so is the feeling of your progression. Being pulled from missions to a safe, at-my-own-pace, Hub area was jarring, and removed the sense of urgency the first game expressed.

It's possible that the Hub provided a better opportunity for story expression, but since the story is nonsensical to me, and not the strong point of the series, I think it's ultimately better to abandon thoughts of improving player access to it (if it's ultimately going to hurt other areas).

The open area was actually an interesting change of pace, and despite my desire to want the game to feel 'quick', I didn't find it tedious. I would criticise the first section and it's overall game time - significantly more (double?) than the missions prior. There's 3 distinct areas to go to, maybe these could have all been individual timed missions? It's also hard to feel a sense of urgency towards your own game-time (if you're aiming to finish missions quick) when you're driving around an open map where everything looks similar, and efficient pathways are indistinguishable against the wider landscape.

Also, and I don't know if this was my aspect ratio and field of view, but the view from the bike was horrendous. Half my screen was bike, and that was worsened any time I was going up hill. Some sections require you to go up walls and I was essentially guessing. I was guessing the right path and, if it didn't work, adjusted next life. Absolutely terrible. Naga was probably an amazing sequence for everyone else, but I couldn't help but rack up the deaths (and the frustration) with running trial-and-error blindly.

All and all this has the combat that I enjoyed from the first game, but I feel the additional aspects they've added hurt the sequel - and something went terrible wrong with spacing traversal elements considering jump/dash distances.

I feel bad to some degree. I liked Ghostrunner 1 so much - but this just isn't feeling fun and it's made worse that I can objectively break-down why.\t\t\t\t\t

I'll start with my highlights of the game before getting into why the game is a sad sequel. Beginning with the visuals, same as GR1 they're beautiful, the designer NAILED the look of Dharma (minus bad textures) and the cybercity aesthetic. The first level is a visual treat and so much fun to replay through.

The soundtrack too, incredible and also nails the vibe that they were going for. High energy, lots of synths and a tempo that makes you want to play through the game as fast as possible.

However, the design feels way off in comparison to its predecessor and the jank is inexcusable, throughout my first playthrough I managed to get out of bounds 3 times and on repeat playthroughs that number just goes up. (Made even worse by the players need to go back for collectables and look around) Collision is horrible throughout every level and getting inside bits of geometry is so easy it feels like the game wasn't playtested at all. Not to mention the horrible movement system and the way the game tries to take control away from you only to kill you by doing so, making for a frustrating experience. The amount of times I've climbed on a dead body is laughable because how does that happen? You can turn down the assistance in the options menu but there's no off setting, just low assistance.

The story is okay, if not a bit hard to follow at first. Little bit forgettable if you ask me. The only really interesting parts were the sections with rahu but I won't spoil them here but they were my highlight of the game, he calls out a lot of stuff regarding morality and the origins of the GR's

The mechanics that differ from GR 1 are fine on their own but they don't stop shoving new stuff in your face throughout the game. The last level includes new mechanics and it makes you feel like you've never actually learnt to play the game to begin improving. You're never testing your skills as much as you're just trying to understand what the ♥♥♥♥ the new thing they've given you is

The bike was a nice idea, and it can be addicting when you get it right but also feels like it wasn't tested at all. You have 0 camera control while riding it and when moving at high speeds and riding on walls, that makes it impossible to react to obstacles. When you died in GR 1, it felt like you deserved it because you made a wrong move that you COULD see coming. GR 2 breaks this by becoming a throwing ♥♥♥♥ at the wall simulator until you remember whats coming up.

The new abilities are okay, the shuriken and its upgrades make for a nice change of pace (when they work and don't switch up the mechanics seemingly at random) but they mostly feel bloated and tend to do the same thing that GR 1 abilities did.

The game is also so unoptimised it made me laugh and it has nothing to do with hardware. The game runs at the exact same FPS with max settings to low settings and Im still struggling to figure out how because the game looks almost identical to GR 1 which runs perfectly fine.

GR 2's difficulty doesn't really come from new exciting enemies with new ways to kill them, (while there are a few new ones that are fun like the whip dudes) it comes from the sheer number of projectile enemies they throw at you at once which again forces you into this trial and error gameplay loop instead of improving your skill. You can remember the entire arena your fighting in and still die because the enemy halfway across the arena you ran past at the start is still trying to snipe you. Leading nicely into enemy AI, it's broken a lot of the time. Half the time an enemy will just stop, for no reason and then once you go to kill them the game will suddenly realise that its broken and the enemy will kill you

If you played GR 1, don't ruin your impression of the franchise with this one. The game needed way more time in the oven and it's honestly just upsetting to see this game release in the state that it's in.

*side note, the GR1 re-cap is in the extras menu and there's no prompt to have said re-cap play before starting the game so anyone who plays GR 2 first or hasn't played GR 1 for a while will find it even harder to follow the story*

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGR2 is not as consistent as it's predecessor but when it hits it feels like a freight train, the opening hours aren't as strong gameplay-wise but once the memory board fills up and the upgrades start hitting the combat can hit a flow that even GR1 couldn't get for me. the addition of the motorcycle segments felt a little undercooked and downright bugged at times (especially the opening chase scene).

my initial review was critical and while i do still stand by those points i would recommend the game regardless, it won me over

also Kira was the best character addition she's the best
--
for context, i love the original Ghostrunner and it's basically one of the best games of its genre. so going into this felt so instantly underwhelming it's difficult to justify putting in more than the scant 2 hours i did. from the bizarre changes from GR1 like making each combat zone so wide and massive to strapping you to a tiny hub and forcing you to listen to exposition, it feels like an entirely different team made some of these segments.

had to put the game down when the third mission places you in a gigantic pseudo-open zone with three objectives spaced obnoxiously far from one another to go complete. if you played Mirror's Edge Catalyst you'll kinda get what that feels like.\t\t\t\t\t
