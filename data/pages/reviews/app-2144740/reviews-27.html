\r\n\t\t\t\t\ttl;dr If you are here because you're wondering if GR2 is as sequels should be - a fun extension on the existing formula you liked in GR1, then it delivers on that. If you're hoping that the game is flawless, unfortunately it has numerous little flaws but I still have enjoyed the game a lot.

GR1 was great, it had a lot of fun mechanics and generally had an interesting enough story while letting you get to the juicy bits right away - solving fun parkour and/or combat puzzles and going fast. This game delivers on much the same, but with a few tweaks to the formula.

First, there's some motorcycle bits about halfway through the game, and there's some other fun movement you get to play around with in the last full level - both are great inclusions. Gameplay-wise this is a step up from GR1, it feels more refined and I feel like the game actively encourages the use of abilities unlike GR1, where I felt like you had to hold onto your abilities as long as possible. Shuriken, Shadow, and Tempest are all interesting and different enough too that they solve different problems. They've also moved away from tetris ability grids to something far more comprehensible while still being deep. All of these gameplay elements and the music and graphics were GR1's strong suits, and GR2 continues in that same vein.

As for the things I didn't like, well, the story is the biggest thing. I also don't know why they included a hub in a significant chunk of the game; it kills the pacing when you're just trying to enjoy parkour/combat fun. This is a noticeable step *back* from GR1, where I believe the telling of the story being woven into the traversal parkour bits was brilliant; Project Hel in particular did a great job of not letting the story or development of characters get in the way of gameplay. GR2 still has that dialogue woven into the gameplay, and those moments are the best when they're telling the story, but when they tell the story in the hub it feels like they copied the worst elements of Bethesda/Bioware RPGs and threw them into a fast-paced parkour game.

The characters are also just.... boring. The best example of this I have is a spoiler which I'll include below, but in summary they took zero risks with the story to the point that significant moments of character development had 0 emotional impact when they should have been a huge deal. In particular, I'm referring to when Conner gets decapitated and the player characters all react a little surprised, but kinda just move on with a few passing mentions of Conner in the following missions. There's no grieving, because Conner is a condescending, corrupt, power-hungry, and unlikable character. Character death should not be met with apathy, and he was neither enough the hero nor enough the villain to instill any other emotion than that. Combine this with such a scatter-shot focus on themes that they couldn't really decide what they wanted to do with Jack, and so when you get to the ending sequence and see what the characters are deciding to do with their lives, Jack's decision comes completely out of left field. It's just a boring, underwhelming, nonsense conclusion to Jack's story arc, and none of the characters who supported him all the way to this point grow or change at all.

Hub/story aside, some of the missions feel a little uninspired at times, or they didn't lean heavily enough into a great concept and weave it into the remainder of the game. For example doing hardcore mode in the first boss fight does nothing to meaningfully change the way you fight the boss besides making him more of a damage sponge - boring, especially because in hardcore you have access to *all* your abilities from the start, so some kind of meaningful interaction between his abilities and Jack's would have made a big difference. To make matters worse on bosses in particular, you can't build combo on bosses so any combo-based builds are kneecapped in a pretty significant way, so even if they did add ability mechanics, my particular build would have to change in order to effectively handle the boss: again, boring.

And as for that last full mission I mentioned earlier, the mechanic they added felt excellent. It was fun, and changed the way I handled movement in a way that didn't feel intrusive - and yet we only get that mechanic for a single mission.... Huh? Why? It was fun! They could have thrown that in earlier and let us play with it the whole game and it would have been a really interesting addition to the parkour, yet they didn't?

Ultimates are yet another problem, your strongest moves are cooldown-based. There are 5 of them which you unlock by increasing your motherboard level. The fact there are 5 alone when Jack has normally 3 abilities reveals they had too many ideas and couldn't decide which to commit to, so they threw all those ideas into one slot. That's... okay, I guess. But not once in my main gameplay did I feel like they were a big enough deal that I really wanted to use them, especially because being gated behind a time-based cooldown made it feel like I would want to save them for a super hard fight that just never came.

As for motherboard level, they unfortunately took a good idea from GR1 and made it not-so-great in GR2. In GR1 they had collectables you could get around the map that are purely cosmetic, which is great. They added gameplay-affecting pickups in GR2 to this pile of collectables, which feels so incredibly disruptive to the pacing of the mission in much the same ways the hub felt like it, especially because you can't teleport throughout the map once you get to the end like you can in Doom, so if you miss an upgrade? Too bad! Go back to the beginning of the level and fetch it there. This also felt like a big misstep, and is something that Hardcore mode does not contend with because you start with all upgrades in hardcore mode.

Which, leads me to my last point. Why was the final boss less mechanically rich than the second-to-last boss? In fact, the final boss reminded me significantly more of the *first* boss than the one before him. Why? Not only that, but that awesome movement mechanic I mentioned that was introduced the level before? It's turned off for the final fight T_T. It is an underwhelming experience when the gameplay leaves you on such a high moments before. Bummer!

Still, despite all of these criticisms I can't help but say that this is still in many ways an improvement on GR1. In my opinion, Project Hel at this point is peak Ghostrunner but I'd put GR2 in second-place. I also am quite enjoying the difficulty spike and mechanics present in the Hardcore mode of the game, and so I'll continue playing through that and likely enjoy it the rest of the way through. I'd advise picking it up on a sale, since it is regularly going on sale for 40% off now, and GR1 was much the same. I'm looking forward to the next iteration on the formula, and I'm hoping a lot of the issues present in GR2 get dealt with before then. Cheers!\t\t\t\t\t

- Combat is far more interesting while still retaining the puzzle-like feel of the first.
- Skill tree is much better and while its no longer goofy Tetris, certain skills have positional requirements that demands some thought.
- Actual boss fights that aren't "platforming lol" or "parry 3 times in a row"
- Some really cool ideas for levels (no spoilers here)
- Soundtrack is a banger
- Hardcore mode feels a lot more fleshed out

The only real downsides are:
- The cybervoid sections are actually really forgettable or extremely unfun.
- The movement changes on the last level are...rough
- Slightly buggy (Dying to a parried attack, climbing over your surge waves (though there's probably sick skips you could do with this one))
- No instant skipping cutscenes on repeat playthroughs
- They made the weebs even weebier

Game owns, def worth the price and is a steal on sale

The parkour running sequences are really fun and satisfying, especially when more abilities are required in quick succession to make it through a tough zone.

The cons:

The new ways the story is told (having to talk to people at "base" etc) as well as the dialogue itself is really annoying and just the opposite of what I appreciated from the first game. In GR1 the story was told as you were running, now the game spends a lot of time slowing you down and making you stop which is very frustrating in a mobility based wallrunning parkour ninja game.

The combat, as a lot of people have rightly pointed out, doesnt have the same flow as the first game. In fact it barely has any at all, the game just splits enemies up in seemingly arbitrary spots and you arent flowing from enemy to enemy in a rhythmic way like GR1. Now you fight, you stop, go to the next group, fight again, stop. It just feels worse and not as satisfying.

I don't like the new skill allocation system as much. I appreciate what they were trying to achieve, but reallocating skills is a pain, making custom builds is a pain cos (without a specific skill) you're limited to the types of skills you've already got. As weird as it was, I prefer the funny little tetris system from the first game.

It's STILL too short. For 70gb of game for me to do a full playthrough in as short a time as I did is really disappointing and I felt it in the first game too (which I suppose is a good problem to have because I did just want more of it).

Conclusion:

There were really fun additions, you'll like it if you like this kind of game (or the first one), but it's hard not to notice the flaws and where they could've (potentially) have done better. Lost a bit of the original magic.

PS should've added the ability to turn the camera while on the bike.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAn improvement in every way from the original, which was already amazing. 3D Katana Zero, with a deep but not overwhelming combat system, and all the tools you need to overcome it. Enemy variety is decent, but it is disappointing that besides two or three units, all three of the major enemy factions feel basically the same.

Cybervoid, and parkour in general, is much improved, and the two major level-dependent mobility tools feel wonderful, though the motorcycle has issues with locking your vision directly forward, making it difficult to navigate through sharp turns or when climbing tunnel walls.

The game suffers a little on the technical level; my rig is a bit frankensteined, but it's no joke - 48 gigs of RAM and an AMD 5700. Still, I had *severe* frame stutters in very odd locations, and always during combat arenas or parkour segments, where a few frames of reaction time is literally the difference between success and a restart.

Overall:
10/10 Gameplay
10/10 Music - seriously, it's perfect
7/10 Story - while there might be some translation issues, sometimes characters would discuss new information as if it had already been revealed, and the main threats of the game felt vague and unimportant at times. Still don't know why Mitra was doing anything. Still, though, the dialogue is well-written and characters feel unique, especially the two former Keys.
8/10 graphics - Art design carries this game hard, it's beautiful. Character faces aren't very well-animated, but the movement, environment, and violence are, and that's what matters.\t\t\t\t\t
