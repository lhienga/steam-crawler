\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOverall, GR2 is a great game and a worthy sequel. The combat and traversal has changed for the better. Despite the semi open world section in the middle of the campaign, the game doesn't feel too different from what makes Ghostrunner special. However, while I do recommend the game it is worth mentioning that the sequel innovates in a lot of ways that are different than the first, not all levels are linear for instance. Another thing I would like to mention is that at times the game can be a bit janky and buggy (failing to be able to run on a wall/getting stuck on geometry), but this isn't egregious. Something that GR2 did considerably worse than the first game is the music. I'm not sure if it was caused by a bug or if this is by design, but some levels straight up do not play music or leave long periods without it. And speaking upon the music itself, it is very underwhelming compared to the first game's soundtrack which was excellent. It was a grave mistake to not have Daniel Deluxe be the main artist in this soundtrack, in fact, he is only in around 2 out of the 23 tracks. Overall, for GR1 fans I still recommend the game despite it's minor faults.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is the video-gamiest video game I could ever have wanted, and it's amazing. The original Ghostrunner was a surprise hit after I discovered it during a Steam Sale, and this could not have been a better follow-up.

For one: I appreciate this game's tight focus and lack of padding... with one exception, which we will get to. There are so many games, old and new, where I feel my time is wasted. Ghostrunner 2 is never like that. The levels progress rapidly, alternating between real-world and Cybervoid sections divided into platforming and combat, and the variety is appreciated. One of my few criticisms of the first Ghostrunner was its, while beautiful, repetitive neon Dharma design. This game does a much better job differentiating environments!

That pace is matched by the story. There are no threads which drag. Each play session (only around 1 hour) felt like I truly advanced in the game and pushed the plot forward. Compared to the first game, this plot is a massive step up. There is much nuance required in tackling a narrative set after the Big Bad has been defeated, which is where Ghostrunner 2 picks up, but the writers were up to the task. Quality dialogue and a much expanded cast of characters also break up the repetition felt in the first game.

The RogueRunner side mode is alright, but it becomes repetitive fast, as it is all set within the Cybervoid. Having to replay all levels to unlock each new reward is discouraging, but roguelites were never my thing anyway. I am glad this is a side mode instead of the major focus of the game.

The graphics are beautiful—not cutting-edge, but enough that you will never notice a difference in motion. Ray tracing support is missing, unfortunately. Picking the DX12 launch option brings up the DX11 game, for some reason. Forcing the game to launch in DX12 using command line arguments revealed why: the ray tracing option is laggy and unfinished. I only played with it for a little while before disabling it. For context, the first game ran at a nice 60 FPS with occasional dips on my 3080. Dharma's many puddles will never quite match the beauty from Ghostrunner 1.

The gameplay is excellent as always. I found myself stuck on "tough rooms" less often, which could be either down to my skills improving (yay!) or this game being more polished to tone down difficult spikes. Still, I would encounter an extra-challenging room every once in a while that made me appreciate the variety of routes and abilities in the sequel.

The boss fights are a massive improvement. The first game's Hel fight was terrible. Parry, parry, parry, parry... the bosses here are much more dynamic. There is still an inescapable jank, especially to the Dismantler fight, but that cannot be avoided.

There is one section of the game I would like to discuss here that is a minor spoiler. If you haven't played the game yet, why not? Go buy it right now and get back here once you're finished!

Ghostrunner 2 introduces a motorbike with extended bike sections. I found their pace to flow smoothly with the other fast-paced levels of the game. The initial sequence in particular was challenging, but a solid tutorial and a good sell on the new mechanic.

Unfortunately, the worst part of this overall excellent sequel is the level "Pillars of Creation." For whatever reason, this level eschews the established linear format for an open area with three objectives to complete in any order. The open design kills the pace and pumps the brakes on what was otherwise a red-hot progression. The dialogue during this section was its saving grace, as the characters and their discussions were genuinely interesting and funny.

Ghostrunner 2 is the sequel I wish other games could execute. While the beginning feels like more Ghostrunner 1, the midgame onward expands the world, story, and design in a way I found quite natural. It does not feel like a forced sequel plot; rather, it highlights how unstable the world was at the end of the first game. The gameplay is stellar and playing it in bursts never fails to hit the dopamine receptors. Highly recommended!\t\t\t\t\t

As of right now, GR2 is in a similar situation. Having completed the game already a few times both on normal and hardcore mode, it gets pretty boring after a while to replay the same levels. The game is good, but don't expect something groundbreaking.\t\t\t\t\t

Hardcore mode is a lot of fun and I kind of wish it was the default. My comments on the difficulty curve and level design don't apply to hardcore mode so far.

Original review:I don't love or hate this game, in some ways it is better than the first. It feels smoother for example.
However, the level design and encounter design is just worse. The difficulty curve is basically flat and doesn't evolve until much later in the game when you gain a new thing that i won't spoil here.

Like, if you've played the first, you probably remember the laser tower and how fun that was to overcome. Nothing like that in this game so far (and I'm in the last level at time of writing).

Yes, the open world level is a boring slog, but it is just one level that could have been split into 3. So I think other people hark on that one too much as the stand-out example of the blasé level design. The motorcycle itself is fun to drive in the curated sections that it is in.

All that said, I think the bosses are better, the writing is better, and the way it feels to play is better. So, it's a mixed bag.
Though the detracting elements mean I'll be more likely to replay the first game rather than a 2nd play of this one. If I could give a thumbs sideways I would. Decided on a thumbs up because I did enjoy most of my time with this one.

P.S. if you're concerned about the temp of your GPU, turn the post processing and effects to low. They won't cause stuttering (or didn't for me), but I would watch my GPU temp yoyo in some areas until I turned those down.\t\t\t\t\t
