\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really wanted to enjoy the sequel to one of the best movement games I've played in recent years, but Ghostrunner 2 falls short of its predecessor in every way other than in the visuals and sounds. The single largest issue I have with this game is the feeling of losing to the game rather than my own skill. What this means is that when playing through the original Ghostrunner, most of the time I would die and think to myself "Oh, I messed up. I need to do this or that differently this time", while in Ghostrunner 2 when I die I have one of two thoughts: 1. "How was I supposed to know that was there" or 2. "Oh come on that was bull".

The original Ghostrunner did an excellent job of making you feel like your skill was the difference maker when it came to success. Ghostrunner 2 on the other hand makes the gameplay less about bridging the gap with your skill and more about learning through error, aka dying repeatedly to things that you would not have time to react to upon the first encounter. This leaves you with a feeling of "well that was ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ but at least I know its there now" instead of it being a skill issue.

This feeling of dissatisfaction is doubled when you are on your fourth try through a room and for some reason the grapple decides it doesn't want to attach you to the rail or wall to run along. Somehow the parkour feels consistent. One time you'll grapple and it'll flow extremely smoothly, and other times you'll feel as though the game bugged and now you have to repeat another large and mostly empty room.

This brings me into the level design of Ghostrunner 2. The simplest way I can put it is that I want every single map shrunken in half and all of the exposition exported to the hub area. With a larger focus on story the team seems to have separated the levels into two different categories. The first being tight, well executed levels that flow very well and allow for your skill to shine. The other being long, drawn out levels that have you jumping between 7 walls instead of 3 or riding 5 rails instead of 1, all because they want to provide ample time for the characters to finish their dialogue. This breaks up the pace of the game considerably, and leaves you feeling as if getting through the level is a chore.

The enemies overall are also more frustrating due to inconsistency. It might be a result of the size of the levels but I'm not a game dev so I can't really tell you what exactly feels so annoying about them.

A quick comparison:
Ghostrunner 1: Tight levels, readable enemies, consistent parkour, responsive controls, excellent pacing and flow throughout
Ghostrunner 2: Large levels full of sections with excess dead time with no interesting parkour or combat, enemies that are more annoying than fun to fight, combat arenas that are too large and are a chore to navigate around (unless you enjoy spending most of your gameplay on a conveyor belt that glides you forward or pressing "E" 10 times in a row to grapple from rail to rail).

The most positive aspect of the game is easily the motorcycle portion, it makes you feel the speed and tightness that the original Ghostrunner nails throughout. Although I'm not a fan of the open world design that has you drive the bike to different mini levels, as it feels like time bloat for the game. The audio is superb, and the visuals are fantastic.

Hardcore movement shooters are extremely fun, but they need to be made in tight levels with responsive and consistent parkour plus fun enemies. Both of these feel like a step back compared to the original Ghostrunner. Again, I really wanted to enjoy this game and I'll be giving the roguelike mode a try to see if it resolves some of the issues the campaign has. With that said, the single largest critique I have of this game is that bigger is not better.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA good step forward from the first game.

If you liked GR1, you'll love GR2. The combat is mostly the same with some substantial improvements (the stamina system does wonders) and additions (not all abilities are ultimate abilities that have absurd cooldowns, giving the player a lot more flexibility to pull off some flashy moves).

The story doesn't DIRECTLY tie into the first game, but rather expands on the world that we saw in the first game. Most (if not all) characters do make a return appearance in the game in one form or another.

The environments are phenomenal, and seeing the outside for the first time gave me goosebumps. The additional traversal options in the game definitely complemented this, from the bike to the wingsuit.

I would love to see more from Ghostrunner, and the ending does set up a decent possibility for a sequel.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR: Highly recommend. I hope the Ghostrunner series gets more entries.

The good

  • Playable on Linux
  • Performs well
  • There is an FoV slider and it goes up to 120
  • The motorcycle is awesome
  • There is another very cool movement option introduced later in the game that I won't spoil here
  • The cybervoid levels are still a thing, now with more movement options
  • Great soundtrack
  • Much better bossfights than the previous game
  • Enjoyable/fluid movement systems; I genuinely have no idea what people are talking about when they claim it's "janky" or similar

The technical problems

  • Can't bind any action to T; the game complains that it's already bound elsewhere, even though it definitely isn't
  • Menuing (in particular, selecting dialog options) is hardcoded to WASD rather than based on your movement keybinds
  • FoV on the motorcycle isn't modifiable
  • Can't free-look with the mouse while on the motorcycle; maybe this was a design decision, but sometimes it makes it difficult to visually parse the environment while on the motorcycle

Conclusion

It's clear the developers have learned from the previous entry, took that experience, and used it to produce this fantastic game. I would have been happy with just more of the same because I liked the original so much, but I think they really knocked it out of the park here.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWould I recommend this game? Kind of yes and kind of no. It depends on what you're looking for.

The good

-Combat is as smooth as ever.
-Parkour feels great and has also seen improvements + additions that fit somewhat neatly into the gameplay loop. All the nice little things like crouch sliding accels are still there and give huge potential for different ways of finishing the levels. The new stuff could have been used a bit more though, imo. There is LOADS of untapped potential in the parkour elements and most of it gets used too sparingly for my liking.
-Upgrade system is way better than in GR1 and gives the user great variety to customise and maximise their desired builds. Probably the biggest improvement of GR2.
-Boss fights were (mostly) fun, fair and challenging. Good multi-stage fights.
-Cybervoid areas. Probably my favorite part as they became even whackier and more grand than in the first game. Still, I wanted more of them.

I tend to enjoy the parkour areas of Ghostrunner the most, the combat is just kind of a nice extra challenge that adds spice to it.


The meh/bland

-Environment. While some parts of the game are outright gorgeous (the Cathedral area was mind-blowing and I wish there had been more of it), the majority of the Outside is bland, sandy and dead...which makes sense but it sure doesn't make it interesting.
-Story + characters. The story itself has some cool ideas but doesn't really flesh them out properly. Both the Climbers and the Asura stay mostly bland and are kinda forgettable. It is also kinda of remarkable how GR1 had way better facial expressions than GR2 (look at the final cutscene of the Architect and then compare that to any of the ingame sequences in this part).
By the end of it, I was wondering what was going on - probably wasn't paying enough attention but that just goes to show how little I actually cared. Missed opportunity to expand meaningfully from GR1.
-The music. While it isn't bad per se, there just weren't any tracks blowing me away quite as hard as Razor, Air or Let Them Know - which also coincides with the fact that I used to be way less often "In The Zone", where I just perfectly executed maneuvers and enemies. The music is still good but not as great as in GR1.


The janky/bad

-Jank. This game suffers from a decent amount of jank. From challenges spawning you almost 180° to your actual goal to you clipping through the environment, not being able to grab ledges, ignored inputs... this game will frustrate sometimes due to all of that.
-Motorcycle sequences. Imo they could have never been good but in their current stage, they're either boring or straight up frustrating. The super fixed camera makes driving around in loops more guess work than anything else and I have crashed countless times due to not being able to see where I was supposed to be going. Either have the camera gently look towards the objective or let us look around a bit.
Also, a good hour or so is spent driving around the wasteland which gets boring very quickly. Gone is the fantastical environment of Dharma (which had SO many potential new locations with vastly different architecture - and thus loads of potential for more varied parkour) - welcome to generic sandy wasteland.
-Game flow. The first GR could be played pretty much nonstop from start to finish. No overbearing cutscenes, no awkward walking around your base talking to meh characters, you sit down and play. GR2's flow suffers tremendously from all the talking and walking in between the fun parts.
I never felt truly "In The Zone" (as mentioned earlier) due to this - either the walking around killed my momentum or the environments/combat areas themselves just weren't quite as neatly/tightly desiged as previously. Probably a case of "bigger is not always better". This might be personal preference, though.
Hope there will be an option to just skip the hub and select upgrades in a menu screen so you can jump right into the next map.
-Level design. Gone is the neat, tightly designed flow of the levels in GR1, GR2 tends to go bigger but loses its grip on the immaculate flow of combat and parkour. The outdoor missions are boring and often confusing as to where you're supposed to go next (an issue I never had in the first game), the parkour elements get used mostly to get to a fighting arena which then is often very dumbed down, often not nearly vertical enough and too linear. A far cry from the first game, sadly, which managed to marry those two aspects so seamlessly. Here it felt like the focus was too strong on either parkour or combat, depending on the situation, making both feel kind of undercooked.


Ultimately, GR2 shows some improvements in its core gameplay but its game flow (which was one of the major things I loved about GR1, it was just no-nonsense gaming) was sadly lackluster for me and the motorcycle didn't feel great - it was more of a neat little addition that outstayed its welcome quickly. Ghostrunner feels best when you're dodging enemy fire whilst wallrunning, jumping, sliding, dashing, slicing - there's so much finesse and fun to be had when doing that...and none of it exists while on this motorcycle.

The game just makes me wish for more - more parkour, longer parkour parts which utilises all of the new movement sets (during combat, as well!), more of Dharma, more colours, more variety. It has all of it but not enough of it - and instead wastes it on boring outside bike sequences and an ugly, suprisingly linear wasteland.
So much untapped potential. Hopefully we'll see it expanded in following updates/DLCs.

It's still a fun game but if you loved GR1 for the reasons I did (parkour, game flow), I would either give the demo a shot or wait for a sale.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGhostrunner was very much a surprise hit for me and others. And I'm happy to say that Ghostrunner 2 is a worthy sequel. It's a breath of fresh air to finally get a game that is very much about the game and not so concerned about being a boring movie walking simulator that seems to be everywhere now. Not only that, but it's also nice to have a game that is finished??? No way! It's like, what should be the standard and it isn't! That being said, I've had one fatal crash and frames would dip below 20 frames but only during cutscenes was this issue prominent. Let it be known however that yes, this is just more Ghostrunner albeit fine-tuned with its combat and some new ways to play. It is still super fun. The biggest complaint I have with this game though is a new character named Kira. And no, it's not because she is a "black female character" although I see it as no coincidence she's there for those sweet ESG points. She's insufferable, incompetent, and down right annoying because of course she has that "MCU" effect and the annoying pop culture references with her. Wouldn't be a thing to complain about if she wasn't such an invasive presence and you always hear her dialogue especially during the outside missions. Remove Kira and this game would be a 10/10.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI was hoping this game wouldn't have the similar issues as Dying Light 2, yet here we are. Just some improvement areas:

- Character is way too slow, it's like playing on slow mode in GR1
- Air dash stops your momentum, which in GR1 you could use it to get a few more meters while you were swinging yourself
- Being in the air doesn't feel natural, it feels like something grabs you, slowly takes you up and down like a child playing with a toy plane. There is something really unnatural about your movement in this game.
- Those priest-like enemies with two leashes on each hand are annoying and break most of the flow. They are dumb, not too hard to kill, but still annoying.
- Sword ninjas sometimes glitch on obstacles and when they start animation while stuck, they instantly come near you and kill you.
- Sword blocking is lazy and not really useful, it's there because I think devs also noticed you can't fly in the air with speed, but some kind of pre-defined animation and you actually take a long time to land. This is especially obvious with machine gun enemies, you cannot just use the momentum and cut them, you have to block a few of those rounds.
- I stopped reading and engaging conversation with NPC's after the first HQ sequence. IT'S BORING AF! Only thing I follow is the conversation between two guards outside boss man's office.
- There are challenges, but similar to Doom, you have to find them and you have no map reveal to view every detail.

I still haven't finished the game, but these are the issues I've seen. Hopefully it'll get better. Still have a potential, so I recommend trying it.\t\t\t\t\t
