\r\n\t\t\t\t\twish i could give this a mixed instead of positive or negative, because its both and neither.

there's more mechanics, and some are good and equally some are bad. the flying jet is such an obvious new thing, but its so fun to use..
....and then there's the bike. and everything that came with it sucked.
the big levels. waay too big and it's very easy to get lost in the map. the bike is also the most bugged mechanic in the game and i had to restart to checkpoint so many times just because of it.

speaking of the levels: the beginning ones were great. and then they turned bad. really bad. i liked the arcade feeling of the first game. this second iteration is definitely more fleshed out: there's a waaay bigger story than in the first game. the scales are way bigger and it shows through the level design. and yet, at some point, it stopped feeling like ghostrunner. and what i loved about this game is that there was nothing like it: a movement-focused first person slash game. that's it, simple! but the big levels, the go from point A to point B to activate a lever in a tower instead of just slash enemies made me miss what the original ghostrunner had: simplicity.

and let's not mention the performance issues and the bugs. throughout the first 3-4 levels all i thought was: man so glad for this bug-free experience. and then they kicked in. sometimes, i just wouldn't die: i would fall to eternity, out of the map; sometimes i would just be stuck, you guessed it, the moment i got out of the bike. again, that damn bike: it was a mistake. if there is ever a Ghostrunner 3 (which im asumming there will be, considering the post-credits scene), please never re-introduce the bike. and there were so many times my fps would just drop unexpectedly, and when i would die all the stutter would be gone. then i would die again: and the stutter would re-appear. idk.

still, it was fun. the graphics are a beauty, the mechanics are smooth and there's nothing like it out there. but man, this just didn't feel right. unlike the first one, this doesn't have as much replayability.

still, appreciate the devs for the game. but even though i gave it a positive review, it's more like a 6/10 than anything.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tMan, I'm a HUGE Ghostrunner fan to the point that I pre-ordered Ghostrunner 2 knowing that I will 100% play and enjoy it. It's honestly a fun game and had a lot of potential, but it definitely needed more time in the oven, especially the bike section. It's a good game, don't get me wrong, it's just worse than the first.
The 2 gripes that I have with it are the addition of blocking and stamina.

Also:
-The dialogues have many awkward silences.

-Performance wise, it has stutters which become annoying when you're trying to speedrun or do the roguerunner.exe mode. Also it just ruins the first impression... (For instance: The first time I've seen an "epic" cutscene of me beating a boss...I had some truly epic stutters which just turned it into a slideshow. And yes the game's on a Samsung 990 PRO, RTX 3080, 64GB RAM and a Ryzen 5900x, the hardware should be more than enough)
-The outside bike level was just bad, with the bike getting stuck on random objects, atrocious fixed camera, not very intuitive paths and just poor level design. The whole open area just felt...random, out of place, boring and empty.

-The enemies aren't consistent. Some give you plenty of time to react to their attacks, while others just instantly destroy you (the projectile ones, they could shoot one in your face as soon as you enter a new area) without a warning, at times feeling like the death wasn't even justified.

-Perfect parrying - taught early on, useful only against 1 enemy which barely shows up. (And some bosses)

-Laser enemies shooting endless beams...Jumping over them is a pain.

-The boss attacks also barely have any windup, basically insta-killing you whenever you see a new attack, thus forcing you to restart the level until you win. Most bosses felt like brainless button mashes, checkpoint, die x5, button mash again to victory. I expected to be forced to perfect parry, learn their attacks, dodge left, right, grapple up, hit a finisher. I don't know man, something, but this wasn't it.

While replaying the game, I did realize that they do have some windup, and it's possible to perfect parry them. However I bet most people will just brainlessly mash buttons since it works, just like I did. The final boss however, was pretty good.

-On the topic of enemies and level design.. dying to a bullet from the other side of the room always feels cheap. How am I supposed to know it's coming towards me? There's also "groups" of enemies which just behave differently on each restart, one time they go near a barrel and you can easily dispose of all of them, the next restart they go heck knows where and you're forced to manually dispose of them...kind of annoying when you're going for faster times.

The final boss and final new mechanic in the game felt really good, and was fun.
Regardless, I'll probably change this review after a couple patches. but for now...It remains as it is.

TL;DR
The game is good, but not polished, thus I don't think it deserves the 90% positive rating it has at the moment. Plus it requires less skill compared to the first game due to introduction of blocking. Stamina is more of an annoyance than something you have to keep an eye on.

8/10 (while the first is a solid 9)

(I'll change the review to positive if the stuttering issues are fixed)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHere's one of those times when the production value skyrockets, everything gets upgraded, expanded, innovated upon, and the end result is worse for it. Props for the devs who clearly put in a lot of passion and effort, but they spread themselves too thin.

Bottom line is though, if you're looking for Ghostrunner 1 but better, this ain't it. Not yet anyways.

As far as the good goes, Ghostrunner 2 - as the name suggests - have two things going for it:
1) All things aesthetic, from the appearance of the levels, to the VFX, to the feel of slicing through enemies is a tremendous step up from the first game.
2) Likewise, the overhauled leveling and combat mechanics not only expand your suite of badass cyberninja playstyles tremendously, but do away with a lot of the less-ninja things about the first games (in particular the somewhat wacky ultimate). From being an inexorable blocking tank, to a zippy maniac who reaches mach 10 after 5 kills, or stealthily evading chokepoints and taking out enemy lynchpins from behind, the potential is extraordinary - and scarcely put to use.

Because here we get to the weird, the bad, and the wacky-ass nonsense:

FIRSTLY! Let's talk narratives. It's clear that the devs wanted to emphasize the story and struggles surrounding various characters from where the story left off. The first game had a simple but well-told story that developed passively and with spectacular pacing, if you don't care you can just jam to the music and ignore the dialogue, if you do then you're in for quite the ride with (mostly) spectacular VO. Unfortunately here you don't have a choice, since between most missions you are thrown into a hub where you small-talk with a cast of blank-eyed characters who will vomit exposition at you if you ask them about the weather. Bakunin especially looks and moves like an inebriated fish. It's nice the devs had the resources to have characters you can interact with, but the purpose, substance, and quality of those interactions was clearly not well thought through and just turns your hub into a psychedelic nightmare.

But if you want a narrative-driven story, be my guest, I love these sorts of things. Couple problems though. First, pacing. The first game took place in the course of a handful of in-game hours, every level led directly into the next without pause nor interruption, you were a cyberninja making his way up the tower of babel while its defenses scrambled in desperation to stop you. You were the instrument that single-handed turned the tide of a war in a matter of mere hours. How about Ghostrunner 2? That takes place over the course of - as far as I can tell - well over a month. You dip in and out of this narrative in 20-30 minute intervals, and between each one people will lament how much harder the times are getting while you get to see none of this struggle actually unfolding, it's always business as usual but with a bit of snotty sadness on the side.

It also bears mentioning that the writing is mediocre and the antagonists that you're supposed to be afraid of are wet noodles who either ♥♥♥♥-talk you or get ♥♥♥♥-talked by you. If you want me to be scared of the enemies put before me, you probably shouldn't have be instantly annihilate one of them on first contact. In fact the only thing GR-74 failed to defeat in this game was a locked door... Likewise, Jack is a non-character, that's how he was set up in the first game too, that's fine until you try to make him be everyone's friend and give him the personality of a mental health chatbot. Also Connor was a fine dude, nothing he did or said was itself all that problematic, it's just everybody else seemed to hate but also need him? At least his 'character moment' was a pleasant surprise from a game that otherwise struggles to convey stakes.

SECOND! Level design, combat difficulty! You have a fresh and well-made new combat system, and what do you do but make the game too easy to require any real use of your abilities and the levels so enormous that all you're doing for most of them is flying around and goomba-stomping individual enemies on individual platforms. You're barely given the opportunity to actually slice & dice dynamically, instead having to navigate some of the most awkward and out-of-place levels I've ever seen in a game while occasionally cutting somebody in half.

And cutting somebody in half is just about it - combat is insanely easy. Gone are the days of Ghostrunner 1's sweaty rages, as you slowly work your own into a perfect rhythm to pass each combat encounter after 20+ deaths. Welcome to Puzzlerunner, where you ride motorcycles, can fly, move platforms, do some Mario stuff, and when you finally reach a boss you can just gimmick them down in the time it takes to microwave your leftovers from yesterday.

THIRD! Bugs! Because the enemies may be soy and helpless, but the walls, surfaces, and platforms you need to use to get from A to B will jank out all the time and kill you plenty more than any actual combat encounter. That's the only time this game gave me a challenge, after the 10th time my character decided to gratify himself against a wall instead of running alongside it...

You put all this together and what do you get? A game I wouldn't've bought if I knew this was the review I'd write. The devs really put a lot of effort into it and for that they deserve commendation, and on a cursory look the game is an upgrade of the first in virtually every way. But as I said, it spread itself too thin, tried to be too many things that they only had half the resources to accomplish and in the end made the core gameplay loop of being a badass cyberninja largely mediocre as a result. I expect the gameplay side of things will be rectified when Hardcore mode comes out, but there's no salvaging a good story that was told as poorly as this one was. Damned shame, but I wish the devs all the best, I'm sure they'll work things out.

Bottom line is though, if you're looking for Ghostrunner 1 but better, this ain't it. Not yet anyways.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSide note after going back and playing 1:

1 just does feel better. I like the things they've added here but the combat in 1 feels more focused and pure. I thought it was a lack of difficulty here, but it's not. This is often harder than 1 in certain spots. But 1 still just feels better... except boss fights. Those are hugely improved here.

Original review:

Right off the bat, the design decisions that they've made for this game give it that feel of a real sequel. Gameplay feels a little smoother than before. They've added a block mechanic, but you trade blocking for stamina... so it gives you something new that takes the same old resource, so it's not actually making anything easier while giving the player more options of approach. I like the addition of allies and actual speaking characters. And I _really_ like how the new boss fights aren't the perfecting of a repetitive gimmick but instead lean into the risk-reward trades that you'd make in regular combat.

I've only beat the first level, but I definitely like what they've done here. I do get the frustration of players from the original that they've made it easier. I don't know if I think that's true yet, but if you just came off of the hardcore mode from the original, then of course it's easier. Hardcore mode was the hardest game I've ever played and beaten. The sequel shouldn't continue up-scaling the difficulty from there, it should only aim to approach and maybe surpass it at the very end of the game but also probably not until the sequel gets its own hardcore mode (it might have it already, I don't know).

After completion:

I definitely feel the same way... though I can definitely see the complaints about it feeling easier. The main campaign is far more accessible but the difficulty definitely exists in the side content. Can't wait for hardcore mode.\t\t\t\t\t
