\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game's good, and it improves on the first game in certain areas like level design (in its linear areas) and enemy variety (even if a good number of the new enemies are absolutely not fun). But it has a number of issues that show that the devs (or whoever was resposible for these decisions) didn't fully undesrtand what made the first game great.

The first game was a tight experience focused on its movement system. The second game tries to do too many things, and ends up feeling messy in certain areas. From the unnecessary HUB and the annoying NPCs to the open area levels, it just feels like the devs thought that the first game wasn't enough. Instead of trying to build on the great systems that they already had, they decided to build around it. As an example, there's a rogue-like mode that's fun the first time you go through it, but it quickly becomes stale. They could have easily removed that mode from the game and nothing would have changed. And, imo, if something doesn't make the game better, then it should not be in the game at all.

It's also sad to see that, just like in the first game, the levels were designed exclusively for newer players, and apparently tried to discourage the use of any movement tech that goes beyond the most basic movement (by placing rubble in annoying places or with the introduction of certain level design elements that make the game feel like an autoscroller, like the rails or the moving platforms later on). Now, I'm not saying that the game should be designed around speedrunners, but rather that they should have embraced at least some basic movement tech and made it a core part of the game, as that movement tech makes the game way more fun to play and the higher skill ceiling makes the game a lot more replayable. Maybe they could have taught the player how to slide-dash-jump at some point and start designing later levels around it, or at least build some alternative harder parts to rewards layers that learn it. Celeste did this very same thing, teaching players how to hyper dash and designing optional levels around that mechanic, as well as other movement tech for that game. Ghostrunner has nothing like that, and so it always feels like playing a tutorial of a game that you've already completed. Solving a puzzle to unlock a grapple point to access a platform that you could have easily reached before just by climbing a wall or ledge-boosting is constant through the whole game. But that's only an issue if you already learnt movement tech in the previous game or if you wanted more depth out of its movement system.

I mentioned rubble before. Rubble and barrels and other random objects were already an issue in the first game, but it's worse here. It's just really annoying to try to move around a level quickly to kill an enemy at the other side just for the player character to stop on his tracks because a random object was in the way and the character decided to cault over it, which kills all of your momentum. These kind of annoying obstacles are everywhere and they are just frustrating. I don't see any point to them. Even in combat, you don't ever need cover from enemies since you can dodge everything by just moving. So these obstacles will just get you killed whenever you try to dodge a bullet and you end up not moving at all because there was a barely visible barrel on the ground next to you. I get it that the game would look empty otherwise, but I'd rather compromise visuals than gameplay fluidity in a game centered around quick movement. If they wanted to add so many enviromental objects, then they should just be destructible and not affect movement at all, tho I doubt that would be a good idea, game performance-wise.

Overwall, it's still a good game, and I'd argue that at it's best it's better than the first one. But it spends too much time in its more mediocre parts. Some remarkable positives to the game are its visual design and setpieces, as well as the bosses which are a massive improvement for the most part. And the movement is still really fun, even if it's slower. Bike has a few really cool sections. Also, one of my favorite new ideas is the inclusion of challenges in the middle of the levels (but these challenges just reinforced my previous point about the game being designed ignoring movement tech. All of the time based challenged are just way too easy and the later ones are just annoying. An optional challenge should be the place to raise the difficulty and require more knowledge about the game's movement system, but alas, you can just run normally through the course and get the gold time reward). It's just frustrating to think that a game could be so much better if it had less stuff. It's a 7/10 for me, but it could have been a strong 9/10\t\t\t\t\t

Examples:
1) Pulling the roboplatform to you makes it stop on you, and you basically finish inside the platform, in most of the time, you also cannot climb on it.

2) The bike is stuck on every little rock, I really don't understand the point of this bike. The locations with bike are dead, empty and unfinished.

3) On the last level, where you are playing outside Dharma City at some kind of ruins, you're just constantly going outside the location through textures. This level design is a perfect example of HOW NOT TO LEVELDESIGN.

4) In the first game, the movements were pretty intuitive and constant, in the sequel, you never know where you land after a dash jump, even after 20 hours of gameplay.

5) The wing suit and its challenge......wow that's just a masterpiece of ♥♥♥♥. You are flying to the point and eventually, for no reason, you're just starting to lose speed in a second and falling down.\t\t\t\t\t
