Basically a love letter to old school SNES and PS1 turn-based RPG's like Chrono Trigger, Final Fantasy VI and Dragon Quest.

The game is pretty fun, with a varied and large cast of playable characters, and a very unique turn-based combat system and character progression mechanics that are like no other similar game I've played before, but at the same time logical and straightforward enough that they're not hard to understand and yielding familiar results.

The only reason this one wasn't the best old school RPG I played this past year was because I also played Octopath Traveler II and I liked it a bit better, though I have to admit that this one has a better and more cohesive story with greater stakes. Plus it leaves the door open for a sequel. Excellent game, especially given that it's an indie.\t\t\t\t\t

Takes game design risks to hold its own? Check.
Weird fan service to classic JRPGs (specifically canning and eating enemies like “Popeye the Sailor Man “to learn their abilities and there's soo much more)? Check. Actually, sign me the heck up.

Me: Decides to 100% the game.

If you’re on the fence for this game. Just do it. I wasn’t sure at first but as I got deeper–this game won’t disappoint. Even at full price, $24.99 USD, this game plays as long and with as much value as classic JRPGs of the SNES era, which were then priced at $59.99-$69.99 USD.

Battle mechanics are great. Takes a unique approach to leveling; no more grinding for XP. Plenty of room to experiment and customize character abilities. Some amazing set piece scenes. Story takes a small misstep at the end but don’t let that deter you. Overall, a well packaged game, impressively done by one developer.

I would rate this game tree fiddy out of tree fiddy.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAlthough Chained Echoes had a relatively slow start, it evolves and metamorphosizes as the story and game progresses into an incredibly satisfying climax.

Characters (6.5/10):
The protagonists of Chained Echoes are certainly noteworthy, as it features one of the few well thought out uses of past lives and memories. Almost all of the character development felt natural and story-driven, and eventual character twists and reveals were executed well. Sienna was by far my favorite character, but every other protagonist except one generally holds their own. However, the redemption arc for one of the protagonists felt a little exaggerated and over-the-top, detracting from their characterization which unfortunately hurts the score for this category.

Gameplay (5/10):
Chained Echoes features a modified version of JRPG gameplay with an overdrive bar that, in theory, specifically incentivizes you to use certain moves at certain times, and tries to prevent you from spamming your most powerful abilities. However, in practice, the method of incentivization has little to no effect as the punishment of overheating is not quite severe enough, so the game plays like your typical JRPG. Furthermore, the game isn’t particularly difficult, but all the common enemies have TONS of health which unnecessarily draws out the playtime, while all the bosses seem unusually squishy. There is definitely some re-balancing needed in Chained Echoes . Also, the Crystal system is incredibly messy and feels unnecessarily difficult to use. However, there are a few things in the Chained Echoes ’s favor, such as the Reward Board system and the Unique Monsters mechanic does bring some intrigue to the game’s exploration, so the gameplay manages to feel just fresh and exciting enough to bring me back to it.

Art (8/10):
The art and music in Chained Echoes are excellent and capture the mood of each area very well. I personally adore the art, which pulls off the extremely delicate balancing act that is trying to make art that makes sense for the game’s story but also pleasing to look at in the retro, 16-bit art style that can feel a bit dated. Chained Echoes also features some great music, which ranges warm and comforting or energetic and blood-pumping and is always on point. That being said, the attack animations feel like wasted potential, as about half of them are generic stabs and slashes, so there could be some improvement there.

Story (6.5/10):
Chained Echoes takes on a concept not often utilized in modern JRPGs: past lives and memories from those past lives. Past lives are a focal point around which the entire story revolves, and Chained Echoes manages to, for the most part, pull it off. The story seems mostly self-consistent with only a handful of plot holes that I could detect, and it was quite an emotional tale of how people mature and come into their own as they gain more worldly experience, both good and bad. Chained Echoes also explores the concepts of ego, resentment and loneliness, and how they warp people who crack under the pressure. It does get slightly preachy at times, and some characters did end up getting undeserved redemption, but many of the lessons learned are just as powerful for the players as it is for the characters. I also like that each of the protagonists gets their own arc. However, I felt that the epilogue could have been longer, and I also felt that the sheer amount of worldbuilding information dumped onto the player at the beginning of the game was rather overwhelming. In general, Chained Echoes's story reeked of over-ambition; had Chained Echoes taken its time and had some fat trimmed off, it could have had a great story.

Overall, Chained Echoes felt very worthwhile and rewarding to play, as although its gameplay sometimes bored me, the other elements were more than enough to overshadow these shortcomings.

Final Score: 26/40 (Good)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLet's start with the tl;dr. Chained Echoes is a good RPG with unique(ish) battle mechanic and a story that holds its own (although it starts fraying here and there in the end) and surprisingly lots of things to see and do. A solid 8.5/10.

Things that I liked, but might not be your thing:
  • Lots of playable characters to choose from, which can affect your playstyle. I'm lazy so I did not use some of the characters once I found a playstyle that suited me. This might feel overwhelming or unnecessarily padded, but I enjoyed the options being there nevertheless.
  • The combat mechanics and the overdrive bar especially, which forced me to weigh different options during tougher fights. Again, it is a limiting factor and makes fights a bit longer on average, but for me it is a nicer alternative to a mindless "spam X skill for everything". Don't get me wrong: I still spammed the skill, but I needed to use something else as well once in a while.
  • The story is pretty good: some reviews have been REALLY salty about the end, which I don't fully understand. Yes, the *insert plot thing here* was a bit unexpected, but for it to ruin someones whole experience seems a bit much. And yes, the story starts to hasten up towards the end and everything is not explained perfectly through exposition, but in my experience most similar RPGs with "increasing stakes" have this same issue (if you want to call it that).
  • The leveling system lessens the usefulness of grinding, which is a good thing. I am a bit of a completionist, and have grinded the old-school RPGs for hours for one random item or character levels for that one secret boss fight, which can be mind-numbingly boring. The flipside of this system is that a different kind of grind takes the place of fights, namely exploration for items and upgrade materials. I explore every nook and cranny anyways, but it can be seen as running in circles for relatively small gain.
