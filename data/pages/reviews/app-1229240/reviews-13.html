TL:DR:
An impressive game with great soundtracks, artwork and a decent story, sadly weighed down by a combat and progression system that never quite finds its footing properly.

Chained Echoes isn't bad , but I can hardly call it good. I only learnt it was made by one dude after finishing it, and on that basis it is most definitely impressive. But that's why I want to point out the flaws all the more, because it really could have been that much better with a little fine tuning.

My main gripe with the game over my playthrough was definitely the combat and its progression. It starts out allright, was a mess in the second act, pivoted back to meh in the third and completely fell apart in the endgame. The first reason for this is the overdrive mechanic.

Basically, you have a bar that fills up if you attack/use abilities or get hit. If its moderately full, your skill costs get cut in half and you get some small stat boosts. If it's too full, all damage your team takes gets doubled, which is usually a death sentence. The only way to lower this bar is to either skip a turn guarding, or to use a skill of a type that is randomly selected and changes every time you do.

Now, I get this is trying to encourage diversity so you don't just spam the same skills. But all it does is add busywork and force you to switch things up, rather than actually encouraging diversity by making your other skills worthwhile.
The main problem with it is how it scales into the game. It starts out easy enough to manage initially, when you have few enemies to fight and no bosses with double or even tripple turns. It was a nightmare for me to manage in the second act, where you fight many enemies and don't have terribly many skills or party members yet, so you often only have skills to lower it available that effectively skip a turn. In the endgame, you have so many options available that the system kind of just manages itself and can be ignored. In the end, it didn't really add anything beyond being a chore. You can adjust it slightly in the options menu, but you can't turn it off.

Secondly, the crystal system. Simply put, crystals are slottable stat boosters you can put in your gear, that you can combine in certain ways to make them better. I heard many reviewers complain about it, and I can understand why. I don't even think it's a bad system, but it's a bit complicated and not well presented to the player. I hear most people don't really do much with it in their plathroughs, and I ignored it for most of the game until I sat down and actually figured it out, since I felt myself hitting a wall on higher difficulty. It'd be too convoluted to explain here, but there are certain strategies by which you can gain very powerful crystals very early on, and the game falls apart completely if you actually do this. The boosts confered by optimized crystals are immense, and with gearing just one character out with an ideal set, that character managed to kill the secret boss by themselves before said boss even got a turn in.

To elaborate on how that happened, there is the third problem with combat: Stats scale in an unbalanced fashion. I still haven't been able to figure out the math, but getting a 20% attack boost can lead to a given character dealing 50% more damage. I don't think it's flat, or if it is, there is some kind of damage floor, but if it is multiplicative, the exact math is beyond me. Point being, if you are ever slightly undergeared, your characters will do next to no damage. There was a time in act 2 where this became apparent when my team was taking upwards of four turns to kill a single overworld enemy. I went back and got a better weapon from a side boss, socketed some basic crystals into it, and found that character to do nearly three times as much damage as they did before, despite their attack stat only improving by about 60% overall.

The worst offender to this system is the agility stat, however. This is most notable on Sienna, the strongest character in the game by far, as she is the fastest character you get. A high agility stat will eventually allow you to take multiple turns in between enemy turns. If you mess around with crystals and buffs later, this can lead to you taking 5+ turns for every single enemy turn, completely wrecking the combat system. When I managed to push a character's effective agility above 100, the enemies just... stopped taking turns entirely. I assume the internal math simply fails when values reach that point.

All this sadly means that a lot of the cast are not terribly useful in the vast majority situations. All the tanks and healers you get basically remain on the bench forever, because all you really need are buffers/debuffers and fast damage dealers to steamroll any encounter with proper setup. The fact that enemies take so few turns later also means that damage over time effects are basically pointless (poor Rob).


The rest of the game didn't have as many issues. There were only a few story moments that made me squint at the screen. The oddest one being a cutscene where a side character gets stabbed and the Healer in your party manages to save them, claiming there is no injury she cannot heal. Literally in the next major cutscene, a main character gets stabbed and said Healer stands there not even trying to do anything or emoting at all, while the death is played as this huge disaster.
Many of the conflicts in the story could have been resolved by people just... actually talking to each other for a few minutes. But I suppose an unwillingness to listen isn't an unusual trait for someone to have when being emotional.

Beyond that, there were actually a lot of moments I really liked, a lot of good twists that were well set up and executed. The game tries a little too hard to shoehorn one twist after the other in, though, sadly hurting the atually good ones in the process.

I had many ui glitches with textboxes overlapping or being off screen in the time I played, but I chalk this up to the developer not properly accounting for unusual screen formats. I don't imagine these things happen when playing on 16:9.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's a solid RPG inspired by the classics, but with QOL from some more recent greats too. I enjoyed that you enter every battle fully healed, making fields and dungeons less about marathons and more about enjoying fights and mixing up party compositions. The overdrive bar is a fun system to plan around with too, where actions you take move a needle left and right on a gauge. Keep it in the green for cost reduction on battle skills! It felt crucial to be focusing on early game, but by the end I wasn't engaging with it so much.

The mech combat was a nice change of pace as well! You'd think having a flying mobile suit would trivialize on-foot combat forever, but environments were cleverly designed so that there are plenty of times where you can't summon them.

Story was surprising. It doesn't shy away from darker topics, and occasionally throws colourful language your way. It's very rare, but I still think the swearing feels very out of place in a game that looks and plays like this.

The sidequests are worth doing, and most of them are clearly signposted on the map when they're available. I was surprised how frequently they actually take you to new locations for their scenarios! There are a few areas that can't even be accessed without them.\t\t\t\t\t

Unchaining Nostalgia with Chained Echoes: An Itchy Affair

If Chained Echoes were a sandwich, it would be the kind that hits that nostalgic spot you didn't even know you had, like a culinary dj vu. This game scratches an itch so deep that you might mistake it for your long-lost love for Suikoden and let's be honest, who doesn't miss recruiting 108 characters with questionable fashion choices?

From the moment I dove into Chained Echoes, it was like rediscovering a hidden stash of '90s RPGs in the attic of my gaming memories. The graphics may be pixelated, but the only thing outdated here is my ability to resist the siren call of turn-based battles and epic storytelling.

It's like the game developers raided the Suikoden nostalgia vault, found the blueprints, and then added their own quirky twists. I half expected to run into McDohl or Flik at some point, but instead, I stumbled upon a group of characters so diverse and charming that even my party members were giving each other side-eye.

The combat system is like a dance a dance where you strategically stomp on enemies with the grace of a caffeinated cat on a hot tin roof. And did I mention the giant mechs? Because, yes, you get to pilot those bad boys. It's like playing chess, but instead of bishops and knights, you have mechs and mages.

The itch that Chained Echoes scratches is the one that's been bothering you since you first laid eyes on a Suikoden game and thought, "This is the beginning of a beautiful friendship." It's like finding an old mixtape of your favorite RPG tunes, but instead of hitting "rewind," you hit "new game."

In conclusion, if you're looking for a game that not only scratches that Suikoden itch but also massages it with a delightful blend of humor, nostalgia, and mechs look no further. Chained Echoes is the RPG equivalent of finding a treasure chest in your backyard, only instead of gold, it's filled with plenty (it's only 12, not 108) reasons to never leave your gaming chair. Happy adventuring!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI know it is a well made game, but I couldn't help myself feeling bored and not interested at all. The battle seems amazing at first, but this overdrive system is just boring and there are only two types of battles:
- No brainers, battles that you try to use some strategy but they are just too easy.
- F*** you and your strategy, this plant reduces all your party to 1hp and kills you with spit.

In other words, it is not challenging. Sometimes is just too easy, even bosses, but sometimes you will find "common" enemies in the out world that will eat your soul. That's kinda boring tbh.

Also, I know about definitions of RPG and JRPG and all of that, but this game is more of an playable anime than anything else. The characters are kinda interesting and nice, although a little bit cliche, but you have no choice or paths of action. Follow the road and you finish quests, check the side roads for some chests. That's it.

The graphics are nice and the animations are actually very well made.
if you are looking for a game to create strategies, to delve into deep battles and choose paths of action... this is not for you.

If you want nice characters (not good, but not bad) and some anime-like story to play along, this could be a good choice. That's it.\t\t\t\t\t
