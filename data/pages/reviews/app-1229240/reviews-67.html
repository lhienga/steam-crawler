\r\n\t\t\t\t\tChained Echoes is a GRPG (German RPG, because the lead developer is from Dusseldorf) that draws from the classic JRPGs of the late 90s and early 2000s. Chrono Trigger, Suikoden, Xenogears, Final Fantasy Tactics, and even anime like One Piece and Berserk - this game is pretty unsubtle about its influences (and thankfully not in that fourth-wall-breaking Joss Whedon kind of way) But this is nothing new, lots of us liked those old games and lots of us have access to RPGMaker, so what sets Chained Echoes apart.

Due to inertia, tradition, and a host of other unsatisfactory reasons, JRPGs have been saddled with a lot of legacy mechanics that hang around like an office HR representative, adding nothing but pointless busywork and a sense of drudgery. Chained Echoes takes these beloved but old and sick mechanics to a lovely farm upstate. Random battles with invisible enemies? Experience Points? Side quests you need a tab open on GameFAQs to find? Replaced with visible on-screen enemies you can always run from; character growth keyed to equipment, and a whole crystal enhancement system you can dive into or ignore like I did.

Instead of XP, you basically get to buy a new skill, equippable passive skill, or permanent stat increase every time you kill a boss. You also get skill points to strengthen individual skills for a few extra percentage points to a stat, but the important thing is that running around in a circle fighting Red Slimes for an hour until you level up enough to take the challenge out of boss battles is now a thing of the past. Now when you get trounced by a boss you'll know in your heart that it wasn't because you didn't "grind" enough, but because you failed to make use of the tools you have. Thankfully, you can try right again from the game over screen,

And speaking of those skills, go ahead and use them to your hearts content, Because even though Chained Echoes has restorative items, you're automatically restored to full HP and TP at the start of every fight. Use your best skills on every monster in the dungeon! You no longer have to faff about with potions and tents and such between battles. This isn't a game about managing a limited supply of healing resources, and more RPGs should realize that they too aren't 1st edition dungeons and dragons either.

What you do have to manage is something called The Overdrive Bar, a gauge that fills while you hit and get hit. It starts in the yellow, eventually goes into the green (where you get a TP discount and hit harder) and finally into the red (Overheat, where you're much more vulnerable to enemy attacks). Defending can lower the bar, as can using the right kind of skill in a given moment. It's a neat little twist that keeps you from relying on the same skills every time you go into combat.

Lastly a word about the story. This is a cool fantasy tale set in a world that seems familiar to fans of this sort of thing, but takes some truly clever twists with the world building and plot. It's about war, mass destruction, and what it costs to stick to your principles. Well paced and plotted with little vignettes that give you some good character moments and slowly reveal interesting aspects of the setting.

Complaints? Well. the aforementioned crystal system is not my cup of tea but it is also optional. Feels like its there because some folk really like crafting systems. Some of the dungeon puzzles are a little annoying but thankfully there aren't any random battles to deal with. All in all, though I can find some nits to pick, nearly everything I dislike in this game can either be ignored or is just a small distraction in an otherwise amazing achievement. Kudos to you Matthias Linda and to your team. You made the sort of game that stands shoulder to shoulder with the giants of our youth.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI can't believe how underrated this game is. This was hands down one of the best modern turn-based RPG's I've played in such a long time. With incredible retro SNES-styled graphics, exceptional writing, fun character progression, interesting battle systems and the addition of an alternate travel/battle mechanic using flying mech-like robots, this is absolutely worth your time and money. If this game was based solely on the story alone, I'd give it a 10/10 but there's so much more to love here. Homage was clearly paid to classic titles like FFVI or Chrono Trigger and everything felt so polished. I never got bored of exploring, the story threw unexpected plot twists and humor at me, and it didn't feel overly grindy.. I loved everything about this game and sincerely hope a sequel follows in the future. This was an instant classic in my book.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tReally great game. If your picking up this game then you probably know what kind of game your getting into. Good combat, a good story and nice side content. Its not perfect but still damn good. I think that more so comes to personal taste on my notes for improvement which I'd love to see the dev take knock out of the park in their next title.

The game is focused on not grinding with an anti level pattern built in. Think milestones in DND. This was pretty good even with there being a lot of seemly useless passive skills for each player. However, there are a couple of grindy mechanics in the game, totally optional, which I think would have still been better served if taken with the same thought. Crystals are a pain. They're grind intensive to get good ones, rng based to find and the menu-ing to combine them is rough. Maxing out all skills didn't take me long but still some grind killing hordes of pigs. Would like to see how you could elevate these systems to have something that doesn't require rng and grind at all. The material for armor upgrades are also a bit grindy but I ultimately didn't invest too much time after spending so long getting crystals and going no where. You can beat all the content without it so all in all just a detractor from the game.

Story wise - no spoilers - I don't think the villains hit for me. Overall I think I needed a little more show not tell to build into some moments better. But it kinda felt pointless. I personally didn't like the "mature" themes in this game used for some characters motivations. I think when it comes to certain topics and human behavior, they need to be taken with real care if your going to add them and it was a little too much for me especially for how the narrative them had the character evolve throughout the story. Would have been stronger throughout and a more accessible game for everyone. Also it likes to really troupe with the dead/dying/in danger kids which is a card I think that is a little over used. The last thing I'll take away from playing this is this question, what did we even do? The world mythos is cool though.

Everything else was right on the money I thought. Thanks for a great game and if the dev comes out with a new banger in the upcoming years they can expect to get an auto buy from me.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's a JRPG with all the grindy ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ removed. No random encounters, no grinding for hours to get individual characters up to speed, no juggling healing items between battles. It provides an incredibly streamlined experience that lets you just get on with the game instead of wasting your time, while still maintaining the vibes of oldschool SNES-era RPGs with some modern spins on it, such as the overdrive system, which rewards you with incredibly powerful buffs if you balance your skills and make use of everything you've got, but punishes you for blindly spamming the "big damage" options.
My only qualms are some very occasional awkward lines of dialogue, presumably translation errors. Very very minor, though.

I'm still fairly early into the game but this one's a keeper. Really scratches an itch I didn't know I had.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t30 seconds into the game you find out that the developer thinks Chrono Trigger is the best RPG ever, which reassures you that the game has been made by someone with impeccable taste and your play time is about to be well spent.

This game could stand toe to toe with the legendary RPGs of the Super Nintendo with ease. It's just an amazing game. Just every aspect of the game is exceptionally done. Beyond the SNES games there's influence from a lot of the other best RPGs over the past 30 years, like Suikoden, and Xenoblade Chronicles.

The story is compelling throughout the whole game, with an interesting cast of characters and unexpected wrinkles in the plot. The worldbuilding is especially well done.

The combat system is just stellar. You have a dozen or so playable characters of which 8 form your party with 4 front line and 4 back line characters. Each member gets 8 skill slots with which make up your active abilities and 5 passives, so there is a lot of customization that can be done. Every battle your party starts with full HP/TP, so you can spam your skills with impunity without worrying about resource management outside of fights. You have a sort of shared party power bar which you build up to enter an enhanced state where your attacks do more damage and skills are half their cost. Each battle is a tug of war of getting into this state and then maintaining it. It's hard to describe but it just works so well.

And the combat is DEEP too. You can set up the party so many different ways by customizing different skill load outs. Do you want to make glass cannons where you try to just blitz enemies dead before you run out of HP/TP? This might not work on a boss. Do you want a sustainable defensive model that keeps enemies immobilized and the party topped off? This also might not work on a boss which eventually overwhelms you since you aren't killing things fast enough. Balance? Well that might fall to glass cannon enemies instead.
There's an avalanche of buffs and debuffs you can apply that all make a huge difference. Status effects are massively impactful and work on all enemies including bosses, but become harder to apply each time. Do you want to paralyze the enemy continuously preventing them from acting early in a fight or wait until the right moment when you know it will land? There are so many combos you can set up, and there's so many different synergies. This is one of the most enjoyable turn based battle systems I have played in a long time.

The leveling system ensures a balanced and difficult experience through the game. You don't gain experience per se, but only skill points (which upgrade your active skills over time) in each fight, so you can't grind for levels. You level up by gaining "grimoire shards" that are awarded by bosses, sidequests, and certain reward tiers from the reward board that tracks your achievements. Each grimoire shard is basically a level for the entire party, and each person can then choose to learn a new skill or permanent stat boost. Most of your power comes from equipment which grants the majority of your attack and defense - so finding new gear feels real good.

Another thing that the game does well is there's just a huge density of content packed into each screen (if you've played Cross Code you can see a similar feel going on there). There's just tons of secrets to find on every screen. Chests, buried treasure, breakable walls, hidden sidequests, secret bosses, secret door behind that bookcase, suspicious notch in the wall leading to a new area, and so on, There's little collectible things scattered everywhere (think Xenoblade Chronicles) you can collect and sell for money which also can be sold as sets to unlock even more rewards at shops. If you explore, you will be hugely rewarded. There's a deluge of secrets to find in the game, and I found that unlike a lot of games, you don't need a guide to do it. The game telegraphs a lot of its secret areas through NPC dialogue or notes, and it feels good when you connect the dots and find rewards as a result.

You get a base like Suikoden and can recruit people to unlock new facilities and permanent boosts.

You get giant mechs that you can upgrade that fight other giant mechs in a slightly different spin on the battle system. Sometimes you can use the mechs to fight enemies you used to fight on foot, and you smear them across the ground, and it feels GOOD.

You can warp anywhere at any time to make backtracking quick and easy. You also have an airship to fly around to explore. You can save anywhere.

And everyone just runs so SMOOTHLY. Loading is near nonexistant. Running around feels fast. Combat actions resolve quickly. You can fly quickly through the menus. Everything is snappy.

Gahhh it's so good. Is it perfect? No. One major misstep is the gem system, which you can slot into your equipment. Unlike everything else in the game which has fixed items coming from fixed locations, gathering gems is random, for no reason. Fusing gems together is unintuitive and poorly explained, and the system of which gems can and can't be merged (as well as having to slot and unslot gems to "purify them") is clunky. They absolutely could have done better here.

This is as close to a 10/10 JRPG as you can get. I really hope they make another.\t\t\t\t\t
