\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game... It's like someone put Chrono Trigger's graphics but in HD, Xeno Gears' themes around twisted religion and robots, and Skies of Arcadia's base-building *shrug* into a blender and molded the remains into something actually really good to play as well as read through the dialog for. I'm more of mechanics-first gamer, but this... this was every great JRPG story I ever played through mashed up into a pretty great package that I didn't want to skip through for one minute (well, unless I was rehashing a part or two a few minutes after I got something missable). Game oozes polish from every nook and cranny.

Its main progression system is well obfuscated, no need to really grind to master skills. You can get by without some of the more tedious parts like the gems or upgrading gear, and learning new skills happens at a good pace throughout the story. There's big power in those other systems if you need it, though. The stable of playable characters felt like a bit much towards the end, but they're all fun in their own ways.

Plenty of side questing and exploration to do. Some side quests aren't even tracked by the game, offering some cute "good for you" rewards if you find them. New dialog and interaction text is all marked before you speak to the characters or check objects. Menus are mostly smooth... but sometimes the input to advance dialog falls off when you least expect, prompting you to hit the button more. Pretty minor.

The big thing for me was the plot gating... Your main character will always say "That's not the right way." when trying to explore a certain place early, and it annoyed the hell out of me until post-game busted apart that gating. It just feels like they could have blocked those areas off more naturally than they did... How is the main character to know what the "right way" is until he finds it? I'm the player. I'm the one that decides when and where I take the right damned way towards plot! Throw an actual gate or more guards in the way if you have to make sense in the narrative. The road north out of Flandern in particular irked me as frustrating because it's just an open path with no obvious obstacles that the characters just remark "nope" to until you have the right side quest. There was no dialog implication that this path leads to the local lord's place and so shouldn't be traversed freely, making the whole "That's the wrong way." line feel much more contrived than an actual obstacle.

...Anyway. Cool game. Very few things wrong with it. I just get bent about narrative quirks that make no sense. This is why I'm a mechanics gamer... Most developers get the plot all wrong. This one got their plot mostly right with the least flaws I've ever seen in the modern day. Good on you.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs a fan of turn based RPGs I decided to give this game a go. The story at the outset was moving too fast for me to get acclimated to the environment. You start off in a dream sequence, which doesn't do a whole lot in the way of setting the stage. Almost instantly you're tossed into a large scale conflict with little sense of what is going on.

Battle in this game is an exercise in tedium, and single enemy encounters take ages to get through. The battle system very quickly introduces an overdrive gauge that requires constant babysitting. Any player action can raise or lower this gauge seemingly at random. Enemy attacks will cause you to reach overheat very quickly, leaving you to spam defend just bring it down. constantly having to manage this in a normal encounter or boss fight is just too much to bear.

This game looked neat in the graphical presentation and sound department. However it just wasn't able to engage me enough to justify playing past the intro. Battle, the core part of the game, serves only to induce anxiety in every encounter. Sadly I would not recommend this game based on that alone.\t\t\t\t\t
