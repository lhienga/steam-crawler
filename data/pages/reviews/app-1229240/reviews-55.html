\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLet me begin by saying: Chained Echoes is a masterpiece.

I didn't grow up on the classics of the JRPG genre, I haven't played FF7, or played too far into FF4. To be entirely honest, the lack of modern QoL improvements and the combat style in those older RPGs scared me off, or made me bounce off them when I tried, despite liking character design and the story. Chained Echoes absolutely does not struggle at all in any of those fronts.

Lemme begin with the combat. Chained Echoes introduces its own twist to the standard JRPG format for combat by introducing the Overdrive Bar. The bar has three stages, the standard yellow, the buffed green, and the overheated red. In green you gain a pile of buffs that effectively incentivize maintaining that state. In red you take extra damage. The bar is raised or lowered by a variety of actions, but most important by a type of skill that cycles every four turns. Be it physical attacks, magic attacks, debuffs, buffs, heals or utility skills, as long as the skill-type matches the one displayed with the bar, then the bar decreases by a good chunk. It provides a new twist to combat, sometimes locking you out of your "best" skill for the situation because you need to raise the bar or lower the bar. This simple system does wonders for keeping the combat engaging, and paired with the lack of random encounters, combat rarely feels like a chore, like it might have in older games.

Art is another massive bonus in this game, in my humble opinion. The environment, the sprites, the character portraits, the bosses, my god. Excellent. Chef's kiss. Each little piece has its own charm, be it the run cycles of the various characters in your party, or the in fight animations of some of the bosses, its all great. Personal opinion though, so take a look for yourself. Hand in hand with Art goes music and sound design is absolutely on point. The soundtrack is readily available on youtube, so go check it out for yourself, but yeah. Each piece perfectly fits the zone and point in the story it was designed for. I will say though, my one critique in this department is that it can feel jarring at certain points in the story where you transition from one space with a certain type of music, be it melancholy or epic or imposing, and it switches with a hard, sudden cut to the new zones music. I think that in that department, the game would benefit from some fade in and out.

World design is excellent as well. The game isn't open-world by our modern definition, its by no means a UBISOFT game or something (thank god), but it *feels* like an open, breathing, alive world. I felt like I could go where I want, where everything was connected. There were secrets to discover around every corner, and the reward board did an excellent job of motivating me to actually find those secrets. There are still some for me to find, even after I've rolled credits that I entirely plan on going back to find and complete. The world just feels that nice to exist in.

I think the last thing to talk about is probably the story. On the whole, the story is fun. Its by no means a literary masterpiece, and if the story was all this game had, without its stellar combat, enthralling worldbuilding, and fun characters, this game wouldn't be the masterpiece that it is. There are some pacing issues, particularly around the turn of each act, and the story likes its twists, but that second one is by no means a bad thing. But yeah, the story has a lot of good writing in it, and its fun, which is all it needs to be.

On the whole Chained Echoes is a 9/10, and probably my favorite game i've played so far this year.\t\t\t\t\t

A fantastic tribute to classic JRPGs

It's become no secret that indie developers are starting to make excellent games in genres and styles that are associated with a company or country, and Matthias Linda and his team have shown that there is now no excuse for indie's to not deliver excellent JRPGs

The gameplay is fantastic, with a nice mechanic that makes even basic battles more challenging and boss encounters requiring some thought and not just spamming your strongest moves, best buffs and debuffs and healing. The roster size and skill system allows for experimental and flexible gameplay. It's also well paced and doesn't overstay it's welcome.

On the presentation side, the visuals are excellent (with some scenes that would make for great wallpaper,) backed up by music that is high class and sound effects which sound hard and crisp.

The plot is very well done and the game doesn't shy away from dark and grim happenings. But it also shows its soft and lighter side and the humour, whether it be item descriptions or NPC dialogue will have you chuckling from time to time.

It does have a few issues; some design choices are weird, as sometimes you can find using a particular menu a bit more troublesome than usual which makes the process of selecting or selling something not as smooth as it could be. Some things aren't explained well or in-game at all (particularly status effects) and the crystal is system is not great. You can do 90% of the game without bothering with it which is nice... But that also exposes just how flawed it is.

Overall though, this is a phenomenal game that any RPG fan should get their hands on and any issues take a huge backseat to the package as a whole, which delivers big time.

Rating: 9/10

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI grew up playing JRPGs, but for the past decade or so I thought I'd never play a turn-based RPG again. Even though I love the format at heart, they are always just too slow for my grown-up life, too long, too pointlessly tedious.

Chained Echoes changed that.

I absolutely adored this game, this love letter to the 16-bit JRPG classics of the 90s. Chained Echoes wears its influences on its sleeve: shout-outs to and obvious inspirations from Final Fantasy, Zelda, and other classics are endless and wonderful, from the Merman hanging out in the moat around the castle to the assassin you pick up at a bar or the the Terra-styled lead character who holds a great, unknown burden and power.

The combat and associated systems are deep and rewarding, with myriad abilities, gem slots, useful status effects and buffing/debuffing, and opportunities to grow and customize your characters. There is a whole, *second* system of mech combat in there too, which, while fun, does feel a little tacked on and lacks the depth of the base combat. Exploration is a blast, and level design is wonderfully done. I also enjoyed the story for the most part--at least, I read through everything and didn't skip anything, which is remarkably rare for me these days.

The game does suffer a bit in its gem system, which is pointlessly frustrating and convoluted, where even for active players you'll end up with an inventory full of hundreds of gems, very few of which are actually usable due to the constraints of the crafting system. The gem system should be significantly simplified. And please give us a way to page through the menus a bit faster.

Overall, an easy 9/10. I look for to what comes next from Mr. Linda.\t\t\t\t\t
