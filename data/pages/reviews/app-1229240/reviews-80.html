\r\n\t\t\t\t\tChained Echoes is a fantastic game that is pretty earnest of its reverence for mid-90's JRPGs (specifically, Chrono Trigger and Final Fantasy 6) that manages to use themes and ideas of those games and turn them well into a genuinely unique and captivating experience. Dungeon exploration is less about exploration attrition and more about combat encounters within being puzzles to solve - how do you survive this attack, what do you do to shut down the enemy on this turn, what skills work in tandem to make certain enemy combinations easier to handle and deal with.

It doesn't tread any really new territory for a JRPG's story, but it works with a sense of scale and proportion that is real interesting - the greater world outside of the continent the game takes place on is known and discussed, their issues are mentioned and talked about, which does a lot to lend itself to feeling like the game world is greater than just where you're at! I liked it, felt like your journey to save the world wasn't the only thing really going on in anyone's lives.

I can pretty whole heartedly recommend this game if you like old JRPGs! After 50 hours, digging into endgame optional stuff, I simply decided to finish the game - and still had several dungeons and boss encounters waiting for me. As a plus, completing the game lets you save right at the final boss' door so if you choose to pick up where you left off and finish off side content for achievements or fun (the reward board, the bonus bosses, etc.) its easy to do. Game good.\t\t\t\t\t

DO NOT BUY
DO NOT BUY

"Finish the first 3rd-ish of the game" Should not be such a challenge that only 33% of players can manage it.

The developer clearly doesn't care about game balance in the early game, and has made it nearly impossible to progress. Do you like absurd difficulty spikes that cause random enemies to just suddenly start two-shotting your characters when you revisit an area? Do you like games that expect you to have to go back and grind in order to kill *every* *single* *boss*? Do you like games with non-traditional leveling schemes that inherently tie your character progression to the story of the game? Do you like it when you literally cannot level up because no matter how many enemies you kill, you'll *NEVER* get anything from it, because ONLY BOSSES DROP LEVELUPS. There are so many asinine, brickheaded choices in this game, I genuinely have to question whether every single one of these professional game reviewers played half of the tutorial and then said "yep, this is clearly the best overall game".

Oh and it's super charming that they intentionally hid all of the balance problems until about 3 hours into the game so that they can be sure nobody will ever actually get a refund.

I feel defrauded by this game, and I encourage people to avoid it when possible. Absolute waste of money.\t\t\t\t\t

Rating? 9 out of 10.

This game is basically a 16-bit RPG Turn Base made in a modern era, not in the 90s. Before going on with my review, I found out that this game took 7 years to made and they had a kickstarter campaign on 2019, you can read these info on Wikipedia.

I started this from a friend's account and 15 hours in, I found out the game is very interesting in a lot of terms.

BGM / OST
- It's a banger. Not all OST vibe with me but it has a great list of OST especially the battle OST.

Artwork
- It's 16 bit artwork, just like Chrono Trigger. But is it bad? It isn't bad to me, also there is a lot of facial reaction and actions in the game that do conveys the story to the player easily.

Story
- The story is quite hard to capture. I have no idea the story is base on what but playing this game really reminds me of Trails of Cold Steel plot. Which brings me to this point. There are no message/text logs which made you, if you failed to read a certain dialogue or text, good luck finding them in a Youtube channel showing the game's gameplay.
- But that is not to say the story is not interesting. It is hella interesting. There's also several plot twist and the ending.... seriously reminds me of a certain manga regarding a somewhat apocalypse man eating world.

Combat
- First of all, the combat in this game is quite unique. Despite seeing elements from Chrono Trigger in the early game, I found out that this game does not uses Level to determine the characters / units stats. It was determined by the Grimoire Shards or GS, which represent the "Level" in this game. You can get this GS from defeating bosses.
- The combat also uses this OverDrive bar where you have 3 types. Yellow for normal, Green for OverDrive where your attack and defense increases, Red for Overload where you receives more damage and you deal standard damage. Also, when you are in OverDrive, your TP (more like MP) are consumed half than usual which helps a lot.
- The stats are also pretty straightforward. We have the usual Attack, Magic, Defense, Mind, Agility, Critical and the HP/TP stat. There are several hidden stat such as Hate, Crit Damage % etc. Not much but I kinda hate it since it didn't get flushed out.

Difficulty?
- The game difficulty for normal difficulty is quite nice. It isn't that hard and it isn't that easy too. Grinding in this game is just for the money and SP (Skill Point) only which isn't really needed.
- Also, you can get all the trophy quite easily to be honest.
- There are several difficulty in this game. We have the usual difficulty setting, then enemy aggressiveness (not entirely sure what is this for since I haven't fully tried out) and the wideness of the OverDrive bar. The wider the OverDrive bar, the more green area you get.

Characters
- Some of the character is pretty interesting but having a lot of optional party members really made the game a bit boring to be honest.
- Glenn. Okay. Seriously. Glenn? Chrono Trigger? I literally laughed when I read his name as the main character. I love this guy a lot and in the game, he can be either a tank or a physical damage dealer.
- Lenne. Like the character a lot and I ship both Glenn and Lenne until that one freaking moment. Still ship them after the ending thou. I wonder how she really felt towards Glenn. Also, she's a crazy stronk magic dealer. Love her a lot as an unit.
- Victor. Ah yes, my first healer and buffer. He seriously helps a lot in most battles but he can't really work alone thou. Like his backstory too.
- Sienna. My crit master and thief. Her backstory is pretty interesting to be honest and the ending really felt nice for her and her special one.
- Amalia. BEST healer ever. I love her a lot as a healer. Unfortunately, she can't really buff people much except as buffing defense. Also, I like her utility and how it helps Lenne dealing damage to the enemy mobs.
- So yeah, I only use these 5 most of the time. The others? Pretty much sometimes. Robb do help a lot in the early game but later on in the game, I threw him in the back as backup for Lenne.

I guess that's it? The game seriously has a lot of potential to grow via its story (the way it is engaged is pretty....bland sometimes.). Some QOL (Quality of Life) functions upgrade such as message logs, crystal crafting shortcut enhancement or filtering. I also wish that the game may implement something like a relationship/friendship story mode where you visit the lore for each character at your own pace.

Seriously love this game and if it had a sequel, I would play it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\thighly recommended. chained echoes is a well-written love letter to any old school jrpg fan out there. playing through, i'm seeing inspirations from so many jrpgs i love, and it's hard not to smile at the mix of nostalgia and freshness the game is able to bring to its inspired ideas. there are so many little QoL things here that make the game a good time to play - challenging, but not in the frustrating ways. health/mana refill after each fight, making things a fight to fight strategy rather than slogging through a war of resource management attrition. little icons over npcs you haven't talked to yet, icons by doors that you can enter vs ones you cannot, etc.

the fights themselves are well done too. the overdrive gauge helps mix things up in a fun way, challenging you to not only spam your 'best' skills, or even not to use the right one for the job all the time - for the sake of keeping your party in the 'green zone' of less dmg taken, more dealt, and half mana costs. there are also multiple variants on fights, such as fighting with sky armors which introduces an entirely new mechanic and way to play. or fighting enemies on train tracks and having to manage the direction of the cart during the fight to ensure certain buffs, or avoiding instant death.

there is a system that unlocks new things for sale based on what types of loot items you've sold to vendors throughout the game - much like ff12's bazaar system. the weapon/armor progression reminds me a bit of the ff12 gear progression as well, only with the addition of weapon upgrades and crystal slotting for much more customization. there is a cool reward board mechanic, which gives you little bonuses (that add up and you can chain together!) for completing bounty-like mini-quests.

most importantly, the story is well written and full of surprises, great character development, and some very emotionally charged moments. the grand scale of multiple nations on the brink of war, and our ever-growing ragtag group of adventurers doing our best to prevent it at all costs, does somewhat evoke memories of ff9 (especially having a disguised princess in our group) but it has no problem standing tall and presenting a rich, complex story all on its own.

simply put, the best word i can think of for chained echoes is: charming. everything about it feels polished and thought out. in a era where turn based rpgs have become nostalgic, many of us are looking for something that feeds that nostalgia while presenting something new. this game hits both of those nails squarely on the head and - like a great book - once it hooks you, it's incredibly hard to put down.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWasted around half an hour dying to an optional boss in Act 1; finally went online to figure out what I was doing wrong, and the general consensus was that it's an "RNG fight" where you're simply screwed if the boss does a certain move twice in a row, which is exactly what kept happening to me.

Well, screw that nonsense; I have better things to do with my time than repeat the same battle over and over again because of factors outside my control. Sure, I could level up and come back later, but why let me stumble into an impossible fight with no warning in the first place? There's nothing about the enemy or area indicating I'm not supposed to battle it yet, apparently I'm meant to either get lucky or die repeatedly until it's apparent I have to return at an unspecified point. It reminds me of Owlcat's Pathfinder games, full of "gotcha!" trap encounters, except at least those show you the level and stats of your enemy so you have some idea how doomed you are, and they have better writing.\t\t\t\t\t
