\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNot a bad game if you are a fan of the genre, but it is definitely rough around the edges and the limitations of its development (the work of a single person) are clear. Thus far the combat is decent and the difficulty is challenging but fair, and is the most engaging part of the game, along with the exploration which involves large maps and no overworld. But there is a lot dragging the game down.

Some more detail:

The Story: 7.5/10
Only about 1/3 of the way through. The story starts off as a fairly dry political tale of neighboring nations. There is lots of NPCs to talk to and lots of worldbuilding, kind of like a Trails game, if you like that sort of thing.
The story does pick up and get more interesting, but thus far in the earlier stages I still see little resemblance to the epic tales of Chrono Trigger or Xenogears. I'm sure that is about to change.
The writing itself is not as good as the overall story, with lots of out of place swearing that feels like it is thrown in for the sake of it, and occasionally some very poorly written dialog. Other times it is written fairly well, and it feels inconsistent. The game's writing definitely could have used more polishing, and the eyes of a 3rd party (or several). There's no shame in getting the advice of a professional if you are selling a game on Steam.

Graphics: 7/10
Graphically the game looks more like Stardew Valley than a classic JRPG, very rough around the edges with a clearly low budget. It doesn't really have the "pop" that the classics did. The character portraits are not great and the worst aspect of the visuals, and lack any emotional variations as well which is kind of immersion breaking.

Music: 7/10
The music is serviceable and does the job more or less, but is completely unmemorable and music is very important to me in a JRPG.

Combat: 8/10
The combat is pretty fun but feels repetitive after a while. There is a good challenge to the game even on normal difficulty, which is great and refreshing. You are fully healed after every battle which is kind of nice, but at the same time eliminates any need for resource management and you just go all out every battle, which increases the feeling of repetitiveness. I understand later in the game you get mechs to fight in which really mixes things up, but I am not there yet.

Other Gameplay: 7/10
The game largely thus far revolves around exploring large maps filling in quest boxes in a challenge board. This is done pretty well. Exploration is good, outside of the weirdness that there are some places on the maps you inexplicably can't go even though it looks like you should be able to. Figuring it out requires boundary mashing.

Unfortunately, the other elements of the character builds and gameplay are just not done very well. There are too many systems that seem like they are thrown in just because and aren't really fleshed out well for good gameplay, including the overdrive bar, the weapon crafting, and especially the crystal systems. Even the skill system feels a little tedious at times and like this game would be better with just a straightforward classic system.

To make things more fun with the current system, crystals should be hot swappable with zero negative repercussions, instead you are punished for using them and fusing them. Weapons should be able to be broken down into materials. Stop with the major negative consequences of building your characters. I'm not sure if there is a respec eventually but free respec of your skills would be great too. Less stress over making choices.

The overdrive bar is a bummer that punishes you for engaging in combat and is a drag on the game. I would have preferred the standard resource management. It does not feel well balanced, and since it goes up every time you get attacked, fighting mobs is often much harder than a solo boss. The thing should go DOWN when you get attacked.

Overall:
I would give it a 7.5/10 so far. I don't mean to knock the author's hard work, it is extremely impressive for the work of a single person, but I hope that he continues to work on and improve the game. There is a solid foundation here that could be built on a lot. Given the low cost of the game I would be happy to pay for a worthwhile DLC upgrade in the future. I would give this a muted recommend, only for people who enjoy this kind of game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm only a few hours in, but so far this is giving me a fix for that SNES era JRPG nostalgia that I did not know I needed.
It feels just like the great games of that era, with some added modern quality of life touches here and there.
The fact that it's made by a solo dev (from what I can tell) makes it even more impressive. The native Linux build works flawlessly.

Edit: Changing this to a negative review after playing more. I do not at all enjoy the way the game forces you to play. It feels more like a puzzle game than a traditional JRPG.
For one you cannot grind to get the upper hand, or try different tactics, so all the battles feel like they have a set way to win. Apart from when RNG makes all the enemies kill your healer in the first turn.

Certain skills don't appear to work at all, Decoy to get aggro for instance. Pretty much every battle is about keeping your health and defense maxed out at all times while *slowly* whittling down the enemies health with your toothpicks.

I am not sure I will come back to finish this, looks like there's way too much left for me to tolerate the frustration.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI love the style of the game and many of the concepts and scenarios. However, the characters are flat and unrelatable. The hero, Glenn, begins the game as an orphan-mercenary, which I like, but after accidentally being agent of a cataclysm, he makes it his life goal to stop cataclysms from ever happening again. An everyman, non-conventional hero-type would more probably make it his life goal to avoid grimoire stones, since an encounter with one of these was what caused the first cataclysm. The princess character is the same regarding her nebulous motivations. We learn she's a goodhearted blacksheep of her kingdom, has run away to learn firsthand of the suffering of her people, but she ran out of money and so in disguise joined the army of the neighboring kingdom. Kylian, the hero's friend/mentor, witnessed a tragedy involving his sister. Does he wish to avenge his sister? No. He wishes to SAVE THE WORLD. If only the developer could have landed on more obvious, close at hand motives for the characters, I could have ingested the rest of the plot rigamarole with grains of salt. Also, the characters themselves, even when in overt comic situations, come off self-serious and sometimes even dour, not in keeping with the jolly graphic style and nostalgic gameplay. Moral ambiguity is fine for post-apocalyptic shoot-'em-ups but do we really need the heroes of a jrpg whining about how hard being heroes is, or whether or not it's better just to let townsfolk remain goblins? Or, if we do need this, can't it at least be funny? Finally, the affinity system is non-intuitive and skills are a bit underwhelming, lot of buffs/debuffs, but there's not even a super Fire or super Wind attack, just the same attack, but you can add debuffs to the enemies to make them land harder. And too many states for this kind of game. Robb has a passive skill called Toxic Resistance, but I'd be hard pressed to remember which of the enemies uses toxic, as opposed to just, say, poison, against the crew. And why can't paralyze simply paralyze people? And why can't I take the trolley in Farnsport? And did the young woman in Basil have to marry a farmer, or did she get exploded in the war? Or was it the cataclysm that caused that? O yes, and not being able to switch around crystals from weapons/armor without them immediately degrading, when you went through all the trouble to farm for three hours to get the perfect crystals, seems that odd kind of stinginess that creeps into video games these days.\t\t\t\t\t
