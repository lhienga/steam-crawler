\r\n\t\t\t\t\tShort summary:

A love letter to classic JRPGs with plenty of fresh ideas, solid presentation and unfortunate tonal shifts.

The Good:

+ The basic idea of fully recovering after every fight makes combat less of a slow battle of attrition where encounters force your party to gradually expend their limited resources and more of a series of genuine challenges that could go either way.
* Having up to 8 characters in a fight means almost everybody gets a chance to shine.
+ Progression being mostly via plot advancement limits grinding. You can still get benefits from running around the same area and killing the same group of monsters over and over, but you actually get a lot more out of exploring, doing various challenges and following the story.
+ Lots of anti-frustration features.
+ Music and sound are both solid. Graphics are exactly as you'd expect, a callback to the last few years of the 16-bit console generation when developers had learned how to get the most out of their hardware.

The bad:
- Some mechanics are rather clunkily implemented. Crystals in particular are a needlessly complex and annoying system that may help you optimize your character builds but you'll frequently find yourself ignoring them to avoid the hassle.
- Not being able to grind your way past a roadblock can leave you stuck at certain tough spots.

The Ugly:
- The writing is best described as "all over the place", with the plot frequently unable to decide whether it is a serious drama or wacky comedy. A lot of jokes also fall flat in part due to the game being written in german by somebody who clearly got their ideas about writing from playing old JRPGs in english, so you get quips that were basically translated twice.
- Some of the available party members never quite find their niche and will likely be sidelined forever once your total crew size goes past 8.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHighly recommend for fans of classic 90s/00s JRPGs.

Story: 8/10 (some threads are a bit loose at the end, but overall the story keeps you engaged throughout)
Graphics: 9/10 (beautifully rendered areas)
Menus/UI: 7/10 (some of the menus are a bit finicky and tedious to use)
Music/Sound: 7/10 (music always fits the tone very well, not a lot of it stands out as memorable. Rohlan Hills, Hermit's Isle and New Wyrnshire are standouts)
Exploration: 8/10 (lots of different ways you can go back to areas to find something new)
Battle/Leveling System: 8/10 (There are no individual levels for MCs. Gaining skills and stats are tied to events in game. It can be a bit unforgiving, but never feels cheap. There's always a strategic way through a battle, but it's definitely not a game you can button mash or grind through).

While it does have some obvious reference points to anime/games of that era, it's never a distraction to the overall story, which was filled with its own warmth and character. Complex battle system and exploration systems and great cast of characters. One of the best RPGs I've played in many years.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI went in with super high expectations, since everyone has been talking about how great Chained Echoes is.

At first, I was critical of every little thing. The maps felt too big and were cluttered with props. Characters dropping F-bombs felt out of place in a JRPG. Leveling wasn't via EXP. Etc. My mind was practically looking for faults.

Slowly but surely though, I stopped nitpicking. Before I knew it, I just found myself immensely enjoying the game. More than I thought was remotely possible. I didn't want it to end when I was at the finale. I used to think I was just getting older and that no games would be able to rekindle this kind of magic that I used to feel when playing a new JRPG back in the late 90s. This game is as good as everyone has been saying, and then some. It deserves a place at the table with games like Chrono Trigger, Final Fantasy, Suikoden, and Secret of Mana as a timeless classic that I will always look fondly on and will likely replay every few years.

___________________

PROS:

- The story is well written and memorable. The plot escalates constantly, full of unexpected twists and turns. They touch on a wide variety of subjects from war & politics, to religion, to science and technology, to death and what happens to souls after death, to psychology, to murder and crime, to poverty, to gods and the gods above them. Chained Echoes hits every note, and it does it well.

- The characters (both playable and non) are all different, likable/hate-able in their own ways, and all grow and evolve throughout the progression of the story.

- There are a ton of playable characters, they are all useful, and they all play uniquely. Its really hard to choose who to bring with you sometimes.

- On top of each playable character having their own playstyle, there are classes you can unlock and equip to people in your team to customize them more. Stuff like Cleric, Warrior, Sage, Thief, Summoner, etc. Each job can be equipped to one character at a time to supplement their moveset with another layer of utility.

- Aside from the perhaps 12+ playable characters, there are dozens of non-playable characters to recruit to your side. Your base ends up becoming something of its own little bustling town. Its something straight out of the suikoden playbook.

- The battle system is extremely well balanced. You regain HP/TP and start fresh with every battle, so you can enjoy strategizing and using your full arsenal of moves for every encounter. You can also adjust the difficulty if you'd like more casual encounters.

- Leveling and getting stronger feels natural. There isn't EXP grinding per say, yet it is set up in a way that it is something you can actively work towards. Weapons and Armor play a large part in your general power level, and those can be upgraded at will at any time with materials you find. Star levels are used to learn new abilities, learn new traits, and directly raise your stats. They awarded from beating story boss encounters and by finishing side quests and tasks from this achievement board you unlock early on. (More on that soon) You also get AP and materials from battles, and the AP you accumulate is used to upgrade your abilities and traits. So even though this game doesn't have exp grinding like a typical JRPG, you can always find things to do to raise your power level in one way or another.

- The achievement board of sorts that you unlock early on gives you ideas of goals to give yourself in each new area you come across in the world. From hunting notorious monsters, to discovering buried treasures, to finding every breakable wall, to things like fight xyz monster with an all girls party. It is addicting to just get sidetracked. This game makes each area of it's world feel like a playground, where you can just go enjoy the content to your hearts content.

- Speaking of the areas/maps, they are expansive.. but they feel purposeful. You are always rewarded for exploring. The maps are visually appealing and well designed. Its not easy to get lost, but there is plenty of room to get sidetracked.

- While all of the areas are interconnected in a way that you can explore most of the world on foot, there is also world map you can access when you get an airship. Think something similar to Secret of Mana, where everything is connected on foot until you get Flammie then you can fly around.

- Fast travel is available and makes zooming around from one place to another while you are working on side content very convenient.

- Its easy to tell what doors you can and cannot enter thanks to clear marking and an easy to read map.

- The music is phenomenal. They obviously were taking inspiration from classics, but they did so extremely well. Some pieces are orchestrated and have vocals. I found myself impressed over and over again when hearing the better tracks for the first time.

- The pixel art graphics are very well done. They aren't masterpiece level like Trials of Mana or FF6, but they are very good and worthy of praise.

- The variety of bosses is honestly impressive. The creators really understood what makes for an interesting boss encounter, and provided tons of fights to look forward to.

- The mech system is actually good. If any of you have played Lost Sphere, you know how it feels to have a mech system thrown in all willy-nilly and have it feel out of place and like it doesn't even need to be in the game. In Chained Echoes it feels right. The mechs add new levels of strategy to encounters and add a new options for traversing through areas.

- They did a really good job of show not tell when it comes to story, lore, scale, and power differences between characters. Its hard to go into details without spoilers, so let me just say that there are many impressive and unforgettable scenes that made all the difference.

___________________

CONS:

- Some character animations feel rushed or like they just used placeholders and forgot. Like when you are climbing cliffs, some character sprites just look less polished. Its mainly just the main characters traversal animations though. I don't remember feeling like any of the other pixel art felt off. It may have been just some of their earliest work.

- In the first few hours of the game, the script had some unnecessary cursing in it. Like F-bombs out of nowhere and such. It felt a bit out of place to me. It was only in the beginning really though, I don't recall it happening again once the game got going. Its like the author was just getting warmed up as far as writing goes at the start of the game, and really got into their stride once they were a bit deeper in.

- I wish there was more endgame content. Like I wanted more optional dungeons and superbosses to open up when it was time to take on the final boss, or even in a post game. There is a lot of endgame content, don't get me wrong.. but there was so much MORE midgame content that I hoped the endgame would be just as beefy.

- I have some complaints about the main character and the ending that I can't go into without going into spoiler territory.\t\t\t\t\t
