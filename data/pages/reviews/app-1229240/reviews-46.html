\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTLDR:
A promising looking JRPG-like game in the vein of FF6, held back by clunky systems, a bit too familiar story and unrewarding combat.

Chain of Echoes is an attempt to recreate the magic of ye olden JRPGs, and while it does succeed in some areas, it's badly failing in others.

Story:
The story follows a gang of adventurers whose paths cross by fate, but whereas in Final Fantasy 6 and Chrono Trigger (the two games CoE borrows most from) it all happens very organically, here the main cast is basically thrown at you very quickly just to get it over with. For most of the early parts of the story you'll just be wondering what the f is happening. Who are all these people and why should i care?
Thankfully, once that hurdle is overcome and the gang sets out it does get better. It held a fairly decent quality all the way to just before the finale where it's all just wrapped up way too fast, leaving behind a giant pile of unanswered questions.
A lot of the story elements are somewhat copy-pasted from FF6, but it was a good story back then and it still works now. However, since the game is marketed to people (like me) who did play those old games, it comes across as a bit samey.

Music:
Functional. Not bad, but not particularly memorable.

Gameplay:
This is where the devs tried to shake things up, but just ended up making everything too convoluted and clunky. Specifically, there are two major issues: The combat flow and the levelling system.

In combat you have an overdrive bar which increases or decreases depending on the skills you use. You'll want to keep it "in the green zone" for as much as possible, as you get 50% skill cost rebates and some stat buffs for doing so. Letting it go "into the red" debuffs your stats instead and increases costs.
The problem with this is that enemy attacks on you ALSO increases this bar, striving to push you into the red. This works against you _hard_ in fights where there are many opponents, or foes with multiple attacks per turn. Some fights are basically impossible to avoid going into the red, all the while the game keeps screaming at you to get out of it.

Combat rewards you with skillpoints, both a pool you can allocate at will, and a set amount to each skill you have equipped on your character. These skills have 3 levels each with usually fairly small differences for each level. In practice, whether you have a skill at lvl1 or lvl3 doesn't affect you that much in combat.
There is no character level-up system, instead you will get a shard point for each defeated boss which you can buy new skills for. This is where the second issue comes into play.
Since you cannot equip more than 8 skills at a time, the best things to buy for the points is generally not new and interesting abilities, instead raw stat boosters that each provide a minor benefit. Very anticlimactic.
"You defeated this huge boss! congrats! take this point and buy a +2 def increase"

Since defeating bosses and enemies does not level you up, grinding enemies won't make you more powerful. Instead, you need to buy or find better gear.
The problem here is that improving your gear is mostly dependent on exploration or just buying from the shops and, once that's done, follow up with quite a bit of tedium.
You need to remove your crystals from your old item, upgrade the new one and slot in the crystals again, and then repeat for all your other characters. This gets repetitive, fast. For each time you want to increase a characters power, you'll need to go through this process, again and again, and all just to keep your dudes from being simply unable to defeat certain bosses.
And as for the crystals? Raw stat increases and a few of the "Counter" types are good, the rest are pretty much all trash, which feels like a huge missed opportunity.

Conclusion:
The game tries to do things differently just for the sake of being different, while the story tries to reference older games for the nostalgia. It can't live up to the pedigree of the games it draws inspiration from and sometimes intentionally actively makes things worse than they need to be.
And then, when it's all over, your mind will mostly just think of all the unanswered questions the game just casually brushes aside.

That said, it's by no means a bad game. I played though the whole thing and don't regret the time I spent on it. It just feels like it could've been so much better if they'd just spent some time on quality of life features, try to invest us more in the early story, fill out the missing parts that felt rushed, and expand the combat system instead of restricting it to rules that feel arbitrary.

I give it a thumbs down, but don't let that stop you from buying it. Like i said, it's not bad, it just could've been much better.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've been playing the game on my Nintendo Switch.

Well, I appreciate the developer's efforts. I'm sure this was a big task for him. That said, I will be honest and say that price of $20-$25 feels overpriced for a product that is not as crisp or as deep as some retro games that follow a similar artistic style.

The positive - I appreciate that battles are very quick. There's not any long intro or exit animation, nor overly long fight move animation. I also found that the music was very impressive for a small developer. You can also tell a lot of thought was given to the creativity of the world map, although player exploration/roaming is very limited.

The negatives -

The world map. Yes, I know I complimented the creativity of it, but the art is also poorly designed at times. There will be many times you will think you can access a certain area, but can't. There will also be many times you think you can't access an area, and not only can, but it's required. It gets to the point that even if you see something that looks like a wall you will press against it to see if you will pass through it or break through it.

There isn't a traditional character leveling system. I'm ok with that. Instead there is an upgrading available for the character and their battle moves through GS and SP. GS will allow you to attain a new ability. SP will allow you improve that ability. GS is only given after a major boss fight. SP is given after every fight. The problem is that it can take several hundred SP to improve an ability............but the vast majority of battles only reward you with 1-3 SP (commonly 1 SP). Well you might think, "Well, I will just fight a lot of battles. No problem, right?". You're right but you need to consider that enemy battles are sparse throughout the map and does not have a re-spawn unless you leave the area you are in. What this means overall is that this game promotes the absolute worst kind of grinding. And grind you shall, because this is not an easy game.

The next problem is that there are story points that feel cliche and even predictable at times. The "screaming flowers" at the inn? You will likely know what's really going on before the story reveals it.

Lastly, I think the small things in a game matter. It severely bugs me that the developer couldn't be bothered to make more than one face panel for each character. The art for the characters isn't great to begin with, but it takes away your immersion of a game when encountered with a bad/emotional situation and your character still has the same facial expression next to the text. Tales of Vesperia knew the importance of changing facial expressions with different situations and emotions.......and that was about 15 years ago............so there's no excuse why the developer decided to phone it in regarding this.

All in all, it's not a bad game. It actually has some good points. I just can't recommend it for the price and for the fact that there are many other better games with a simpler style, due to age or creativity, and cheaper price.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhile I have qualms with the way this game fully recovers your health in-between fights, and how the story seems to swerve just to avoid an expected conclusion at times, it's pretty good overall.

Admittedly, I generally just tried to force my way through most encounters, and there wasn't much that demanded that I put together a strategy ahead of time. That's probably a limitation of the way the game uses milestones to grant your characters levels, effectively granting a new one with every defeated boss. If you could grind, you could also attempt to avoid grinding and put together novel combos between your characters instead.

That being said, there are some more difficult fights that are optional that required some forethought and planning. The game gets to shine a bit more when it gets to put some pressure on you. The mech battles are fun, and they give you direct control over one of the game's unique mechanics, the Overdrive bar. It can feel more like a cudgel in standard fights, forcing you to pick less than optimal actions to avoid burning resources and taking more damage from enemies. In mech fights you can almost freely manage it, which is a nice contrast. The mech designs are all pretty rad, in my opinion, as well and there doesn't seem to be any one equipment set that's totally useless when you're trying to equip them. There are some weapons on the mechs that definitely synergize *better* with each other though.\t\t\t\t\t
