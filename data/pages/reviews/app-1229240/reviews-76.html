Unfortunately, at just under 5 hours into the game, the combat difficulty has escalated to the point that even the simplest battles are taxing or--in some cases--even impossible. This is despite having never shied away from battles. Encounters are not random and unless you loop through the same environments over and over again to respawn those encounters, you won't have the necessary experience to level up. To make matters worse, there are too many ways to spend skill points. Choosing the wrong combination will leave you unable to move the story forward due to impossibly difficult combat.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tJust finished the game along with a lot of optional stuff. I'll keep it brief.

Pros:
-Combat is intuitive for a turn based game, the battle gauge keeps you on your toes when fighting challenging enemies (until you get to the point agi is pumped and all fights are ez)
-Story is good and keeps you guessing what's going on. I will say the ending IN MY OPINION was disappointing and left some things unanswered that hopefully a sequel will tidy up
-Optional quests/fights left a lot for me to do aside from the main quest. You can unlocked extra characters and the elite mobs you can hunt down were always something fun to check out

Cons
-Art/graphics While some scenes and areas are really nice looking, a majority I found left a lot to be desired.
-Music/audio Nothing memorable honestly
-Some quality of life things would be nice, I hate the purity system on the stones for your gear. I dont want to run around for hours to have a chance of finding what im looking for. Likewise I feel like there should be a way where you automatically dictate which skills/spells will level rather than going in and spending points manually.

Overall review : 7.5/10 The story and world building was what captivated me. The characters are interesting enough and the way they interact is entertaining. The combat system is refreshing and I mean, mechs. If you're a fan of JRPG's it is something I'd recommend picking up for sure.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFantastic SNES-style JRPG. Story, graphical style, and gameplay are all a beautiful homage to the nostalgic heyday of RPGs.

It's that last point, gameplay, however, where this game has taken the classic era JRPG baton and really run with it. It's done so many little things to improve on the gameplay formula, notably: swappable party members mid battle (similar to FFX), no need to rest at inns or find save points, and grinding is largely optional with bosses gating your major upgrades. The result are prefectly scaled battles every time, always with some element of strategy and danger. Although, on that last point, I did find that while there were a few challenging encounters early on, by the late game *everything* was more or less a cakewalk.

Also regarding gameplay, each character feels distinct and fills a role well. It touches upon the the team-techs of something akin to Chrono Trigger, though not exactly. You can set up combos between party members, such as applying Oil to boost Fire attacks of another character (like FF Tactics), or set up ailments like Paralyze to set up a certain redhead for major damage. It lets you get use out of just about everyone, while also getting to pick and choose your favorites most of the time.

If you're a fan of oldschool JRPGs and would like to experience what they might have been with decades of hindsight and understanding of what makes them really fun, I think you'll really enjoy this one.

All that said, I do wish the game was a little longer. I played it like I would have years ago blundering through FF6 or Chrono Trigger the first time, and still finished the main story, most of the board, and every side quest I could find in under 40 hours. Finishing endgame content will probably push it closer to 50 - 60, which is, admittedly, pretty on point for a "classic" JRPG. But this *is* a modern game, after all, even if an homage to the classics, and it's not uncommon to see 60 - 100 hour modern RPGs when all the side content is taken into account. Maybe I'm spoiled by modern games, and maybe I'm just wishing I could play this very cool game a little longer. Probably both.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you're like me, you probably scrolled down to the reviews just to see the one that sold you on the game, and there's a ton of them here already. I almost never write reviews simply because I don't really have anything constructive to add that hasn't already been said.

And yet here I am, writing a review.

As of writing this, I'm 43 hours into playing the game, albeit some of that was letting it sit on my desktop while I did other things, and that's okay, because point 1 is the music.

Music: I thoroughly enjoy the music in this game, and in some areas, it can change it from being some drab maze with mushrooms and fungus everywhere, to a haunting experience. I'll probably add some songs to my playlist, but I do wish I had the ability to shuffle songs in some areas, especially while I'm grinding. That brings me to point 2. Grinding.

Grind: This isn't a big part of the game, not gonna lie. I know some others mentioned there is a steep difficulty curve, and that's not incorrect. However, grinding isn't going to get you as far as you might think. Experience gain isn't quite the same as other RPGs, where you just run around and attack and farm XP... no, not in Chained Echoes. This game uses an SP system which might net you 2 SP per battle most times. Skills and Passive abilities rely on SP gained to level them up, and this counts for both Passive and Active skills. This pushes me into point 3 - Difficulty

Difficult?: Yes, it is. A single wrong tactic can wipe your team pretty easily if you're unprepared. Luckily there's the ability to start a fight over from the beginning, and it even throws you into your menu so you can swap equipment and skills before trying again. The only times I've run into problems defeating an enemy though, is either because I was trying to fight something I had no reason to, or I forgot about the other mechanics of the game, like the achievement rewards system, which give SP and Arc(Gold Coins), to pay for skill upgrades and weapon/armor upgrades. Point 4, customization!

Customization: This game is pretty indepth in how it lets you change how you play. Battles run on a tag team system with your primary and backup character, and each character has an open Skills tree that you can pick and choose from once you gain skill points. Each character also has a weapon, armor, accessory, and "class" item. You can forge upgrades for weapons and armors, and attach crystals to each into slots. The crystals can be combined and upgraded themselves, and provide bonuses like ATK, MND buffs, or even cast auto-spells on your teammate at the start of battle. Those are just a few examples. I have a tenancy to focus on each characters strengths, so the crystals really let me buff out each to my liking. This brings me to point 5 - Story and Characters

The Filling: Chained Echoes is a love child of every 90s and early 2000s video gaming tropes and this is no joke. You'll find references both old and new in the story, in the art style, in the combat, in the skills, in every aspect of the game. If you're a fan of SNES era RPGs, this will feel like a remaster of some of them including Chrono Trigger, Final Fantasy, a little bit of XenoGears, etc etc.

I've only finished Act I, but I plan on finishing the game and hopefully unlocking all of the achievements, simply because I feel like I'm experiencing something old, all over again for the first time.\t\t\t\t\t
