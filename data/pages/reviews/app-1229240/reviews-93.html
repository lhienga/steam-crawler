In which case, stat leveling is very important.

It's not that this is a bad game,
Loved the art style. Just things are more hidden than they needed to be, you really have to put in work to find what you wanted to find - I must stress this point - Too much work, to the point where its right infront of you and its still really hard to tell that its there.

I wish there was more to mech combat, it seems over simplified to where its boring.
If given the chance, I would have avoided it completely cause of how its setup. Unfortunately with no level ups and how important stats are in the game.. That isn't plausible, I don't think.

I guess I went into my pre-order, hoping the Mech combat would resemble classics like Xenogears, but it comes nowhere near it and falls short of even non-mech combat.

Lots of skills early on become next to useless late because quite simply a skill that does the same thing, better pops up..
Which in a game like this where each level you place on the stat page (at least for a casual playthrough) matters.

Because again there is no traditional "LEVEL UP +HP +DMG +DEF +LUCK" whatever of a traditional RPG, Its just "You gained a skill point for killing this unique monster :) "

I'd rather have level ups - - its a personal preference thing, that in this case did not fit my preference.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game is solid, and has the fundamentals of an RPG down to the letter. This is the direction I want games to go.

I want to put that first to underscore any harsh criticism I may have, as I wish for a second game, one that improves upon game mechanics and story themes.

Story
Pros:
Without any direct spoilers, the story is full of tragedy and themes about war, failure of the human condition to improve and uplift the lives of fellow man. While at points it does tell of some success at these endeavors, I feel these are overshadowed by the bleakness put upon the world. It tells of the failure of weapons to keep peace, of how the road to hell is paved with good intentions. Lots of play with traditional JRPG tropes, many recognizable roles. Characters and dialog felt like their persona shown through without much assistance.

Cons:
The criticism I have for this is that it tends to be full of despair, and heartbreak without much recourse for correcting the mistakes except through the passive nature and advancement of the game. Hell, that may have been the intent, as it's a very thought provoking despair. But, I breathe for those more lighthearted endings in my JRPGs where we do see at a micro and macro level that things will get better.

The story elements came at weird times, where you'd get multiple large revelations within minutes of each other, so you wouldn't have time to process the ramifications of one before the next set in.

Suggestions:
Story is up to the writer, so if this is the story you want to tell, I can eat worms for all I care.
Farnsport seems really rough for a long time in the game. Would suggest some intermediate stages as it recovers from Frederik's takeover.

Gameplay



Pros:
Each JRPG usually has it's basics, then extends itself in a novel fashion to a new mechanic. The basics were polished, but The Overdrive mechanic (this extended feature) breaths a lot of life into the genre. Even after 60 hours, I still would get caught off guard by it as a boss would turn around and kick my ass. The saving grace here is that the game pairs a reload into battle and 100% runaway mechanic that make your attempts more frequent, more thought provoking as you attempt a different strategy. It doesn't hinder game play with an unnecessary amount of time between you and getting back into the fight. Healing felt intentionally weak with the health pools not reaching insane heights, so that you couldn't just sponge damage without constant intervention. Characters tool kits felt distinct and unique to make them all fill niches, even for such a wide cast. Switching out further enhances the ability to use certain members for combo effects.

Cons:
Character balance seemed off, as some clear standouts would crush your opponents while others felt like weaker versions in the more general aspects of play (such as damage output and support abilities). Tanks seemed less necessary than other games, while hate allowed for control of attacks, you were probably better off using a support type to buff your glass cannons and just grind enemies to a pulp. Emblems were few and far between, and while significant, were more used to enhance an existing characters role rather than redefine it or provide a twist.

Itemization felt the weakest out of all other aspects of the game, as it was pure power buffs from one item to the next. Crystals and their progression felt as deep as the game would allow, but like Emblems did not alter the way the game was played, but just enhanced the existing structure.

Agility stacking seemed like the worst offender of balance, but I hadn't experimented after finding my particular strategy, so there could be others out there that out scale it.

Suggestions:

Diversity of effects would assist here. Not only in the crystals and Emblems, but in what the basic attributes do, or don't do. I feel like the sky is the limit, and as long as more depth was given to the diversity of tactics available. Rob's kit is in the right direction for this, it's just the payoff seemed worse than a glass cannon getting funneled buffs.\t\t\t\t\t
