\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat an amazing game! Few RPGs this long manage to stay engaging and fresh for their entire length, but this game has just so much stuff (mechanics, locations, characters, etc). All the more remarkable that it was made by a single developer!

Successes:
- Combat. One of the deepest and most engaging combat systems in an RPG I’ve played (old or new). Challenging but rarely frustrating…you can occasionally die to a random mob, but you can immediately retry the fight. I particularly enjoyed the way you put characters in pairs that can swap back and forth freely in battle…it opened up some really interesting tactical choices.
- Plot/World/Characters. The story is complicated and thoughtful, with a vast roster of complicated characters who rarely fall into stereotypes. Despite death and tragedy on a global scale, you can make the case that no character is truly evil. The deep roster of PCs was colorful and fun, and I enjoyed spending 60+ hours with (most of) them.
- Production Value. The art, the maps, the music…everything has been carefully and expertly crafted. A tremendous amount of thought has gone into the player experience, and it does a great job avoiding lulls, deadends, or frustrations.

Mixed Bags:
- Sky Armor. At the halfway point in the game, you gain the ability to switch into giant flying mechs operated by four of your characters. It’s extremely badass and it opens up the world in an exciting way. Combat in the sky armors has some interesting mechanical differences…but it’s boring in a way that regular combat rarely is. The long sky armor sections were the only parts I was impatient to get through (and I ultimately turned down the difficulty to make them go faster).
- Interface/Menus. Choices and the information you need to make them are often several menus apart, and the number of different buttons you need to purchase a single item is truly astounding.

Failures:
- Crystals. It’s a relatively minor system in a game chock full of systems, but the crystal system is worth noting as perhaps the only unmitigated failure in the game. You will pick up more crystals than anything else in the game, but the rules for combining them aren’t intuitive and the UI for managing them is brutally inefficient. At some point, it stops being worth the time it takes to move your old crystals to a new weapon. After picking up hundreds of useless crystals, I hit a cap…which felt like both a slap in the face and permission to stop worrying about the damn things.

Overall, Chained Echoes is a resounding success which is fun, compelling, and badass almost the entire time. If you loved FFVI, Chrono Trigger, or the like, then I’m not sure there’s a modern game I can recommend more.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs far as retro-styled JRPGs in the current era of gaming go this one is definitely up on the list. It has a pretty interesting story, solidly developed characters, and a combat system that does just enough new stuff to be interesting without overcomplicating everything.
I do have a couple of personal qualms, and they largely come down to choices made in game design rather than actual problems. The biggest of these is that after every combat the party heals fully. While this makes for tense battles that can really give you a run even in more minor fights, it takes away from the grander experience of dungeon-crawling and working your way through the world trying to manage resources. You don't really have to worry too much about running out of items or keeping yourself away from fights so your health and MP are in a good spot for the boss fight. I understand the choice, and it's a perfectly fine one, I just personally don't enjoy it as much.
That said, the battles do feel tense, and I love that. They tend to be challenging and make you really think about how you're approaching your turns. There is a tracker that keeps a fine line between boosting your skills and doubling the damage you take and it's a fun game to try and keep that where it needs to go through choosing skills, defending, and swapping characters out. Each character I've used (which I think is about half of the ones in the game) feels like they bring something unique to the party and they can play their roles well without being too niche or too general.
So overall, really good game. Interesting story, creative and challenging combat, and good feeling to the balance so far. Just some design choices I'm not a fan of, but I appreciate that they went with something that stands out instead of making another attempt at a ChronoTrigger clone.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game goes pretty hard. The art is gorgeous and the leveling system is fresh. The plot and character development isn't always perfect [EDIT: WHAT? The plot is actually pretty dope : o. There are a few moments I'm confused about still but v entertaining lol] yet it hangs together. The gameplay is longgg. There is so much to explore.

I was a little thrown by how many characters there are but it keeps things interesting strategy-wise. Also love that the class emblems are very interchangeable.

Gathering crystals and reforging gear can be a little tedious but some of the buffs are very worth it: my favs. being the critical damage possibilities, poison and paralyze extender, a bunch of the counter buffs and the different types of strikes that increase elemental magic hits.

Difficulty is well balanced. It's not a breeze but it's not dark souls difficult. I love figuring out optimal combo moves within the party and deciding which buffs to forge which weapons with. With so many characters by the end there's a lot to choose from. (I didn't love the mech fighting as much cuz it felt limited by having only four ppl. But mech mode does allow for some good exploration / travel.)

Oh and the overheat system is cool. With a lot of RPGs you figure out a combination of moves and just repeat them every battle, but this forces you to switch if up and make use of your tag in party members.

If you are looking for a new rpg to play, try this for sure.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGreat game. If you enjoy the best of the 16-bit-era RPGs, like Chrono Trigger and Final Fantasy VI, you'll enjoy this too. If I had to highlight some criticisms, it would be that the story is a bit convoluted and several of the characters are underdeveloped. Your main party will revolve around 6 or 7 characters that you use throughout the game, but as many as 12 will join. The last few characters who join are very easy to ignore, and don't have many scenarios that really highlight their usefulness. That's a shame, because some of them are pretty cool.

Aside from those nitpicks, this is a very good game with a lot of heart. The equipment and crystal systems are highly intricate, complementing a quite enjoyable battle system. The pixel art is beautiful, and completionists will feast on the bevy of side quests and extra content. Highly recommended.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI have a tendency to get rather long-winded when talking about games - both the ones I recommend, and the ones I rag on for twenty minutes or so. But this time, I'll try to be brief: Chained Echoes takes everything that was great about the Golden Age of JRPG's, and elevates it to a new standard. Its inspirations are both obvious and wide-ranging, going from Chrono Trigger and Final Fantasy VI to Xenogears - but unlike many other such 'throwback' RPG's, it doesn't just wallow in nostalgia, it actually UNDERSTANDS what made that era so great: The willingness to experiment with the genre, to come up with fresh takes on the basic formula of turn-based combat and skill-progression! Something that the studios who actually made those great games, back in the day, seem to have since forgotten in their hunt for 'safe investment' and 'guaranteed sellers'.

So yes, it's just as great as those games were, back in the days of the SNES and PlayStation, and enhanced with all the quality-of-life features we've grown used to today - and some really beautiful 32-bit pixel-art. A must-play for anyone who still has fond memories of those venerated titles of yesteryear, but find their supposed successors falling flat.\t\t\t\t\t
