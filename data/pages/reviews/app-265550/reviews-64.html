\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDead Rising 3: not as good as DR1/2 but still a fun time. To hear the fandom discuss DR3 you'd think it was a disaster. In many ways it is better than before, putting huge amounts of zombies in greater variety up in a larger no loading world. Your character controls far better. It's easier overall, giving the player a wacky power trip as they get settled in with always on super powers, overpowered combo weapons and many new convenience features. All that said if you enjoyed DR1/2 you will enjoy a one and done of DR3. It takes awhile for the power curve to get knocked out of alignment, the story is the same charming nonsense and there's plenty to do and experience. Let's discuss those changes in detail.

DR3 is the story of Nick Ramos, inheriting the title of most bullied man in the world after Chuck successfully shed it in DR2. Nick needs to help his friends and surrogate family escape Los Perdidos, a delightful Californian city where four large districts and a length of the freeway have been quarantined following a zombie outbreak. "Spoilers" follow but there may be more than there first appears to this outbreak and a sinister government organization might have a hand in things. Be still my heart, but I think there may even be complications in the initial escape plan. DR3 makes the first big change here: the time limit is far more forgiving, four times as long as any savvy DR player needs to plan their mayhem out. In fact running through story missions as fast as possible gives this game a run time of maybe three hours. The exploration and side quests more than make up for this. There is a Nightmare mode available from the start that lets DR vets play it with a proper time limit and a lot less of the easy mode features added to DR3, and I appreciate the speed runnable nature of the main story after I tried to get a Nightmare clear, used one of the bad endings to shorten Chapter 7 and got auto-saved to a new story without my clear. Whoops. If you're wondering, do Ch. 7 properly(go to the karaoke bar, etc.)

The opening of this game sets the tone quite wonderfully: there are more zombies than ever and hundreds can be on screen at a time. Navigating the crowds is initially a dizzying undertaking and it continues to be delightfully involving as you play, the game getting a proper soundtrack that really emphasizes the tension. Zombies are the same near-filler they always were, grabbing and striking often, ramping up at night and now with a second class of heavier, more dangerous zombies. A new variant emerges later, adding an extreme melee danger to groups and making zombies even nastier than they are at night. I do miss the gas zombies here as kings are much more passive and the occasional pack of DR2's run ending hit squads would've been the real cherry on top for challenging design. Still the zombies are just flat out better in this game. Hearing the howls of heavies in a tight spot is a good chill.

To balance out the increased zombie danger level the player controls are dramatically improved. Nick moves with a smoothness that Frank and Chuck dream of. Running, jumping and fighting is the best in the series and the smoother movement really enhances the added verticality of map design and options for evading zombies in a pinch. And yes, Nick can run. He can sprint and it suits the larger map well, though it only takes about four level ups to make sprinting so good it may as well be infinite. Driving is expanded on dramatically and adds to player power, cars being available often and being nearly invincible even from the start. The only real challenge to driving comes from learning the one erratic, complicated line of unbroken travel that can take you across the whole city. The in-game map is often not entirely accurate, walls can be put up between missions with no warning and some things are unlabeled such as one way ramps.

The power scale starts off well. A big change is that combo weapons can't be made by experimentation. A blueprint must be found first and ironically you're much weaker at the start of DR3 than in DR2. Once you have the knowledge Nick can combo anytime and anywhere. DR3 also has magical infinite resource chests in each safe house, allowing you to pull anything you've picked up or made before out of thin air. There's a light cooldown timer but it lines up in either mode that you can grab some of what you need, go do a mission or two, and come back to a full meter. This definitely simplifies things... however, DR2 was secretly as easy once you knew what to do. Chuck had access to blended drinks, high tier weapons(nails, bats, MMA gloves) and abundant healing(coffee creamer) the second he started DR2. Later you find the infinite gun shop and I'd say DR3 is just cutting to the chase on something that happens in both games. DR3 does have some better weapon balance, such as one-time use explosives coming in stacks per craft. I found myself using many more weapons in DR3 that I'd craft once and never again in DR2.

Although you eventually become omnipotent this is tied to dutiful leveling and Level 50 in particular. This also matches the previous games, where difficulty drops dramatically once you've got full inventory, life and move-speed. I think where DR3 becomes too easy is that many later combo weapons are wildly overpowered(the Splitshot could be called the Fullscreen Death Wave), vehicles are far too safe and available far more often, and all clunkiness has been removed from survivor interaction. There's no need to stay near them, they teleport to safety once you out-range them and most of them are replaced by easy "kill a small crowd of zombies and this idiot leaves to die alone elsewhere" pop-ups. DR3 greatly simplifies one of the series oldest staples, an undeniable source of frustration, but doing so removes the highs and lows of a genuine pain in the ass challenge. While technically an improvement and more appealing to more casual players, DR3 streamlines so much that it loses some charm.

Psychopaths are still here. I've heard a gripe that there's "only" 7 but those are the listed mostly optionals in the achievements. Story bosses add another 7 and put DR3 in the same league as DR1/2. They also benefit from the better engine and are more varied in combat. The story of DR3 isn't great but does the job, about as insane and comical with hits of pathos as the rest of the series. It's a wild B movie ride to see once. The tone is grimmer but this is DR writing through and through at heart and the psychos are on point.

The DLCs don't add much. At least they're free. Mostly an excuse to run through the city far too many times picking up mindless collectables. DLCs 1/4 are middle of the road with 4 being almost entirely pointless busywork, DLC2 suffers enormously from an irritating mission control whining at you every 3 minutes on the dot but is also workable, DLC3 is the only one that could be bothered to work in actual encounters/bosses and is the best by default. If you can't get enough DR3 then they are... there, but don't expect big satisfying additions to the story or even the gameplay.

At the end of the day and this too long review DR3 is a good game but suffers from losing some of the much tighter focus earlier games had. DR3's map is bigger but that is often used to add even more travel chores, DR3 has a bigger push for fun exploration but sometimes it is pure padding and tedium, DR3 offers a fantastic chill and rampage gameplay set up but some of that deep satisfaction from mastery and planning is lost. DR3 is easier but it takes awhile to get there so the first playthrough matches DR1/2, trying to make due until you get some levels built up. I'd have appreciated some more challenging variant modes like DR1 had but DR3 is still solid, a great $20 or less buy, a good $30 game, a nice addition to the trilogy that rounds it out with a more accessible and casual game, albeit at the cost of focus.\t\t\t\t\t
