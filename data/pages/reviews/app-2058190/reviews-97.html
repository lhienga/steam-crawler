I will talk now about Lost Judgment.
Lost Judgment is easily one of the best games I have ever played.
The game has a strong story, very deep. The developers chose a difficult theme and that's bullying and I say they did a great job. This game helps in bringing to light the many problems we encounter/hear regarding bullying and the bullies. A great story with a difficult theme, very well delivered.

Lost Judgment is an awesome and well deserved sequel to Judgment. I would dare to say that this one is a little bit better than the first one but it's just my opinion. I hope there will be a third game. We need more games with this kind of themes.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFirst: Don't mind my low playtime, I've played this game basically every single day since it came out a year and change ago on my Xbox One.

Second: I'm going to be using the Japanese names for the fighting styles in this review: Waltz is Crane, Flash is Tiger, and Flow is Snake.

Now with that out of the way, the actual review: It's about ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ time. Lost Judgment is the best work RGG Studio has produced in a long time (and, given that Ichiban remains the main protagonist of Yakuza and Kiryu returning for Yakuza 8, that "long time" is starting to look an awful lot like "ever".) The music is top notch, likewise the fight choreography. The combat is the best in the studio's entire catalog. The writing is top-notch, bucking long-standing RGG trends that I'd always found insufferable. The minigames are fun with the high school they're linked to being about as fun as you can get a high school to be and an equally fun narrative surrounding its students. Lost Judgment is a complete package that's earned my 2021 GOTY spot and, thanks to both this Steam release and the Kaitou Files expansion releasing earlier this year, has very handily usurped Sifu and Midnight Fight Express for my 2022 GOTY. But... why? Why do I hold it in such high regard? Let's get into that!

First of all: Yagami. This isn't his first outing, but it is the first I've been able to join him on and I love the guy; his dynamic with his buddies (whether it's the original crew from Judge Eyes or newcomer Kyouko Amasawa) is wildly enjoyable. Whether he's cheering up a bullied child and encouraging her not to give up on her dreams or being repeatedly thrown for a loop by Amasawa's, well, loopiness, Yagami's interactions are always a blast. He has the "Just Some Guy" charm of Kiryu-era Yakuza bosses down pat. His fighting styles are all graceful and well-designed with additions like vaulting and wall-jumping to shake up the combat and his choreography benefits greatly from the fact that Yagami has to actually avoid and defend against attacks.

However, the biggest reason I like Yagami so much is that for once in their career, RGG Studio have managed to create a protagonist that isn't patently insufferable. Kazuma Kiryu very quickly establishes himself as a sanctimonious hypocrite who prefers to run away from his problems until someone close to him gets hurt or killed and is wholly uninterested in committing to his platitudes about confronting the past and who he is, while Ichiban Kasuga is essentially the Japanese Wade Watts: a loser with no personality beyond "lol video game references xD!" who drags the entire game down into Satan's wine cellar and whose status as hero and protagonist is facilitated purely by the creators violating the narrative to jack him off at every opportunity.

Yagami, on the other hand, has NONE OF THAT ♥♥♥♥. His ideals are consistent and he has no qualms with committing to them; joined Seiryo High's Mystery Research Club as guidance counselor so he can investigate the main case freely? He will gladly see that job to completion. Strongly disagrees with the main villain on the nature of the law? He will just as lief throw hands for his ideals.

Likewise, while he's certainly more relaxed than Kiryu, he's not childish like Ichiban: He'll kill time at the arcade or on the Mega Drive he's got at his office, but when he's kidnapped and strapped to a chair the man with the chainsaw who's about to vivisect him doesn't abruptly transform into some lol so random xd enemy design with a terrible pun for a name. When one of his coworkers has their house besieged by gangsters, the building doesn't abruptly turn into a dungeon with turnbased enemy encounters because "OMG DAE it's just like Dragon Quest!!!1!1!"

Next up, combat: With RGG Studio deciding that LAD's braindead ungabunga which number biggest "combat" is going to be the future of Yakuza, it's up to Lost Judgment to continue the tradition of beat 'em up combat that is very easy to learn and hard to master, and it does so with gusto. But, not content to simply reiterate on what Kiryu had or what Judge Eyes gave Yagami, Lost Judgment decides to take that combat and make it more so. Both Yakuza and Judge Eyes have always had had light RPG and fighting game elements, but they've always been just that--light. Lost Judgment leans much more heavily on both by introducing status effects and allowing you to cancel attacks outside of super mode and the results are delightful. Juggling enemies is incredibly satisfying, and status effects are balanced just right to spice up the gameplay while keeping it intrinsic.

Additionally, the fighting styles themselves are a blast: Waltz, your crowd-control style, is loaded with graceful kicks and sweeping multi-hit attacks that make it as effective against a single enemy as against a crowd. Flash, the dedicated "strike first, strike hard, no mercy" single-target style has elements of bajiquan and a hold-to-charge mechanic that succeeds at making you feel the oomph of the charged attacks, while also providing you with a potent counter in the form of the franchise's mainstay Tiger Drop. Flow, the new style introduced with LJ that focuses on defensive prowess and throwing enemies, has a parry mechanic and strong options against armed enemies, as well as drawing very heavy inspiration from Wing Chun.

Boss battles are also some of the best in the franchise because they're where everything comes together, backed by some stellar boss design; a recurring boss in the game has a number of the same options Yagami does, bringing his fights as close to Yakuza PvP as we're likely ever gonna get. However, there's also some other neat tricks: Mortal Reversals, and QTE design.

Judge Eyes' controversial Deadly Attacks return here, but they don't lock your health bar anymore and dodging out of the way and hitting 3 (B/Circle for you non-Tekken folks) at the right time performs a Mortal Reversal which does a massive amount of damage to the boss. On top of this, LJ breaks away from RGG's single-input QTE design to occasionally introduce multi-step QTEs often designed to evoke actions you can perform in gameplay that resemble what Yagami does if you succeed.

And then there's the writing. Lost Judgment has what I consider to be the best writing of any game RGG has released so far. With the Tojo and Omi disbanded as part of LAD's campaign to make its protagonist as special as possible, LJ doesn't have the benefit of established lore; it has to build everything from scratch and it does so wonderfully. The game moves with surprising speed given how much work it has to do in comparison to its predecessors, and the antagonist factions it introduces leverage that atrocious decision excellently. The mystery in the game is legitimately compelling, and the game's climax is one of the best I've ever seen; it gets the "TV legal thriller J-drama" thing it's going for absolutely DOWN, and that makes it all the more badass when Yagami clinches a court case in the coolest manner imaginable. The high schoolers and their stories are all intriguing and do an excellent job of getting you way more invested than you probably should be. The antagonists are absolute gigachads who break two of RGG's main molds: One is a genuinely morally gray antagonist who has just as much of a point as the protagonist and manages to break the pattern of RGG villains getting capped literally the second they show anything that vaguely looks like doubt and the other is an unapologetic villain who has zero interest in redemption and stays evil until the end--and, much like Lost Judgment as a whole, they are very much a breath of fresh air.

In conclusion: Absolutely buy this game. It's RGG's best work yet and its mere presence on Steam alongside Judge Eyes is a good sign that there's more Yagami in our future--and I'll drink to that. Karii!\t\t\t\t\t
