\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI usually start my reviews with some general statement about the game as a sort of digestible single-verse sentiment. My feelings towards Lost Judgment are so thoroughly entwined with adoration that this is the best I can come up with:

Lost Judgment isn't just RGG's finest game, or a landmark title for the genre, but a work of emotional maturity so engrossing that I would eagerly recommend it to anyone that plays video games.

There are your hallmarks for a RGG game- ridiculous side quests, an abundance of minigames, and a definite appreciate for tongue-in-cheek humor at the best of times- but the main story readily surpasses any and all praise I could heap upon those aspects, individual or collective. I won't act as though it's a high point for literature, but as far as video games go, there are few that hit me with such an emotional weight while still remaining thoroughly grounded in reality.

Lost Judgment looks at the bitter and cruel side of humanity. It takes our aptitude for social violence and radical justification and drags it out into the spotlight, contrasting it harshly with a real commitment to truth, both arbitrary and otherwise, and weaves a beautifully dark story with those themes. I really liked Yagami in his debut game, but Lost Judgment made me love him, and I can say the same for pretty much every last character in this game, but none more so than the antagonist, who did absolutely not a single thing wrong.

Though I skimmed over it earlier, Lost Judgment's main host of side content is tied into a school setting, with Yagami placed in a variety of clubs under the guise of a counselor; this partially acts as a reasoning for you to get involved with minigames, and partially as an umbrella under which many distinct substories are joined together in pursuit of a single culprit causing problems for the school and its students. I absolutely loved this design decision; as a Persona fan, it really scratched a similar itch that series managed to reach.

The combat, too, is RGG's absolute best to date. Taking four individual combat styles and allowing them to be swapped between with ease mid-combo takes things one step above Yakuza 0, and hit way closer to a real character action game than I ever could have expected. I do feel that the third, original new style, is a touch underpowered in comparison to the two from the first game and the DLC style, but I had similar gripes with the former in Judgment, and they've been tweaked to perfection here.

I always try to remain balanced and fair in my reviews, but when I look back at my 130 hours in this game, I can't think of a single lasting fault. A few underbaked mechanics, an underwhelming side story here and there, but nothing that amounted to anything truly detrimental.

I adored Yakuza 7, and to this day consider it to be one of my all-time favorite games.

I'm shocked how much Lost Judgment blew it out of the water.

My highest possible recommendation.\t\t\t\t\t
