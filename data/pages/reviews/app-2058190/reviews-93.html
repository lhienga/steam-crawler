Pros
+ Great graphics
+ Fantastic new characters
+ Great story
+ Incredible combat; IMO the best in the series
+ 3 (+1 DLC) polished styles of combat; massive improvement from its predecessor
+ Say goodbye to the tedium of running; you can now call taxis from your smartphone and skateboard around town
+ Side/school stories are phenomenal
+ Wide array of side activities; robot wars, boxing, burnout bike racing, skateboarding, dancing, on top of the usual RGG ones
+ You get a Detective Dog

Neutral
+/- Great direction towards parkour and stealth mechanics, but said mechanics are somewhat unpolished and occasionally buggy
+/- Lack of upgradeable storage space; makes things more challenging, but also requires more trips to shops
+/- Dialogue gets a little tiresome at times. Take a drink every time Yagami mentions "Sawa"

Cons
- Drone racing and VR Dice & Cube are downgrades from the previous game
- Ingredients for extracts are a tad tedious to farm

Review
Lost Judgment is phenomenal and an upgrade from its predecessor in virtually every aspect.

The combat system is exciting and buttery smooth. Dragon Engine combat was kinda rough in Y6 and YK2, with Judgment being a little boring. But combat in Lost Judgment hits all the sweet spots. You get 4 types of combat styles, and switching between them is seamless. While Crane Style was pretty bad in Judgment, this game makes it fantastic for dealing with crowds.

Story-wise, this game impressed me with how it managed to develop a quality story around school bullying. Some parts of the story are also considerably darker than the RGG norm, which was definitely an interesting change. Most say that Judgment's story is superior, but I actually prefer this one for tackling real world issues and the subject of morality.

But IMO, the best aspect of this game by far are the school stories. Yagami should give up being a detective and become a school guidance counselor instead. There are 10 different school stories (quest chains) in this, and a number of them have fantastic character development. I never thought I'd be so committed to overseeing the championship victory of a school's robotics club.
School stories also introduces a real star in Amasawa Kyoko, and I would be really disappointed if she didn't return in a potential third game.

Performance-wise, the game ran perfectly and not once crashed in my entire playthrough.

TL;DR
I loved every moment of this game. Easily one of the best games in the RGG franchise, and I doubt anyone would leave feeling disappointed.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOn replaying, Lost Judgment's story smashed a lot of my pre-conceptions from its release a year ago. Unfortunately the rest of it is as I remember. The side content and especially the music is a step down from the original,

School Stories are very hit and miss and some take a while to open up, and they overshadow side cases/substories as the main side arcs on offer. But on the other hand again, it's the best iteration of combat in the entire series so far. Unlike in Yakuza 0 and Kiwami, the different styles actually have some overlap which means they aren't completely disjointed. Tiger and Snake to respectively take on singular or clusters of enemies is a much better and more practical idea than two balanced, a light but weak and a strong but heavy style. LJ also actively rewards you for switching up styles during encounters, and with the increased style switch speed and new Snake style for counter-offensive plays it makes for good fast-paced fun. Unfortunately the style themes are ungodly bland even without the comparison to JE's, but the rest of the soundtrack is about on par.

The game takes place primarily in Ijincho and under the shadow of (Yakuza 7) the disbandment of the yakuza, which is unfortunate given the game was announced with the promise that Yakuza and Judgment would be parallel. Instead, LJ has to pick apart the pieces Yakuza left behind while Yokoyama grasps at the bottom of the barrel for any excuse to quell his separation anxiety for Kazuma Kiryu and actually have the guts to say to the world "there was no way around him appearing in Yakuza 8. Honest." RGGS shot themselves in the feet with the Ijincho map; it's a confused map that's comprised of samples of districts and offers nothing of value. It's so big and lifeless and I guess they felt the need to justify the time and effort that went into making it by reusing it and... not actually improving it. In LJ you get a skateboard for quicker traversal, which would be great if either map were actually designed around this. Instead it's only really viable in a few long stretches of road in Ijincho, if Yagami doesn't decide to just get off the board whenever he feels like.

Speaking of maps, Kamurocho returns but like in Yakuza 7, it's really only a formality. So little actually takes place in Kamurocho in this game that it may as well have been gutted, with taxi fares from Ijincho just directly taking you to Genda Law Office, given that it's the one place in Kamurocho you visit the most. Without Don Quijote, and given that many of the elements unique to Judgment like Paradise VR and Iyama's hermit den suspiciously all migrate to Ijincho, Yagami Detective Agency may as well up and move there already. RGGS are trying to move forward while desperately clinging onto the past and the end result is a sad Kamurocho with relatively nothing to do, in back to back games.

The story is pretty interesting. It starts off very disjointed though, back and forth between sexual harassment and groping, and... high school bullying. Once it starts to tie itself together it does so nicely, and I lament all the pre-release reviews complaining about the perfectly fine handling of its sensitive content just because they're sensitive topics. I recall the story being far messier, but in actuality it wasn't as bad as I remembered. It's still messy in places, but I still enjoyed it more than any main series Yakuza stories.

Overall, it does things better, and it does things worse compared to its predecessor. It's relatively a solid entry though (when are RGGS games not messy and rushed?) and one I also prefer to anything in the main series. It says a lot that Furuta can still write a good story even while running damage control for Yokoyama's main series stunts. If he had written LJ, the reason behind that video would be Mirror Face. And nobody wants that. Again. As someone speaking out of love for Yakuza (believe it or not) but also with an ability to call out the low points of the series without any rose tints, it honestly baffles, and worries, me about RGGS' future output with him in charge. I can only pray that future Judgment titles remain in Furuta's hands and continue to have as little to do with Yokoyama as possible.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you liked Like a Dragon you'll like this game. Probably.

There are a ton of similarities between the two games and that makes sense given that it's the same genre, studio, locations, etc. However, there are quite a few differences, and they're not always positive. So let's start with those.

Let me start by saying that in RGG games especially, connecting to the protagonist is important. In Like a Dragon you had Ichiban; a loveable goofball with a loud suit and a botched haircut that he eventually embraces (you can't change it in the game, ever). In other Yakuzas you had Kiryu; a stoic dreamer honor bound and allergic to smiling.

Yagami is somewhere in the middle. The boring, weird, stupid middle. He's a middle-aged man wearing teen idol fashion (skinny jeans, wallet chain) with a haircut that looks like a feathered bird's nest. His personality is bland and predictable; a generic good guy with few defining characteristics or interesting idiosyncrasies. I've never given less of a ♥♥♥♥, basically. I don't care if he lives or dies, as long as I get my SP points so I can punch dudes harder. That's not a very immersive experience. I feel like I'm being forced to go on a really cool road trip with an excessively boring person going through a midlife crisis.

Now let's talk about one of the game's main features; the detection. Detecting? Detectiving? Anyways.

When you're "investigating" a scene you're just zoomed in a bit, and you move the cursor around until a button prompt and audio cue from Yagami ("oh!" "interesting!" etc) occurs. You hit the button and you receive more information on that object. Eventually you get a notification that you've found all the things and the investigation is complete.

When speaking to someone, you can question them, and/or choose multiple prompts. Usually the player's choice doesn't matter; you just go through all of the options until the dialogue is exhausted. When it does matter, it still doesn't because choosing the wrong option whether presenting evidence or asking a question just leads to "yeah maybe I shouldn't have chosen that" and then you try again. No consequences. No branching dialogue. The game just mocks your decision a little and you try one of the other few options.

You also have a handful of gadgets. One is a long range listening device that you use exactly the same way you investigate a scene but now you're looking for a sound source instead of evidence. Another is a device that finds signals coming from things like electronic bugs so you follow a beeping sound and why am I still talking about this. You also slowly and tediously tail suspects, wait for the right shot with your camera, and do other things I also don't care to get into.

If anything, the detecting just cripples the fun. The world is explosive, combat is violent, and the activities are an ADHD wonderland (or nightmare). But when you throw in a "walk behind this suspect for 10 minutes" moment that happens quite often, it impedes the pacing and makes you want to just get it over with. When you're pixel hunting for a stupid hot spot or shambling through a parking lot listening for beeps it feels like someone ripped the controller out of your hand and replaced it with a book.

And that's the end of the negatives. The cosmetics are subjective, and yeah I can be picky about my protagonist so take that with a grain of salt. As someone that plays protagonists as an extension of themselves (versus the puppet master mentality) I can't even play games like Ni No Kuni because I can't play as a 13 year old boy voiced by a 33 year old woman. I tried. It was weird.

The detective stuff might bring you some immersion or maybe it was simply mandatory to include those mechanics to really drive home the whole "I'm literally a detective" thing, but I don't know why they couldn't just leave it to the writers to make you feel like one without mini games who were (spoiler alert) the real killer all along.

Moving on.

The combat is solid. It's polished, predictable Yakuza combat with beautifully garish visuals, absurd animations, and flawless sound design. There's enough diversity between combat styles, moves, and stat boosts to keep you chasing upgrades and experimenting in fights. There's an incremental reward system for doing specific things in a fight which can yield more skill points too, so mixing things up and making things weird is rewarded.

The graphics are the Dragon Engine so obviously they're gorgeous. Environments are detailed, the lighting makes the city feel real and alive, and character models are detailed down to sewing patterns in their clothing and/or a subtle blemish on their cheek. But more importantly than any of that you can actually DISABLE AA so the game looks crisp, unlike the extremely popular forced TAA turning sharp, vibrant experiences into vaseline smeared fever dreams. I'm looking at you, Cyberpunk. And Playstation ports. Like, basically all of them.

The writing is... something. Something pretty good, most of the time. I mean, I know there's a cultural divide here, and things literally get lost in translation but the game's tone and writing constantly fluctuates between melodramatic soap and cringe-comedy for kids.

Sometimes swearing is done very well in games and it conveys a real emotion or emphasizes a point with elegance and tact. And sometimes it's just a bunch of bad words thrown together at random in rapid succession by an uncensored Speak & Spell in the hands of a 12 year old with a learning disability. Lost Judgement does that a lot. Like, a lot. Like too much.

The main murder case is engaging and interesting with some unexpected twists and turns depending on whether or not you knew Bruce Willis was dead the whole time in that one movie with a young Keenan Feldspar. It's not groundbreaking, but it's pretty damn good.

Minigames and side activities including side stories (quests) are varied and mostly entertaining. Some are intentionally shallow and goofy while others have real depth and strategy. There's a VR board game, fully functional Sega Master System, Battle Bot simulator, Boxing, Drone Racing, and all the other things that already exist in most Yakuza games.

Aside from that, the game is absolutely a recommend from someone that beat Like a Dragon three times but I'm told it doesn't count if you're just bored and drunk so take it from someone that beat Like a Dragon just one time this game will scratch the itch if you're reading reviews because you need that Yakuza itch scratched. The combat and RPG elements alone will keep you addicted, but maybe you'll stick around for the story. And maybe, just maybe, you'll find the detective mechanics kinda cool just kidding seriously they're terrible.\t\t\t\t\t
