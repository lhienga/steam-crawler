\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLike all the Ryu ga Gotoku games, Lost Judgment is good, and worth playing, but I'm sad to say that I think Lost Judgment is the first game in the extended series to really disappoint me.

I often put up with rough mechanics to watch an interesting story, or allow fun combat to carry me through a plodding arc, but Lost Judgment is the first of these games to just feel clumsy on all counts, and I hope it is not indicative of the direction of future games.

Compared to the games on either side of this one, Judgment and Like a Dragon: Ishin!, both of which I enjoyed, this one feels like a misstep, Certainly, Like a Dragon: Gaiden is incredible, so hopefully they bring those developments to bear on the Judgment series as well.

The story requires suspension of disbelief at essentially every turn. I felt the same way about Judgment, but I chalked that up to my experience with the pharmaceutical industry, which most people wouldn't be expected to share. That's fair.

By comparison, I'm not an expert on Japanese jurisprudence -- maybe they really don't have clear laws against overt contempt of court or blatant criminal conspiracy in Japan -- but I found Lost Judgment's insistence upon the untouchability of its linchpin antagonist very hard to swallow.

Every chapter makes at least one substantial leap of logic, sometimes even contradicting an assumption that needed to be true for a previous leap to be accepted.

It's also depressing. Making a video game about bullying is a tough sell to begin with, but when your ethical dilemma is whether or not it is acceptable to /torture and murder/ bullies who drive others to suicide, you have to be real careful that your protagonists don't lose sight of the fact that IT IS NOT OKAY, and start treating the villains like they are worthy of admiration, which totally happens here. By the end of this thing, I didn't want to be on /anybody's/ side.

The combat just feels... incomplete. They introduce the new Snake stance very early as a way to engage high school delinquents without injuring them, but it quickly becomes apparent that it is far superior to either Tiger or Crane stance in the vast majority of circumstances, at least without upgrades. It deflects attacks from behind, disarms basic combatants with a free QTE, and turns enemies into projectile weapons at the end of almost every combo.

It is GREAT FUN, but it is so effective that I finished 90% of the game on Normal or Hard without ever spending a single point on a skill. Eventually, I was getting one-shotted often enough by bosses that some health upgrades were a must.

Street combat is always trivial, even on Hard, but boss combat is dangerously unpredictable on Easy, full of staggering interrupts, unblockable stun combos, and even instant-kill QTEs. You can have a boss down to their magic pixel after a long fight, only for them to lock you into an unblockable that eats half your health and downs you, starting you over. This happened to me often enough that I started to use extracts (magic potions) to cheese my way through boss fights on the second attempt, simply because I wasn't willing to put in the time again.

You encounter bosses with four and five health bars quite early on. It feels like the combat engine designers took this one off, and their B team throws the kitchen sink at you to keep you from realizing. None of the big fights in this game feel challenging, they are just over-long and full of cheats and damage sponges. The game pulls shenanigans like three back-to-back corridor fights against a boss with a hard counter against frontal assault, not to mention his regenerating entourage. It just feels like punishment.

The game also misuses its detective game mechanics; instead of having you find one thing in a room or choose one piece of evidence to reveal, on a number of occasions the game forces you to find all six elements of a parkour course in first-person before you can attempt it, or forces you to cycle through eight of the ten articles of evidence in your possession to have the plot re-explained to you so you can progress, but still laughs at you if you choose one of the remaining two equally irrelevant pieces of evidence.

At one point, the game takes away your smartphone, and when combat starts you realize it really has, removing your character's ability to access your inventory, equipment, crafting, and settings menus. That's not clever design, it's desperation. To add insult to injury, pausing the game in this circumstance informs you, "You don't have a smartphone right now!" and upon unpausing, your character does a little pocket-patting dance that causes him to drop anything in his hands and become uncontrollable.

Even considering all of this, there is still a fun game in Lost Judgment, but the one thing I really can't forgive is how often the game goes back to the well of snatching defeat from the jaws of victory -- on many, many occasions, you spend a ton of time and effort defeating a boss only for them to win anyway.

I understand that sometimes it is necessary to have an incomplete victory to progress the plot, but I mean, these are literal cases of the boss you just brought to zero health sucker punching you in a cutscene, and then utilizing that advantage to get one over on you, whether that means stealing a plot device, hurting you, or hurting someone you care about. There are better ways to handle this. I've played ten RGG games that handle it better.

If the player wins, let them win. If you can't afford to let the player win, don't let them fight.

On a final point, I really enjoyed the high school framing of the major minigames and subplots, but choosing to organize it as an unlocking progress tree was a mistake. You can still make progress on the side story if you are not good at the minigames, but man is it slow, and if you don't like one of the minigames, you're forced to do it over and over again anyway to get to the next one, which might also suck.

The dance club rhythm game is fun. Virtua Fighter 5 is Virtua Fighter 5. The robotics club... 1990s edutainment... thing... is unthinkably terrible, in a game that also includes drone customization and real live third-person camera drone racing.

Contrast Like a Dragon: Gaiden's Akame Network, which is there when you want it and completely unobtrusive otherwise. I understand folks thinking that was too simplistic -- it probably was -- but I appreciated being able to do substories on my time and at my speed.

A lot of development resources went into Seiryo High School, to the point that it is almost a third city destination, and I think I would have rathered a less anemic Kamurocho or Ijincho, both of which feel empty in comparison to earlier games.

Bleh, I dunno. It's fine, honestly; I was just led to expect better by this studio's track record.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is absolutely incredible.

Honestly, this was my first venture into a proper JRPG since Final Fantasy 7 in my teens…I’m 36 now and had hit an age where I was finding it hard to get anywhere near the levels of immersion from games as I did as a child.

My God this game had me as immersed as I possibly could be.

Every night once the wife and I put our kid down to sleep, I got to go and live my life as Tak in this game. I wasn't a dad in my mid thirties anymore, I was a lean, mean crime fighting machine with former triads as friends and such combat ability I feel like I could kick Donnie Yen's ass.

I wanted to take part in every school club, complete every side quest, and explore every inch of the city and then there’s the combat.

Holy hell it’s the most satisfying combat system I’ve ever come across. The graphics, the realism, in some cases the difficulty - it’s SO satisfying.

But most of all, this game had me feeling like I was the living character in one of the all time great films - the storyline, the character progression, the surprised, the intelligence in the script and crime solving side, the gang vibes - this finally gave me the feeling I’d longed for, for a long time.

That feeling when as you play, in the moment, almost from the get go, you tell yourself “this is one of those experiences that once I’ve completed it I wish I could erase my memory and do it again, so I’m going to make the most of this and savour every second”. And boy did I.

And yes, you will too. I cannot think of a single flaw in this game, I got to enjoy -

Skateboarding.

Building and fighting robots.

Detective/mystery club.

Dance club (yup, even this had me completely hooked)

Motorcycle racing

Drone racing

And so much more.

I have this game right up with the heaviest of my nostalgia rankings -

FF7, Mass Effect Trilogy, Red Dead 2, Skyrim, Fallout 3/New Vegas, Half Life 2 when it first came out.

Don’t live your life counting yourself as any sort of gamer, until you’ve played this, at least try it, you won't regret it!

11/10.\t\t\t\t\t
