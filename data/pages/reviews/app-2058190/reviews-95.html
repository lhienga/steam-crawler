\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRGG does it again, they took the already excellent entry title, Judgment, and improved upon it in this sequel in just about every way. For one, the annoying Mortal Wounds from the first game have been removed and you can now perform a counter-attack (unique to each style) against Deadly Attacks called a Mortal Reversal, allowing Yagami to stay on the offensive with a well-timed quickstep. Crane and Tiger have been rebalanced to better compliment each other while the new Snake stance is a wet dream for those who love landing parries and grapples. The OP DLC-exclusive Boxer style combines power and evasion that will decimate bosses with a flurry of punches for those who prefer to literally throw hands. Neither style is objectively better than the other and the game encourages you to cycle through them frequently for their various movesets hence keeping the combat very dynamic and fluid.

The story is, at the very least, on par with Judgment's. Once again Yagami is investigating yet another murder but this time, there's a very heavy dose of bullying centered around it so a good majority of the game's content features the new Seiryo High. I will say, it is immensely cathartic taking down school bullies, especially as someone who was once a victim to it. Returning side characters have been given more screen time to flesh them out more as individuals, particularly Sugiura and Tsukumo, while the newer ones are given strong introductions to make them instantly memorable. Even if the gameplay isn't to your liking, at least you get a compelling Japanese crime-thriller to experience.

My only real complaints are some of the new school club minigames being somewhat wonky to play (looking at you Robo lab and Motorcycle club) and the mandatory scripted stealth segments. On Hard difficulty, enemies will be alerted near instantaneously and you must sit through a 5-10 second animation of Yagami being caught and knocked out with zero ways to escape--it's frustrating when this is the same guy who could walk into a club and comfortably take on a dozen street punks (this is thankfully fixed in the Kaito Files expansion).

As a PC port, Lost Judgment runs buttery smooth with very little technical issues on my end. It seems RGG Studio has progressively gotten better with each new release on Steam and I hope they maintain this level of quality. It is a no-brainer $60 title and if you already have it on PlayStation, buy it again just for increased frames because it plays like an entirely new game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat makes a Yakuza game a Yakuza game?

To me, it's three things: the heart-wrenching story, the silly mini-games, and the general sense of hopefulness and goodness in humanity. This game has it all and even more.

Yakuza's main story has always have had serious tones, and this game is no different. The main theme of the game is bullying and how the law fails to protect and save the people it is designed to do so. You will feel despair, you will feel happiness, Yakuza games have never been shy from killing off both one-shot villains and characters that are important to the protagonist's growth. At the end of chapter 7, there is a huge misdirection that will completely break you: you think Sawa will be killed since the villain has captured her, the villain literally tells you that Sawa is not important to them so she will be left alone, but no she will die by their hand when you find out in an extremely emotional scene. This scene hits me hard; I had to actually stopped playing for the remaining night, and when I picked up the game again, I just went into mini-games to kind of move on. It is a bit of a fridging in my opinion; her death's main purpose is to provide an ideal for the protagonist to cling on further into his story.

Yakuza has always have had "good endings" for most of its side stories (you can also make the case that most main stories also have a good ending). This is what I mean by "a general sense of hopefulness and goodness in humanity," in that the side stories make you feel good and hopeful, if not often laugh out loud at the silliness of it. I like a good story and if the story calls for a sad ending, I understand if that is the logical conclusion for that story, but at the end of the day we play games to escape reality, and isn't it better to escape into a reality that makes us feel happy?

The best thing this game did right are the School Stories: they have a satisfying conclusion that ties in all the individual steps made in-between, and they allow to live through that high-school experience one more time; for many folks, high school was the best time of their lives so I can see the appeal. I thought the advisor mechanism is a pretty smart story framing device in this respect. In Judgment, there is a Friend system but it is extremely limited: your friends can help you in open-city combat if they happen to spawn nearby, but that's the extent of it. In Lost Judgment, you probably won't finish all the School Stories until you finish the main story (i.e. Premium Adventure, which is a downer), but the conclusion is a grand reunion of all the students you have met and changed their lives for the better, which makes all the efforts you've spent into the sub-stories pay off. And the conclusion falls into the "good ending" category that I mentioned earlier, so even a bigger plus!

Talking about finishing all sub-School Stories, the Robotics Club sub-story is extremely unbalanced; you will want a guide in order to finish it. Essentially, the strategy I went with was to ignore everything else and went straight for putting a block into opponent's home base. I got lucky with the final battle and finished it in only one try; for some of the previous battles, I had to try multiple times and got extremely angry when my own robots would block my path (I learnt to work around it), or when the opponents simply kept destroying my robot so I couldn't anything. Generally, when the game design takes the agency out of your hand due to RNG, it's not very fun.

The Boxing Club sub-story is a blast. It is challenging but not unbalanced (like the Robotics Club), and I don't think you can cheese this very much. There is no difficulty level in this sub-story, unlike the Dance Club sub-story that replaces the dancing mini-games Yakuza games are famous for. If you're not good with rhythm games, you will still be able to make progress.
