\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf Judgment was the surprise that convinced me Ryu Ga Gotoku Studio could take chances with a brand new cast without reference to the old, then Lost Judgment is the proven reality that taking chances has become this developer’s routine day at the office in the best way possible. I am consistently impressed with each newer title in this franchise (counting the Yakuza games obviously) by the audacity to keep pushing for quality and quantity at the same time. Part of what makes these games so special is the constant yearning for improvement from title to title, which is why I can’t help but wonder how someone without prior history playing these games would feel if they jumped into Lost Judgment without knowing sheer volume of details preserved and evolved since the first Yakuza and its nine-games series, discounting spin-offs.

I felt the need to bring this up, because if you are a reader who has not experienced these games, you can start with Lost Judgment, but I feel like you will appreciate it more by experiencing Judgment, first. You will also likely have an emotional jaw-drop if you have also played the Yakuza games beforehand, because what causes me to nearly go off-tangent spewing paragraphs of metaphorical love-making for these games has been seeing just how far RGG has come in terms of honing and perfecting their art.

Lost Judgment’s story revolves around modern-day bullying in Japan, and while I am not going to analyze the depth of this (as I am not well-versed in the history of this issue in that country), I will focus on the quality of how this story is presented. The characters introduced are strong and believable, and the journey Takayuki Yagami endures is one of extreme highs and lows, and while the highs are joyous, cathartic moments of glory, the lows in particular show a new display of bravery from a writer’s standpoint. Without spoiling details, I will say that the ending took a lot of balls, and I imagine opinions will be divided regarding the hero and antihero dilemma this story leaves you pondering; this is possibly the most bitter-sweet ending since Majima’s conclusion in Yakuza 0, and I feel that the antagonists of Lost Judgment might have been the best of this series as a result. No one was allowed to be one-dimensional, here, which is a fantastic achievement.

The title of the game ended up being the two defining words I thought of when the credits were rolling. That earns chef’s kisses, aplenty, for that.

Before I write about gameplay, I’d just like to make a special highlight for the heavy-hitter side-story saga, the School Stories. I always make it my priority to play the saga-driven side-stories in all these games, and this one is the best, in my opinion. There is a massive cast of memorable characters, and I couldn’t help but feel like some personal wish-fulfillment was unfolding for me, personally. I’m a huge fan of the 90s anime Great Teacher Onizuka (particularly the heart of it, despite the humor), and if you’ve ever wanted to enjoy the feeling of playing that series’ most rewarding moments, this saga will be a special treat for you.

The gameplay improvements (mainly combat) are taken to a phenomenal extreme, here. I mentioned in my Judgment review that we had finally reached the best of both worlds, since I always felt that the newer-engine games lost that charm of combat-depth that 0 had achieved before the engine-shift. Well Lost Judgment took a look at what Judgment accomplished and said, “We’re just getting started.” Something in particular I really enjoy is how Yagami feels strong, right out of the gate. In Judgment, it felt like we were developing a low-level combatant into a street-demon. Here, despite having to rebuild upgrades again, he doesn’t feel weaker at the start. My memory could be fuzzy, here, but I honestly felt like, despite stat reductions, his actual move-set mostly felt like it was where we left-off at the end of Judgment, which makes his subsequent increases feel marvelously satisfying. I always disliked having to rebuild Kiryu’s kit from game to game (with the exception being 6, since I felt his slowness added charm to the aged-theme of that game), but it doesn’t feel like you are rebuilding Yagami as much as you are just improving him.

Beyond combat, gameplay is serviceable, and that’s fine. I’ve seen complaints that the detective mechanics, stealth, tailing, lockpicking, and climbing are all lacking depth, but the way I see it: These mechanics served to sell me on the context of the character we were playing, and that’s all they needed to do to engage me. If I want an in-depth exploration of these mechanics, there are plenty of immersive sims to pick and play. Here, these mechanics are little sprinkles to break up the combat sections, and they never really overstay their welcome. If anything, they tend to be fun little pauses, which then leads to the inevitable hyped showdowns to be, well? HYPED!

Some little details: I felt that Judgment had a superior mood, giving strong noir vibes to it, which is sadly toned down in Lost Judgment. While I would have enjoyed more of that dripping atmosphere in the sequel, it was great to revisit Yokohama, post-Yakuza: Like a Dragon. Fans of that game will be pleased to see plenty of nostalgia-references slipped in with subtlety (shout-out to spotting Ichiban Confections!) without taking away from Lost Judgment’s focus. The soundtrack was excellent, and the mini-games introduced were superb (the best of which came from the School Stories).

Overall, it goes without saying that you deserve to experience Lost Judgment if you have made it this far in these games. If you are a potential player who has not experienced these games, while you are free to start here, I truly feel you will get more out of the experience if you have, at the very least, played Judgment. It’s unrealistic for me to advise you to play 9 long games, back-to-back, just to enjoy the world-building and evolutions of the setting in this one, but I also feel that I don’t think I would feel as attached to these games without the build-up. The world of these games is so consistent that revisiting locations like Kamurocho and Yokohama always fills me with memories, like revisiting an old neighborhood where you grew-up. It’s special, and I can’t think of another franchise that puts me in that mindset like this one.

Enjoy!\t\t\t\t\t

- Takes place mainly in the huge Ijincho map from Yakuza 7 / LaD, instead of Kamurocho for the hundredth time
- Actually adds a ton of minigames this time beyond just drone racing
- Now has 4 fighting styles instead of 2 and they're all fun and situationally well balanced.

The story is complex and interesting, if a little sappy or preachy sometimes. Running around a high school as a man nearing early middle-age trying to get all the kids to think you're cool is vaguely depressing but still a lot more fun than the relatively stale detective sub-missions in the prior game.

Also with how much Sega seems to be leaning into questionable DLC practices lately it's weird they don't offer the soundtrack on Steam, something I'd be willing to pay for instead of the single-use consumable/weapon micro-transactions they're hawking with Ishin.\t\t\t\t\t
