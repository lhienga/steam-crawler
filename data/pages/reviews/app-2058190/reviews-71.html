\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHaven't finished the game but I don't think I'm jumping the gun in saying this is one of my absolute favorite RGG games I think I've ever played. On top of the electrifying combat and improved styles from the base game, this installment brings not one, but TWO (if you bought it with DLC like any normal person should) new styles that drastically switch up the gameplay in more ways than I can describe. This game's ludicrous amount of fighting styles is reminiscent of Yakuza 0, and it possesses the same versatility in combat with the added touch of Judgment's many trademark moves (the counterattacks are SOOOO AWESOME!!!). Maybe it's just that LAD left me craving the olden days of Yakuza combat, but this was certainly something I was missing for a long while. Years, probably.

But there's more to love! And, surprisingly, it's the game's story! Having played through a total of 9 RGG titles with the same general setting, tone, and conspiracy plot, it's hard not to get a little disillusioned with every twist and turn they throw our way with every passing installment. I think the peak of my annoyance with the franchise's winding plots was Yakuza 4 (you all know what twist I mean), but the series gradually found ways to keep it fresh as it entered Ichiban's journey in Y7. Upon playing Judgment, a game lauded for its superior story to its sister franchise, I was a little unimpressed with its structure, finding it to have a pace comparable to that of a TV show, not a game. I went into Lost Judgment with lower expectations, considering how more people seemed to dislike this one's plot than the first.

And yet, this game managed to blow those expectations away. A more focused narrative than the first, with every aspect of the main story mattering in uncovering a puzzle that's less concerned with the well-treaded ground of organized crime syndicates like the Yakuza, Jingweon, or Triads and more concerned with a simple, unifying topic: Bullying.

It seems a little stupid, at first. Yagami and the gang solve bullying! But, oddly enough, this game takes no hard stances and throws no softballs dealing with the subject, as well as the nature of culpability, guilt, and (obviously) justice. In many ways, that makes this game's main idea seem more thematically relevant than its predecessor, with a complex issue in the case of right or wrong that can't be watered down to a simple conclusion. Characters on both sides of this debate are sympathetic, and don't (SPOILERS FOR JUDGMENT 1) have their character motivations chalked up to desiring power or fame in the final stretch of the story. Essentially, it's almost groundbreaking how mature this game handles its subject matter, especially considering how childish RGG can seem with some other installments and their quandaries of philosophy and morality.

This game runs great on a LAPTOP too. What's not to love? I'll update my thoughts after finishing, but I have to say this is definitely up there.

UPDATE:

Yes. This is my favorite RGG game now. All-timer final boss. Legendary.\t\t\t\t\t
