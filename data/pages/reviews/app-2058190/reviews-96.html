I personally found the story for LJ much more gripping and thought provoking compared to the first game. Dont get me wrong as I found both games' story fantastic, but while the first game's story is pretty black and white, LJ's story is much more gray and interesting. Compare the all their past games' story, LJ's story is probably the most thrilling and gray narrative they done so far.
I really love this game. The combat. The music (Unwavering Belief is one of the greatest final boss themes in gaming imo). The side activities (holy crap boxing is fun!). The side cases and the School Stories! And I still havent done the Kaito DLC yet as I want to do everything I can on premium adventure and replay the game on legend.
I seriously do recommend this game (And Judgment! Play Judgment first dammit!) and seriously thankful that the Judgment games are finally on PC!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI bought a PS4 just to play the Judgment games, and I was not disappointed whatsoever.

Purely cause of this one.

The story is one of the best in the Yakuza series (Even though it's a spin off) purely cause it's grounded and tackles a problem that relates to many common folk.

The combat is also the absolute best in the entire series.

There are 3 styles in total (4 with a DLC, but that seems to be automatically given when you buy the game)
Each of which are extremely useful in almost every situation (Compared to Judgment where Crane becomes obsolete halfway through the game).

Crane: centered towards evasion and crowd control, but it's speed allows it to even be useful against bosses.

Tiger: all about damage and heavy hitting strikes, nuff said.

Snake: Perfect for when you're surrounded or up against enemies with weapons, useful against bosses as well.

Boxer: The "DLC" style, think of it as a fleshed out far more unique and far more fun Rush style that Kiryu had.

All of these are always usable from the very start till the credits roll and combined with juggling and fast style switching allows you to pull off stylish combos and decimate foes.

It's clear that RGG saw what was popular and what was despised in Judgment, and decided to fix everything and all that was popular was doped up on enough cocaine to even make your average Miami citizen overdose.

Here's hoping RGG implement this amount of options in combat and uniqueness in their other games that follow the usual real time combat. And here's hoping Like A Dragon 8 turn based combat follows in the footsteps of the Judgment series, 7 being the seed, and 8 being grown to such a degree that even people that don't like such combat in games are able to like it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLost Judgment is a fine sequel to Judgment, both being spin-offs of the Yakuza franchise. It has all the familiar elements of the previous game with a few changes here and there. Like the tailing missions for example. There are much lesser of them now and the mechanics themselves are more polished. Yagami also gets a brand new fighting style, the snake style, which is very fun to use. The two old styles have changes as well that make them more distinct. The detective part of the gameplay is also more fleshed out and is more engaging than in its predecessor. And thank god that annoying mortal wound feature is not in Lost Judgment.

However, some mechanics from Judgment that were removed here, like the shortcut paths, expanding inventory, certain abilities, etc. I feel should've been kept in. I keep finding myself trying to do something then remembering it's not possible in this one.

The story deals with a much heavier and much more real topic than the rest of the series, ie. school bullying and suicides. This works as a double edged blade because while the story is definitely more hard hitting, the typical Yakuza series nonsensical plot elements are still there and those are made more jarring by the more serious theme. However, it is still a very good story and it shouldn't be skipped.

On the other hand, the school stories side storyline could've been way better. I didn't get through it because it gets very repetitive, especially the robotics club. that one in particular.

I have done the Kaito Files campaign as well but I'll review that on the DLC page instead.


PS- God bless the skateboard. I hope that's a thing in every new entry in the series going forward.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNear the peak of RGG. Great setting, lots of side content, solid characters and an in-depth story. The only criticism I have is the main plot isn't as compelling as the first Judgment. Unlike the first, this one is more of a philosophical exploration of justice and its consequences. So it's toned down from the usual RGG thriller motif. The hand holding in this one was almost as bad as the first which I find to be mildly irritating. There's no room for allowing the mystery or story beats to settle on the player, everything is explained, re-explained, examined and explored in detail with chapter recaps as the cherry on top. Yagami is motivated in much the same way as Kiryu but motivated by the truth at all costs rather than a bushido morality. This becomes a fairly strange handicap for the story in Lost Judgment because more than once I found myself disagreeing with Yagami and not being persuaded by his arguments or actions. I think I enjoyed that aspect and it played well within the story even if it was a little strange but they tied it up neatly at the end.

The highschool setting was great though and it had a lot of fun side activities to go along with it. Definitely much stronger being in Ijincho than Kamurocho like the first game. Although some things do play out in Kamurocho and having a 2nd city is a benefit to the story and gameplay. and thankfully, unlike some past titles in the Yakuza series, there's unique stuff in both cities so they didn't get lazy (or w/e the reason) like they did in Y7 (LAD) or Y6. Highly recommend for hours of fun and a much more chill Yakuza/LAD experience.\t\t\t\t\t
