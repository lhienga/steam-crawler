\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game's difficulty relies too much on working to "get ya" when you're not looking. But "not looking" in this case means you left the tiniest gap in vision for one of your patrol units and a single zombie got through, attacked a house, and completely ruins your mission that you're 30 minutes into.

The tech tree during the campaign is huge, which could be interesting, except I've played for over 30 hours now and I'm still only something like 1/8th of the way through it, the first massive portion giving you nothing but damage/health buffs for units and buildings and not actually introducing any new mechanics. When looking at how many more missions I have to get through before I can make new, interesting units, I feel like I'm about to go to work, instead of feeling encouraged or excited to play.

Your saves are stored in a binary format and not editable and also checksummed, so if you want to copy+paste a backup save, you can't. You have to use the in-game "save backup" feature to go back. Also, the developers are against modding the game. That is a huge bummer when it comes to a game such as this. The map editor is neat, but it would be so much cooler to add new zombie types, buildings, and mechanics to give the game more depth.

The concept is great. The art and music are awesome. But the implementation and mechanics are unforgiving, and if you use lower difficulties, it makes the game more tedious rather than just more manageable or approachable. I really, *really* want to like this game! I hope someone else comes out with something similar, because the recipe is almost complete, it just needs some tweaks to be really great.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR: Do you like Starcraft 2? Do you wish is was missing all the charm, interesting upgrades, interesting enemies, interesting missions, cool character abilities, and unit customization that made the campaigns worth playing? Do you wish every mission was 2-5 hours and started you with nothing but a command center that was awkwardly far away from resources? Then this game is for you!

For real tho,

I played the whole campaign just to give this game as much of a chance as possible, but it's just not a great game. It's flat, it's lifeless, it's shallow and repetitive. The "strategy" in this strategy game is minimal. You can beat higher difficulties by basically just tightening up your margins on resource management and micro-managing your troops, which aren't interesting types of challenge. The base building isn't particularly interesting, it's just plopping down houses so you can get enough workers to mine the resources to build more houses until you can afford defenses. The fact that every single strategy mission starts you back at square one with nothing but the command center and a handful of troops wears thin fast, especially since there are no check points, and if you fail a mission you have to completely start it over.

Oh yeah, you can't load a save. The game *has* an auto-save function, but it's not to load a save you think you could salvage, since there's no command to reload. I suspect it's just to keep players from rage-quitting if the game crashes on them late in a mission. This never happened to me, so it's just speculation.

The mission variety is... an odd topic. There are 3 types of missions: "strategy" missions where you build a base, "tactical" missions where you control a single character exploring a facility and gathering intel and artifacts, and "attack" missions where you have a set amount of resources to build defenses before being attacked from all sides by a swarm of infected. These three mission types have some variation within them, and do not tie together in any meaningful way aside from some high-level resources used to buy research (which are awarded by all three types and therefore don't justify the need for three different types of mission when one would have been preferred).

Strategy Missions
I already broached this topic, but these are bland and repetitive. Every single strategy mission starts you out with one building, a set amount of gold and wood, and a few weak troops. Some tell you "kill all infected", some tell you "survive all waves of infected", some tell you to hit a certain population or gold income. These three missions are always mixed and matched with no other objectives. Well, I guess you always have the objective to keep your command center alive, but that's kind of assumed. The early game is 100% micromanagement of troops as the hunter unit is terrible at everything. Then you get dudes with armor and guns that are indistinguishable from space marines at a glance and completely change the way the game plays... about 2 missions into the 50-mission campaign. So the interesting changes are now all done. Everything from mission 2 onwards is just "shooty armor guy but bigger" or "wall but stronger" or "shooty tower but longer range." You build houses until you can mine resources, your mine resources until you can build an army to claim land, you claim land until you can build defenses, then you build houses to get closer to the arbitrary population requirement. It's not interesting. Sometimes there are quirks to the terrain that add some intrigue, and there are exactly 2 interesting enemies: ones that jump over walls and ones that spew AoE acid/venom. 95-99% of infected in this game are just damage sponges with a slow melee attack.

Tactics Missions
These were such a horrible disappointment.
At the start of the campaign, you pick a character. I thought this would be, you know, a CHARACTER, someone with agency and a role in the story and personality. No, this is a prop. All you're choosing is what type of weapon loadout you want to have in the tactical missions. You never get to change it. You never get new abilities. Your chosen character levels up, but all you can do is make numbers go up. The character upgrade tree is horribly unintuitive the first time you see it, and the order of the upgrades makes no sense. I got my character Damage Upgrade V without ever touching 1, 2, 3, or 4.
The missions themselves vary in quality a lot, but all have one thing in common: you walk through hallways full of infected that have just barely too much health to take out without kiting, and when the undead are gone, you hover your cursor over EVERY. SINGLE. ART. ASSET. until one lights up to notify you that it's click-able. There's absolutely no indication of what items you are or are not supposed to be able to interact with until you hover your mouse over them, and there's SO MUCH STUFF in every room that it's impossible to adequately check everything. These items turn into research points (which are vital and if you miss any you may end up underpowered in the final missions) and empire points (which are your resource in Attack Missions, but I found it was fine if I left 10 or 20 behind in a mission). Some items just give you grenades or healing kits, though. Grenades are the closest thing to secondary abilities your character gets, but they're just a way to skip kiting for one giant mob of enemies. They're not necessary or interesting.
Several of these missions amounted to big boxes full of roaming undead that I had to painstakingly kite, isolate, and kill for an hour or more before a door revealed a treasure trove with all the interesting stuff behind it. I would actually rather be handed a literal Skinner Box than slog through that. Many showed obvious signs of unskilled level design: levels going right up to the borders of the map, hit boxes on environmental hazards being very poorly shown to the player, long straight hallways with every interesting thing branching directly off it, etc. Some were cool, though. There was a lab full of specimen containers that was frustrating to play but had a lot of good environmental storytelling (more than the entire rest of the game, in fact).

Attack Missions
These are just boring. The game tries to trick you into thinking you need to build defensive barriers and strategically place troops around environmental features, but in almost every single one of these, I found it was easier just to spawn in as many not-space-marines as possible and let the zombies walk face-first into their deaths. The point you're defending is meaningless from a story perspective, as it's an unpopulated outpost, and the missions are just watching your troops mow down thousands of enemies with some of the worst programmed crowd AI since WarCraft 1.
If the zombies had more variety, spread out more, or didn't immediately destroy barricades, these might be interesting challenges, but as they stand, they're the same limp noodle of a level over and over but with bigger numbers.

Obvious Inspiration
Here's the part where I unfairly compare this game to a AAA title from over a decade ago.
This game obviously pulled a lot of inspiration from StarCraft 2 (directly or indirectly). The design of the soliders, the UI, the commands, the crowd movements, the unit types - they all bear striking resemblance to an early mission in Wings of Liberty where you're defending a colony against infected humans who come in waves and you need to run around and burn out their infected villages between those waves. It would not surprise me if this game got its start as an SC2 custom map. This is not a criticism, obviously. It's not a flaw to be inspired by a good game or to have gotten started as a mod. However, They Are Billions takes the format and formulas of SC2, strips out the most interesting components, and stretches that across 50 missions.

Would not recommend.\t\t\t\t\t
