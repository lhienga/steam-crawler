\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is similar to The Long Dark; in that the campaign was made AFTER the survival mode. And hence, I consider both They Are Billions and The Long Dark as survival-mode games. I have not touched the campaign in either game, though I have seen both.

The survival mode of They Are Billions is 100% worth the purchase if you enjoy single player strategy games, such as any of the classic tower defense games. There is a STEEP learning curve, and I recommend playing on easy difficulties first to learn all the maps and game mechanics. I also recommend watching adept playthroughs/streams of this game to learn all the tips and tricks, without which you will NOT be able to complete all the maps on the hardest difficulties. Or you can learn by trial and error (which may frustrate you to no end!).

After 261.7 hours, I have finally beaten map 4! (The desert map. And for beating this map I made use of the manual saving mechanic outside of the game. It's a workaround for the hardcore rule of survival-mode, where you cannot revert to previous saves in-game. There is no option to play without hardcore mode in survival-mode.)

I love this game, though it is rough around the edges. Since the game's release, the devs have updated it somewhat. But the UI and AI is certainly not as polished as a game like Starcraft II.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThere is a substantial amount of negativity around They Are Billions, and having sunk a considerable chunk of time into the game, some of it seems warranted and some not. The sheer amount of hate on the campaign, with numerous big reviews heralding it as the worst thing ever, is not really fair. While there are notable frustrations and design flaws, and the campaign is pretty darn punishing on harder difficulties (not really doable on hardest difficulty playing blind), it's moderately enjoyable if not really that grand. I still haven't managed to slog myself through all of it, as I enjoy playing things for the hardest challenge and the scoring system is quite frustrating, but I plan to eventually.

That said, if you view the game as solely the campaign it seems pretty easy to be disappointed. The campaign may have its holes and flaws, but Survival mode is really where They Are Billions shines. The map procedural generation is really quite spectacular in the large variation of maps it can produce, and going for achievements as sorts of pseudo self-imposed challenges adds quite a large amount of replayability to the mode. Playing with no towers, no units, only rangers, etc adds a huge amount of fun problem-solving to the normal game experience and has been both a frustrating set of challenges to complete but also has a sense of accomplishment with each difficult challenge you manage to conquer.

Make no mistake that They Are Billions is a brutal, punishing game and does not forgive mistakes -- especially early. Especially on harder difficulties, you have to balance quite a few strategic decisions to be successful. There is a really interesting and unique noise mechanic in the game that, while unintuitive and frustrating at times to figure out, actually creates a very interesting and dynamic environment that maintains a consistent difficulty even as you gain experience. You need to clear out zombies to expand territory and have adequate resources to deal with fixed waves that hit every few days, but expansion and the process thereof create noise. Noise attracts lots of bad things, and the more chaos you create the more potential there is to be overrun. Large army clusters will invite large responses -- you best be confident in your ability to deal with the consequences if you're going to ball up and push hard to claim territory. Indeed, should one zombie slipped through your defenses, you're likely going to be tapping out and going next.

I have raged substantially at the game, both for what seems to be unfair deaths and also for my own stupidity. From the reviews, pretty much everyone who plays this game does. Frustrating as the deaths may be, however, the game is anything but random -- and the high level of difficulty kind of makes failure the point until you get really, really good and approach a very high skill ceiling. As you learn and get better, you realize you have a lot more control over things than it initially appears. You, the player, are creating every instance of noise that the zombies will respond to, and they only operate within this framework. Tactics and thought, which can be done very fairly with a pause-whenever-you-want mechanic, should not be overlooked as a crucial part of being successful. This is not a 7000 APM twitch-RTS game (except the players at the very top level actually do play it like this as a self-imposed constraint), but rather a deeply logistic strategy game that expects you to think about every decision. Pretty much every single failure I incur is from my own overconfidence or greediness.

When all is said and done, They Are Billions does have its substantial shortcomings -- especially with some of the campaign tedium. These deficiencies, however, are balanced with an overall exceptional tactical RTS framework when playing Survival mode. The game sells itself on a brutal difficulty curve, and to this end it is very successful; if you're looking for a casual RTS that doesn't punish failure, this game is not it. That said, if you're looking for an RTS which has a sharp difficulty curve with a high skill ceiling, and enjoy being challenged, They are Billions is that title.

Far from perfect, but certainly enjoyable.\t\t\t\t\t
