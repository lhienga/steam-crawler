\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn the time ive played this game, I have yet to win a single survivor game. Yet in constant defeat, this game still manages to be entertaining enough to replay over and over. The crushing difficulty sets the tone very well, and every minor advancement in your city feels like a major victory. The sheer amount of things you need to be keeping track of is very demotivating for new players, but this is the games way of forcing you to think creatively about ways to diminish and automate the things the game throws at you.

For the first few games I played, I would die to a single zombie wandering into my town, and infecting a building. Most frustrated new players gave up at this point, but its really the first lesson you need to understand: controlling the incoming zombies to your town, and using walls to plug holes to slow them down. Once you can give yourself enough time to build up an economy, the real meat of the game presents itself. The core gameplay loop consists of making sure your economy is balanced, while simultaneously keeping your military strong enough to survive your encounters. The game rewards fast expansion, and punishes you for not taking into account all of the things itll throw at you. For example, disturbing the resting hordes around the map cause some of them to start making their way to your town. Once i understood this, early deaths became much less frequent. Mindlessly placing energy towers towards empty areas is a good way to die unexpectedly. All it takes is one of them to be infected, and the domino effect begins. Learning to use your units and walls to prevent this domino effect from ending your game, is crucial. My favorite mechanic of this game is that you dont know the direction of each wave until the day of, forcing you to keep all your front lines equally supplied, instead of mindlessly stacking defenses on a single side. The sense of urgency in the final waves, to rapidly build your defenses as the zombies become increasingly powerful, is something this game masters.

conclusion: I am completely impressed by the amount of challenging layers in this game, and it will surely take me hundreds of hours to master it. For many this is not an appealing gameplay loop, but for me, the replayability is insurmountable. Easily one of the best city builders ive played\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA year ago I streamed an 800% campaign as a guy with 20 hours in the game. I had a lot of fun (and frustration, but we're all masochists here) and made it to within 3 missions of the end of the campaign on stream. I stopped not due to loss, but because of work, and never finished the series.
\tThis morning, having not played in a year, I decided I wanted to try to finish that old 800% campaign, but needed time to remember a few basic gameplay details: So I loaded up the old save and fought a single wave to get reacquainted.
\tWell of course, in my pre-stream preparation I made a grave sin: I backed up the original save to practice from, then restored it after 8 minutes of gameplay.
\tBlammo. Whole campaign gone because the developers have decided save scumming is the root of all evil.
\t
\tI'll happily pour THOUSANDS of hours into games with persistence to learn hard mechanics and complex dynamics. Check my profile for From The Depths or Ark playtime: Games where you need a thousand hours and a degree in theoretical physics to barely get by. Meanwhile I loved the challenge, and streamed topping the Neter Godly Campaign's all-time scoreboard with gimmicky animal based fleets on FTD and used to run 36 hour streams 1-v-200'ing Alpha Tribes on Ark as a means to "teach newbs the game wasn't actually that bad" to try and get more people into the deeper parts of that game.
\t
\tBut there's two key differences between games like Ark/FTD and TAB:
\tThose are persistent games. I can die, fail, lose and struggle for countless hours. But every time I learn something new, and accomplish something new, the world doesn't forget that on my next failure. The world continues whether I win or lose, my designs and blueprints in FTD live on, my experience in Ark lives on even if my base is wiped and all tames die. The world doesn't hard reset AND time gate content, asking me to learn each individual slice as slowly as possible to draw out the amount of the playtime with artifical difficulty.
\t
\tSee, TAB isn't actually that hard. My NewbPocalypse series unintentionally proved that: Even Lowlands on 800% couldn't bottom out my score. What's hard about it is that, if you play Ironman (NewbPocalypse was, to be clear- but I'd had 20 hours to save scum and learn prior to that series), you will learn exactly one new mechanic per failure. And those failures will be tens of hours apart. And this wil continue until you know "just enough" to start making moderate progress: At which point, the pieces fall into place all at once. You understand the sound mechanics better, and know better roles to utilize units for: You spent tens of hours on hard resets feeling like the game was impossible, until suddenly.. It's a cake walk. And it is because the game lacks persistence that it seems difficult coming in.
\t
\tThis save checking system is designed to preserve that illusion of difficulty. Force the player into long play sessions, with sudden hard resets. So they spend most of their time going over the content they already learned, and very little getting to explore new systems and concepts.
\t
\tAs a game developer myself, I feel this is a terrible design strategy: One to maximize playtime metrics without providing more content: Make it take longer for the player to get hands on experience with the systems that exist, draw it out, and make the inattentive belive it's "difficulty."
\t
\tIf you'd prefer a difficult game, make a boat float top side up in From the Depths while travelling at the snail's speed of 20 meters per second. The game will alow you to utilize everything from the first second, and by the time you can simply do that, you'll feel a far greater sense of accomplishment.\t\t\t\t\t
