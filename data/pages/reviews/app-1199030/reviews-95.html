I actually came across this on the steam sale by chance and had no idea it existed. As someone who enjoys a bit of grimdark and has played a couple rounds of Hearthstone when it was still good, I figured I'd give it a shot.

My oh my, if that didn't turn out to be one of the better choices in my gaming-career. This game is a real gem, from the storytelling, to the witty humour, and last but not least, to the cardgame itself.
The rules are easy to understand and you'll quickly find yourself experimenting with cards that synergize in different ways. Outside of the card battles I find myself to be very eager to find a new encounter and listen to the very well done voice lines and stories they represent.
The first time I heard Fuimadane and Danheim in the game as one of my first battles began, I knew this buy was a great decision.\t\t\t\t\t

A top-notch roguelite deckbuilder RPG in an atmospheric lore-rich grimdark setting
A wyrd little gem. It came out of nowhere. A random recommendation.
I picked it up and now I enjoy the heck out of it!

The setting is a mythical dark fantasy world in full 3D with a very unique "wyrdnes" mechanic.
The very own fully voiced lore is rich, has a touch of norse mythology, is partially deeply philosophical or comes with some sort of wack monty python humour. The quests are attached to the complex storylines and go way beyond "kill 10 slimes" or "bring item X to npc Y".

You explore & fight in a diablo-esque isometric view with 3D cam rotation.
Combat is turn-based and visualized by the cards in your deck.
Equipment consists of Runes with different buff effects for your weapon-slots & armor-slots, and a bunch of usable items like potions. Leveling up lets you chose new cards and passive buffs.

Mechanically it's best described as a mix between Slay the Spire (you progress through 3 random generated acts with encounters, loot, merchants, events; collect cards & items; slay 3 bosses and 1 optional final boss) and Hand of Fate (you have to make irreversible decisions; individual storylines need to be progressed in multiple steps; story-events change the world in future runs; lore-driven progression).


Summary
Pros
+ very active & responsive devs
+ rich fantasy lore with a good touch of philosophy and partial british humour
+ all dialogues and storylines fully voiced; charming narrators
+ great soundtrack, can't stop headbanging
+ nice visuals and great artworks
+ 9 character classes and huge amount of unique cards allow for tons of different play styles
+ massive amount of unique character skills allow for even more variety on each character
+ stratetic and challenging turn-based combat
+ every run a random generated seed
+ good pace of meta progression; ascension system; high replayability
+ achievements!
+ wyrdnes!

Cons
- little lack of different bosses (8 in total); can get repetitive
- despite every run being different, at some point events & encounters may get repetitive
- no "classic" equipment, as in: armours, weapons, rings, etc
- useless leaderboards due to exploiters holding upper ranks with billions of score
- no further multiplayer feature


Recommendation
Recommended at Full Price
if you
- either love Slay the Spire (the probably most comparable game) or
- like these tags: [rogue-lite] [deckbuilder] [turn-based] [RPG] [Grimdark] [Fantasy] [Lore]\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGames in dark fantasy settings are a little bit like games about the zombie apocalypse: There are so many, you really have to be doing something special to stand out. Tainted Grail definitely pulls it off. The art, writing, and well executed mood alone are worth the price of admission.

I'm not usually one for deck builders but I'm enjoying this one. Take that for what its worth.

Now I don't often write reviews, but what motivated me to write this is a super minor detail. A button.

Being a roguelike you die a lot. When this inevitably happens, you are given two buttons. One lets you start again, and the other that says 'I need a break' which takes you back to the main menu. As someone who will compulsively play a game, deprive myself of sleep, and then have to deal with being braindead at work the next day - this 'I need a break' button is a good nudge in the right direction when it gets late.

So many games design systems to target the reward centers of your brain in order to keep you playing as long as possible. Though this game is addictive, I appreciate the devs for choosing not to go the full Skinner box. It shows that they have respect for their players.\t\t\t\t\t

Each new challenger touts its own USP and Tainted Grail's is that it has a story set to a gritty, Arthurian backdrop, which, honestly, is pretty damn good. The environments ooze murkiness, with corruption having twisted all the land's inhabitants. Everyone you meet is eccentric and warped in some way, and they all have a story to tell with voice acting galore in every interaction. The village segment carries some serious Diablo 1 vibes, even containing a guitar strum in the opening soundtrack that sounds almost a direct copy of the one used in the Diablo village theme. All in all, the ambience of the game really captures the "weird Arthur" theme that it's aiming for, and if you’ve been looking for a more story-focused, thematic deckbuilding experience then Tainted Grail has you covered.

However, mechanically I feel like the jury's a bit out so far. I'm not deep enough into the game to know if it has the creative deckbuilding staying power compared to Slay The Spire. My reason for this concern is best explained by comparison.

In Slay The Spire you're given a basic class template that can go in any number of directions during a run. Each class has its own mechanics and keywords that it plays off of, but the path you end up down depends on which combinations you build into courtesy of the RNG. One game you'll play a thousand armour Ironclad that tanks every hit and smacks back in giant one shot wallops that stomp the enemies flat. In another, you might play a HP-loss focused Ironclad that juggles HP sacrifice to fuel a series of quick and powerful attacks. Each game with the same character will feel vastly different, and your strategy and approach for handling each fight will need to adjust depending on how you’ve built in a given run.

By comparison, Tainted Grail tries to load more upfront elements into each character to predefine your build strategy. The current nine characters belong to one of three factions (fighters, archers, mages), and characters in the same faction share the same pool of cards that they'll draft from as they level up. Straight off the bat this means the variety of having nine heroes to play is really a falsehood because, with the exception of their starting differences, there're really only three separate "classes" of character to play. However, to do the system some justice, each character focuses on different aspects of the class card pool, so they will play out meaningfully differently to each other by valuing cards in the shared card pool higher or lower relative to another hero in the same faction.

Same-faction characters use the same starter deck (with one or two differences) and are distinguished by each having their own ultimate ability (which is charged up in its own unique way), their own passive ability, and a specific starter card that will always be in your hand each round. These three changes doesn't seem like much of an addition, but they really guide the build strategy of each character.

For instance, in the melee class, the Wyldhunter gets a strong, single target attack as their ultimate, which is charged every time they make an attack, and their passive stacks a vulnerability effect on enemies that are hit multiple times in the same turn. This means you're heavily incentivised to take card effects that let you make multiple attacks in a single action because that's how to make the most of those starting abilities. Comparatively, the Pathfinder (also in the melee faction) has a passive that gives them stacking bonus damage for every round they don’t take a hit. Furthermore, their ultimate lets them draw a larger number of cards and gives them additional actions to have big attack turns to make use of the stacked damage they’ve received over turns of blocking, thus they value defensive cards much higher than the multi attack cards a Wyldhunter wants.

The result of this design is that you have to play to your hero’s basic synergies regardless of what comes up in the drafting options; ignoring the innate abilities of the hero to try and build something different is inevitably hamstringing yourself. Admittedly, how you implement a build that plays off of those innate abilities will play out a little differently each time, but you’re locking yourself into a style of play at the character selection screen. By comparison, in Slay The Spire you’re handed a much blanker slate and given the freedom to build into whatever comes your way, figuring out a specialisation on the fly.

All this said, I don’t know if this is necessarily a bad thing. Tainted Grail is trying to go for theme, so giving each hero its own vibe means you know what you’re building for and perhaps have a greater immersion in the build design process. However, I suspect that repeat playing a character might feel more stale than the myriad possibilities and unknowns you can get from a Slay The Spire run.

Back on the positive, there’s no shortage of complexity in Tainted Grail’s tactical card play during fights, which makes it very engaging and, I suspect, holds a sizeable skill ceiling for players to finesse. For instance, each hero’s ultimate can theoretically be activated every turn, but they get stronger if you choose to hold off and allow more charges to accumulate. Knowing when to hold and when to fire are key decisions that don’t always have clear answers to them.

Another less fun area of complexity comes from damage calculation. For some heroes this is no issue whatsoever, but classes like the Necromancer and the archer faction heroes would really benefit from a combat calculator to do some of the work for you because there can be a lot to keep track of and its easy to overlook something that ends up hurting you a lot.

Overall, I like Tainted Grail. I worry that it doesn’t really have the strength of deckbuilding possibilities that defines the genre, but Tainted Grail is trying to sell you a story as well. When a company splits its resources in this way then the result can be a half-baked product in both areas. Developer Klei tried this with Griftlands and ended up with merely okay RPG mechanics and okay deckbuilding, but I think that Tainted Grail has got decent enough deckbuilding and excellent world-building that justifies the division of resources in its production. Is it the strongest game in its genre? For sure no, but it’s a decent enough entry if you want to take a different hero deckbuilder out for a spin.
