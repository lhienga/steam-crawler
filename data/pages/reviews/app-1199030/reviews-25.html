\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTainted Grail is a very competent deckbuilder which I enjoyed thoroughly.

Each run will take a fair bit longer than in most deckbuilders, because Tainted Grail puts an emphasis on atmosphere, storytelling, and mood. As such, each combat encounter will come paired with some sort of narrative encounter. I like them, and they're one of the reasons why the game stands out. In general, Tainted Grail is a more artful game. The developers are clearly fans of more grotesque aesthetics, and the designs, music, and writing are carefully crafted with intent and love. If you're a fan of that sort of thing, you'll find a lot to appreciate in this game.

There are quite a few classes to play. Nine, in fact. Well, mostly. There are three base classes, Warrior, Archer, Summoner, with three variations of each. It's true that means that there aren't exactly nine classes, but each does play distinctly enough that you will find plenty of enjoyment in learning each one. In fact, that turned out to be my primary motivation. I did increasingly difficult runs with each class until I felt that I had a handle on it, and then I moved on to the next.

Unfortunately, once that motivation was sated, I did not find a replacement. There are leaderboards to chase, combos to master, and more difficulty options to unlock, but I felt done with the game. However, that should not be taken as a criticism. I thoroughly enjoyed my time with Tainted Grail, and I highly recommend it as one that you can pick up, enjoy, and put down when you feel done. It's okay to not have a game that you feel the need to play endlessly.

(If you do buy this game and intend to complete the story, pay attention for the 'Training Grounds' encounter. Take the most difficult choice in it as soon as you can, because the resulting quest is long and annoying. That was my only complaint with the game. Eventually I couldn't be bothered to reach the end of the story and looked it up on Youtube, because it was frustrating and unfun to keep reloading the map, searching for specific encounters.)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI can't believe this game isn't more popular. I think I like this even more than Slay the Spire and, dare I say it, Inscryption? (at least gameplay-wise; Inscryption's atmosphere is still out of this world.)

Yes, it's yet another rogue-like deckbuilder but with significantly more lore and world-building than others I've played and with what feels like a less steep learning curve -- not to say that it's too easy, only that I feel like I got into the groove a lot faster than with others.

Although the King Arthur mythos has been done to death, this only uses Arthurian myth as a vague jumping off point. There is none of the stereotypical knights and whatnot here, only a mysterious "Wyrding" that was twisted an entire realm into a sort of a medieval post-apocalyptic setting -- which again, sounds overdone but is handled with a refreshing mystique. I got very invested in gathering together what few Wyrdness-tainted survivors I could find and freeing those that did not wholly survive from their own interesting purgatories.

As others have said, the one major drawback is that it's possible to screw up your plot progression by choosing the wrong dialogue option. My only other complaint is how RNG-dependent some of the events and encounters are. I lucked out with a couple, but I have a feeling I'm going to spend more than a few minutes running around Bad Trip Forest without a candle hoping I run into an NPC I've been missing.

Those criticisms aside, I cannot recommend this game highly enough to anyone who enjoys deckbuilders. The different classes are interesting and feel fairly balanced and it's incredibly satisfying once you get your card engine up and running.

You don't even have to wait for it to go on sale, it's absolutely worth the full price of $19.99.\t\t\t\t\t
