\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSolid game for a good 20 hours, but this game should still be in beta, as there are quite serious bugs and tuning issues overall that makes this game a bit too grindy. It also has a few things that keep it from being a great roguelike that you can just pop into like runs being too long.

Good:
- It's a fun deckbuilder
- deck building uses interesting mechanics that would not be possible with physical components
- 3 top level classes with 3 specialities within them (9 total) that all play quite differently
- very nice story intertwined with the repeated runs that makes sense
- music, soundtrack is 10/10 (portrait art is great too)

Bad:
- progression is very slow + grindy (in a bad way)
- runs can take a while depending on which class you choose (some classes are aggressive with quick fights, other classes build up slowly and boss fights can take a long time)
- the game is a 3rd person view in a 3d world which doesn't seem necessary (and introduces bugs, see next point)
- clear bugs, most notably enemies are misplaced around the map, which lets you grab most rewards without fighting the guardians of the loot
- single runs can take a bit too long for a run based game (2-3 hours, sometimes more)
- I feel done with the game before having really finished it, only playing some classes a single time.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTainted Grail: Conquest is a turn-based deck building game that is set in a dark fantasy world that's tangentially based on the Arthurian Legends. Honestly, it probably works in its favor that it just loosely mentions Arthur and Merlin and then does its own thing. There's a lot of baggage associated with those legends, most of it is pretty cliche at this point and Malory's 'original' version of the story is a pretty rough read. That said, the story is just okay, it's good enough to keep you motivated, but it's not the main draw of the game.

It's a rogue-like game, you have a central village and you have 3 areas to explore off of this hub where you fight three bosses. After you beat these three bosses a path opens up to a final boss and, if you're victorious, you get to unlock a new difficulty level and start all over again. The enemy variety was pretty good and the enemies were unique enough in terms of tactics and gimmicks that they didn't feel like reskins of each other. Each run takes about 2-3 hours to complete. There are some side quests that persist between runs, and you'll have to complete all the side quests to see the true ending. It took me about 30 hours to do this, so a fair amount of time for a rogue-like.

The game offers you 9 classes to play with. You'll only have one to start, but the others unlock as you progress. The classes are divided into 3 groups of 3. Each group has a set of 96 cards to build their deck from and each class has an unique ultimate ability and a pool of 41 passive abilities that they acquire as they level up. Every time you level up, you get to select one of 3 randomly selected cards (you could also choose not to take any cards), and every even level you also get to select one of 3 randomly selected passive abilities. At levels 10 and 20 you get to pick special evolutions of your ultimate ability instead of the usual passives.

While I enjoyed playing the game, ultimately I found the high amount of randomness in your deck and character progression was a bit of a drawback. There is a certain joy to be found in making the most of what you have at hand, however, the three runs I lost, I lost because I did not have anything to work with. Sometimes the RNG gives you an embarrassment of riches that lets you plough through the content, other times it gives you cards with no synergy and passives that work against each other. You don't really build your character as much as hope a pathway to victory emerges and try to steer your character in that direction.

Despite this I enjoyed the game. It lasted just long enough that it didn't overstay its welcome. You can also see from the screen shots and videos that the production values are very good for this type of game. I'd give it a 7 out 10.\t\t\t\t\t

Fall of Avalon was fun but kind of unstable and rough, so I decided to let it cook a little before actually playing it. But the setting and tone was interesting, so why not play the card game meanwhile?

Turns out I do like deckbuilding games.

Somewhat unstable with animations, probably because I was playing from a Hard Drive on Linux, so if you can play it from a SSD, it's probably going to work better. That's the only problem I had. The atmosphere is amazing, the story and characters are great, I would murder someone for a tabletop RPG book of the setting. As for mechanics, the game has a lot of stuff going on and it's great to do combos. Played mostly summoner/blood mage/necromancer because I can't help myself, but each class has it's cool cards and mechanics going on.

Loved it, and I'm excited for Fall of Avalon now.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFun for awhile. The game seems unfinished. Almost 2 years after release and you can still talk to guarded NPCs without first fighting the enemy by staying just outside the aggro radius. Needless to say, getting benefits from NPCs without risking anything destroys the balance of the game and should be avoided unless you want easy wins. And speaking of balance, the devs aimed too high with the number of classes. Sure, there are lots of characters to play, and some of them are really interesting, but some cards are absurd, while others are laughably bad. If you make a mistake and pick a bad card, you're pretty much stuck with it because card removals are very rare. There might be an argument that some cards could be good if there was more enemy diversity. Seems like every fight goes pretty much the same, and there aren't really any enemies that punish turtle builds so you can just stack defense which leads to very boring gameplay if you want to win. Will I play more in the future? Maybe, but there are better roguelikes more deserving of my time right now.\t\t\t\t\t
