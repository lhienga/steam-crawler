\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDo you not have this yet? That is a mistake, quick, fix it, add to basket and check out.

Why? Okay, I will attempt . . .

Do you enjoy deck-building card battling roguelikes? If so, this is a standout top tier example in many respects. Variety, theme, haunting thematic visuals and audio (terrific audio!) and longevity.

It can be compared to Slay the Spire, Roguebook, Griftlands and other similar games. It has some absolutely stand-out features that make it special though. These being . . .

*Role Play & Village Building*

A huge hook of this game is that the meta-progression from run to run is first establishing your own village and then satisfying the unique desires of the people that settle in your village. The people that settle all become vendors of some special range of bonuses and items, requiring their own special currency earned in-game in various ways.

In a fantastic twist that I have not seen in any other similar game, the quest-lines for your villagers, and other non-village NPCs, carry over between runs. Some quest lines have six or more stages, and you may advance them from run to run. This does not take up any 'busy work' as you do not need to track, and the journal provides a very effective recap.

*Darkly thematic visuals and audio*

I was initially irritated by the need to walk around a 3D world. It felt unnecessary and I preferred simple node-to-node movement seen in similar games. However . . . the visuals are just gosh dang gorgeous and especially when you operate *without* a Wrydcandle, meaning everything is being distorted by the in-game effect of "wyrdness". It really does upgrade the immersion factor, and the audio is a beautiful match. You absolutely have to listen to the cultist chanting and tell me it is not spooky creepy awesome!

*Enormous variety and replay value*

There are about a dozen character types to play and these each have a huge range of cards, and a huge range of passive abilities, and huge range of runestones that can add extra effects, and a huge number of consumables . . . .and this adds up to provide amazing variety, with lots of very viable and different styles.

There is a flipside, which is that moving from one character type to another presents an initially steep learning curve. How the heck does this one work? This really adds to the interest over time though, as it feels like playing a very different game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFilled with magnificent artwork and story telling, but unfortunately hampered by an atrocious graphics engine, poor balancing, and thematic inbreeding.

Balance is all over the place depending on class and enemies you encounter. Some enemies will be significantly tougher for some classes to what feels like a plain unfair extent. The same goes for bosses. This does not seem to be an explicit design decision rather than an implicit one by having a few too many classes.

The graphics engine feels outdated, choppy, and unnecessary. This can largely be ignored, but those cool 3d-models for enemies sort of fall apart when the animations look cheap, and the different classes look too samey. In this case, simpler would definitely have been better.

The thematic overlap between classes is too apparent. While the three summoners have a different behavior at their core, and different playstyles, it feels cheap when you essentially get the red/green/blue flavor of the same class. Time could have been spent adding depth instead of breadth. This would probably also make balancing easier.

However, in spite of all my complaints, you'll probably enjoy this game if you can look past the issues described! If you think you can't I'd suggest spending your time/money on Slay the Spire/Monster Train/Banners of Ruin/Griftlands instead.\t\t\t\t\t
