\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPros:

Atmospheric
Variety of skills & heroes
Engaging

Cons:

Early game too grindy
Balance issues ( numbers should be toned down)
Story is not interesting ( well gameplay is, so this might not be that important)

Gameplay:
You pick one of 9 heroes (u unlock them through progression as all other roguelikes) who are divided by 3 main factions.
1- Melee (Berserker, Wyrdhunter, Pathfinder)
2- Archer (Apostate, Sentinel, Zealot)
3- Summoner (Necromancer, Blood Mage, forgot the last)

Each run you face enemies, try to get bunch of different resources, unlock NPCs etc to progress and eventually beat the game. And doesnt end with beating the game. You can sink in a lot of hours to discover all characters, their passive skills, playstyles and so on...

Overall i would recommend it to the lovers of RPG roguelike deckbuilders.
Certainly it has its own tone. Has some flaws but overall very good game.
Hope they can make a DLC or expansion on a new landscape, like a snowy nordic area u can get through portal from town.
After repetitive plays same landscape feels a bit boring.
But flaws are understandable as i guess its not a large team, they have created a very good and i would say addictive game.
They surely deserve support.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is fun. I don't know that it has the long-term depth that you often want in your rogue-lite deckbuilders, but it has been a good time playing. It does well what many other games do poorly.

This is very much a rogue-lite rather than a rogue-like with lots of unlocks and advancement. None of it is really necessary. The baseline difficulty is low enough that you can win comfortably even with no unlocks. This means the learning experience does not feel punishing, and you get more variety in builds rather than needing to optimize tightly to have a chance. The unlocks give you more options and support playing at higher difficulties.

Many of the unlocks come through storylines. They are charming. In a dark, horror setting, you are building a village and saving souls. You are undermining the schemes of demons and Death, redeeming or destroying monsters. Good times and a good way to space out content, along with merging the mechanics and story by embracing the randomness. Rogue-lites are random, the Wyrd is random -- your character is wandering in mystic darkness and taking things as they come. I criticized Across the Obelisk for very similar rogue-lite points to what I am making here, but it works here.

The game claims nine classes, which are better understood as three variations on three classes. You play a warrior, archer, or summoner, and each of those three have three sets of ultimate and passive powers. It provides variety to gameplay. Each set of three uses similar mechanics, but they feel significantly different due to the ultimate and passive powers. A Summoner builds up strong monster summons while a Necromancer eats its own summons like popcorn; a Sentinel is a flurry of arrows while an Apostate uses the same arrow cards to build up to single huge shots; a Pathfinder gets bonuses for every turn it takes 0 damage while a Berserker gets bonuses for taking damage.

The interface for moving between combat nodes is pretty but perhaps unnecessary. The game has lovely graphics and good style, but it is somehow the most resource-demanding game on my computer. I can only hope there is a bitcoin miner buried in the code or something, because my laptop should not run this hot for this. One nice detail of the layout is that the boss is present on the screen the whole time, a glowing figure you can plan around. It is a much more visually compelling way of putting the boss on the map -- have it stand there like a titan, looming, waiting.

My overall assessment is that it works. It clicks. Every run has a satisfying beginning, middle, and end, as you rise from "I'm not sure I like this class" to having all the pieces come together for your overpowered combo of the run. OK, that is not the most satisfying end, because your win is certain well before the punching bag final boss.\t\t\t\t\t
