\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDarkest Dungeon's clone. Not as good, but not horrible.

The game is complex (at least complex enough), fun, atmospheric. I actually liked it slightly more than Darkest Dungeon just because I find the gameplay much more fun.

Cons - the writing is a bit simplistic, the story and the writing style resembles Thea a lot, which is extremely annoying. Writing feels like "what if Thea, but in Arthurean setting."

One thing about the atmospheric part, Darkest Dungeon genuinely felt like a horror game, good writing and good intro, interesting dialogues play a very important part. This game, unfortunately, lacks all of it. There are encounters with interesting dialogues, sure, but overall they aren't particular important, they barely influence the gameplay and they feel like an afterthought.

Bottom line, if I were to introduce someone to the genre, I would have recommended Darkest Dungeon over Tainted Grail. It is hard not to appreciate the efforts put into this game though, and at the very least the technical implementation is not bad.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR: A superb, dark, tactical card game. Excellent graphics, subtle-animation, good sounds make it immersive. The game mechanics are rich and rewarding.


Full review

Tainted Grail is set is a small isolated village, surrounded by a magical darkness, known as the Wyrdness. Your character (portrayed in 3D, 3rd person) ventures out to deal with the monsters that lurk outside the safety of the village stockade and, in doing so, rescues villagers, recovers treasure and learns clues about the fate of the world. It is a fairly light story line, but it is still compelling. The Arthurian-elements of this game are passing references only, but

The card mechanics are creative, with a lot of variation. There are elements of chance, but you feel like you have choices and can make progress through clever tactics and astute deck building. Unlike other games, this one doesn't make you feel like you are the victim of a random-number-generator. However, you can gain some modicum of progress through hard-slogging through it too, so you can accumulate collective wealth and skills to sustain your endeavours until you figure out a workable strategy to get past any challenges. There are multiple difficulty levels and you can adjust on each run-through stage to your liking.

The graphics are good, with plenty of options. The card artwork, with micro-animations, is sublime. Much of it is voice-acted, and the voice acting is pretty good (the odd mis-pronounced word, but nothing major). It flows well, and you can skip past it and just read the text if you wish. The music is good although, perhaps some more would be better... you do start to hear the same tracks over and over, once you played it for a while. But I've not found the need to switch the music to zero yet.

There are different character classes that you can unlock. The classes are VERY different. And you don't have to do them sequentially, so you can chop and change between them to find one that suits your play-style. The starting class seemed one of the harder ones, but perhaps that's just because I was still getting used to the mechanics.

Replay value is good. There are 3 levels per playthrough, but with 9 different character classes, there are at least 27 levels of game play. I'm not much of a card-game player, but I managed to get through the first class in about 20 hours of playtime. I''ve now about 70 hours, and I'm still enjoying it.


Conclusion

A visceral, macabre atmosphere (so not really suitable for sensitive or younger players). I don't know why, but I was expecting something like Darkest Dungeon, but this is very different (both are good!). Quality is superb. This is a really polished piece of software. I've not had a single crash yet. I definitely recommend this game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGood game, fantastic music, haunting atmosphere, and unique deckbuilding mechanics between the different classes and subclasses. This isn't my go-to favorite genre of games but I thoroughly enjoyed exploring the world and learning about the NPC backstories and everything else. Its still a work in progress, but if you can get past that it's a great game.

There are some major balance issues between the classes. The apostate can completely outclass any other class once you start unlocking some of their better cards. By contrast, the starting class really needs some TLC because I can barely beat a single boss with it. My first "win" was with one of the melee characters, but once I learned how to use the apostate... oh boy. arrows for daysssssss.

each run seems to depend on you lucking out and getting one or two really good cards early on but that's part of the fun of RNG and rogue-lites. Every run could be a winner, until it's not. Each time you go out into the wyrd you're most likely going to learn something new-- a new character, a new enemy, a new location, etc. etc. You also get permanent upgrades along the way, such as unlocking better runes that you can encounter out in the wild, more starting potions, and the ability to sell and buy random cards while in town that can make a big difference. You also learn what NOT to do, which is even more important.

With some polish this will be a 10/10 in my book.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPretty fun deckbuilder, albeit still a very obvious work in progress.

The world, mini side-stories, and voice acting are fantastic and the clear shining point of the game. Enemy designs and battle themes are also stellar, even if the quality of the actual enemy battles can be inconsistent. The card-based combat revolves around the typical 3-energy "Slay the Spire" style, and is passable but not particularly innovative.

My main issues with the combat are the balance and huge amount of meta-progression. I won my first run pretty easily, and have fully beaten six of the nine runs I've played, with lots of passive abilities seeming very obviously overtuned. There are nine different classes (from 3 class archetypes), and they have interesting and unique differences. While the individual classes keep combat fresh, enemy variety is pretty poor and the final boss (and unlockable, "true" final boss) are very underwhelming.

The main hub of the game includes a number of unlockable vendors that have tons of meta-progressing abilities bought with persistent currencies, ranging from things like more health potions to straight up flat damage and max health upgrades. I played for about 12 hours, and was nowhere near to unlocking most of these upgrades, and am not really sure what the point of including such grindy upgrades is in the first place.

Overall, if you enjoy the deckbuilding genre I would definitely recommend checking this game out. It's enjoyable, and has a larger emphasis on presentation and story than its contemporaries, but can't quite compete with the top dogs of the genre like Slay the Spire and Monster Train.\t\t\t\t\t

It's meant to be this cryptic, but it just lessens the motivation to understand the story. Forgive me for that connection, but in souls-like games, the story is always present, in items, environment, spells, weapons etc. And while I think the story has a great potential, its framing is terrible. The uniqueness of the characters keep it up for a while, but you forget your mission objective as soon as the next run! The gameplay is set up really well and I enjoyed every class. Even though the pathfinder, in my humble opinion, is overtuned and easily completes the game on the highest difficulty. Plus there's only an approximate 20% chance of you having a run fail just because of bad luck. And for a random card game, that's a miracle! There're also extremely well underlying soundtracks and the ambience noises are hauntin and fit the atmosphere. While I believe the graphics are satisfactory, they shouldn't require as much Pc performance as they do. To sum up; I would've liked a bit more storytelling and better performance. 6/10\t\t\t\t\t
