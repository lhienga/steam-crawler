\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA hidden gem.
It feels like an RPG with card game slapped on top. This games gives a feeling of being in that world, rather than on a flat surface. ....I'm just saying, I can't feek the "You're fighting for your life" feeling some games try to sell when I'm presented with 2 cards smashing against each other.

Gameplay(9/10)
Core gameplay loop is very similar to other games in the genre, but it has some design choices that shift the gameplay enough to make it stand out.

So take a game like Slay the Spire, make some variations of each character with different (spammable) ultimate skills and passive skills, that gives each character their own playstyle. Your choice of character determines your playstyle and not RNG.

One interesting twist is that you have consumables that are actually disposable, rather than being rare and too valuable too waste. At some point you start getting them every battle and they become a tool in your arsenal, and not a niche clutch saver that vast majority of roguelikes make them.

Classes.... OMG THE CLASSES
The best part about these is they are different and not overly balanced! I like it when classes feel different and that is rarely the case when there's a damage/survivability bracket they have to fit for balancing reasons.

There are 9 classes, each of which has unique Ultimate skill, an ability card (special card that comes back to your hand) and a passive trait, which dictates the playstyle of each class.
The 9 classes are divided into 3 "factions" that represent melee, archers and summoners. Classes under 1 faction have access to the same cards and mostly the same traits.

All classes are viable, but some of them will require some luck to survive early stages, while others steamroll through everything and only get stronger.

Persistent world
In each run you get a new world (with identical structure) and the only permanent thing is the hub. With that said, you meet NPCs and there are choices you can make with them that will leave permanent effects. In some cases there are 2 options and 1 gives you an upgrade, while the other one permanently locks you out of it. In some cases both options have drawbacks.

I wouldn't put "choices matter" tag here (because most don't), but some choices do matter.

Quests
Quests are mechanically similar to Hades, where you have to keep playing to find specific NPCs, except the quest chains are not as long, so you don't have to run for 100+ hours to find that 1 NPC 30 times for some trinket or achievement.

Progression (9/10)
This is one of few games that manages to pull off having fairly boring progression, while making it impactful.

Another huge achievement for this game is being a roguelike in design, without showering players with mountains of completely useless random items.
Permanent progression
  • Healing - count/power
  • Runes(equipment) - drop rate/slots(to equip)/availability
  • Basic stats
  • Candles - count/duration. (Fire/Light is a common resource which allows you to move around and explore)
  • Deck - extra card draws/ability to remove cards.
The list is incomplete, but as you might see it looks boring. Somehow all of these upgrades feel VERY useful. Some of these can be buffed by various traits and NPCs for that run.

In-run: Equipment
You get runes in each run, you can make them stronger, by fusing them AND you can freely swap them out. I don't know why changing equipment feels like a mindblowing feature to me, but it does.

In-run: Traits
Traits are random upgrades you get during a run. Some are universal and just a plain upgrade (like increased money drops), but most are class-specific and supplement your chosen class in some way. For example, melee classes get traits to negate every other hit, or first hit every turn. Wyrdhunter (first class) gets a special trait that makes their passive trait trigger quicker.

In context of traits not everything is a flat buff, but almost everything is useful if you're conscious of the options it gives you. When used correctly traits often result in broken feeling runs where you either become untouchable, or get insane damage multipliers, to a point here you can 1 hit kill any non boss enemy.

These are admitedly lacking in terms of variety to make this game infinitely replayable as it advertises itself, but I can see at least 50 hours worth of gameplay without running into a similar playthrough.

Presentation (8/10)
Probably the most important thing they nailed is the feel of the world, character and just being in the game world. The game feels as if I am controlling a character in a game world and not playing a card game, which makes it feel unique. This is a lot like an isometric RPG where combat is done through card battles, whereas many other games (like Slay the spire) feel like they are card games, where there just happen to be a character standing while you draw your cards and "the world" is non-existent.

I rarely appreciate story (this game is no exception), but I did like that it was self-aware, while incorporating that awareness into the game.

Graphics(7/10) are not amazing, and animations are pretty boring, BUT they are about what they need to be to carry a mechanics focused game and to get points across. I certainly prefer this over 2d images shaking on screen or better yet, 2 cards smashing together.

While on topic of graphics, this game has a visibility radius (you can also think of it as fog of war, which looks great in this setting. It dynamically shifts between making enemies invisible, to blur, to complete visibility.

With all that said, I think these graphics will not age very well. They already look ancient.

Music[(9/10)[/b] is not the kind of stuff I would listen to outside of a game, but it sets the atmosphere really well. I rarely noticed the music was there until I started paying attention. I think it's just in the right spot between ambient (i.e. sounds you can barely call music) and melodic.

Bugs
There are at least a few bad bugs in this game. I've noticed a few cards not working as described and I'm guessing there's more that I missed.

Example: pathfinder class has access to card called Outbreak which does 25% base damage +75% for each block you have on you. This card with 2 stacks of block should do 175% damage, but if the character has damage multiplier basic attack (100% damage) hits harder than this card.

There's also 1 card that reduces the cost on specific card type and that card sometimes works, but fails at other times.

It's not too bad, but you have to pay attention to avoid buggy cards in future run.\t\t\t\t\t
