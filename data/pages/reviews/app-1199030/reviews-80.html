\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you like deckbuilder roguelikes this is a must play. It perfectly embodies the phrase 'easy to learn, difficult to master.'

Story: I think this is probably the weakest point, but its still pretty good. The concept and setting is very unique and intriguing, and there are several NPCs you recruit for your town (and others you just meet in the wyrd) who have fairly interesting plots. The conclusion of these plots can vary and may alter future rewards. For example, at one point I made a pact with some cultists and now I occasionally come across shrines where I can offer *ahem* meat for rewards. The NPCs you meet set up the atmosphere very well, however, the main story feels a little underrepresented. It gets introduced well within the first couple runs, but afterwards falls by the wayside. Perhaps there is a satisfying ending that I haven't reached yet, but the main story really isn't motivation for booting up another run.

Music: very good. The sounds and instruments used are unique and suit the setting well: tribal, mysterious, suspenseful and energetic. After looking into it, I was surprised by how few songs are actually present in the game, because the music never felt repetitive.

Gameplay: this is the reason you should play this game. There are three 'classes' each with three 'subclasses'. Each class has a unique pool of cards (barring a few special cards that can be acquired by special means for every class). Then each subclass has a unique ultimate ability and method of generating ultimate points which may fundamentally alter your play style, plus a few unique cards to support their unique playstyle, and unique starting skills that dictate their playstyle (for example, Watchers of Tuathan have cards that flip over after use so they have a different effect next time you use them but the Apostate starts with a unique skill so that flipped cards stay in their hand rather than going to discard). For example, while the Summoner and Necromancer both belong to the Moonring and thus share ~90% of their cards, the summoner usually wants to keep only one or two summons out on the field at a time to make them the ultimate powerhouse. Meanwhile the necromancer constantly cycles through summons, turning their dead summons into ghosts and their enemies into ghouls, and occasionally turning themselves into an almighty lich. There is an incredibly wide variety of enemies, and one of the most important tactics for beating a run is recognizing what kind of enemies your build is capable of tackling to level up, and which you are better off avoiding all together. Nothing is worse than trying to fight a group of Wisps that ignore all damage below a certain threshold with a character that specializes in many small chip attacks. There are many ways to increase your power: the most obvious is normal deck building. You also have a selection of items (consumable and reusable) which I find myself using an astounding amount of. Even in other roguelikes, I find myself hording and then never using consumable items, but something about the design of this game has me using consumable items more often than in just about any other game. Level ups provide passive skills that can reinforce a play style while level 10 and 20 mastery skills offer skills that can radically alter your play style.

After over 40 hours of playtime, amounting to around 30 runs, there are still several tactics I know I've slept on, and even for my favorite class I know there are tactics that I've only just begun to take advantage of. And the attitude of the developer is very promising. We just received the first major update, which includes three(!) new modes for free, and the developers really seem to listen to community feedback.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game looks very nice graphically. The UI and controls are good. Controller support is good.
Most everything else is not good in the game.
First, the deck building is slow going. You don't feel like you have much choice in what you are doing with the deck. Resources to manage the deck are extremely limited as well.
The map element of the game is pointless. The map has no meaning other than just a time sync to watch your character walk around. There are no interesting locations or paths really. Just a way to activate the next encounter. No interesting decisions with the map. The quests have no real guidance, just randomly you wander and maybe you run into them.
The worst part of the game to me is the stupid candle system. These burn out really fast and are extremely limited. This prevents you from exploring the area as you may want to. You are constantly having to activate another candle and they last almost no time as you wander the pointless map system to get to the next encounter as fast as you can.

Overall, just a poorly done rogue like/deck builder system. The encounters are somewhat interesting, but since you have such limited control of your deck and no time to actually enjoy powering yourself up before you are forced to run along before the stupid candles run out, it just is not enjoyable.\t\t\t\t\t
