\r\n\t\t\t\t\tYeah, this one deserves a review.

I've played quite a few deckbuilder games in my time, and it's always a pleasant surprise when I find one that manages to engage me with new, interesting and varied mechanics. Tainted Grail is such a one.

The forty hours playtime are across the last four or five days or so, and given the retail price of this game that makes it an absolute banger. Tainted Grail is a fascinating take on the deckbuilder genre, with a full blown macabre gothic Arthurian-Legend-Gone-Wrong-Police-Called storyline attached to it. You travel through increasingly difficult areas of an insidious forest filled with deformed, demonic bad guys, building up your character to get them ready to face each area's final boss.

The first great aspect of Tainted Grail is the story and characters. As you explore these areas, you will meet a variety of messed up, fully voiced individuals who can trade things with you, give you quests to complete, fight you or even join your settlement and provide permanent legacy bonuses that persist even when your run ends. This is certainly a deckbuilder with all the juicy meta-progression elements one could hope for to keep replayability at max, and it's all implemented in a logical yet thematic way. The voice acting quality varies but is generally between C to A tier, nothing hammy, nothing lifeless.

The second great aspect is the nine different character classes you can unlock and choose from when you start a new run. Given that the core gameplay loop is Draw Cards/Play Cards/Kill Stuff, the playstyle variety between each and every class is almost poetic. I nearly started typing an entire paragraph spoiling the classes here, but I'll hold off in the hope that you'll want to discover them for yourself. Suffice to say that not only is each class's deck different, but the WAY you play that deck is also completely different, and this makes experimenting with each one a total joy.

The third great aspect is the Candle mechanic. Similar to Darkest Dungeon, you have a limited supply of candles that you light as you explore the monster areas. When you encounter monsters, you will receive bonus cards into your hand every other round or so. When you receive one of these bonus cards, you can either play it for an immediate effect, or leave it in your hand and receive an alternative effect at the start of your next turn. Sometimes the choice will be obvious, but occasionally this will prove a fascinating tactical dilemma.

In addition, the effect of these cards is dependent on your candlelight level. At maximum light, their effects can change the course of the entire battle in your favour, but at low levels, these cards will actually punish you in battle if you DON'T play them (such as increasing the cost of your other cards etc). In the early game, this presents a crunchy issue of resource management - do you risk heading into the next fight with low light because you're saving the last candle for the boss? What if you don't make it that far?

In addition addition, your candlelight burns down the longer you spend in battles. So if you think you're really clever spending 100 rounds lifestealing off the last mob you've caught in a damage loop, think again. You cannot afford to dilly dally, either on the world hub or in battles. Time is against you, and I love it.

Sadly no game is perfect, and Tainted Grail does have a couple of flaws. First one is some UI issues - occasionally cards lag out when you click on them, and sometimes animations of selecting cards is alarming slow, making me believe I've accidentally clicked on the wrong one while skimming through my hand at the bottom of the screen.

The other slight gripe I have with this game is the obfuscation of key information which can result in a run ending. Sometimes mechanics just don't work the way you might assume they would, and while the tooltips are generally very good, they sometimes don't tell you the whole story. Here are some things I found out "when it was too late":

- Armour is capped at 75
- Minions with Taunt multiply damage taken from AOE affecting allies
- Duplicating the effect of a card that flips does not activate the same flipped side twice
- "Lose 5% Max health" does not mean permanent max health
- Enemies can trigger each others' damage procs

These circumstances are annoying, particularly when they don't kill you outright, but rather render your deck build impotent in some way, but in the case of Tainted Grail, the prospect of Game Over was simply an exciting opportunity to try another class, another build.

Love it. Still playing it. Go get it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBeen playing for weeks now (and still do), time for a short review.

I must admit, I'm a total noob on deck builders and rogue-likes and discovered this gem by incident. The graphical style got me, so I purchased it while on sale. It was waaay down on my shopping list, but I got a few bucks to spare.

Damn, I was addicted from the very beginning! Did not see that coming. Surprise purchase of the year for sure.

Every run is different, the quests are very well written (and spoken), the whole atmosphere is so intense. NPCs are fleshed out and memorable. So much to discover. Cool gameplay mechanics, awesome soundtrack as well. It's a piece of art, really. You just feel the love that has been put into this product.

Why am I still playing? I love experimenting with different builds and setups. The more you play, the more possibilities open up. How your character turns out during a run and how well a build is functioning is what still keeps me interested.

One thing to notice: The camera is a little fiddly at times, especially when you have a lot of cards and minions on screen. It CAN get annoying, but that's just a minor issue.

So, if you are still wondering if this is for you: Give it a shot, you won't regret it! At the very least, put it on your list and grab it on sale. You deserve to play this. Don't miss out on this one!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe stylish graphics gave me classic Diablo vibes. The gameplay is easy to grasp and interesting and the soundtrack sets a nicely foreboding atmosphere for this deck building roguelite. There are various classes to play and the many unlockable cards and skills and different game modes that bring in variation for many hours worth of gameplay.

My only gripe is that some of the card descriptions are misleading. For example, while a specific class uses a "gain barrier" card for creating an additional shield of hp that remains until it's broken, another character gains this barrier ONLY for the duration of ONE round. Also, there are cards that have a flip side, but for what I can tell, there's no way of reading what says on the flip side before the card comes in play again.\t\t\t\t\t
