\r\n\t\t\t\t\t6.5/10 - It's ok.

In short:

Good ideas overall with a lot of unlocks and permanent progression, a bit on the easier side (after I won my first game I had a winning streak with all characters one after another with upping difficulty every time. Eventually, I won a game with them all in this winning train which is kind of crazy for a card game). Performance is unreasonably low for what this game is, tiny map with not that great graphics and effects.

Pros:
- atmosphere, art, setting is great, really an arthurian nightmare

- 3 factions divided into 9 character classes

- a lot of permanent progression and card unlocks

- overall good design of enemies and game loop

- gorgeous 2d art for quests, characters and cards

- fun quests that are quite original, writing wise

Cons:

- this game does not look good tbh, on a glance it's ok but there is nothing to compliment when you take a closer look graphics wise + animations are super stiff and janky in battle but that's a minor problem, it just looks not great

- performance is unreasonably high for what this game is, entirety of the game is a small map with a tiny village hub at the center and 3 zones connected to it, that's it. And yet it is rather taxing on the CPU. My old 1060 GPU would max out and currently ryzen 5800x is also getting high usage but why? I guess it is just made to be as inefficient as possible. Don't expect stable 60 fps on mid range system.

- ascensions/difficulty scales a bit too slowly,

- starting class (Wyrdhunter) feels kind of underpowered compared to the rest, it just feels like a bait to present the game as harder than it actually is,

- environmental objects like statues, trees, crystals etc. spawns in town and paths but player can walk through those so it's not that bad but it just looks bad, is annoying and well, immersion breaking

- character quests result in permanent resolutions which might be bad for the player and this is a roguelite so basically play with a guide or be ready to start a new save to "fix" your choices on a new slot or stick with bad/sub-optimal NPC interactions in your every run when you meet them in the field\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTainted Grail (not to be confused with the board game by the same name) is a rogue-like deck builder with a grim dark, Arthurian legend inspired fantasy world. The models and atmosphere in the game is very dark souls like, which also means some pretty gross and grotesque imagery.

You start your runs by picking one of the 9 available classes (all but 1 need to be unlocked). This gives you a starter deck, and as you level up you gain passive bonuses and new cards to add to your deck. What is impressive is that all the classes feel very, very different. While they split up into 3 types (summoner, archer and melee fighter) each class has its own take. The game starts of very difficulty, as you have few meta upgrades but for me I fairly quickly got to a point where I could assume I could win every run. Every victory unlocks a slightly higher difficulty, which means eventually the game does get very hard (its fairly easy till level 15 or so).

There are also a TON of upgrades, meta progression and things to unlock. You have like 9 different currencies to spend on various upgrades, and it can be a bit overwhelming, but it does an excellent job of always giving you some kind of reward. There is also a lot to explore, as you meet characters on your runs that give you quests that unfold over several runs and encounters.

Story wise the game is also quite impressive for a rogue-like. You are trapped in the wyrdness, a strange fog where horrible and impossible things happen, and you are not the only one. You encounter people with all sorts of problems, you encounter monsters, and even locations. Most of this is done through text, with the overworld just containing a simple representation fo what you are meeting, but the idea is good. And the goal within the story is to encounter all the different characters and help them out.

Other production values are quite high as well. There is not much in the way of animation, aside from the barebones basics, but the various models look creepy, gross, and good. The music is standout, with inclusion of celtic and norse influences that really place you into the setting.

The only real letdown is the voice acting. The narrator is pretty much the only good voice actor, with everyone else's performances being off in some way. Sometimes they are clearly not familiar with the context and put emphasize on the wrong words, sometimes they put on hokey accents, and the entire cast is laughable small with voices often sounding the same. Sometimes voices are also just horribly mismatched to what they are trying to accomplish, like the goat (you'll notice that within 5 minutes of playing the game).

Lastly, I really appreciate the representation of women in this game. So often the fantasy genre has a problem with having to make women sexy or just ignoring them, but this time all the women feel like they are part of the world. How many times have you played a game with a dozen male characters ranging from beautiful to ugly, from cool to gross, and then have like one either sexy or generic female character. In Tainted Grail, just like the men, many of the women are disfigured and transformed, some are victims, some are powerful, some strange... there is just a pleasant variety. So A+ there.

Bottomline, Tainted Grail is an excellent rogue-like, and while I can complain about things like the voice acting and lack of animations, everything still pulls together for a fun experience.\t\t\t\t\t
