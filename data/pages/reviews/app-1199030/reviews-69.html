\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game took me for a surprise. The narrative and the voice acting gives life to the world and brings an overall great deck building and roguelite experience.

Initially, I didn't like the flow and progress of the game; however, game's mechanics grew after the first three or four runs. 9 classes to pick from that unlock over progress, each with their own card base. Difficulty choices increase as you clear each run. You eventually unlock an additional boss near the end, which is a comparison of damage in leaderboard score.

Lots of replay value for the small purchase price. I've played 30 hours at time of review and still have not completed the game's main story -- involving the saving of all villagers from the corruption (wydrness described in game).

If you like deck building card games with some strategy, this game offers that and then some. Good pickup for single player enjoyment.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA thoroughly enjoyable roguelite type game in the vein of Slay The Spire.

The basics are similar - you have your deck, you get a set amount of energy each turn to use on cards that attack or defend, you add new cards as you kill enemy encounters and level up, you grab loot which can be more cards, stat boosts, money, consumables and whatnot, and you kill the boss on each level to get to the next one. Beat a final boss and you "win" the run, and get to start again. Runs are quick, often thirty or forty minutes to complete.

One twist on the formula is there's two types of defense in this game - barrier which are flat damage reduction against incoming attacks, and "blocks" which negate attacks entirely (but are more vulnerable to multi-hit attacks). Different builds and encounters call for using each type differently and it's a smart inclusion.

There's three main class archetypes, warrior, summoner, and archer, and each archetype has three subclasses. Each subclass has a slightly different starting deck and a different passive skill and ultimate skill that vastly change how each class plays. The subclasses also have a unique pool of passive abilities you pick on level up that can provide serious synergy to your build. Each of the three archetypes shares a pool of common cards however, so the three Warrior classes are all offered the same cards from a random pool when they level up. After each run is over your achievements are converted into score which then unlocks both new cards for your archetype's pool, new passive abilities for your selected subclass, and sometimes new outfits or subclasses. Once you run out of those to unlock you get other random things you can use like resources.

There's a major focus on incremental progression in this game. You start with a mere one class available, base stats, an empty town with one lonely bum by the fire, weak healing potions, and a bare minimum of card options. However, once you step out in the world, each enemy encounter is guarding something. That something is often an NPC character, most of which have their own personal quests and some of which you can recruit back to your base after completing them. Once at your base, NPCs offer new customization options, like permanently boosting your base stats over runs, permanently improving the effectiveness of each type of consumable item, removing cards from your deck, and more.

Each NPC in your town usually also unlocks the ability to start gathering a passive resource exclusive to them. The seamstress improves your passive stats with blood that now starts dropping from every encounter. The scientist converts your remaining gold at the end of each run into tokens that she uses to improve item drop rates. You can improve pretty much everything permanently, from the amount of health your healing potions restore, how many cards you draw each turn, and more.

The NPCs outside town have a lot going on too. They might give you a small reward normally, but after you complete their quest their rewards become magnified.

As a result, once you get a hang of the game and start amassing wealth, your power level rises exponentially and things feel very good. The game counters this by a steadily rising difficulty curve that unlocks the next level each time you successfully complete a run, although you can play on any difficulty level you've unlocked prior so you can go through a quick run stomping everything on base difficulty if you want to have fun or are just looking to complete quests and amass resources.

The story is nothing to write home about. NPCs are mildly interesting, and voice acting is decent (with some of them being pretty good, like whoever did Death)

While the game is an immense amount of fun, there are a few issues. The fact that which NPCs and locations show up each run is random makes things rather frustrating once you start running out of quests that you stumble on and want to complete something specific. Like the Armed Wanderer needs you to talk to the Scavenger who needs you to get something from the Old Merchant who needs two items from shrines and then back to the Scavenger and yadda yadda - but that might take you 6-7 runs total even if each of your targets show up each run due to the sequencing in which you encounter them. Also, since you don't start gathering the specific NPC resource type until you recruit them into your town, the ones that you pick up last sort of come at a disadvantage since you're behind on their scaling.

There's also a bit of, let's say, balancing issues between the classes, in that some of the subclasses are very very clearly better than others. The base Summoner class for instance seems far inferior to its other two subclass counterparts. The archer archetype in general is super weak compared to the other two as well - while I could still win runs regularly with them I was never able to assemble a completely broken deck like I could with the Warrior (and the Pathfinder subclass specifically is bonkers).

There's also I've found a bit of exploitation you can do with being able to avoid enemy encounters and steal their rewards by creeping close to their encounter zone and taking the loot they're guarding without triggering the fight, which certainly can't be what the developers intended. Combat typically runs quite smoothly, but if you use too many cards too quickly (as you very often start doing once you get huge synergies going that let you play half your deck or more in a turn) the game starts getting confused and stutters, especially when you duplicate a card's effect.

Those issue are all minor though. If you enjoy this type of game Tainted Grail is well worth your money and time as it's immensely fun.\t\t\t\t\t
