\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAbsolutely fantastic game. It really was the perfect fit for me. I don't get on well generally with roguelikes where you die and that's it. This game sidesteps that problem by having permanent progression worked into it, as your village, and the upgrades you unlock, persist in each subsequent run. Meaning you'll eventually beat the final boss. But that's far from the end of your journey, as that unlocks the next difficulty level, and you run again. Each time exposing more of the side quests and stories that you must beat before you get the true ending. It took me 70 hour plus to reach that point and I enjoyed every minute of it.

Not to mention the game has a staggering number of classes to play through and experience at max level. You start each run as a lowly level 1 with a basic deck, and you head out from your safe space village into the "wyrdness", basically an all consuming twisted miasma which distorts and corrupts all who dwell there. You explore the overworld until you meet an NPC, or you encounter a battle with horrifically deformed monsters. The battles are the real meat of the game.

If you're familiar with deckbuilders this will all come second nature to you. Each round you draw a hand, and you typically have 3 energy units to play with each round, with each card costing between 0-3 energy to play. And from there you can navigate battles with a truly extensive variety of options available to you. You can stun enemies, put up blocks, deploy minions, setup retaliations, execute any number of special moves. Not to mention there's a torch mechanism, where based on the level of the flame you keep, other cards are sometimes shuffled into your hand. Not playing those cards can either have a positive or negative impact in the next round. It truly is wondrously diverse and constantly interesting, and I just could not put it down.

Now there are some un-intuitive mechanics. Towards the end I thought my game had bugged out, as I ran at least one entire run without encountering any new story when generally there was always at least one or two story pieces per run. Turns out I had to wander about in the wyrdness without a candle, which up to that point the game had been telling me in no uncertain terms I should never do.

But small nitpicks aside, you would be doing yourself a disservice to miss this game, I cannot recommend it enough!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI found Tainted Grail quite a fun and different kind of deck builder game. It's got the same sort of core mechanics you would expect from a deck builder; build and prune your deck, variable encounter tree, consumable resources, etc. Some of the things that made it different and notably enjoyable to me when compared to something like Slay the Spire was the significant story and notable progression/permanent upgrades.

Whereas most deck builders games tend to have a loose story wrapped around the game to give it some coherence, Tainted Grail has a significant [mostly] fully voiced story that you unlock piece by piece which has kept me engaged for 40 hours so far with more to go. Different choices unlock different rewards and allow the player to progress down unique paths to their own taste.

The classes and unlockables add a lot of constant variety to the game. You can double the amount of card types and passive perks available to each class through, maybe, a dozen playthroughs and all of the abilities that are unlocked don’t feel like they 'dilute' the pool. 90% of the items unlocked are valuable to your run and just add some fun variety to the play style to ensure every run isn’t doing the exact same thing. And, when you get bored of one class, change over to one of the other 9 classes and learn a whole new set of skills!

I would, however, say one of TG's weaknesses is the occasional 'upgradeable' cards and skills. Most other games put large or growing pressure on fights that go too long so when you get a card that starts off small but gets permanently stronger or have an ability that gives you a small but repeated heal, it can cause a derogatory gameplay loop of sitting in a fight and just cycling your deck a dozen times on fights you've whittled down to level up or use background abilities. Technically you use up your 'candle' while in a fight but the negative effect is usually negligible in a fight you've already 'won'.

I can still handily recommend it to anyone who's interested in a deck builder game but wants more than just cards and fights.
