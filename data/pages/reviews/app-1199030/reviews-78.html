\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAn exceptional Dark Arthurian Rogue Lite. Easily my favourite game of the year so far. Game completed with True Ending Reached.
Pros
- Story is Excellent, balances the quirky and the macabre deftly. The story intertwines thematically with the Rogue lite nature of the game in a fashion that puts it head and shoulder above other like games in regards to storytelling.
- Music and sound is Excellent, everything hits the right beat, appropriate to the atmosphere and builds upon it.
- Game play is Excellent, The card play is intricate and the wide range of classes were all well crafted, enemies and boss fights were challenging but not unduly so and when you click with a class the killing sprees you unleash feel earned. Quests were well written and worked perfectly in a Rogue Lite format.
-Graphics are Excellent, for a studio of this size and experience I'm impressed. The style suit the story and atmosphere they were shooting for.

Cons
- One very annoying bug. There was a quest bug that delayed me reaching the end game for quite a while. However the bug (All Mother Altar) has been fixed.

This studio set out to make a board game and decided to also make a video game (two in fact, the second should be released this year I believe?). Apparently their board game is phenomenal but I know their first game most definitely is. 10/10
Watch this studio.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTainted Grail: Conquest is honestly one of the best deck builders Ive played in a long time. Aside from the actual card-play, which is fantastic, the meta-progression is engaging and impactful, on par with some of the best Rogue-likes to date as well. Each run you feel more powerful, and fall deeper into the world as you meet with NPCs and further their questlines, similar to a Hades style progression. Its got a horror-fantasy feel that I immediately fell in love with. The art, music, and voice acting all comes together beautifully. The in-between fights sections feel like there could have been a bit more, but with the amount of content here Im really not complaining.

There are 9 different classes to play, from 3 different factions, and they all feel totally unique and powerful in their own way, each with their own set of Passives, Masteries and Ultimates. The difficulty feels appropriate, and scales well as the content evolves with NGs. I absolutely recommend this game to anyone who enjoys deck builders, Rogue-likes or just fantasy in general as the atmosphere is really spot on. Excellent game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOn my eighth run at this rogue-lite, with only three of the possible characters unlocked, it dawned on me that I was going to have my first finish. I couldn't have been more disappointed.

After dutifully killing all four Guardians, I was told by my narrator that I was to begin again, do the same thing, but instead of killing the fourth Guardian, I was to go somewhere else and fight another end boss. Then the game asked me to review it; so, here I am, whether they like what I have to say or not.

Both Slay the Spire and Monster Train have this mechanic of the extra end boss to kill, both have a mechanic for unlocking it. In both of those games I've actually wanted to do any of that, not so with Tainted Grail. I felt a little tainted myself once I realized that I'd stumbled all the way through and didn't care to go back.

The art style is decidedly dark, not just in theme but literally dark. Most things on the map are intentionally muddy, but the gothic styling is relentless. The adventure map, such as it is, resembles something you'd see in a King's Bounty game if color weren't allowed and the maps had to be boring, same-same forking tracks.

While the game does deliver on shuffled around encounters and fights, the maps are instantly boring. I can't help but to feel the time and money spent on rendering 3-D models for such simple maps could have been invested in other parts of the game.

The encounters sometimes have interesting choices and most of the voice acting connected to them is okay, but unnecessary. Some of these found characters act as vendors, others come to permanently join your village (in rougelite style to become a permanent vendor) and offer upgrades that you're unlikely to need.

There are about 10 currencies in the game, but I barely used any of them. All of it feels like it was supposed to be bigger and more ambitious than what it delivers.

Very early on I found a couple combinations of abilities on more than one character type that created situations in which only the worst shuffle of my deck and a boss enemy presenting overwhelming force could generate a loss. I didn't spend a lot of time scoping out abilities, nor am I especially good at rogue-lites, despite enjoying the genre, but I found more than one game system that appeared completely broken.

The fights can be extremely long and tedious due to shields and armor that can make some foes (depending on the cards available and your draw) take a long time to kill. Also, there are some super annoying mechanics at work. The first time I saw an enemy not just split into two foes for me to tediously kill, but then split a SECOND time into two more little hit point bags, I wanted to tear my face off.

I dumbed through to the end of the game, got the false ending that every rogue-lite offers, but I'd no desire to go back and work at all the other classes I'd yet to unlock. The couple I did poke at appeared to use many recycled cards (i.e. most of the Sentinel's cards are in the Zealot's deck), so I doubt there's nearly as much depth as having so many classes would indicate.

A real strike against this game is its attempt at story. I do not expect every game to have an elaborate story or deep lore, but the breaking of the third wall to tell us that we're in yet another endless hero's journey's (ala Joseph Campbell), without even the hint of an explanation as to why or how that could possibly matter to playing the game really turned me off.

One of the most appealing features to me in rouge-lites is that they are usually very loose with their lore, so that you can piece things together as you encounter the procedurally generated elements and create your own narrative. I'd much rather no story than such cynical winking.

Lastly, I've no idea what the name 'Tainted Grail' has to do with anything. I think that says rather a lot about the game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTainted Grail: Conquest is a well oiled, well polished deck building rogue-lite. The core gameplay is fun and extremely addictive. The developers seem to be very in tune with the community and have clearly put in a lot of effort to deliver a game that is as beautiful to look at as it is enjoyable to play.

The three main categories of classes (archer, fighter and summoner) are further broken up into distinct variations that all feel different to play, offering a lot of variance and replay value. Enough persists over runs to make progress feel meaningful without taking away from the overall rogue-like experience.

I've thoroughly enjoyed my time with it so far, and still have a lot of story elements and quests to complete that warrant more runs. Pick it up if you like deck building or rogue-likes.\t\t\t\t\t
