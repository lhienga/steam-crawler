\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAt the time of writing, I have reached over 55 hours of playtime with the game, and have completed the main campaign, Chaos Theories, five original challenges in Challenge Mode, and the subsequent DLC campaigns.
I purchased the game about two weeks before writing this during a sale that sold the game and almost all available DLC for roughly 55$ USD.

As far as the Main game goes, The Campaign introduces you to the main components of the game like taking care of the dinosaurs, building operations facilities to help with park upkeep, and satisfying park guests. Even if you've played the first game, this one does show you a few different and new mechanics that make going through the tutorials worthwhile.

Dinosaur variety is much better than what I remember from the first game. I only really tried the base game of Jurassic World Evolution, but the land dinosaurs alone feel like they have much more variety. Obviously, with getting almost all of the DLC I got to play with more dinos than if you'd played just the base game, but I'm confident you can make a satisfying park with just the base game's creatures.

Chaos Theory still teaches you new stuff, but after getting you settled in the atmosphere leaves you with a final goal that usually revolves around getting five stars on your park. Most of these were finished my first try, with the Jurassic World Chaos Theory being an exception. By the time you complete all six Chaos Theories, you should be a pretty well adjusted player with all of the features and build extravagant parks.

Challenge Mode is pretty basic, still asking you to build a park in a given location, but limits you in some way. One location might have a restricting map that forces you to make awkward enclosures while another limits you to only carnivores for a riskier park. I had fun with this, but only completed the first five as the others are harder versions of the Chaos Theory maps and other bonus challenges.

The sandbox is the place I've used least and definitely feel I will end up using the least. With the sandbox you have no goals, no limitations, and can build your park wherever you want with unlimited resources. I can have fun with this, but in my short time there I found myself bored with the "goal-lessness" of it all. This is likely a problem with my playstyle, and I'm sure there are options that would better suit me, but for now I see myself replaying or creating my own challenges in challenge mode more than messing around in the sandbox.

Jurassic Park fans will absolutely love the game. Many of the characters from the movies make an appearance with their respective actors portraying them. The base game includes Ian Malcolm, Claire Dearing and Dr. Henry Wu, with the character of Owen Grady also being prevalent, though this is with a different voice actor. The characters from the first game also return to fill in for character archetypes and perspectives of certain situations, as well as providing information about dinosaurs.
The DLC also added Dr. Alan Grant, Ellie Sattler, Kayla Watts, Lewis Dodgson, Soyona Santos, Barry Sembene and Ramsay Cole, all of which are voiced by their movie counterparts (to my knowledge). The only issue I found was with Ramsay Cole's voice, as it sounded like the recording used a not-so-good quality mic. The game is centered around everyone being in a voice call, so it's not that jarring, but it's still weird to hear only one of the VAs with a poorer-quality mic.

Jurassic Park fans might be dissapointed with some of the Chaos Theory levels depending on what movies are their favorites, as some of them received better treatment over others. For instance, Jurassic Park and Jurassic World's had you aid in the creation of their parks and make them successful compared to what happens in the movies while Jurassic World: Fallen Kingdom has you save the dinos without the ingen sabotage. These are pretty great, but the ones where you save/take the dinosaurs off the island, in particular Jurassic Park III and Fallen Kingdom, go by really fast and often end anti-climatically. Jurassic Park III feels the worst of the two as you basically get told 4-5 times to tranquilize dinosaurs and send them off-island to prepare for Jurassic World, with the "finale" showing a few raptors kill the Spinosaurus before taking it's body off-site along with the others. I know Jurassic Park III doesn't fair well critically, but I would have liked one of the two Isla Sorna based movies to have me build the research facility that was present before the hurricane destroyed everything.

The biggest draw for the first game was the visuals. JW Evolution prides itself in it's incredibly realistic dinosaur visuals and personalities with many animations for each dinosaur. I can confidently say that the visuals are still amazing, if not slightly better given the new dinosaurs and locations for different lighting.

Overall, if you are a fan of Jurassic Park and World, you will find some enjoyment in the game, regardless of if you enjoy these town/park tycoon building games. If you do plan on getting the game, buying the DLC bundle is the way to go. The extra campaigns and new features are fun to use and try out, and the additional dinosaurs add that extra uniqueness to the different paddocks and parts of your park.

I would rank the game at a solid 8/10, with the two points taken mainly because of the retail price for the base game and all the add-on content. It's worth the price I paid, but I'm not sure I'd enjoy my time as much if I'd paid full price.

TLDR: If you like Jurassic Park, and you like city/park builders, buy this game.
