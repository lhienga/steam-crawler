\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTLDR: Side Thumb. The performance is so bad I won't be playing anymore, but the game is fun enough to play if that weren't an issue.

The game play is fun, and there are so many scenarios and hero's to try out, I'd love to play this more.

BUT

The performance is miserable. This is a pixel art game....? I should be able to run this game on a toaster. Even from level 1 on a tiny map against 2 AI, the game still takes too long to plan its moves. If you think that's bad, just wait until you end up in combat with 1000's of units where the game bogs down to negative speed. I went looking for answers online, but the best I could find is that it might be limitations of the game engine. To that I say: I don't care, find a way to fix it. I played 29 hours, most of which was probably a single run on a huge map, and only because I'm a glutton for punishment.

For the record I play on a mid tier gaming PC with a Radeon 6600, and checking performance it does appear to smash the GPU pretty well. Maybe a better graphics card would help, but what a joke that would be, buying a better graphics card for a pixel art game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI got this game on sale. I would not say that I regret it, per se, but I don't feel like it was a terrific purchase. It is certainly not a game without a lot of good stuff to it; artistic style is great, combat feels engaging and creative, the spells are various and useful, the factions are fun, the music is pretty good, the map customization options are nice, the turn-based feel makes it nice for both casual and focused playing. It's really, honestly, not terrible.

But it doesn't have any big +'s for me. It's just fine. The game feels shallow, like an early access game. The game has a lot of polish but the mechanics don't go super deep. I could see myself coming back some day and changing my review to positive if this game comes out with a big update. But it's basically checkers, once you figure it out you can not really lose and playing the game out to its finale is a bit of a slog. So far, I have enjoyed playing half-games, until it's clear that I'm 'over the hill' toward winning and then quit and play a new game. It does not feel worth it to chase things down and finish computers off, it's a bit tedious.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHero’s Hour is a turn-based strategy game and a remarkable reinterpretation of the classic Heroes of Might and Magic franchise.
I played most of the tutorial of Hero’s Hour, then played a single scenario as the “order” faction in the “No Man’s Island” map. I do not think I will return to this game. The map design and some minor mechanics just need more polish before I will play again. All of the bits under the hood are working, though, and this might be a great game for someone who loves Heroes of Might and Magic (HOMM) if the developers iron out some of the wrinkles.



The Good

Hero’s Hour makes several quality-of-life improvements to the HOMM Formula:

  • The battles are no longer turn-based, and that’s a good thing. They are mostly automated and can be a thrill to watch. I would say that battle is “less strategic” than HOMM, which isn’t saying much, but there is some strategy involved: the game allows you to choose which troops to send into battle first. For example, you might hold your undead units in reserve and send your human archers out first against an army of skeletons, then cast “destroy undead” which your human archers will be immune to.
  • No limit (that I could find) for the number of unit stacks in your armies. You can recruit six stacks of units generated from your town, then go get six more different types of units from a neighboring town. I could not find a limit to the number of stacks under my hero’s control. And there does not seem to be a morale penalty for mixing units of differing factions.
  • Those “creature generator” buildings that spawn a certain number of units each week now generate “caravans” at the end of each week and deliver the units directly to your town . This means you don’t have to create a hero whose only job is to travel from “dwelling to dwelling” collecting units every Monday morning.
  • Like weekly “creature generator” buildings, there were not any buildings that generate weekly resources, either. The game now gives your “lieutenant” hero (you know, the one who stays at your castle and does basically nothing) something to do by generating resources and artifacts each week to go and collect: it’s not as simple as visiting the windmill near your castle every morning, either. The resources are randomized and are always guarded by monsters to attack. So this way, you can level up your defensive heroes without sending them out into the world.
  • The map supports WASD controls and you can use the mouse wheel to scroll in and out. It works great, although there can be some pop-ins if you scroll out or in too fast.

    The Bad

  • Luck and morale do not seem to have any negative effects. I am not sure if this is an intentional part of the game’s design, or if I just luckily never dipped below zero for any character. There is a “retreat” mechanic but I never really got to see it in action, even when my team’s morale was zero.
  • The game does not seem to let me delete save games until I have completed a scenario. So that 90% complete tutorial save-game is just going to sit there on my “load game” screen forever until I go in and complete it?
  • The spellcasting is annoying. In order to open your spellbook during battle, you must move your mouse cursor to the bottom of the screen. But that action, moving your cursor to the edge of the screen, is also how map-movement works for mouse controls. So you try to cast a spell and end up moving the camera down, off-screen, in the fog of war below the battle. So then you have to move the camera back up to the battle every time you want to cast a spell. It’s annoying.
  • I could not find a way to re-generate all of my spell-points. They could only regenerate slowly over time. In HOMM, there was the “well” building which your hero could visit to replenish all spell-points at once. One could argue that not having a “well” in this game is a deliberate decision for balance reasons, and I applaud their risk-taking if that is the case here. But what ended up happening in my campaign is that, in an effort to preserve spell-points for an imaginary upcoming battle against an enemy hero, I ended up using hardly any spells at all. They become a precious commodity that I didn’t want to waste.

    The Ugly

  • There does not seem to be a “summon boat” spell. Context: there was a neutral castle on an island in the middle of the map, which I handily conquered. After I took the castle and exploited the island for all of its resources, my “General” hero left the island and I established a “lieutenant” hero to stand back and occasionally deliver fresh troops to my “General” hero on the front lines. NOPE. My lieutenant couldn’t leave the island, he was doomed to stay on the island forever.
  • Oddly enough, there were sections of the map that the game would not let me explore. I would just run into a wall of black “fog of war,” as if that section of the map just doesn’t exist. At first I thought this was a bug, or perhaps it was some sort of ability of my AI opponent (to obscure my vision), but I realized it may be an intentional part of the game design, during week 11 in the game, I finally found my opponent in a corner of the map which was explorable. I think the developers intentionally sealed off sections of the map so they could not be explored. But if so, why did they use fog of war? Why not a different graphic, or just create some impenetrable mountains?
  • There were some wide-open, empty stretches of water in my map. I probably spent a week of in-game time trying to explore nothingness. Which is hard to fix, HOMM was guilty of this too. So I understand why they created unexplorable areas of the map (see the bullet point above), it just seems they may have done a bad job at it. Fill those oceans up with stuff – and if you can’t do that, then the map is too large for the number of players.
  • On the subject of number of players being too small for the map I played, the scenario I generated said there would be three factions. There were not. There were only two factions on the map, me vs. one AI-opponent. And after I completed the scenario, the game displayed a graph showing some simple statistics which confirmed that the world never spawned the third faction. This was despite my intentionally choosing a three-player map.
  • My playthrough, which admittedly was on easy difficulty, involved me putzing about for 11 weeks going against basically zero resistance through islands of monsters and one neutral castle. At the end of week 11, I finally found the enemy, who had never left his starting island, and I wiped out his entire empire (of one castle) in 3 days (1 to take the castle, 2 days to kill his remaining heroes). It was just a very anti-climactic game – 11 weeks of dreariness for 3 days of actual fighting an opponent.

    As I mentioned in my introduction, the core of this game is solid and could be worth your attention for a specific type of gamer. The game engine, the graphics, the faction and unit design – all of these are great. But one very uninteresting playthrough on a map that reminded me of “quantity over quality” has turned the game sour in my mouth. If you think the game is worth my second look after the developers have added some polish, let me know and I will try again.

    EDIT - Holy crap, I just looked at my achievements and I got the achievements for winning (1) a "normal" map and (2) an "easy" map, despite my single playthrough on what I thought (and felt) l like an easy map. If that was normal difficulty, there are deeper issues going on with the AI.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game SLAPS. It's good, play it, love it, enjoy it.

Pros:
-Get that HoMM-like happy chemical generation
-Varied factions (A lot based on the HoMM ones so you can chug that nostalgia)
-Actually somewhat fair A.I. (No seriously, the A.I. is at a disadvantage until Challenging and only on Hardcore does it actively get more stuff than you do. If you're losing that's just called skill issue my friend)
-Modding! Though it's not very extensive due to engine limitations, its pretty damn great and the mechanical side of a faction is fairly easy to do - if you can make sprites, give it a shot

Mixed:
-Still in development, this means patches are still somewhat frequent and the balance between factions isn't there just yet. It also means you can voice your thoughts and suggestions and actively help make the game better, the dev listens to the community much more than most devs do.

Cons:
-Depending on your computer, fights with a lot of units can get a bit laggy
-No online multiplayer, though Remote Play Together works just fine
-No campaign (yet)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFantastically little game, you can sink a lot of hours into it. It's not perfect but it has a lot of potential, and it's still fun in it's current state.

The Good:

Super fun, surprisingly complex game, with lots and lots of different builds and strategies. You can spend many hours learning and mastering each faction. The auto-battler is such a great way to improve on the original HoMM series by speeding up the slowest aspect of it, while still keeping it fun and interactive through unit commands and spells. You can use Steam's "Remote Play" feature to team up with your friends and work together to take over the map.

The Bad:

Lots of balance issues, but the devs are listening to players and adjusting accordingly.

Combat is currently very swarm-based, and having the largest snowball is usually the best strategy, (though how you achieve this snowball, and how you support it with spells/skills will vary a lot).

The AIs are prett one-dimensional, and don't really change up their strategy much, with the hardest difficulties having to cheat to be a challenge.

Optimization issues - some effects will cause the framerate to practically freeze, no matter how good your machine (*Cough* Midas Touch *Cough cough*).

Lack of online multiplayer - this is the biggest flaw in the game right now. It's possible to team up with your friends in a hotseat mode by using the "Remote Play Together" option in Steam, but it's impossible to hide your actions from the other players while doing this, so it's really only viable as a team PvE, and not for PvP. The lack of human opponents also accents the limitations of the AI.
