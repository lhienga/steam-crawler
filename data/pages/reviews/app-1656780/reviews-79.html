These are all so many peacock feathers sticking out of a very, very, very cooked goose of a game. This is beat-for-beat 1996 turn-based strategy nostalgia-bait gameplay, complete with abominable pacing, a deep burden of metagame knowledge, and a whole lot of clicking "Pass Turn" for resources to ripen. Benjamin Hauer has tried to dust off this old drumstick by throwing a bunch more RNG spices into the stuffing, but only serves to underline the fundamental flaws that make Heroes of Might and Magic games pretty much unplayable in Current Year. Apart from the *stellar* art, this retread shows all too clearly why turn-based strategy went the way of the dodo.\t\t\t\t\t

These are the issues I see with the game, but I still love to see so many great ideas in a HoMM like genre, which i think might be a spark of greatness in future!

Check back on it in a few months, maybe the kinks will have been ironed out.\t\t\t\t\t

A Lot of Options in Search of Variety

Heroes' Hour gives you all kinds of fun toys to play with: a dozenish factions, a few hundred units, all sorts of heroes, spells, talent trees, artifacts, faction mechanics, and more. You can get a few hours of fun just out of exploring each factions' binary choices - Might hero or Magic hero, unit upgrade 1 or 2 - and several more out of optimizing that.

Unfortunately, you'll rapidly begin to notice some problems: The maps all seem very same-y, and the AI always seems to try to rush you. If you beat that rush and push back, you win. Most games are decided in 20-30 turns, with the rest being cleanup.

Congratulations, you've found what's wrong with Heroes' Hour: while there's a wide variety of toys to play with, you'll mostly be doing so on a very familiar map against a mysteriously similar opponent you will use the same strategy to defeat. Every match ends up feeling the same, even if you play with different toys.

Here's why that happens: the map generator does a terrible job generating variety in its nodes/biomes, and the AI cheats - just not in the way you think.

The AI is accused of cheating quite a bit. The truth is, on Normal difficulty the AI is actually at an economic disadvantage compared to you. At Challenging, it's on equal footing. Higher and it starts to get bonuses.

Where the AI cheats on every difficulty is exploration: the AI is maphacking. It doesn't actually have to do any exploration whatsoever to find resources or your base. So you'll watch the AI level up a bit, flag many of its starter mines, then immediately beeline to the gold mine in the nearest neutral node and flag it. It repeats this until it happens to wander into a neutral node you're exploring, and suddenly it's week 3 and the AI is on your doorstep with an army that's fought a minimal number of combats.

When this happens every game, there's only so much time before just playing with the toys isn't enough to hold your interest.

This game desperately needs an AI rework. All AIs cheat - they're finite state machines versus a creative human - so "good AI" is just a matter of how obvious their cheating is and whether that cheating leads to a better or worse game. HH's is very, very obvious and leads to a worse game, thus it's really bad.

After that, the node map generator needs a "complexity" setting. Nodes are very plain, basic, and predictable, mostly being guided tours along a road which almost everything can be seen from. Every node is connected via a road and a guard post. Where are the no-road connections, or no guard posts? Some of these options are buried in the cryptic editor, but few players are going to dive into that mess just to make a more interesting nodemap. These need to be options at match creation, not buried in nodemap editing.\t\t\t\t\t
