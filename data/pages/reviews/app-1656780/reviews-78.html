\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe concept of the game is genius, and I gotta admit I love the gameplay, but some things make me put this more as mediocre than good.
1. The AI is too aggressive even at the easiest difficulty.
2. The game does a bad job at explaining how it works. Not even the tutorial has helped me as much as trial and error.
3. Some factions seem imbalanced.
4. Multiplayer is local only
The multiplayer experience is a bit ruined by making it local since the element of surprise essentially does not exist in local multiplayer. As much as I want to blindfold my friends, you can't get away from screen peeking. However if online multiplayer was added, I could see this as being a really fun PvP experience, or even a fun co-op experience.
I'm recommending because it still has a lot of potential to be great.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFirst few hours I didn't really see anything special about this game. But the depth is actually amazing. the builds you can go for are varied and wild. The AI is terminator level threatening.

If you loved Heroes of Might and Magic, this is a way better imagining of it in my opinion (I could only play H&M&M for a couple hours before i got sick of it). I like the auto-battles vs the hex based slow combat of H&M&M.

It is an autobattler so you don't directly control your units for the most part; but you can still set them up at the start and give them SOME on the fly commands (like a cavalry flank).
In battle the best contribution you make as the Hero is the precise injections of spells into the melee.

Will you summon Air elementals in front of your melee too soak hits? or are they better suited raiding the enemy archer line? maybe you prefer artillery summons behind your already formidable knights. Maybe whats best is just to call down some comets to scatter your foes.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI think Hero’s Hour is fantastic. Here’s what you can expect based on my first 25 hours with the game:
  • HoMM-based TBS: heroes with spells, skill trees, equipment and level gains lead armies to claim resources and fight various factions for control of map. Bases can be upgraded to produce more units, provide bonuses/new spells, improve economy, etc.
  • Different factions with different playstyles. They are not perfectly balanced against one another. This is deliberate. As a Conquest of Elysium fan, I like this aspect a lot.
  • Combat is auto-battling in real-time with pause. You can spend mana to cast spells (summoning, buffs/debuffs, elemental casting, necromancy, etc), and you can provide some light directions to your army. It’s both more and less involved than you might think, and it strikes a good balance.
  • The AI is aggressive, sort of dumb, and pretty easy to predict. I would guess that its resource gain per turn is weighted to give it an economic edge against the player. This has not felt unfair to me on Normal difficulty.
  • Despite claims to the contrary, you do not need to rush to win. I play on big maps with very few enemy factions (usually just one or two), and I take my time. Do I restart during week one sometimes? Sure, but not nearly so much as I do when playing CoE (for reference).
  • Plenty of room to experiment with army composition, skill and spell synergies, and build order.

    I haven’t played a game that’s had me wanting “one more turn” so much since CoE5 came out. Give it a chance, take your time to learn the factions, and enjoy. HH is really promising, and the devs have been all over bug and balance fixes in the days since launch.
