\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHero's Hour is a cute pixel game that is heavily influenced by the Heroes of Might and Magic series, but is packed full of it's own flavor and personality. The real time combat system is a welcomed change from the turn based system we have all known and loved for years. If you like the HoMM series, then I can safely say this game is for you. You will know everything, including the hotkeys. I could see this being very fun to play with a friend if you have plenty of time.Now if you are not a HoMM gamer, then this game will be a sweet little treat that will introduce you to one of the best niche game genres. The introduction will not come easy as many game concepts are not fully explained and the tutorial is lacking.

For Beginners:
I must say the tutorial is a little wonky and you can easily mess it up, but don't let that discourage you. The AI can be quite strong at times on any difficulty, so expect a slight learning curve. The game seems to be the most fun on Easy/Normal mode due to the AI overscaling after 4 weeks. I think this game is a few patches away from true greatness but worth a buy right now.\t\t\t\t\t

A super cool Heroes of Might and Magic inspired game. There are components of it that almost feel like a Carbot reskin. If you've played HOMM you are familiar with 80% of the game. The combat has turned into an autobattler with light strategic touches.

Right now it's "hotseat" multiplayer... I don't think that's really a thing - although it may work if the "Play with Friends" option on steam works.. I'll have to try that.

Super cool - well worth the price for anyone who like HOMM 3. Enough alterations to make it fresh and different.

This game would be amazing if true multiplayer could somehow be worked in.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRight. So, I played only for a short while, but I have to tell you my first impressions while they are still fresh. And Hero's Hour has made quite an impression on me.

Full disclosure, as every Eastern European from a certain era, I used to play a lot of Heroes of Might and Magic 3. It is one of my favourite games of all time, to which I still come back every now and then. No wonder that I started playing Hero's Hour with a lot of preconceptions about what this game would be. And I got my ass kicked all the way back to playing tutorial.

Core concepts are the same: build your city, assemble an army, train a hero, start exploring the map, pick a fight with its denizens, claim mines, dwellings, resources and artifacts, preparing for the inevitable moment when your army encounters another player's. But then...

...Hero's Hour does stray a lot from this well known schematic. Literally sweeps the rug from under your feet.

First thing you'll probably notice: the combat happens in real time. Your units quickly fill the small battefield with their chunky pixel bodies and start throwing themselves at the enemy. You get to issue basic commands to your units, you can attack with your hero or cast spells, but that's it. It's a colorful, chaotic mess, which veterans of HoMM might find hard to stomach at the first bite. But bear with me!

That said, if you give it a chance, this system also does have some awesome merits. For instance, did you know that you are limited in how many creatures from your hero's army you can field at the same time? Once some of them start dying, you can and will send reinforcements from the reserves to fill their place. Some units are naturally better against particular enemies than the others - and while losing them is inevitable with the lack of control you have over your forces, at later stages it becomes vital to deploy your units in the right order (and in the right formation!), supporting them with appropriate spells and skills.

The game smartly counteracts the fact that you will be losing a lot of units. One of the most important buildings you have to create in your town is the Infirmary, which allows you to automatically recover some of the fallen ones. That's right - the battles are no longer all-or-nothing and one crushing defeat is not enough to lose the whole game entirely. This makes Hero's Hour a lot more beginner-friendly than the original that inspired it.

I haven't played all of the factions yet, I tried three, maybe four of them, but these I tried do feel varied and play entirely differently. For instance, Dwarves' main mechanic seems to be building up Grudge, which allows your creatures to temporarily come back alive when you cast a spell. It combos very well with Bloodlust, which powers up your units for every dead Dwarf. It seems natural to send in the weak Miners first, and when some of them die, your tougher warriors can charge in and make quick work of enemies.

On the opposite side we have Pillars of That-Which-Was, which is like a mythological China faction, complete with martial artists, blackpowder gunners, anthropoid pandas and dragon-summoning generals. One of their main skills allows your general to sacrifice some of his life points in order to keep his troops alive - I found swarming the enemies with cheap, weak units a very efficient strategy even late into the game. Everybody was kung-fu fighting while my hero, built specifically for that purpose, tanked up all the damage, ensuring that the brawling never ended.

Oh, yeah! Character classes are very varied also. Each faction has two classes, each class three starting heroes with different specializations and skill trees. Some of these abilities are downright insane - how about one that consumes an unit of Mercury every day and, in return, provides you with a small amount of shapeshifting mercury monsters? You let that hero camp near a mine (which boosts its effectiveness!) and after several weeks, they return with a force to be reckoned with!

One of the in-game descriptions suggests an existence of hidden, rare heroes with even cooler abilities. Can't wait to see what this is about!

Hero's Hour has many signs that the developer, too, loved HoMM, but saw its flaws for what they were. This is why adventure map spells, such as movement-boosting Celerity or classic Town Portal, cost you no mana, but instead go on several days of cooldown. No more winning the game on one turn just because you rolled up Dimension Door. Excellently, elegantly solved.

Hero's hour is immensely fun. If you are the series's veteran, you will have to break through the wall of initial insecurity, because things that feel similar turn out to be completely different and it is easy to resign then - but if you manage, if you convince yourself that you just need to learn the ropes anew, you're in for a great time.

Also, technical issues - some folks report the game crashing to desktop. Happened to me once during the two-hour playtime, but the game autosaves every turn and loads really quickly, so that's not really an issue to me.

Alright. Enough yammering, back to playing! I will update this review once I play some more.\t\t\t\t\t
