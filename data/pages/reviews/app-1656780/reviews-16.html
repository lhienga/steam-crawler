\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA fun take on making a Heroes of Might and Magic with real time (mostly auto) battles and roguelike elements.

If that doesn't mean much to you, the gameplay loop is basically: You get a procedural map set to certain parameters and randomly one of your faction's six hero to start. Each day you will move your hero and their army on the map to engage in battles, loot resources, claim buildings, ETC. You will also choose one improvement in town daily. Through the early game, you will mostly be fighting neutral mobs while you amass resources and power up your forces. Eventually you will break free from your starting zone, and begin to engage the other player characters - conquer them all to win.
Frankly, for me, Hero's Hour scratches the same itch to build an army, explore a map, and feel dread that it could all come crashing down with every conflict if I misjudged my odds as Conquest of Elysium, but with a few key differences. For one, Hero's Hour takes much less time to play (not counting those games of CoE where either the bots or myself get demolished by random elephants by turn three). In a similar vein, it just feels more lightweight in a way I can't really explain - There are still hundreds of unit types, there are loads of abilities that you can and should try to synergize with your abilities and spells, and there's even the added elements of spellcasting during battle and needing to chose how you want to upgrade your town... but somehow the game still just feels like it takes less of my brainspace than CoE. It might just be because everything is more digestible and less obtuse, but still - it makes it more pleasant to play when I don't feel like burying myself into a game that hard.
For negatives, there aren't many. The game doesn't really have a story or anything to that effect - this is a game for the sake of the game itself; it's to entertain the player. If you need some broader or grander goals than "win the game", you might not stick with this one - but if that's what you're looking for, you'll find loads of replayability here. Some of the balancing feels like it could have used some tuning - in particular, and I'm keeping the vague to avoid tainting any opinions, I found that one type of hero for one faction built around their base ability made their whole faction so grossly that I ended up walking into encounters with no units and leaving with armies... multiple times per turn. Finding busted strategies is part of the thrill of these games, but I think that one was a bit too busted, and too easy to build for at that.

Overall, I do recommend Hero's Hour! I wholeheartedly recommend it to fans of Heroes of Might and Magic or fans of the Illwinter Games looking for someone a little less involved but still fairly deep. I'd also recommend it to folks who like a good autobattler, but want a little more engagement. It's a really fun way to while away a couple hours without needing to invest an entire afternoon. There's a ton of replayability if you like the game for itself - though you might find it lacking if you wanted more than achievements to strive for.\t\t\t\t\t

- Fresh take on the HoMM formula, accompanied by well thought-out game design choices. For instance, the realtime battles feel very different, and you'll take more losses than you're used to, but the game has skills and buildings for loss mitigation (e.g., infirmary, available early for all factions, rescues 50% of slain units). In general, the theme of sacrificing army for benefits, or compensating for army loss, is much more present here than any other HoMM-style game I've played.

- Diverse factions and units. Despite most of the units feeling a little samey in combat across factions, unique faction buildings and hero abilities can make for very different gameplay and strategy. One faction has a super cool trait where you only have one real hero, but you can hire "avatars" that can have armies and engage in combat - but they have all the skills, stats, spells, and equipment of your main hero in a fight, as if your hero was there with that army.

- Unit balance feels pretty good, and the "power" limitation on fielding units makes for interesting tactical choices when you have a large army.

- Skills system is very fun and allows for a lot of different character builds. You can really focus in hard on stuff like making a hero amazing with swarms of weak units, having powerful and spammable spells of various sorts, permanent army unit creation over time or after battle, and so on. Really excellent variety even within some of these niches. The breadth of builds goes far beyond what would be allowed by any of the HoMM games for sure.

- Great neutral enemy implementation - early start feels balanced, guarded buildings offer a variety of benefits that extend beyond what I've seen before in this sort of game.

The bad:
- Faction balance, aside from units themselves, has some standout issues. Some faction towns have incredibly powerful unique buildings, way beyond what others offer - I can't imagine losing with a pyre town in a game that goes more than 8 weeks; the demon portal requires a lot of investment but is patently absurd once you've paid into it. Some faction hero abilities are also way stronger than others (human faction casters are the biggest offender, as far as I've seen). However, take these issues of mine with a grain of salt as I've only played 20 hours. Perhaps balance is better at a more "professional" level of play.

- Some skills feel very strong and others rather weak, or middling in a quick game, but absurd on a large map. Additionally, economic support skills are far too powerful on secondary heroes for how cheap additional heroes are. Even if I could only get 1 rank of a single support skill per hero, I think it would still be optimal to hire like 5 a week. That hiring one hero can bring you, say, 60% unit cost reduction and 20% increased unit production *in all towns* if you're lucky feels weird and bad. I have mixed feelings about bodyguards as well - a skill that gives free units to the hero in battle to fill some of the gap between it and your strongest hero army. They're highly disposable and fun to use, but it feels like cheating to get so much army out of such a tiny hero cost.

- Content is limited, despite there being so many factions. You've got a good variety of preset map seeds for procedural generation, much in the same way you'd do HoMM3 map generation, but that's all you get. No campaign or scenarios. There's a handful of what seem to be player-created maps, both fully predetermined and procedurally generated, but it doesn't seem like there's much volume for user created content. This is honestly my biggest gripe with the game.

- AI sometimes feels wonky and irrational. They can build up armies real nice on challenging and hardcore, and in a battle they cast spells effectively so they're a challenge within that context. But on the world map, it's hard to tell what their priorities are. Sometimes an "impossible" enemy will ignore one of my heroes in range, and do... not much with their turn. They'll often leave some guarded resources behind when they clear camps. It's not always bad and they're still pretty competent, but it surely doesn't feel anywhere close to playing against a person.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm still giving this game a thumbs up because of it's indie vibes, but while the game has a lot going for it, there's also a lot against it: The number of factions / monsters / skills makes it difficult to learn and some mechanics are very cumbersome. For many things and on several screens you're missing important information to understand the game (e.g. item gives you +1 level in some skill, there's no easy way to find out what the skill does) etc.

The AI feels kinda dumb so the game becomes repetitive and boring after a while. Heroes also can't die and can't lose items, so it just becomes a battle of attrition in the end. But that's not even the worst. When playing longer games the performance just becomes a real issue. With my i7/15G/rtx2060 I was getting 11 fps before closing the game and just giving up on playing it because it was too slow. I don't know how that's possible for a 2D game but alas.

If you enjoyed Heroes of Might and Magic 3 there is a LOT to like. The real-time battles are welcome and interesting twist to a otherwise pretty truthful homage to the original game. It will surely give you some nice nostalgia ~vibes~ but it kinda fades pretty quickly when you start finding the rough edges.\t\t\t\t\t
