Ugly graphics, inconsistent sprite work - highly detailed buildings next to tiny 8x8 unit sprites make me want to pull my eyes out, worse than a 20 year old game; and yet with artificial limits on the number of units in battle because the game lags when large armies are fighting.

There is some fun to be had, as there's a lot of creative factions with novel ideas to try and exploit, if only against neutral units around the map or up against the massively cheating AI(if you up the difficulty). The game is also in active development, so there's always another "cool" faction to look forward to, but doesn't seem like much work is being done on other aspects of the game right now. The spells are fun and strong, and the hero levelups are impactful. It feels nice to grow in power and take over the map, but once you've done it a few times you'll find there's not much else to do.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is a massive reboot and refresh on the tired Heroes of Might and Magic (HoMM) formula.

Some Examples:
1) In HoMM there was one artifact on every map, unfairly placed, by the time you got it on a bigger map the benefits generally didn't have any meaning because someone was already too powerful--in Hero's Hour there's basically one in every zone that's much easier to find, slightly less powerful but also buried with lots of resources.
2) Character progression is guided by clear trees that don't restrict you as much in how you develop your character.
3) You can hold many more (infinite?) "stacks" of units and not just 6 or 7.
4) You get stuck less often behind "monster locks" where you can't proceed for a week or two while you backbuild your forces.
5) Everything feels more balanced and well thought out mechanically in the initial zone development for every race.

The battles are the biggest and clearest overhaul. Before in HoMM it was abstract -- kind of like chess-piece spreadsheets with HP slithering across the map in turn. Now it's actual little units being rendered in a live, real-time combat where you can tangibly see the performance of individual units against one another. There's a little challenge mode that does an excellent job of teaching you the majority of the monster mechanics and how to leverage certain units' advantages against one another.

The retro-pixel design is probably the only place where you "lose fidelity" but it actually works to the game's benefit. It makes it super easy for modders to add new races and the way all the units are rendered and animated on the battlefield it's probably worth it in terms of saved frames, less stress on you computer, and ability to fudge scale. Moreover there's plenty of juice to the graphics so that even though they're byte-sized characters, they're still quite entertaining to watch.

The battle mechanics overhaul brought with it a total change in how this game manages magic spells. The magic is still powerful but feels far less overpowered, barring a couple exceptions. There are some magic mechanics and some abilities that aren't balanced well in the mid-to-late game as they let the armies of certain factions essentially absorb all the enemies while other factions' armies at best can only avoid losses. But the developer is constantly patching and balancing so I have no doubt this will be massaged as time goes on.

One other change that would be virtually free but have a huge benefit would be with the hero design mechanics. Each faction is pretty much locked to six heroes and the tree of each hero is fixed. That means if you know what you're doing you can min-max beat your opponent, which I guess is good for competitive play, but seems rather rigid for the "I just want to have fun" crowd as randomness would yield greater replayability. I would propose each of the fixed skill trees get turned into a subclass, and whichever hero gets whichever subclass could be randomized (while still matching warrior portraits to the might subclasses and mage portraits to the magic subclasses for aesthetic purposes). By doing it this way the game could add an infinite variety of heroes (in the form of new portraits) without actually losing any of its actual gameplay mechanics. That's just an idea though, the current design makes sense from a simplicity standpoint.

All in all it's a super streamlined and refined game experience. Nothing to really say other than great job and fun game.\t\t\t\t\t
