\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is awesome. It's a very well refined HoMM clone with a lot of its own spin on it. All the HoMM stuff is there. You build up your towns, recruit heroes and creatures before marching them out to bash the snot out of the baddies while capturing resource generators and the like. No campaign, which is a bit of a disappointment, I really enjoyed those in HoMM 3 in particular.

Bit of a steeper learning curve I'd say. At least in the early game, and there are a few key differences that seem to trip people up when they first start playing. It is a game that punishes you if you don't plan ahead and focus on using the strengths of your primary hero to amplify a particular strategy and I think that caused quite a few people to bounce off it pretty hard.

The overworld has a lot in common with what you'd expect. Early expansion can be quite challenging, and due to the random nature of the map, as well as even the random upgrade path within your starting town I did find myself with quite a few false starts in the beginning. Ramping up a capable starting army within the first day or two is very important and I think this is the first thing that tends to trip new players up. Basically if you haven't got a significant chunk of your starting zone opened up and explored within the first week or you loose an early battle you should consider restarting. It's difficult to bounce back from a major early setback. There are a few nice QoL features like how in addition to being able to shuffle troops around without a hero and the various independent recruiting sites will automatically send their units to the nearest castle or hero. You can also garrison your surplus heroes by mines in order to boost their income and provide some defence.

The maps themselves consist of zones to segregate the factions. The zones themselves are randomly generated, but their position relative to each other and any connections are preset. These will either be linked to a faction or labeled as 'neutral' depending on which map you pick. The faction zone is basically your starting area to get you access to the resources you need as well as a place to level up your primary hero. Neutral zones will have an independent castle in them as well as more advanced resource structures and is where you will likely first encounter the enemy AI. It lulled me into a false sense of security in my first game and I expanded quite leisurely poking about with different units and skills to see how the pieces fit together. Ended up getting my teeth kicked in on like week 3 when the AI hero invaded. If there is an in-game encyclopedia or anything I wasn't able to find it, but besides, there is just so much stuff it would take ages to read through so expect to learn by failing, or reading/watching a bunch of guides.

The battles are a ton of fun. Realtime, and very chaotic. Not as tactical as the hex based battles traditional to the genre, but again they reward preplanning and leveraging synergies between your hero (who is perfectly combat capable themselves) units, and spells. This is where your hero build really becomes important. Read the skill descriptions, figure out which units you have access to them that synergize with a particular skill and leverage that. Keep an eye pealed for anything that gives % increases to combat stats and/or HP. The mastery skills, legion and champion are three to really look out for. Also look out for anything that will let you freespawn units. Bloodwarping, Mastery skills, and summoning are good examples. Also, as of this writing at least Bodyguard is a critical skill for your secondary heroes though you can get 1 rank of it for free from the Champions Statue though I have at least heard murmurings of a nerf, so if you want to exploit it do it soon. The battle UI is a bit awkward and could likely use some refinement... The edge scrolling with my dual monitor setup is a frustrating to say the least, shuffling units in and out of reserves particularly in the battle setup is a pain, the little pixel blood splatters and other particle effects can make it difficult to see how the battle is going at a glance and casting spells is rather finicky, but overall it works well enough and the fights are a joy to watch.

Combat magic kinda deserves its own little mini section too. There are a ton of different spells, and other than a few of the dedicated caster heroes, exactly what spell you get is randomized so you need to adapt them to whatever strategy you plan on running. You can summon units mid fight, grant a wide variety of buffs and debuffs, do direct damage, and all that sort of stuff.

Towns work pretty much as you'd expect from the genre. Each faction has unique units, and each faction plays very differently. Particularly since every faction has access to their own unique hero pool each with different skill trees which opens quite a bit of variety within factions. Towns are upgraded as you'd expect. One building per day, one batch of recruits per week. I think all the recruitment buildings except for the Horde faction have at least one upgrade, and most of them also allow you to pick between two distinct recruit options each week as well. All the factions have at least a few unique buildings that do cool things, and they all have the markets and mage guilds and that sort of stuff that we're used to. That said, there are some different buildings available that can be easy to overlook, the Infirmary and Champions Statues are critical pieces of infrastructure and you'll eventually probably want hospitals as well. Pay attention to your units as well, particularly their abilities and again how you can synergize them with your hero skills. It will pay off. Leeches from the Enclave Lizardmen faction combined Legion can be terrifying for example.

Heroes are probably the aspect of the game that differentiate it most from the traditional entries in the genre. Basically each hero has a unique skill tree. You'll find the same skill on different heroes, but it will usually be in a different location. The skills you have access to and the ones you choose to focus on are probably the most important part of the strategy gameplay loop. Get skills that boost your combat ability, leverage that to take harder fights with fewer casualties, gain XP, repeat. Planning out a build is quite important. Some skills will feed off of each other in terrifying ways. For example Liara from the Horde faction gets Archery, a skill that boosts the damage of her ranged units. She also gets Troll Mastery, which gives her free trolls which are a ranged unit. Finally she also gets Champion, which boosts the HP and Damage of all units with 9 or more power, which trolls... are you seeing where I am going here? They are all % based increases too so it can start stacking up like mad.

Now as far as the AI goes, it seems... decent. It is very much based around the horde of troops philosophy, and this can make them quite challenging, particularly while on the defensive if they can get access to re-enforcement faster than you. It will likely win a war of attrition especially on higher difficulties with its resource bonus. Given the importance of hero builds though you start to see the limitations. I think the AI tends to pick skills randomly and mash together units into armies without much thought. As long as you aren't doing the same you can likely crush most enemy armies without too much trouble. The battle AI on the other hand is pretty smart. It seems to use spells effectively, recognize when it is likely outmatched and avoid the fight and that sort of stuff. It is a pity that there is no online multiplayer available, especially for asynchronous multiplayer but it would likely be a pretty tall order especially given the importance of the real time battles.

Anyhow, if you are still with me, thank you for coming to my Ted Talk. Heroes Hour. Great fun. You should play it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHero's Hour. A blatantly direct love letter to Heroes of Might and Magic 3.

I've read a lot of comments complaining about this game, and frankly I'm a little surprised they all seem to be around the same sort of idea. You need to play a certain way to win, there's really only one way to do it, et cetera et cetera. The thing that surprises me is that all of the issues people are whining about existed in HoMM3, and not only did they exist but are sort of central to the concept of the game. "No Necropolis" was something we just said if we were going to play pvp. It was simple, yet eloquent. It perfectly captured the fact that HoMM3 was an imbalanced mess that we all fell in love with.

In my opinion, Hero's Hour succeeds on two things very well. Capturing that feeling of nostalgia while mixing in enough differences to make this a whole new experience worth chewing on. I can't honestly find much to complain about, so far I've really enjoyed it. I noticed the AI could use a bit of work, but also noticed them doing things that ai in HoMM3 would never do that surprised me very much. Bravo, this game is fantastic. To anyone writing negative reviews, I'd imagine you would feel differently had you played Heroes 3. It's a shame, really, as this game is a pretty impressive faithful sort of homage.

For the record, in response to the negative review that claimed that you 'HaVe To RuSh To WiN', Bud, you know that that is how these games work. And worked. And will continue to work. That's not a secret strategy, that's like knowing the car goes on the right side of the road (In the US). It's common knowledge, and if you can't handle even the most basic of concepts like that, perhaps you should become a game journalist.

TL;DR: Buy the damn game if you played Heroes of Might and Magic 3. If you didn't consider it anyway. It's a solid piece of work.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHonestly I hate leaving a bad review, but it's very vanilla with what feels like less strategic depth and more rpg-city builder aspects that are also generic. Battles are almost fully automated and is all stat checks. Maybe get it on sale, but not for full price. That's at least if you want a strategy game.

I want to say the short time I had was after beating the tutorial and it dumped me into the game, I could tell that the game was not getting any more interesting and it just would continue to "get more units to take camps." This is why I'm leaving a negative review and why I have so little time in the game. It shows everything it has in the first 20 minutes and it's rinse and repeat. There are much worse games out there, but at least they had some promise moving forward or were what they advertised. I expected there to be strategy or some amount of diversity in units like an RTS or turn-based strategy game, but unit type doesn't matter when the strategy is all about stat checking.

Nearly no challenge for a game selling itself as a strategy game. Little story for the RPG side. Might be a good background game, but not a game to focus on. Definitely not worth full price. Got my refund while I could.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\ttldr; Really good game. RTS elements are good, a lot of factions to choose from and a lot of stuffs that can help in increasing the chances of your victory. But it has still a lot of room for improvement.

Cons though,
-Not a lot of reward for replaying
-No online PVP... yet
-Pixel text is good but most of the time, it is clunky because the font size is too small
-Some UI are overlapped and unreachable
-No option to change resolution to maybe fix the UI problems
-The Heroes doesn't have that outstanding difference in spells/utility. *or maybe I haven't played enough through*
-No Lore behind the characters. I recommend even just funny ones to relate to the players.

-*just an opinion* For an RTS-esque game, which will most likely be PVP in the future, it isn't FREE. I think it would be better if they started it out free to play so they can get more traction with the players. There is not a lot of new RTS games coming out. People are coming out of the MOBA hype. MIcrosoft just bought starcraft. The HYPE is in upcoming RTS games. This is the best time to steal some RTS players and hype it at the same time perfectly before Starcraft 3.

I recommend to use skins as a mode of income. Also hide some of the faction's/heroes progression in single player mode/ranked missions so players can feel a sense of reward for replaying. Also, it makes the players get more familiarized with basic mechanics of the game. Making everything available bombs the new players with too much info and advocates smurfing for veteran players since they are all in the same playing field.\t\t\t\t\t
