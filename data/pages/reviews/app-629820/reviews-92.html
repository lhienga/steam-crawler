\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhile the basic premise of the game is entertaining, marauding around as an increasingly large and lethal shark, it fails to delivery in a number of respects. Most notably, the gameplay is *incredibly* repetitive, showing a sharp lack of creativity. The storyline has you progressing through "zones" of this world, and the pattern in each zone is the exact same:

* Go kill a specific target, which is usually a beefed-up version version of one of the normal aquatic creatures in that zone, but is sometimes a boat of humans on the surface. Once you kill this target, go kill 3-4 more (different type) targets scattered around the zone.
* Go kill 8-12 of a specific type of aquatic creature at a specific location, which also has a number of larger predators roaming around it that you'd ideally like to avoid. Once done, go do it again, 8-12 more of the same creature type at specific other locations, 3-4 more times.
* Go kill 8-12 humans at a specific location. Usually this involves you comedically flopping around on land eating people until your O2 gauge runs low, refilling it in the water, and repeating, but occasionally you get to eat swimmers instead. Once done, you guessed it, go do it 3-4 more times at other specific locations on the map.
* Once done, find and kill the apex predator, which is a very beefed up version of one of the creatures in the zone, though they really aren't that difficult or dangerous. Each apex predator awards you an evolution item.
* Go to a spot, watch a cutscene, possibly fight a boss fight, move on to the next zone.

Wrapped around that, you have a GTA-stype alert system, where if you kill enough humans in the area, hunters are spawned, and continue to spawn until you hide from them for about a minute. As you kill those hunters, you gain "infamy ranks", and at each rank a boss spawns. Killing each boss grants you an evolution item.

Other than that, the basic idea is to just kill and eat everything you can to generate the 4 types of organic currency used to evolve your evolution parts, which you can mix-and-match to change up your playstyle. However, those evolution parts are wildly different in balance and effectiveness, and the set bonuses means that it's rarely useful to *actually* mix-and-match them, and instead just pick one of the 3 sets of items (bio-electric, bone, or shadow), and use that, with the only "mixing" coming from your pass trait slots.

There's also a collection game around that, with each zone having 10-20 landmarks (collecting all of the landmarks for each zone also grants an evolution item, so it's semi-required), 10-20 chests that contain massive amounts of resources and XP in each, and then 10-20 floating license plates that are there just for the sake of completion (and function, in some cases, as jump puzzles, though the *controls* for jumping around on land and in the air are *atrocious*).

The story was both trite and rather boring. It involved no twists, it had precious little in the way of redeeming qualities, and served more than anything just as an explanation for why you have Jerry from Rick and Morty narrating your life (which was, incidentally, the *best* part of the game, imo. Chris Parnell nails the humor in the dialog).

Overall, the base gameplay loop gets boring about 1-2 zones in, the evolution system is about 10% as deep as it seems, the storyline felt like something written by a 6th-grader, and the <20 hours of gameplay time to complete the story, for a $40 price tag, is laughable. I hard recommend *against* buying this game, regardless of how fun it might seem to swim around as a shark, because the novelty of that concept is the *only* thing this game really boasts (well, and Chris Parnell's excellent comedic delivery).\t\t\t\t\t
