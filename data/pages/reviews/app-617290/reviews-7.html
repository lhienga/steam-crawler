\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI tried playing this game a few years ago when it was given away for free on PSN. At the time, i sensed something unpleasant and decided to bail out even before starting. Now, several years later, I gave the game a fair second chance.

Before I begin, it's worth noting that I didn't try playing the game in co-op. I did some research beforehand and found some enthusiastic people claiming that the game was excellent to play solo.

That being said, i played solo and with very low expectations.

The only thing I had in mind was that the game was marketed by fans as a "Dark Souls with guns," and as I am a reluctant fan of the studio's old games (when it was called Vigil), i knew it would likely be a somewhat uncreative mishmash of various other titles, presented competently but without much personality (I was correct on this point).

I played for a few hours, and what I saw didn't make me want to continue playing. But it's not a bad game in the practical sense. The gameplay is responsive, the combat is well-balanced (for co-op), the performance is great, bugfree, and it has a relatively competent visual style.

For all intents and purposes, it's a functional piece of software.

But it's also a lousy game to play solo. And no, this is not "Dark Souls with guns."

So...

This game gives a very bad first impression.

You are introduced by a tutorial as subtle and unintrusive as a jackhammer at 5 in the morning (which I recommend skipping right away). It takes a while to get started.

You can smell the overblown AA budget in the first few minutes.

Darksiders has always been a shallow melodrama, but at least it had a kind of mythology giving texture to the game's fiction. It also had charismatic characters and a playful comic book tone. Good music. Various elements to enrich what would otherwise be a forgettable Zelda clone. Remnant seems more empty and soulless than a game made by Ubisoft.

I'm genuinely puzzled about how "Dark Souls with guns" became something. Has no one ever played Dark Souls before? It's as if the game is just a handful of mechanics and not a carefully curated single-player experience with planned encounters with varying difficulty, a strong emphasis on enemy variety, level design, atmosphere, and a implicit storytelling structure that emerges through the player's experience and that can be enriched by their curiosity.

No, my friends, just like any other game previously made by this team, Remnant is a hodgepodge of copies of other games organized cohesively and without a drop of originality. And it doesn't take the best from its influences. Just a thing or two.

It could be worse, of course, but everything I saw, just made me want to play the games that served as the foundation.

Remnant seems to alternate between a poor man's version of The Last of Us, with its generic post-apocalyptic visuals, less attention to detail, and less personality than Darksiders 3 and its repetitive, closed, procedural setups of tunnels where you are relentlessly harassed by waves of harmless enemies.

One moment you explore wide and empty streets with simplistic, artificial ambiance, exploring destroyed buildings and shops to collect resources, killing a minimal variety of enemies practically unable to deal with you.

Afterward, you go down the tunnels. Basically labyrinthine dungeons with different flavors, also infested with repeated enemies, but implemented even more randomly than on the surface.

I basically progressed, encountered a strong enemy surrounded by small enemies, dealt with them, walked a few meters, and the same random setup appeared again. And again. And again. Sometimes the stronger enemy was different, but mostly not. This five or six times until the tunnel exit, where i arrive at another section of shallow exploration in the streets and buildings... To then arrive at the next section of tunnels with the same enemies and the same random combat set-up...

I did this three times.

Above, the loop's pace is enjoyable, even if the combat lacks dynamics or any sense of structure. You simply walk and kill enemies thrown in your way without much consideration. Below, it's just tedious.

Sometimes a stronger, spongier enemy appears, and then you have to be a bit more careful, but it's nothing noteworthy. Nothing that feels planned or out of the ordinary.

I played quite relaxed on Hard, and if it weren't for the high damage dealt by enemies per hit, I don't think I would have died even once until the point where I stopped.

You also don't feel like you're discovering your way, nor do you feel any apprehension when you reach a new place and don't know what you'll have to deal with. The design seems so simplistic and predictable that it's genuinely hard to be surprised or have any sense of immersion.

After three hours of playing, I had already killed hundreds of the same type of enemy and faced a boss (which was basically a slightly stronger, but red, version of a normal enemy) with an incredible arsenal of three different attacks for me to learn and that would be considered a complete joke if not for the artificial tactic of inflating difficulty with adds.

After five hours, I was still killing the same enemies, walking through the same underdesigned spaces, using the same weapons from the beginning.

Well, this game doesn't compare to Dark Souls in practically any aspect. And frankly, even Darksiders 3 did a better job of copying.

Some mechanical elements were directly taken from From Software's design (just like in Darksiders 3), but in practice, Remnant is simply a common third-person shooter with a generic integrated RPG system and stamina management to limit dodge abuse.

Shooting enemies that pose no threat to you doesn't have significant appeal beyond a few hours.

Melee has that sense of weight and deliberation expected from a "Soulslike," but it's so dispensable that I barely felt the urge to use it outside of specific and convenient situations.

As a cooperative experience, it has brainless, loose, and uncommitted design to avoid confusion. It seems deliberately simple and mediocre so that social interaction fills the gaps that would be filled by good game design in a purely single-player game.

It's a game made to be played with others, so I don't recommend wasting time with it if you don't plan to do so. It's not the worst thing in the world, and it could, being very optimistic, improve later on, but without a doubt, it seemed like a waste of time to me.

So... for those planning to play solo, I don't recommend it.

Edit: I went back there to play some more.

The new area, basically identical to the previous one but with a different color filter, featured enemies with weapons, which considerably alleviated the unpleasant experience of eliminating enemies before they could do anything.

Which didn't mean much. After almost an hour, I had gotten fed up with the repetition and when I found another descent into tunnels, I decided to stop playing. Again.\t\t\t\t\t
