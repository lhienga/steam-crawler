\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRemnant: From the Ashes is a shooter with strong RPG elements imbedded into its combat, set within an invasion of tree-like monsters that has the protagonist travelling to distant words trying to find a way to end the take-over.

Unfortunately, I cannot recommend the game, but I’m still going through the good things first, in case there is someone curious.

The first time I got a hold of this game, its loop really got my attention. You travel across the land, collect a simple set of material while killing enemies and bosses, go back to the base and try to craft weapons and upgrades. This is really well made, with sections that are not too long to become extremely tedious and a pain to backtrack, but aren’t too short to kill off all tension. If you aren’t ready, you gonna get trampled.

The crafting of new objects and upgrades is not overcomplicated with a thousand different types of resource (although it falls apart from other side). There are simply 4 grades of iron and a set of “unique” objects that might be added for crafting.
The “boss weapons” as the game calls them, are weapons crafted from materials exclusive to a boss under certain conditions (kill them before X, kill with Y), which adds a nice dimension of challenge to every boss and gives a nice closure to their presence, as weapons sometimes reflect one of their attacks or the challenge that was fulfilled to obtain the required materials.

You get a main weapon, a side one and a melee. Then three pieces of armour and three accessories. Builds are simple to discover on your own and, as every bought or crafted weapons is permanently added to your inventory, to swipe around. It’s nice being able to lose against a boss and just switch your build form the checkpoint to try out a new strategy.
Both ranged weapons have also access to what are called mods. These are abilities that you charge up as you deal damage to enemies, to unleash at some point some kind of special power: Healing in an area, seeing through walls, getting a caustic shield to damage enemies that get too close… Even sometimes the special powers allow to use special types of projectiles that are a weapon on their own. These are also quite interesting to keep in mind during builds and, honestly, they can make a great difference. There are some enemies that don’t give enough breathing room for healing, or some are better to face while having a decoy, against some other you just need the edge of the auto-revive ability…

Again, regarding experimentation of builds, abilities and such, this game is really great.

Another positive is the atmosphere. Not always, but most of the time is worth a second glance. I really enjoyed this depiction of a post-apocalyptic earth being consumed by the huge trees of blood. It was a nice change of pace from the deserted cities of nothing but sand and nuclear waste around the streets. The visuals of the lush jungle and the swamps. Exteriors in general. They are very neatly put, while interiors usually fall a bit uninteresting.

And overall, the game feels well enough as an experience to play it. Gunplay is serviceable, the character might feel a bit too slow but compensates with a side-roll with immunity frames, damage dealt has good feedback, damage received has… Sometimes too good of a feedback.

So, what does fail? Well, these good mechanics aren’t really put to good use.

First, the whole build system. It’s really nice conceptually, but it's horribly exploited. The thing I mentioned about having no more than 4 types of basic resources is because from time to time I’ve fallen into a game that requires you to manage a stupid amount of different resources for the most basic of upgrades or uses. This is of course common for games that sell these very resources, to force “lazy” players into buying them, cause they would rather pay to get them that to go through a thousand loops to get a 10% chance of getting a 3% of the resources they need for something. It’s underhanded, but it’s obviously effective. I don’t approve it, but I can make a sense of it.

Now this game doesn’t sell anything (hey, another plus for it) nor does require 80 different types of resources. Yet the way you get the builds feels so unrewarding… First, given how every enemy drops iron almost always, one begins to wonder why do you even have the resource in the first place; at the point the game is, it feels like there wouldn’t be a real difference for the first 19 upgrades (now I’ll say what happens with the 20th rank) if you just -bought- them. Second, every rank adds the same bonus every time. It’s linear and not that interesting. The whole upgrade system turns into upgrading whatever you can, usually going for your most recent upgrades to build on the already performed ones. There is no strategy or sacrifice. I guess you can play around with the bonuses linked to the armour sets themselves, but I really didn’t notice these special abilities in particular.

And that segways to the third big issue with the upgrades: They don’t really feel that impactful. I guess that once you’ve upgraded to the max level there would be a difference between having it on and not, of course. But as only stats are increased, very little, always the same amount… It gives of the vibe that you don’t really improve.

Speaking of the max level… What is up with the surprise material?

The last upgrade for any piece requires a special material, which goes the complete opposite way. Not only it is extremely rare, but only 4 appear for each run of the campaign.

And if the 20th upgrade rank was something special. If it added some new ability. If it changed the skin. If it added a bigger amount of stats than the previous ones… I could understand its existence. But it’s just the same, for everything. It’s so pointless. The only reason I had to upgrade gear to the last rank was to stop seeing that 19 in the menu.

On other issue, we’ve got the story. The setting itself, is interesting enough I’d say. The lore and design of the enemies. Not bad, I guess…

But oh boy, is the dialogue so shallow, so stereotypical. It takes absolutely no risks, the characters have absolutely no personality. How can every being in this universe be the same kind of “intense” and “wise beyond knowledge” type of character. I got so bored of being spoken to in bloody prophecies. I swear it, I would rather have a completely silent race instead of what we got. And the overarching story, which is looking for the founder of the stronghold that serves as your base, is all there is to it. You follow him around 3 different planets, do some “side” mission, meet him and face the final boss. End of story. There is very little changing or happening and the stakes feel pointless because anyways back on Earth humanity has been already almost wiped out.

This ties into the bosses, which in themselves are a “neutral” point for the game. There are some quite interesting to fight and there are some that are cheap and unrewarding. But all of them have in common that they barely tie into the story. They just protect the next area, they serve as obstacles, but you don’t know about them until literally the second you start fighting them and, if it wasn’t because of the aesthetics, they would have been interchangeable.

And last, but not least, this ties into the common enemies. Some are interesting on their own, but this game relies way too much into “tribeperson” type of enemies. Most of them are physical and besides special enemies that rely on “energy” projectiles, only one weapon is used by a single enemy in the whole game. The way the enemies fight doesn’t feel very engaging.

So, to sum up, I feel like this game was a great wasted opportunity. Like they had the basics for a nice game figured out but got cut during development and left a cask of a game. It will get repetitive for most people. There are probably better alternatives. Not the worse, but not a game you “need” to try out.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFrom everything I played it felt repetitive I have played more on PS4 and hadn't picked it up in years but I decided why not try it again on Steam. I love souls-likes and the idea of Dark Souls with guns fills me with excitement. After replaying the first bit of Remnant I remembered why I hadn't thought about it in so long: It's repetitive and boring.
Maybe it's because I'm playing solo but I found it very unenjoyable and didn't want to play the game at all. Enemies felt spongey the controls feel arcadey and this may be unpopular but death should feel more like losing something. In Elden Ring you lose your runes and Nioh is the same, but this game you don't lose anything you keep everything but the enemies respawn. I find this makes me not feel like there's any stakes which does hamper enjoyment for me. I could go on but I don't feel like this game deserves a in depth analysis no offense to the devs it runs absolutely perfect on Steam deck so I applaud them for that however it's the core game which is what I dislike.
4/10\t\t\t\t\t
