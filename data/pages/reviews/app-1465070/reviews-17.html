(Valve Index)
- Overall look of the game is still quite dated, with an odd buggy filter over everything most likely due to post processing
- Some inputs completely do not work, like putting items into your inventory (made sure i had the correct official bindings and everything)
- Insanely unintuitive weapon controls, you have to press the grip button once to grab something, then let go and press it again to drop it, with no option to let you hold the grip button to hold weapons, like any other game would.
- No individual finger control after over 2 years, it was ok on launch, but was expected as QoL at 1.0 among many other things
- Controls are insanely stiff, as they were on launch.

A lack of any sort of real progress over 2 years followed by a price hike to a game that was already expensive for what it was. Devs blamed it on UE5, saying they wasted time trying to port the game before reverting back to UE4 due to apparently bad CPU optimization in the engine. That is not the fault of the engine but is purely an issue caused by bad coding, planning, and general infrastructure. Many other VR games have been made in UE5 and others like Pavlov have been ported to UE5 further into active development than this game is.

Since around early 2022, the game has stagnated with updates and was seemingly abandoned. It was a neat concept at the time and was fun despite its many drawbacks, and where was promise of a UE5 upgrade with more content on the horizon. Since then it has been disappointment after disappointment and it is clear that the developers decided to cash in on what they could, begin a lackluster marketing campaign last second and release an unfinished mess with frustrating "new" mechanics, and an overall pricier, and worse for it experience.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWas hoping the full release would be better--and it is--but it still has far too many problems that keep it from being fun. Also the game itself labels itself as early access still despite being out, which is a fun bug.

Two categories of issues, jank and intrinsic gameplay problems. Intrinsic problems first because they're more debatable and if you don't like them, you'll probably never like the game. Unlike games like Ancient Dungeon, you can't aggressively attack enemies and expect that to work. You have to pause between attacks because rapid attacks count as light attacks and do severely reduced damage. It doesn't match with how the actual motions play out physically and there's no audiovisual indicators when your full attack is ready, so it requires you to practice to get the speed down and then just mime slow-motion movement to deal with combat... which sucks. I don't want to play-fight, I want to actual-fight. As a result, for me, the combat feels sluggish and disassociative and unsatisfying. It makes me keenly aware that I'm playing a game, forces me actively to be aware of that. That sucks.

As for jank, you grab things by grabbing them once and let them go by grabbing them again. Especially with Index controllers, this absolutely sucks. I drop weapons mid-combat all the time and while trying to put them into quick slots and while dodging. It's a bad system. I assume it's better for Meta controllers or something or they'd have fixed it by now. I couldn't find any options to swap "toggle grip" to "just grip" which I'd much prefer. Shifting weapons between your hands feels incredibly awkward as a result, shifting your grip is triggered by tapping trigger which is weird and the opposite of what I think of when moving my grip. Leaning over objects like chests pushes my character backwards so it's a struggle to reach objects. All in all, interacting with the world is just unpleasant, which is a bad place to be for a VR title.

Second part of jank--and I assume the game enforces awkward slowness in attacking to force you to engage with this system--parries and guards seem sus. It felt like I managed to parry or shield myself a lot and the weapon just sort of awkwardly wobbled around my sword or shield and still landed the hit. In the end, the easiest/most consistent way to parry an attack is just to block the enemy's arm entirely, which they'll let you do because it's not like they back up or anything. Realistically attacking the arm would be a good way to parry but actual opponents wouldn't let you, so it's unsatisfying that it's the obvious approach here.

The fact NPCs don't have animated eyes is super, super creepy. Their heads can bob and move a bit with an absolute dead-eyed stare. It makes sense that you're mostly fighting the undead so they don't have to make them look like people because they can't. It ends up making the largely pretty good visuals end up deep in the uncanny valley in a way worse graphics ironically wouldn't.

Which brings me to my conclusion: Play Ancient Dungeon instead. Grabbing and interacting with stuff feels good, flailing is punished but aggressive attacking is rewarded, and enemies seem honestly more physically realistic in how they animate and move (and they are ALSO much more aggressive than the sluggish skeletons here). It is a simpler game for sure but it nails the interaction and combat in ways I don't think Legendary Tales ever will. I had hoped Legendary Tales would feel much better after the long hiatus, and they've improved a lot of the systems that were just outright broken before, but it just lacks basic engagement. A complete, busy game has been built around a system that never quite worked.\t\t\t\t\t
