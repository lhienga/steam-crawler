\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs everyone here I got this one for nostalgic purposes. The game is one year older than me and I'm not sure around what age I got to play it, but English is not my native language and this is the first time I actually got to understand the dialogue and story. I'm sure as a child I never finished it as well.

I do want to write a complete review and not just make some comment about how nostalgic this made me, so here it is.

STORY:
As a children's game, this works really well. The story is simple and linear with clear stakes but not without some little extra details here and there that make it cool to explore. It's very cute how well it's sold through simple storytelling mechanisms how the characters work. If a kid playing this has seen a cartoon or two and read some kids' books they'll understand each character archetype and the small jokes very easily.

ART and ANIMATION:
It's super charming in my opinion. Truly got to appreciate the work put into it as an adult. The flurishes of animation each time Freddi and Luther go in and out of an enviroment are so cute. Love the cartoon style as well, some great expressions and squish-and-stretch use. The environments are super endearing without being too cartoony or light, everything does feel like the bottom of the ocean. It has a very Don Bluth-esque to it all, dark where it needs to be. It isn't (kid) scary, of course, but I think it's due to how much younger the target audience for this probably is. And gigantic shout-out to all the animations in each location, every plant and rock and small fish peering through the edges as well as the more outlandish ones (not getting the camel out of my head, I laughed out loud because the creativity and fun of it all is very earnest).

GAMEPLAY:
First of all the inventory being bubbles a child could easily navigate and always know what they have on hand looks so good. Everything in this game is charming to me. It plays super well and the map is simple enough that I believe a child wouldn't get lost in it as well as complex enough to sell the adventure. The items cleary tie to each use and when they don't, a hint will come up (like Ray saying what the gizmo is for). I do feel sort of goofy for wondering what some things are for, like the luminous shell I never got to use (but barely tried using it anywhere really). Game lenght is very good, everything is balance for a nice one-sit playthrough. I don't know if that'd be good for a child even if it's short, though, and maybe a reminder of the last place we knew a bottle was or something would work. I'm not saying all this as patch notes or anything, of course, just reviewing it earnestly for fun and appreciation of this 29yo game.

The Minigames:
There are some minigames and places that don't make it obvious right away they're not linked to the story, like the theater, and I believe this might be confusing for a little kid, but after a couple loops they'd probably figure it out. As for me I could watch that little frog forever he was very cute. The turtle minigame is sort of hard to quit, though, forcing the player to move the cursor the second the arrow flashes running from Freddi's continuous wanting for feeding turtles. Last there's the math minigame, always a good add for kid's games, but I'm left wondering if making it optional makes sense. Only kids that already like math are going to engage with this one or is it a relaxed enviroment to try? Either way I do think it'd work better as an obligatory puzzle since you can quit at any momment and difficulty is optional.

CONCLUSION:
It's just a fun ride. Extremely charming animation and art, obviously done with care. An endearing throwback for anyone for sure and hopefully a fun experience for today's kids as well. I'm really very happy to have found out this is on Steam (by browsing the point and click section and getting pleasantly surprised).\t\t\t\t\t
