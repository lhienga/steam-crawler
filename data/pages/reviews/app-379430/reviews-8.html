\r\n\t\t\t\t\tKingdom Come: Deliverance stands out as a gem among RPG games. Set in a big open world during the Middle Ages, it's filled with old-school secrets, quests, and authentic medieval people who actually live their life every day in-game. There's a real charm to this game — the world is beautiful, and the developers clearly have a genuine appreciation for the period and region.

The graphics are excellent, with a very detailed world where every house has an interior and every village has its own life. NPCs have their own routines — they wake up, go to work or the tavern, and then go to sleep at night.

You start as a peasant who can't even tie his shoes or read, but slowly through gameplay, you become a master in the skills you use. Every skill you learn feels useful and rewarding.

The combat can be seen in two ways, either very bad or very good, with little middle ground. On one side, it really shows how you can be absolutely awful at something until you practice it. On the other hand, the AI hitting you with scripted moves is something that I don't quite appreciate.

TL;DR; This game grew on me. At the start, I was dying constantly, and the limitation on saves was frustrating, as sometimes you can lose hours of progress if you forget to save. However, the voiceovers, plotline, and historical tidbits made me stick with it, which I'm very glad I did. An easy 8/10.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA game with amazing historical accuracy. I mean, except for the part where Henry has to get drunk to remember things (save game), but even that pushes you to embrace realistic consequences.

The graphics are amazing. One of the best I'd ever seen when it was released, on par with triple A titles from the time like Final Fantasy XV and Assassins Creed.

The studio didn't have much of a budget, but after leveraging new tech (at the time) like raytracing and scanning an actors face and motion in high definition onto the game it feels great. It's fully voice acted too, and it's well done, which is another plus.

The combat, again, is really well done, and unique compared to other games. Don't expect to main-character your way through enemies. It'll take planning, timing and/or sneaking and skill to do that, and you'll have to gain experience first.

Experience is gained Skyrim style, in the various skills, but in a way that really fits the game narrative and setting. Henry might gain skills unusually quickly, becoming a powerhouse in a couple of (in-game) months, but he still has to work for them, and combat wise, you have to learn when to take risks.

Overall the story's really fun and believable too, though I would recommend following it along instead of running around the place for 50 hours first, because the game plays out better if you haven't already seen all the new places the story takes you to, and don't feel at risk anymore in the combat.

As one last tidbit, it's really fun how the game teaches you what medieval life was actually like (at least in rural Eastern Europe).\t\t\t\t\t
