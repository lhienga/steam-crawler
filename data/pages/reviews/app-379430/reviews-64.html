\r\n\t\t\t\t\tKingdom Come Deliverance, an realistic medieval RPG that combines a flexible environment for players to have their own adventure, with a main story quest that provides some direction.


With that said, the game does have some serious flaws in teaching new players. The tutorials that it has are actually quite good in my opinion but it rushing the player too quickly into situations where neither the player, nor Henry the character is ready for.

As such, minor unavoidable spoilers, the player will put into combat situations where they are going to be frustrated for 5, 10, 30 minutes, until they find some way to either luck or cheese their way to progression.



But there is a way to avoid this altogether. Again minor spoilers, to put it simply...
Combat is 3 things.
1-Player skill.
2-Techniques and abilities - These are things that are unlocked in the game.
3-Henry's stats.


The game will force you to learn a few things but if you keep well into immersion with the main story questline, it will seem like "oh you need to go do this tomorrow morning. Ignoring this will probably have consequences."
That's not true. Some things in this game is time sensitive. Some things aren't, even if they seem like they would be.



I extremely highly recommend that people do the following.
1-When the main story wants to you to go talk to a Sir Hans Capon, don't.
2-Instead, go back to the Captain that you can train with, learn all of the remaining techniques, level up Henry's stats by sparring with the Captain for some time, and also just get comfortable with combat as a player. Player skill matters too.

That is extremely important. I jumped into the game, played around like normal, and got smashed twice in the main story quest. It was frustrating, I rage quit, uninstalled the game and left a bad review. I came back a few days later because the good points were so alluring and intriguing and gave the game another try. This time focusing more on learning the combat.

What I found was that the game didn't give me natural time within immersion to learn key important combat techniques.

Me getting smashed wasn't my lack of skill alone. Sure I'm a bit better, but learning those combat techniques and leveling Henry's stats allowed me to finish a previously frustrating fight in just a few minutes on my first try.
Not because I cheesed anything or got lucky, but because I was actually had unlocked mechanics and had better stats.

And to be clear, it's not like I maxed my stats. My first attempt, my combat stats were around 4-6. My second attempt, they were around 7-10.
The max level, I believe is 20.



Now about the game itself.
There is a main story, it provides some direction, but that's not the main focus of what makes this game good or not. At least, not with 2 savefiles with about 10 hours each.

Let's start with RPG. You play as Henry, a predefined character. You are not soldier, you are a young village man. Not going to say what happens, you will find out, but what should you expect?

The first thing is that this game is flexible and allows multiple solutions to a particular problem. Sometimes the solution is go to talk to someone. In which you might use reasoning or perhaps your own appearance to intimidate or inspire confidence.
Other times, the best solution isn't to talk at all. Though you will probably try to get an understanding of the situation.

There is acts of criminal, you can steal, trespass etc. This can have consequences, can also harm your reputation in particular areas.

Or you can find other ways, such as picking flowers to make a bit of money for something.

There is a diverse variety of skills. Herbalism, alchemy, maintenance (repairing swords and stuff), houseman, house riding, etc.
It seems to be a somewhat open world with a main story quest that provides direction. The main story seems to be considerably more important than Skyrim's main story for example but I might be wrong.


Combat. Combat is also very interesting, significantly more interesting than Skyrim in fact.
There is unarmed, two-handed weapons, shortswords, longswords, maxes, axes, shields, actual armour, bows, etc. There is a lot of options.
I'm going to focus on swords.


Combat with a sword has 6 different types of attacks.

Stab in the center, and 5 directional attacks (from up, right, left, bottom left, bottom right).

In addition, these directions are also guard directions. Attacking from the direction the enemy isn't guarding in makes it more likely that you'll hit.
With that said, simple attacks often won't hit because NPCs have a brain and don't want to die.

And that's where techniques come into play.
The game will forcibly teach you Feint-Attack and Perfect Block.

They are rather self-explanatory but the key thing is that these are learned techniques that you unlock in the game.


These techniques are very important, the game doesn't force you to learn key important techniques such as...
Riposte and combos.
That's why you should go train in game.

Riposte is an automatic counterattack that can happen when you perfect block. There is a concept of timing for Riposte but it also depends on Henry's stats - There is luck involved in whether or not Henry actually does riposte or not.

Combos are a specific series of attacks, done with a certain kind of timing. Combos are learned techniques, the game will teach you one for free and the others you unlock with perk points.
From my understanding, the purpose of a combo is, if executed properly, you will get a guaranteed hit on a specific part of the enemy's body. As such, combos can allow you target specific weak points on the enemy - Which yes, the enemy's armor does matter.

Based on my time sparring with a particular NPC, I'm also under the impression that stats influence whether or not combos actually activate. So your timing is important, you gotta do it properly, but I think there's a luck mechanic in whether or not it's executed properly. So yea, Henry's stats still does matter.



Summarized, this game seems to be a mix of a RPG with main story direction for the player, combined with an environment with tons of skills, and mechanics, and things for the player to interact with and create their own adventure.

So far for me, the realism and flavour of the game makes it more interesting and intriguing than a game like Skyrim, but that might just be the rush of something new for me.



All in all, having started 2 play throughs, about 10 hours each. There are some serious gotchas and fun-ruining experiences that the game does not handle well but if you prepare for them it's completely fine and seems to be a fun and interesting experience.\t\t\t\t\t
