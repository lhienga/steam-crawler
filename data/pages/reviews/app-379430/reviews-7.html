\r\n\t\t\t\t\tKCD is a game very confused about it wants to be. The main three ways this hurts the game are way Henry wroks, the quest design, and the technical problems.

Henry is the main character, a voiced protagonist, whose main story has him from zero to hero. Even though this is a rather generic plot in character progression focused games, the specific story, the acting, and the cinematography all combine to make his story work. It is quite clear what the main plot and Henry want, and the player has some leeway of how to complete it. All of this doesn't work as well outside of the main plot. In the open world, he provides far less guidance about what to do. Some of the side quests have him have him do good things to be good; others have him do dumb or mean things. In the open world, he provides some options as to how to resolve a situation for the player to choose (and sometimes, none of them are good), the reverse situation of the main quest. Other games also have a linear main quest and a bunch of optional, freeform, side content, but Henry really stands out for how he holds everything together during his moments, and how clearly missing he is outside of them.

KCD's quest design, the specific moment to moment experience of resolving a situation, has some big problems. There are two kinds: the linear quest and the investigation quest. Linear quests have you go do some tasks in order, advancing their plot at each step, possibly with some optional tasks for extra credit. These quests work well, even when they don't make much sense. The main plot, as a whole, follows this format. The less linear, investigation style quests, work very badly in a game so clearly made for the other format. There are certain recurring problems.

The journal is unhelpful with precise information. Often you will discover some key detail that won't be in the journal. Location directions have this problem most often: someone will give imprecise directions, and your journal will make them even worse. This is a problem because you can't just turn off your brain as if KCD were some other open world games, that is, follow quest marker, attack enemy, use dialogue option you fulfill criteria for, repeat. Some of the quests and objectives are unmarked. Some "successful" speech checks make things worse. You can, and probably will, fail objectives and quests, even main ones. KCD is ambitious with how well it expects from the player to understand what's going on, and a good journal seems almost mandatory to keep track of everything that has happened, but the builtin one doesn't live up.

Some of the quests don't make much sense. The worst offender of this is the missions related to the bandit camp. It's easy to have accidentally found it before or during the relevant ingame quest, but the game doesn't actually care that you did. you still have to find it by following the story. After that, you're given a task to sneak inside it and sabotage it; it's not possible to sabotage it stealthily, so you either take down the enemies or outrun them from an objective to another. In general, it's very easy to break quests or the game by doing things out of the expected order. It's also easy to miss out on things by not doing some unmarked objective. When you do something creative, there's usually a 50-50 chance that you're doing something good and expected, or going to break something. Even if you don't do anything creative, it's still very likely you'll break quests, though, or find that you need to do something too absurd to progress them.

KCD's quests encourage focusing on one of them at a time and avoiding taking on multiple things. Many missions are, or at least seem to be, timed. If you want to avoid failing them because you ran out of time, the clearest way is to avoid talking to people who probably have quests for you. There are minor objectives in the game, which are considered parts of its bigger quests. When you complete their associated quests, these minor things will be considered failed or missed out on, even if there is no logical reason why you can't do them afterwards. Playing to account for these things results in a lot of traveling, to be thorough in a quest before taking on the next one, and in making long pauses in the main story because who knows when the next non-timed main objective will be.

There are still a few big technical issues. The game crashed a few times on me, including once when trying to save after playing for quite a bit. There is some geometry in the world it's easy to get stuck on/inside of. I had a bizarre bug where all the generic guard dogs were missing, but they came back after reloading the game. One of the final cutscenes had the wrong subtitles, which is not cool when playing in Czech. Since subtitles are usually written above the speaker's head, a few times they were too far away to show up or distracted from something else happening at the same time. The loading screens often let the game actually start running before fully loading it; usually this is not important, but it does mean that the very first cutscene started playing its subtitles before actually getting video or audio.

I didn't say anything about the gameplay systems proper. You don't really play KCD for them, you're playing it for the story and characters and quests. There's relatively little to say of the gameplay. Everything works well enough, you're expected to do a little of everything and the game generally explains how to well enough. The only big thing to note is that the combat is simple, but explained very badly in the game, which caused a lot of people to think it's bad. It's ok, but could be better with the relevant mod, as are the other systems.

KCD tries to find a middle grounds between the linear story and the open world, between character logic and player logic, between common sense and heroic story. A few times it works, and it works very well, but mostly it alternates between each extreme. In the moment it's hard to tell what the game wants, and after the moment, I still don't feel like I understood what it wanted to be.\t\t\t\t\t
