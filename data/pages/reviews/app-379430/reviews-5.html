\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe combat system is the make-or-break of this game. Sure it's got lots of attention to recreating the medieval world, well written characters, and good voice acting, but if you don't like the uncommon dueling-based combat system then it will be one of the most frustrating things you ever subject yourself to. I previously played up until about a third of the way through the campaign before dropping it for a year, this time I started a new campaign and then gave up shortly after the tutorial.

At the beginning you move and strike like molasses in January, with the targeting feeling both sticky and slippery at the same time. Your enemies move like molasses in JULY, allowing them to interrupt your attacks with their own, while you're incapable of doing the same thing. So until you train up the only thing that works is waiting for them to attack, time a perfect block, and counter attack. Not because you're guaranteed a hit - they still block your ripostes at least half the time - but it means they only interrupt-strike you occasionally.

Of course the more you fight the better your character gets. He strikes faster, perfect block windows are more forgiving, and he learns moves that bypass enemy defences. This represents Henry going from a peasant to a trained fighter, but in practical terms it means that the later you get in the game, the less skill is demanded of the player. The game helpfully provides you with a trainer to practise fight with as often as you want to grind both Henry's stats and your grasp system... After several mandatory fights, one of which can be against two armoured soldiers if your stealth's bad. I desperately wanted to just spend an hour working on my technique, since the directional strike, dueling-focused system isn't something that I've come across before, but instead I'm getting spitroasted by a couple Cumans who have better equipment, block 90% of my attacks, and respond to my feints by either perfectly adjusting their block direction or just interrupt with their faster attacks.

I was in the middle of winning the above battle after a half-dozen attempts, when I realised just how much I hated the time I was having. I remembered what the next parts of the game were, and I knew I was going to hate those fights nearly as much. I could grind Henry up to be a combat god after the mission, but it would just reduce the time I spent *losing* the combat system I hated engaging with.

If you like this kind of combat system then KC:D is probably one of the best games you could play. I loved everything about this game EXCEPT that combat system, but that alone was so immensely frustrating that it made me hate it overall.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI guess you either have a love or hate relationship with this game.

This is mostly due to the fact, that the mechanics are very rough around the edges.
Many players will dislike the combat system and have valid reasons to do so. It requires a steep learning curve at first and is disappointing in combat versus multiple enemies, not because its realistic, just because the lock on mechanics are really bad.
However, when you give it some time to grasp, you will eventually love the combat. The system is very unique and way better than most button mashing systems you see in this game genre. Knowing about the advantages and disadvantages of certain weapon types will be rewarded greatly. Just try to use some piercing weapon against a plate armor, and then try to use some hammer instead. You will quickly understand the differences ;)

Having a somewhat realistic take on bows is also fun. It doesnt really require a crosshair.
One has to understand first, that you are just the son of a blacksmith. You dont know nothing about fighting,
archery or any profession. Naturally you wont even hold your bow correctly without reaching a specific skill-level first.

Not being able to quick-save constantly actually forces you to make decisions up front, instead of just enforcing the victory by stealing, failing, loading, repeat. Many will dislike this at first as well, because its just not something you are very used to in modern games.
But if you really think about it, is it a good gameplay experience if your decisions have no consequences at all, because you just save-scum through the whole experience?
This is debatable and depends on the type of player, and the system used here is most likely not the definitive answer, but it manages to make your actions matter. It will hurt you sometimes, but thats a good thing in the end. At least in my humble opinion.

The game design reminds me of Gothic, and I really like that. Its because you are actually just a normal person, knowing ♥♥♥♥ about combat or any profession. Attacking a guard on level 1 is basically suicide, and this reminds me of my first adventure into the forest of Gothic.

Since Oblivion I really liked the idea of gaining proficiency in your skills by actually "using" them. Which means fighting will increase your fighting skills, actually gathering some herbs will increase you herbalist skill, and so on. This creates some deeper level of roleplay immersion.


The world building is great. The story is captivating. There is a lot to like.
When you open yourself up to learn some of the mechanics, and ignore some of the rough edges of the game mechanics, you will eventually have a really great time with this game.\t\t\t\t\t
