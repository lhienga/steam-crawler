\r\n\t\t\t\t\tKingdom Come: Deliverance (KC:D) is a 2018 role-playing game developed by Warhorse Studios, a video game developer based in Prague, Czech Republic. The game is co-published by Deep Silver and released on PC and eighth generation consoles. The game takes place in Bohemia in the year 1403, during the late Middle Ages. The player plays Henry, a Blacksmith apprentice from the village of Skalitz, pursues justice for his murdered family while a conflict brews in Bohemia. The game takes an uncompromising approach on historical accuracy and gameplay realism. Does the game get your intrigue?

KC:D is one of the most uncompromising, unforgiving, but most rewarding experiences I have ever played. The level of detail down to the micro level is astonishing. Some examples are keeping clothes from being too dirty or worn out to prevent some lower stats like charisma and speech from literally trying to increase literacy skills by learning how to read. The core gameplay is first-person and very slow pace, but the immersion level in its environment is first class and overwhelming dopamine sensation. The soundtracks composed by Jan Valta and Adam Sporka are noteworthy and wonderful to listen to. The composers have done an outstanding job.

In some ways, KC:D detracts from other contemporary games by testing a player’s patience and tolerance. It’ll turn people off, but if the player keeps going, it’ll be worth it. A role-playing experience at its purest form because the main protagonist is literally a blacksmith apprentice who does not have any specializations at all like being a guard/knight or master huntsman with good stealth. The player literally must earn those skills and will be useful on the adventures ahead.

KC:D is also infamous for its determined stance on not simplifying gameplay mechanics to its overall vision. As mentioned earlier, KC:D might detract some players. In the prologue, the tutorial is confusing and overwhelming because the tutorials are mostly text-based. Admittedly, I didn’t pass the prologue for a while until I pushed myself on continuing. The combat gameplay is very realistic, and I do like it, but the auto-targeting is a turnoff because the game will remain on lock towards an enemy unless the player hits the sprint button to run away from overwhelming odds.

The saving mechanic is controversial because the player must make Saviour Schnapps at an Alchemist’s bench to manually save the game. Making the schnapps isn’t hard but felt detrimental. An inconvenience at best. A burden at worst. The voice cast in the game are great and outstanding, but some feel miscast by some characters’ age. An example would be a major character who is an old gentleman, but the voice sounds young. Some bugs persist, but those are minor and don’t affect the overall experience.

KC:D is one of those games where many people would play the introduction and didn’t expand it much further because of the overwhelming tutorials and its lack of clearer instructions. It’s a fair assessment. After the hurdles in the prologue, KC:D is one of the most majestic, magical, and profound experiences I ever played on PC.

The protagonist’s evolution in the story is great and interactions among characters are outstanding. The soundtrack reminds me of Elder Scrolls IV: Oblivion back in the Xbox 360 era. I still listen to KC:D soundtrack on my treks and travels to a national park. The most perfect mixture. The game aged gracefully and gained a lot of respect as time passed. I purchased the game on the Lunar New Year 2019 sale and took a few long sessions to fully appreciate the game. I am glad I had the pleasure of playing this great game. KC:D has my strong recommendation but be aware of the heavy tutorials and micro realism. Pass through these hurdles and you’ll get an experience of which very few games achieved.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAn excellent game with good attention to detail, and one which rewards slow paced play and immersing yourself in medieval Bohemia. The game holds your hand less than most, there are multiple ways to solve problems and the game lets you figure them out for yourself.

Combat is gripping and engaging, and more realistic than I think any other game I've played. You have to pace yourself and if you are overly aggressive you will probably get cut to pieces, but a little bit of thought, patience and quick reflexes will see you victorious. A lot of people complain that it's too hard to fight multiple enemies, but it really really should be that hard, when was the last time you or someone you know got into a bar fight against multiple opponents and didn't lose badly? Fighting multiple enemies is a worthy challenge that leaves you feeling good when you eventually win.

Some people have a problem with the save system which deliberately makes save scumming extremely difficult, but I'm fine with it. In any case, even if you mess something up there is usually another cause of action you can take to rescue the situation.

A few words of advice... You NEED to train with your weapons in order to become even halfway competent in a fight, if you think you can just pick up a sword and fight with it then you are severely mistaken and the game will make mincemeat out of you. If on the other hand you train with Captain Bernard, attain a basic level of competency with all weapon types and learn the advanced combat feats which allow you to perform perfect blocks and master strikes, then things will get a whole lot easier.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI played this game fairly hard when it first came out, and for some reason drifted away, didn't really play it for literally a couple of years. Have recently come back to it and given it an honest go on the Hardcore setting. I love it. It's a super realistic look into late middle age life. You are noone, with nothing. Unlike most games, you're not some superhero that with minimal effort just demolishes everthing. Even late game, you have to think carefully, move carefully, choose your battles wisely. You are just Henry, the son of a blacksmith, after all. I've really been enjoying the challenge of the hardest difficulty setting, with no map markers, no nothing. It feels super realistic, you have to navigate by landmarks and such.

Getting drunk with a priest and walking the fields at dawn pretending I was a sheep, walking Theresa along the river... I felt actually felt pangs of longing, wishing I'd lived in a simpler time. It's just so well done. The combat is extremely difficult (remember, you're just the son of some blacksmith, no hero at all) and at times, unfair. You must learn to read the terrain around you and use it to your advantage. After all, a fair fight is a losing fight. The game does NOT hold your hand at all on the hardest difficulty. I'm glad I came back and gave it another go. Extremely well done game, one of the best devs in the business. My only gripe would be the "lock onto a single opponent" fighting thing, It can be very unfair in a 1 vs multiple engagement and quite clunky to switch between opponents. If it had a free fighting style like Mount and Blade, it would be one of the best if not THE best realistic melee combat simulations ever made. Just my opinion.\t\t\t\t\t
