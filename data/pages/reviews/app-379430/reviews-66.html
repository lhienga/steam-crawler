\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI like the game but can not recommend it and would not recommend it to anyone other than dyed in the wool R.P.G. fanatics. This game suffers mostly because of it's nonintuitive combat mechanics, they are truly horrible. The save system has a lot to be desired and switching the "use button" between "E" and "X" is a solution to a problem that didn't exist in the first place. Also, using a voice actor who sounds like he's a teenager sounds really weird when coming out of a characters mouth who looks to be in his 60's or 70's. That took me right out of the story. That being said, in my opinion all other aspects of the game are great. It's very refreshing not having to battle mythical or made up monsters and zombies for a change! There are too many zombies in games now. I feel like the coolness factor with zombies in video games has been over for almost a decade now. Props to War Horse for thinking a little outside the box!

I write this about 2 days after the above review. This game is so full of bugs I can not complete the game. Some characters, the bailiff being one, are in areas that can't be reached. Or if the script for any particular mission isn't followed the way the developer expected you to, the mission glitches.
For example; if you choose not to get drunk with the priest, you're left with an open mission and no way to get the information you need to complete it. And there doesn't appear to be a intuitive way to gather the needed information to go further, and no way to cancel the open mission. That's pretty lame; and its a shame because this completely ruins the game. I don't want to invest time in something I can't fully utilise or complete. I will never purchase a game from Warhorse again as it seems they can't be trusted to fix clearly documented bugs and issues with the game. There are rumours of a sequel to the game. If so, there is no way I'm going to spend money on a sequel to what will probable be yet another game full of glitches and bugs, with either poor or no maintenance. Sorry Warhorse, but right now I feel the name of your studio should be changed to Lazy Donkey.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA diamond in the rough. That’s the title of a YT review for this game I watched recently and I wholeheartedly agree.

I’ll start with this if you’re considering buying this game: give it a chance. Your character is Henry, a blacksmith’s son. You basically start out like a toddler, skill-wise. You can barely fight, can’t do much at all. Half-way through the game you become a one-on-one killing machine. There’s a learning curve about combat (and other things) in this game but once you’ve gotten the skills you need and the hang of it, everything gets easier.

This is a first-person medieval rpg that makes you work for everything. Your skills at the beginning of the game are practically non-existent (they increase in time, as you practice), you have to craft potions to even save the game (or buy them) although you can save the game during rest and it will auto-save at key moments during quests, most food deteriorates in time in your inventory if you don’t consume it (past a certain point you get food poisoning if you eat the “bad” food), you constantly have to wash yourself throughout the day to keep appearances, you have to fix all of your equipment (armor, weapons, clothing), you don’t have any kind of crosshair while aiming with your bow, you have to sleep (otherwise your “energy” goes low and you can’t focus), you have to eat (otherwise you get debuffs and eventually start losing health). Bad behavior (stealing, trespassing, attacking random people) has consequences. Life was rough in the 1400s. With all that comes a lot of freedom and immersion though. Every single thing mentioned above is not something I ever found to be tedious or frustrating; it only helped with immersion.

Combat is very unique in this game. In my personal opinion, the developers went for something and they mostly got it right. The feeling you get after winning a one-on-one fight with someone is amazing (it gets easier about half-way into the game; in my opinion there should have been some more work done to balance things out later in the game because once you master it, it does become a bit easy). Combat against more than one enemy is something else entirely. You can have your character maxed out close to god-tier and still be able to fumble and lose to a group of enemies if you’re not careful, kinda just like in real life (another aspect of this game that I absolutely love). While you focus on one enemy, others will mercilessly hit you from every angle until you’re overwhelmed and dead. You have to approach combat against more than one enemy strategically and you can do so in many ways: you can sneak where they sleep at night and either poison their food or kill some of them (or all if you’re good) in the their sleep, you can attack them on your horse, make them come out in an open area and pick them off one by one, you can shoot arrows at them (you can also dip your arrows in poison that you’re able to craft or buy) then run away as to get out of melee range, shoot again, repeat until there is one or two enemies left which you can handle in melee combat or any combination of all of these tactics (yes, enemies that have been killed in their sleep at night will be dead by morning if you do decide upon a direct attack then). There are some aspects that are janky - particularly the targeting system during combat (you basically auto-lock on an enemy and have to press a key/button to unlock or turn and move away) but honestly that is still something you learn how to handle and it was not something that made me dislike combat in this game at all.

When doing side-quests (mostly) I did find some bugs where I had to revert to an older save because I would get soft-locked from what was supposed to be the intended outcome of the quest. Sadly, these bugs still exist even 5 years after the game came out. However, most of this is still manageable as long as you save before starting a quest and read up on how to get around these issues.

Even with all the negatives, what this game does get right it does so in a fantastic, brilliant and unique way and overall manages to get you to live something that is as close as possible to a knight’s tale. I played this game in December 2023 and I put in around 100 hours and enjoyed every single moment to a degree I never thought possible.

The story is brilliant and it is indeed a very story-driven game. Everything is voice-acted. Even interactions with commoners on the streets of a village. The voice actor for Henry - Tom McKay - delivers every single line perfectly, as if this character was meant for him (kinda reminds me of Roger Clark/Arthur in RDR2). Without going too much into it (it’s extremely rewarding to experience the whole story without any spoilers) you are basically thrown on a path to knighthood. All major events and characters are based on real events. The personal story of Henry (and some of his allies) is fiction.

This is a true gem of a video game. I haven’t been this immersed in a game since I played some of my old favorite classics way back when they first came out (Eg. Fallout 1,2, Morrowind, Skyrim, Mass Effect, Red Dead Redemption 2, God of War). It will forever have a special place in my heart.

(I’ve completed the game on GOG but decided to purchase it on Steam as well to support the developers)\t\t\t\t\t
