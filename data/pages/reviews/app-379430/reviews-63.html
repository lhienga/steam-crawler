\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt takes a bit to get into the gameplay mechanics, but it's a great game.
The world feels very realistic, and with the limited save system it makes you think about the consequences of what you do more than in other games: for example, if you're caught by some bandits in a forest, you're most likely going to die unless you're very high level, and you generally can't save scum your way out of the situation. This applies to the story and quests too.

Tech wise the game looks and runs pretty good. I nearly managed to lock myself out of the main quest by doing things in an unexpected order due to a bug, but this seems like an edge case situation. Overall it's not too buggy. It also runs on linux/steam deck with no issues.

And if you're at least somewhat familiar with central european culture, you'll find a lot of it in the game with the mostly-historically-accurate bohemian setting.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a one-of-a-kind medieval RPG which is designed for those gamers seeking a hardcore and immersive experience. Within the first dozen or so hours of gameplay, most new players will get very frustrated, and may even come close to dropping the game all together. If you can persevere through this initial learning period, pay attention to tutorials and do a little extra homework on youtube guides, I promise the middle and late game experience will more than make up for the early-game learning curve.

BUY THIS GAME IF:
1) You seek realism to the nth degree. Limited fast travel and save options (even for the normal game mode). Realistic combat mechanics with directional strikes/blocking and combinations. Music, terrain, characters that immerse you into the high medieval era. Period-accurate clothing, weapons, town architecture and societal layers. A morality system that is more grey rather than black and white. Having to repair armor, weapons and wash clothes after any amount of travel or combat. Having to figure out quest actions with very little hand-holding. A very accurate in-game codex to explain some of the characters and environmental background for this game.
2) You want medieval realism rather than high fantasy; trade dragons and monsters for a whole bunch of characters, most of whom are neither good nor bad, but fall somewhere in the gray of the morality spectrum (and many of whom are based on real-life figures).
3) Beautiful, but also eerrily empty, country landscape - the forests and farm country are some of the most scenic terrain that I've seen in any game. But because it is portraying real-life Bohemia, you will find much of of the game map empty, rather than full of NPC's and interactions. There are enemies which you can come across and animals to hunt, just don't expect the same density of NPC interaction that you might otherwise find in a Bethesda game.
4) You want a combat system that prioritizes individual, small-scale combat rather than large-scale NPC warfare. The game provides an ultra-realistic directional combat system, much more detailed than what Mount and Blade and similar titles offer. With that said, you are unlikely to find yourself able to take on more than 2-3 enemies at a time due to a) historical realism and b) the combat system getting a bit janky with higher numbers of NPC's.
5) Beautiful story-telling and character development. The main character (Henry) starts off as a nobody, lacking any special skills or talents. You literally have to build this guy from the ground-up to be able to combat aggressors, persuade characters, earn offices and titles and even rebuild a town. You really do feel like you've earned everything that you have by the mid and late game. There are also some amazing interactions and relationship building with some of the key NPC's. The standard townsfolk offer decent dialogue as well, but are definitely not the main focus.
6) A game that allows for some sandbox gameplay but is ultimately focused on the main storyline. You can wander off to free-roam and do side quests, but the player is highly incentivized to follow the main questline to earn money, titles and gain experience. The one caveat to this: take some time after the prologue to focus on basic combat training (with Capt. Bernard) before progressing with the main questline...the extra combat skills gained (both in-game and real) will make your life much easier.
7) A game that forces the player to really learn the mechanics. You will actually need to understand how to fight, hunt, craft, steal, horse-ride, run, sneak and you will need to make good decisions on how and where to employ those applicable skills. Such decision-making heavily influences the game's progress and quest outcome. KCD is not a game where you can simply grind your character into some unstoppable combat or stealth god, though certain mechanics (like master strikes) do create some unbalance by the late game.

DON'T BUY THIS GAME IF:
1) You want a level of polish and optimization that is associated with a Triple A game. The game in it's current state does run relatively well and looks amazing at the higher graphics settings, but there are certainly missions and some NPC interactions where the lack of polish and optimization shows at times.
2) Want a casual gameplay experience. KCD IS NOT A CASUAL GAME, EVEN WITH THE NORMAL MODE. The quests can be challenging at times, and you will need to pay attention to the narrative details in order to complete them successfully or achieve a desired outcome. There is very little hand-holding in this game. The Tutorials for this game are not very well-done (my one main gripe with the developers), and you will often resort to youtube guides to truly master the combat, crafting and overall leveling mechanics. Your reputation with NPC's (guards, townsfolk, merchants) will suffer if you conduct certain actions.
3) You get frustrated easily. You may play through some entire missions only to realize that you got an undesirable outcome or lost a certain NPC. You may struggle to understand how to even complete certain missions on the first playthrough. You will find the odds stacked against you in the form of ambushes or random enemy encounters. Even after you have gained skill levels, it is very difficult to fight more than 2-3 enemy at a time, and you will learn that death is not far off if you become careless or reckless. All of this is to say: this game is neither easy nor forgiving.\t\t\t\t\t
