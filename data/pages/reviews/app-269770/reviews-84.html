\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Secrets of Grindea Beta displays an inviting pixel-art style game, but does so much more than that; the gameplay mechanics are precise and satisfying, the layout aspects are convenient, the arcade mode adds a nice roguelike experience and the main story (what we have by now) flows naturally (and you can play with friends throughout all of it)

--- Game Stats ---

-> Story: 7/10 (simple but works)
-> Gameplay: 9.5/10 (this game's highlight)
-> Visuals: 8.5/10 (vivid and expressive, although the characters dialogue images look generic)
-> Audio: 8.5/10 (The rpg fashioned themes always fit well on the context, although not exactly memorable)
-> Overall Experience: 9/10 (The difficulty pleases both the casual and the hardcore gamer)

Keep in mind that this review applies for the current Beta state, but the developers are notably willing to finish their game in the best way possible, hence the work to keep the testers informed of updates and so on; definitely worth a try.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA dear friend of mine told me to pick up this game, and I was happier for doing so. Yes, the game's development moves at a snail's pace - it's three guys going out against the world to make a fantastic retro RPG with a memorably comedic story that plays on common tropes, and a challenging quasi-bullet hell combat system that calls to mind some of the more challenging Dark Souls boss fights. And they're doing a great job at it. Whether you play it alone, or with up to 3 other players, you're in for a challenging ride that is shameless about its elements of grinding for cool loot and experience for its even cooler upgrades. While it has a range of accomodating RPG/Adventurer play styles, I would not reccomend this game for people that hate bullet hell. You'll end up being so frustrated by your own clumsiness that you'll want to put it away for good.

At least, that's where I am with it. But even though I'm upset with that, it's of my own doing and should have no bearing on the painstaking amount of work and effort the developers have done to make their own game. Despite my present state of giving up on this game because of difficulty with this one boss, I would seriously consider coming back to this game if I'm still alive by the time it goes to 1.0. Considering the amount of content that's already there, and Steam's generous refund policy, two hours will be more than enough time to make an informed decision on whether you'll buy into what the Pixel Ferrets offer - a truly original ARPG that, while isn't quite finished yet, has already lapped most early access games in terms of challenging yet engaging story and combat.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tUltimately what happens when Secret of Mana collides with modern gamecraft. It's an Early Access game so be wary of that, but so far it seems to hold its content's worth and enjoys a cute and charming narrative with characters who, while token, aren't just wallflowers. The game takes its world quite seriously but the world itself is not terribly serious, which had me dragged up in the game's silly pace. Things like visiting Santa Claus and battling mobile pumpkins hail to the days of SNES. Combat is basically one button to attack, and skills set on various keys.

The skill system is where the game shows off its feathers, and it should be spoken for that this is probably where the game both A) needs the most improvement! and B) enjoys the most distinction. I personally play a character who uses a one-handed sword, and some buffing magic with an ice spell so that I can support people in combat. And this is just one of many potential builds you could make; a two-handed swordsman with summoned minions, a full-on mage who masters all elements. A spell-blade who wields sword and magic. Besides active skills, there are three pages of fifteen passive bonuses that include effects as mundane as bonus attack, up to adding a portion of your magic attack to your basic attack, or adding the ability to chill or burn enemies on hit. Needless to say, you can go in a lot of ways.

Exploratory or perfectionist sorts fear not, for the skill system is handled at anytime through your menu and resets are just a small gold expenditure away.

All-in-all, it's going to be interesting to see where this goes. I could recommend this now as it has great multiplayer value. It's not what I'd call plush with content like some other games, but it's not lacking and what it does have keeps one busy and keeps one on the right track. The game doesn't feel empty or devoid of content like some early access titles can.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA fun solo experience that is plagued with a myriad of various small bugs that somewhat spoil the game’s overall feeling. Not a bad game by any means, but I would hold off on a purchase until the full version is out.

The multiplayer is an entirely different experience, with lagging players and teleporting bosses and weird cutscenes. It was, again, a fun experience, but be ready to encounter rough development patches.

I wasn’t too fond of the leveling system, where you choose from an assortment of weapon powers and spells and passive buffs. You can refund your points easily enough, in case you run across a build you want to try or something, but if that is the case, it should just be some sort of badge system that you allocate your points into and can freely reassign.

The quest system was...interesting. I’m not really sure if I care enough about quests in this game besides the main one? The rewards for doing them are nice, but it almost always boils down to “go here and kill so many of these” and I understand that this is standard fare for these types of games, especially a game literally having “Grind” in its name, so I won’t complain about it.

You are also encouraged to find these cards that drop off of monsters after killing loads of them, and the cards have different buffs on them but they usually raise your effectiveness against that particular mob. The mobs are your typical grouping of monsters, slimes and bears and such. They get more interesting as the game goes on, but in the end, you are just fighting the same types of attacks and a.o.e.s and you get items you can craft with and such.

In all, it’s a pretty average game that has some neat bosses that have desync issues in multiplayer, with a power-up system from old RPGs that really needs a retuning, and an overall charming feel to the soundtracks and overworld.
