\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a great game, however if you're not into grinding for XP and loot well... This isn't the game for you. If you didn't realize that by reading the name, then at least there's a character in the game named Luke that you'll probably have a fair amount in common with. Anyway, for those of you that enjoyed games like the early Mana Series (Secret of Mana, Legend of Mana, Seiken Densetsu III/Trials of Mana, Adventure of Mana) you'll probably enjoy this game. Arcade mode grew on me a lot, it reminded me of the enjoyable chaos of older arcade games like Golden Axe and for the record is a lot more fun to play with a friend than story in my opinion.

Taking into account that this game is Early Access at the time of this comment, I will say there are some mechanics I'd really like to see improved:

1. Card Drop Mechanic: I think the number one thing is that even though I know grinding is a core concept of the game I think it really needs some sort of a cap. It's one thing to kill 100 slimes on the off chance of a card drop, it's quite an annoying other dealing with low chance drops on beefy boy mobs like the Solem. I think there should either be an increasing chance of getting a card per kill, or a guaranteed number of kills (possibly specific to each monster) that will get a you a card. Why? Because at least half the time I've played this game so far (about 34 hours) roughly 20 of which can be attributed to farming the Solem Card, Echo of Madness Card, and the Ancient Statue card alone. Honestly, I'm just jazzed that I got lucky on the plant guys and didn't spend hours on them too. But real talk, my brother and I farmed a Pecko for a whole hour non-stop and neither of us got the card in multiplayer story yet. This is the reality of having no cap and relying 100% on RNG.

2. Melee Skill Variety: In my opinion, there are 4 melee skills worth using. 2 (arguably 3) in one-handed and only 2 in two-handed. For example I want to like berserk, but it's just not a very realistic option because there's no stun mechanic so when you go to wail on enemies you end up taking a lot of damage and if you're not wailing on enemies your EP quickly becomes non-existent. Anyway, my point here is that magic has a lot more usable variety than the melee skills offer in the 12 spells (though the end-game also comes down to two spells generally). You may ask, "dude, why don't you specify the skills you're talking about". I'll tell you, because I don't want the stuff I like nerfed to the point where I'm forced to use mechanics I'm not interested in. My ask here is that more scrutiny is put into making a set of skills that have viable mechanics to use at end-game to make for different play-styles. Example, if you were to cause the 2H jump attack to suck in enemies in a nearby radius and stack them for a big hit that'd be cooler than diving in an getting caesared by a bunch of pissed off mobs and would make it notably different than using a basic attack which currently is a much better choice (because of combo starter, potential ice/slow or fire burning + +dmg% on status combo).

3. Support Skills and Non-existent Healing: I think the developer could come up with some clever ways to add healing mechanics rather than leave them out in the interest of making the game harder. I really wanted to play a support class because I generally end up taking on the role of a tank or DPS as a necessity in most multiplayer RPGs even though it's not my preferred approach for multiplayer. I'd prefer to make other people's play more fun my assisting, but I really don't feel the support skills and the absolute lack of party healing ability make it so you can take this path. It'd be really nice to see an option for this in the future.

This is all I have for now, though I may come back to update this information as things change. I hope if you get this game, you get it for the right reasons and that you enjoy playing it as much as I did so far. We're still in beta and the game cuts off at the tower, but I'm really looking forward to the updates/next portion of the story myself.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBearing in mind that this game is still incomplete, this is one of the more fun games I've played in recent memory. Secrets of Grindea does have its faults - the bosses being a bit too spongy being a more minor problem - but it what it does do right it does well. The story is interesting, and the characters that drive it are engaging. Even better are the animations, which are smooth as anything and are enhanced by the cutesy artstyle.

Mechanically, it's also pretty good. You have the option of one-handed and two-handed weapons and a shield, the former two also coming in magic flavours (giving you Magic Attack and having a beam at the end of Standard attacks). It also has a classless system: Levelling up makes you stronger, but the Experience Points (For active skills) and Talent Orbs (for Passives) you get can go towards any kind of build you want. Want to be a teleporting brawler? Maybe a Pyromancer with a little snow buddy or thundercloud following you? And if you don't like it, you can just reset the individual talents, either one point at a time, or all at once for a bit of money. Brilliant if you think of a better strategy for fighting a particular boss, and have a bit of extra cash. Or maybe that passive wasn't as good as you thought it might be?

The game does have it's problems as I said. The bosses tend to be a bit spongy, which can drag fights out, and the difficulty isn't quite as smooth as one would think or hope. Perhaps one of the most egregious problems is starting the museum quest; there is no indication you can get into the upper part of the museum. Contrariwise, it seems you have to wait for the Museum owner to appear. I'll give you a hint; there's a side entrance that doesn't have a connecting path indicating there's an open door. There's also the next major area after the place the museum resides, but that's lategame, so I'll just admit my love/hate relationship with the last dungeon and leave it at that.

All that said, it's a solid game at its core. It pokes some fun at the more irritable or bizarre quirks other games have, has a colourful main cast, and a WHOLE lot of references to virtually every other media. It's fun, a little quirky, and a joy to play.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tVery strongly recommended! This game is just what it looks like in the trailer, but better.


Positives:
----------

The art is ridiculously good. Not only is it pretty to look at, but it checks all of the boxes for by-the-books pixel art: it's bright, high saturation, and easy to parse. UI elements are clear, and in-game sprites are basically a case study on how to do pixel art. The animations are also excellent, making liberal use of stylized distortion frames to make the world feel alive.

The story goes from what appears to be a parody of RPGs to something much more. There are even a couple of plot devices used that are normally total mine fields which destroy most movies, books, and video games that try employing them, but they were well-navigated by the Grindea team. I'm impressed by the writing and execution.

The gameplay is very fun. Your active abilities are tightly tied to your character build, but diversify significantly as the game progresses. Even passive abilities change how certain mechanics behave, instead of simply giving the trite "+2 damage" (although those are available as well if you prefer to be a less active player!). Controls are responsive. Some abilities allow for rapid reaction and animation interrupts, whereas other are more committal. There is a good variety of build options, and some very creative, unusual builds work well too.

There is a great mix of puzzles and other to-do's outside of combat. Between the game load screen and the world map, it's is pretty transparent for completionists to understand how much they have and haven't found. Some optional puzzles are easy and others may leave the player scratching their head for a bit, but it's all fair difficulty in my opinion. I've 100%'d the game every content patch, and it's been satisfying each time to dig in and find everything.

There are Normal and Hard difficulties, both of which I found to be appropriate (although I spent 95% of my playtime in Hard mode -- for me personally, it represents the "real" game). Normal mode lets you casually play through and see how enemies work, but Hard mode forces you to understand the mechanics. Some of the newer abilities in more recent patches make certain parts of the first few boss fights much easier (teleport, in particular), which is a little unfortunate, but I think it's well worth it for the gains those abilities bring to the game.

There are a couple of other modes outside of the main story. At this point, they've matured well as the developers have listened to a lot of community feedback. I found the story mode to be most engaging, so it's where I spent most of my time, but check out other reviews for info on the other modes.

Grindea is a great tribute to the SNES-era Adventure/RPG classics, but it also brings some of its own flavor as well.


Negatives:
------------

Multiplayer is almost awesome, but suffers badly from desyncing. For everyone except the host, enemies are oftentimes not where they appear to be, you take damage from something that isn't on your screen, and lag is constant.\t\t\t\t\t
