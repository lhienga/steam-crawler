\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game seems to have its attention split between two very different genres, and it's almost hard to tell which is the main attraction. On one hand, there is a very compelling and beautiful campaign storyline, with interesting characters, fantastic puzzles, and a terrific plotenough to get me choked up at one pointeven despite the fact that it is mostly farcical parody.

On the other hand, there is an 'arcade' mode that is a classic rogue-like, complete with permadeath resets, procedurally generated levels, and progress in the form of a starting town that gets built up over time to give perks and bonuses to the player. It is also well-done, mainly because it uses the same enemies, combat, and level system as the campaign.

This isn't necessarily a criticism: both halves of the game are well-made and compelling to play. Either half could have been its own standalone game, or one could have even been a spin-off title. There's enough content in both of them for that. Instead, the developers have basically made two games within one title. The criticism is that the game is in early access, and it would probably be a better use of the developer's time to finish one half of the game, release it, and then add the other half in a later update.

But like I said at the beginning, it's hard to tell which half of the game is more important to the developers. The reason I say that is because of the way the achievements are laid out.

There are eight achievements for the campaign, and most of them revolve around a fishing minigame. It's strange that, for a game all about grinding monsters for loot (to the point of making the entire plot of the campaign about the collection of the rarest monster drops), there are little to no achievements related to actually collecting said loot or rare drops. There isn't even an achievement for beating the campaign, which stands out as particularly strange.

However, there are twenty achievements for the arcade mode, which basically involve perfect runs and 100% completion: exactly what you would expect. It would seem the arcade mode (the mode you would expect to be the smaller minigame) is more of the main attraction than the campaign mode. That's also not a criticism: similar things exist in say, multiplayer first-person shooters, where the draw is not the campaign, but the multiplayer combat.

All of this may just be a side-effect of an early access title, and the campaign may be the thing that needs to be better fleshed out before release, but I think it's important to explain this dichotomy in detail for a review because this game may appear to be a story-driven RPG about killing monsters and collecting loot, with a rogue-like minigame. In reality, it's probably more accurate to say the game is a rogue-like about navigating procedurally-generated maps to get further with each attempt, with an RPG campaign minigame.

But I still love it, and still highly recommend it. It's a great game, and I'm excited for its future. I hope this review quickly becomes obsolete with future updates from the developers. In particular, I would like to see them *really* expand on the campaign's achievements: achievements for completing main quests, side quests, 'starring' each area of the map, completing each temple, finding every monster drop, crafting every item, and filling out the monster album.\t\t\t\t\t
