\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR - Ryza is really good and pushes a lot of technical development in their game engine, needs some polish but is otherwise a big step up from older Atelier games.

The combat? I really like the combat. Use basic attacks to generate AP, use AP to use skills and generate CC, use CC to use items. After using up items, instead of waiting for the long cooldowns just switch party members and continue comboing harder. So addicting. Fighting the weasel roast for fun is actually enjoyable. Wish that we could crank the difficulty up on the first run of the game, I want to use my OP gear and items on the enemies instead of oneshotting. Massive step up from the active turn combat system from Ryza 1, it's arguably an entire new system and plays faster and more fluidly.

The crafting? I played Sophie 2 before Ryza 2, so going to Ryza crafting is really jarring. In Sophie 2 you spend sooo much time synthesizing and minmaxing the grid, maximizing items feels great. You can't just get all the best gathering items and expect endgame items, you work for it and it's addicting. In Ryza you just want to finish crafting and autobuild everything to get to the more fun part of the game, which is exploring and fighting. Either that, or you keep delaying that one crafting item because you just want one or two more good gathering items, only to completely powercreep it with a better item (that you also don't bother crafting because difficulty is too easy). I basically only had three main crafting periods: at the beginning of the game, at the midgame after dungeon 3, and then once more for the final dungeon 5. I guess it isn't too surprising: Ryza doesn't start off as an alchemist, but as an adventurer. This carries over to the core of the game, where you do the alchemy to progress what you REALLY want, which is the exploration and combat.

The story? It's not really important, I thought the main story was fairly cohesive but not too surprising. Arguably intentionally done so that players could just drop the story at any time (handheld game). However, the slice-of-life and the characters are what really stand out. A bit tropey, but the characters are quite likeable. The designs really stand out, facial expressions and eyebrows are excellent and expressive while the character movements are quite fluid. Suffers from the unfortunate JRPG symptom of characters awkwardly rotating in place, while the other animations are perfectly fine. Also the generic NPCs that kind of stand around, walking places but might as well not exist.

The graphics? The art direction is designed to run on lighter handhelds like Nintendo Switch, the game is very atmospheric in place of realism. The artistic expression is extremely vibrant (I turned off bloom sometimes, because it really is that bright, you will see). The textures for the characters are nicely detailed. However, the landscapes and terrain are a bit lacking (though this is something I feel ALL JRPGs suffer from, they sacrifice landscape textures for character textures). This is arguably a good tradeoff for something supposed to run on a handheld, it doesn't hurt the experience compared to ugly character design.

They could still optimize the engine more, especially for Steam Deck. On Steam Deck, you get from 15-30 fps, especially 15 fps during the lantern sections and the most open areas. Use the atelier sync fix, your fps will hardlock to 60 on Steam Deck after initial loading of areas and it's insane why they left so much performance on the table. For PC, set power plan to high performance or max performance. Don't forget to plug in the controller. Set resolution to native, graphics level to low and then work out what you can increase from there (recommend a general order of texture/geometry quality to high, then render distances long, then shadows/bloom/AA/DOF/SSAO on for greatest visual to performance gain).

The music? Super nice. Instead of the orchestral, Disney-esque music from the first, it's more of an upbeat, faster soundtrack during combat. And the music, especially during crafting, gets quite emotional and is really notable. You're not going to dislike the music, very good composer they have.

In comparison to Ryza 1, Ryza 2 is a clear step up in technical development, and brings back all the nice characters plus more. Combat is astronomically superior, exploration is more vertical and engaging, while the crafting is about the same. If you played Ryza 1 and liked it, get the second. For those who didn't play Ryza 1, you will feel pain from going from 2 to 1. You want to play in order. You will not understand the characters without 1. (Also of note: there's a Ryza 3 coming out. Open world and even more adventure - might want a head start)

Completing the main game takes about ~40 hours, and a few more plus googling to complete all the achievements. I would recommend to get it on a sale if you are looking for laid back games, it's a nice value for your time. It delivers a character-driven story with slice-of-life events, and doesn't overstay its welcome.\t\t\t\t\t
