\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI have mixed feelings about this game, and honestly I don't recommend it mostly because this title can be safely skipped between two other Ryza games.

There are things to like about Ryza 2, and some systems and ideas that show promise, but are underdeveloped or made tedious. I liked the ruin exploration system. You first do preliminary check of the ruins, which activates ability to check the ruins in details. Then you collect artifacts and notes you find in the ruins to compare them to illegible notes you have. By finding artifacts related to notes you figure out history. It's sweet, but it becomes tedious later on. The requirements to activate "full ruin" become more irritating, and the design of the ruins becomes worse, so you are forced to backtrack on huge, boring maps.

As Atelier series went away from time management they seemingly replaced it with tame wasting. Maps in Atelier 2 are very big, and many of them are made in a way that makes walking on them inconvenient. They tend to be a zig-zag where they might look like areas, but really there's only 1 linear path, that just takes you all over the map. And there are no shortcuts, and the game requires you to come back there multiple times.

I also don't like the new alchemy system. Instead of having recipes that consist of 3-5 ingredients you have huge maps of ingredients. By the endgame you'll be putting 9+ ingredients to create a base item, then improve it further putting even more items. Sometimes you'll put in 8 items to notice you're missing 1 item to change the recipe into another item. It's very tedious then to put the items back. It seems like game developers noticed it's tedious, as there's an option to put in ingredients automatically. But by the late game you might not want to use it, as it might use up good properties on some items. Instead of carefully selecting best items you're just stacking the same property many, many times until you get +60 or whatever the limit for given property is. It's not really fun to use.

You're showered with items - to the point where I had to stop most of my gathering by the late game, as I would constantly run out of room in my container inventory (not the basket). There are systems to get rid of inventory items, but they are pretty slow to use, and I didn't find it fun to stand next to an NPC for 2 hours selecting items I don't need just to clean up my inventory.

The story in this game is pretty much like a filler episode. Nothing of importance happens. It's not that uncommon for Atelier games to be light on story, but usually it's saved by fun characters. But in this game every party member and NPC with event chain is very one-note. Most of the event chains could be summarised in one sentence. And every event for the given character is pretty much the same. "We're looking for flowers". "We're looking for flowers in different place". "Again we're looking for flowers". Funnily enough the character with most fun scenes was Bos from the first game. Older games had characters feel more like people, even the first Ryza.

This game introduces alchemy skill tree instead of alchemy levels. This means you can unlock alchemy abilities in the order you want. But the items on the list are not described until you unlock them, so you might end up like me not unlocking very important functions until very late in the game. This skill tree is also the reason why container gets filled so quickly - you get passive buff to the number of items you find. By the end game gathering just a few times will get you full of items.

The enemies on maps are often put in a way where you can't avoid them. There's no upgrade for the pre-combat smash to instantly kill them, so even if they can't do anything to you the fight will be mandatory. The combat system itself - I didn't like. It's kind of action and turn based mix. You collect action points by doing basic attacks and blocks to use your specials. But your AP is limited by your current tactics level, and the battles go SO SLOW on the lower levels. You start the battles on level 1, or, if you hit enemy avatar before fight, level 2. Out of 5. You need to get to level 3-4 before the battles become kind of fun. But by the time regular enemies die. If I got into a fight with level 1 it was so slow it took less time to escape and then attack the enemy outside battle to fight again, but at level 2.

At level 5 you can drop your levels by using a finisher, so then you have to build your levels again with slow gameplay. With stronger bosses you might want to use a finisher twice or so. Your attacks also collect item points which allow you to use your items. You can use multiple items at once, and if you use certain combo of items it will launch special attack. But if you use multiple items the wait time will be pretty long. Unfortunately this is made bad by having all playable characters share "crystal improvement points". Those points allow you to improve how good your character is at using items. The battle exp and level is synced between characters, but the ones you don't invest crystal points into end up pretty bad at using items. And near the end of the game I only had 3 characters with decently improved crystal - and I was near the max level. I would need to farm for hours to allow other characters also become decent at items.

I also found myself struggling at some point, when difficulty got a sudden spike. I was killing regular monsters without losing any HP. Then a boss just destroyed my team, and I had to defeat it in order to progress. I made good enough equipment to get trough it, and very soon afterwards even stronger bosses started jumping out one after another and my equipment or levels were not up to the job - I'm not sure which. I had stronger equipment locked between items I couldn't obtain yet, and the levels weren't going up too fast. But each time I visited the map Ryza would say it looks dangerous. Anyway, it was rather frustrating later on. Those were not optional fights too. Maybe I would have been fine with it if I liked the alchemy system and wanted to experiment with improving my weapons. It didn't help that bosses were very deep on the map so walk of shame to them took a very long time.

The graphics are fine, but some textures are really low res. The shirt of Ryza had pixelated seams, while the leather on Clifford was very high quality. The NPCs are noticeably much, much worse quality than any of the playable characters. The game also has very unfortunate DOF, which would make character faces very blurry in cutscenes when the focus should be on their faces. I had to disable DOF completely.

Overall if you're going trough Ryza series this is a good part to skip. There's some enjoyment to have out of it, but it takes a lot of time to complete, and doesn't have enough to reward the time investment.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAtelier Ryza 2: Lost Legends & the Secret Fairy is an objectively better game than the first due to the many QoL improvements from its predecessor. I enjoyed my ~40 hours with the game and revisiting my favorite apathetic Alchemist.

Over, I think that Combat in Ryza 2 experienced a much more significant level of improvement compared to the Alchemy. I found myself feeling the opposite way from my previous review because synthesis was not the highlight of my experience this time around (Shocking... I know!!). To put this into context, Tao's speedy style of play made him a really popular choice for my general adventuring shenanigans. It was fun building up longer combos, generating AP at a faster rate, and then getting into a flow of using skills and items. Alternatively, I think the 7 playable characters lend a good variety of play styles that can keep things fresh throughout the somewhat short adventure. My only gripe with the combat is that it doesn't not fully utilize the perfect guard mechanic due to how the camera functions during battle.

In comparison, the Alchemy in Ryza 2 felt largely the same, with some added mechanics on top that I feel didn't shake things up for me. The base or the soul of the process stayed the same, but the few things added at the top layer of end-game Alchemy felt tacked on. I completed Ryza 1 not that long ago and since the synthesis felt too similar, I got disengaged with the whole process early on. It's still a deeply satisfying mechanic, but I believe the best way to appreciate Alchemy in this sequel is to space it out from the first game. I can't believe Ryza forgot all the she learned and created over the last 3 years!!!

To accentuate the greatly improved combat was the whole exploration diary/ruins system. I think it was a fantastic move to divide lore into research items and vestiges that combine progression with comprehension of historical events that are relevant to the present. It's a good mechanic that doesn't drag on and it was a joy to explore the beautifully designed ruins and dungeons. The exploration was easily one my favorite parts of the game.

Ryza 2 is a great 40h experience that builds on the first. It's an easy recommendation for a casual JRPG or anyone new to the franchise. Ryza needs to get her together though!!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRyza 2 improves on practically everything Ryza 1 did, and then some. It seems like Gust was being highly experimental with Ryza 1 to see how they could modernize the Atelier series, but they nailed on everything that works in 2.

The unique gimmick of this game is exploring ruins to piece together what happened there a long time ago. It gives Ryza as a sub-series a very unique feel compared to the others, and is a different kind of exploration for reasons other than gathering materials. This along with the addition of new traversal tools like a rope, a giant animal to ride, and the ability to swim, the environments are much more dense and expansive which makes Ryza one of the best Atelier games to flat out explore like crazy. The main hub being a massively populated town is much more interesting than Kurken Island from Ryza 1 since there are much more NPCs to talk to and more sidequests to receive from them.

The biggest improvement is the combat, which I already felt was one of the best mechanics in Ryza 1. It's got the same core foundation, but you can now chain regular attacks into skills into other skills, and even swap party members in and out of combat mid-combo. The UI makes it far easier to execute commands in quick succession, and the change of core items being something you save up points to use in quick succession also adds a lot to the strategy of the game. It's excellent, fast, and frantic.

They also more properly explain the importance of item quality to make better items.
