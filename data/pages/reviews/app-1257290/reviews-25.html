\r\n\t\t\t\t\tJust what I had hoped the successor to Ryza 1 would be.

It's more of the charming Ryza world and feels just like the first game. It feels very comfortable jumping into it after having played the first one, but they've made plenty of new features and improvements to the systems so that it still feels fresh and updated without being unfamiliar or cumbersome.

There are new layers to alchemy that you can unlock through an alchemy skill tree (although you can unlock everything), opening the tool wheel while next to a resource node now displays the items that can be harvested with that tool, plus new layers to the town, exploration, and your atelier.

One of my favorite updates is what they did to combat. A lot of it is the same, but improved. For example, the tactics level in fights is now upgraded by using your tactics points actively. In the first game you had to spend points directly to level up tactics, which usually meant I'd just auto attack enemies until a high tactics level. In this game, using skills levels up tactics, so now I'm constantly using skills and other active combat interactions.

If you enjoyed the first game, you'll enjoy this too. It keeps all of the elements that I really loved about the previous game and world, and added more depth and QoL changes. Really Enjoying it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo, from someone who liked and played most of atelier games: This game is a bit hard to recommend with certainty because it is a very craft centered game. If you are in for the combat and how it works, sorry, but most of the important game mechanics is gonna be around the crafting system, although combat does have a few nice things. However, the crafting system in itself is incredibly well done, and with way too many options for you to choose what you want and how to do it, not to mention different ways to get around to your own thing.

In short, the game has a lot of depth with the crafting system. So basically, if you think you can enjoy that, go ahead and be my guest. The story is pretty nice with its up and down moments, and you usually do end up liking the characters and their development. Do keep in mind though, that this game does have some relation in both gameplan and story to the first one, so if you are gonna start now I highly recommend going for Atelier Ryza 1 first and ONLY then trying out this one. Otherwise you are gonna get spoiled and quite confused on some stuff (finished the game a few hours ago, so I can confirm, story does end up confusing if you didn't play the first game).

If you don't really enjoy thinking a bit to optimize usable items like bombs/heals/debuffs, equipment and even gathering tools, or you are not a fan of crafting stuff in games, then my friendly advice is to look somewhere else.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAnother excellent Atelier game. The story is fun/decent so far as per normal with a good focus on the adventure part of adventure. The way they acquire the egg and the compass was badly done but it's easy to get past that. Combat feels better and crafting works really well with its new twist. I dislike that gathering has no effect on the SP you gain however, as even 1 SP per gather would be rewarding.

I disagree with a lot of people, I find the exploration journal to be really engaging and further my feeling of adventure. It's a lot of fun piecing it together and reading through the clues and information it gives you. I also like the animal companion Fi. I agree that it should've been an adventure companion and not a story focus. Atelier games are always strongest when they build on their well developed characters. Focusing too much on Fi takes away that aspect.

The game's biggest weakness is its start, which I understand. Atelier games have always started slow because they begin by building your connection with the characters as well as building the setting. However as a sequel and coming from the position where the majority of characters are already developed and understood the slow start ends up taking away from the story rather then building on it. It's like having a foundation and a building on top, only for you to place another foundation on top of the building before continuing to develop. Having never done this before I understand why it happened, just thought I should point out that the start of the story can be a lot more engaging if done differently.

Always worth recommending Atelier games.

Edit: The character interactions are really flawed in this one unfortunately. Their lack of experience with sequels shows. Fi is a good addition and the animal being used as a crutch is a symptom and not the root of the problem. Excluding new characters (which still revolve around old ones), the character events mainly focus on the already established relationship between them and each others past. When you have a time-gap and a sequel, what is interesting to the player is how the characters have changed / what they have achieved, not what has already been. This is why the story is one of the weakest once you get farther into the game. All Atelier games have a simple storyline, but most make up for it with their great character arcs. This one unfortunately didn't know what to do to achieve that and fell flat. Hopefully they can learn from this.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is an early review, as I'm only a few hours into the game and a fair amount of that time has been spent in traditional Atelier fashion: Gathering and Experimenting. As such, this is more just my observations on what I do and don't like about the game so far.
Like:
- The gameplay loop is familiar with some fresh spins
- Most systems, like synthesis and combat, will feel familiar to anyone who has played Ryza 1, but will be easy enough to pick up for anyone not familiar with it. (Before Ryza 1 my favorite synthesis was Firis, and Ryza blew that out of the water)
- With a little intuition its easy enough to ramp into synthesis loops early on
- So far the characters are highly likable, though that may also be nostalgia as I was a huge fan of the Ryza 1

Dislike:
- While the combat handles well (and personally I'm a huge fan of the active timer system), it feels like you gain too much too quickly. Playing on normal, it was absurdly easy to level Ryza and Tao to 25+ before you even get a stable third party member
- Story/Gameplay segregation: So far, two major cutscenes have left me speechless, both dealing with Volker. First is Ryza's reaction to the price of rent. "I couldn't make that in a year" except as a returning protagonist she has canonically gained that much money from single fights in the past, as an alchemist she can make an absurd amount of money synthesizing items with the Expensive trait, and within an in-game week after completing Volker's task I'd already made about that much off combat and delivery quests without stopping to do synthesis even once. There's no reason for Ryza to react as negatively as she did. Second, in a random event, Volker stopped in and said "You're not holding up your end of the bargain and helping the people". Between the first meeting and that random event I've already started nearly every available citizen questline and reported well over 2-dozen item or monster-slaying deliveries. These just left a bad taste in my mouth. I realize these things are scripted, but there should at least be some connection between the scriptwriting and your actual accomplishments outside the main plot.

In the end though, I will still wholeheartedly recommend this game to anyone looking for a wholesome, cute, or lighthearted JRPG experience.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tYes, a good chunk of you are probably on this page because of THIGHS, or other related pieces of anatomy. But for those of you who are staring at this weird anime game and don't know what the heck you're looking at, allow me, as a skeptical newcomer-become-fan, to enlighten you.

\tAtelier is, at its heart, a crafting-focused RPG. You can level your characters and learn new skills and all that jazz, but you will live and die by your ability to learn the mechanics behind alchemy—the game's form of crafting—which allows you to take a bunch of plants, rocks, monster bits, and more to create weapons, armor, bombs, and maybe the cure for cancer. Seriously, though, you could probably finagle yourself a healing item in this game that heals every conceivable status effect at once; alchemy is that crazy in-depth.

\tIf you want a clear-cut comparison, I'd say Disagaea is an apt one. Like Disgaea, Atelier looks like it's your run-of-the-mill JRPG with some quirky mechanics on the side, but the more you play it, the more you realize that the real game is learning to abuse the weird mechanics until you can *break* the game.

\tLike the previous entry (and probably all previous entries from what I've seen), this is both Ryza 2's greatest boon and its most fatal flaw; the mechanics are interesting and powerful enough to make you want to delve into them, but they're so powerful that you can actually trivialize the game's combat on the hardest difficulties given a thorough enough knowledge and understanding of how alchemy works. There's a middle ground somewhere in there, but by the end of the game you typically end up on one extreme or the other; you either barely scrape by with basic alchemy and feel like the game's difficulty spikes are sharp enough to impale your entire party, or you spend more time in alchemy menus than you do in the game's dungeons and laugh maniacally as you instantly end every combat encounter with a single bomb strong enough to crack the planet in half.

\tIn short, Atelier offers that strange brand of fun where the goal of the game is to trivialize the game. I didn't think I would enjoy the first game, but I did, so I'd recommend people at least give it a try.\t\t\t\t\t
