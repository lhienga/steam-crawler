\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's Ryza 1 with better combat, which is honestly all I wanted. I expected more narratively, but the core "summertime adventure" feelings from the first game remain intact while giving us some extra lore to chew on. The soundtrack bops and matches the setting, and the crafting remains mostly unchanged with a few extra features thrown in. Nothing feels specifically worse compared to the original, but it did feel less climactic at the end, almost like it stops just short of the goal. Altogether, Ryza 2 is incredibly easy to recommend, especially if you enjoyed the first game. Take your time with it and explore everything it gives you, cuz chances are you'll be left wanting more by the end.\t\t\t\t\t

Pros
  • Takes place 3 years after the events of Kurken Island (Ryza 1), Ryza 2 is much larger in scope for content and diversity of choices you can make in terms of synthesis options. Enjoyed the story or synthesis system of Ryza 1 = Play this game
  • Alchemy level is gone, instead obtaining new recipes and alchemy skills comes from a "Skill Tree" which you can upgrade via points gained through synthesis and questing (no more synthesis limitations based on story)
  • Literally everybody is a returning character; even the flippin goat from Ryza 1
  • Synthesis is unchanged at its core (Put in materials -> select or transfer traits -> finished product), in addition, players can now use "essence" which allows you to boost an effect on a material loop; this opens the door to creating further customised items
  • Core items can now be optimized towards your preferred element or category, players can now select and enhance core elements; and with the introduction of "Core Drive" (basically a Fatal Drive for core items) CC usage and item chains play a more impactful role in combat
  • Alongside traits & effects, Ryza 2 introduces EV-Effects; they function as additional bonuses you can transfer to your core items to give them even more power
  • Combat has been changed from Ryza 1, players tactics level now increases independently without AP cost, players can now chain both skills and core items given enough AP, & players can now switch between playable characters by utilising the new "Shift Skill"
  • There are many side activities that are available when taking a break from the story, furthermore, the puni companion and weasel roast make a return in Ryza 2
  • The reputation system functions as a perpetual option for getting cole, gold coins, & sometimes even rare materials
  • Very relaxing OST, the Atelier theme will also be engraved into your brain
  • Seamless performance, beautiful graphics and cutscenes, no frames lost, no crashes encountered
  • Bos {Better Be Playable In Ryza 3}

Cons
  • The majority of monsters are the same design from Ryza 1, there is also a severe lack of bosses (can expect more from future DLC)
  • Highly recommended to play Atelier Ryza: Every Darkness & The Secret Hideout before this title to fully understand the story/characters/setting
  • Short game like Ryza 1 and can be completed in the same timeframe from 25-40hrs; there is always NG+ and new difficulty options unlocked, however, no new content has been added at this time
  • Bos is not a playable character

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is the first time I've 100%'d a game this quickly.

Ryza 2 was a blast and even more fun than Ryza 2; for the most part I find Atelier story lines to be quite boring, not really a fan of slice of life games, however Ryza 1 and 2 both got me hooked through the crafting aspects of the games.

When I play an MMO, the thing that consumes most of my time isn't the plot, or the leveling, or the quest grinding, it is really the crafting/blacksmithing that gets me hooked. Atelier Ryza 1 and 2 both got that down pat.

Ryza 2 (via plot) expands upon the crafting and makes certain things more sensible (with reason, via plot gate-locks) and makes the crafting/smithing/alchemy a delight to play. The sound effects, music, and overall crafting system got me hooked from the get go. My hours at this time says 92.6, I would argue 80 hours alone were spent crafting various items/gears/seeds etc. that were unnecessary to advance the plot. It's that good.

The plot itself is basic slice-of-life fare, the combat isn't very difficult (I actually played most of the game on easy/normal because I hate grinding levels). However the crafting really hooked me in, I found myself working on expanding the recipe trees "just cuz" and ended up super overpowered because I had managed to accidentally min/max weapons 1/3 of the way through the story. But you know what, that's crafting for ya.

I personally enjoy Stardew, Don't Starve, Recettear, various farming/vending/smithing type games, but the one thing that always keeps me hooked is the blacksmithing and crafting aspect. And for that, Ryza is the game of choice.

Scores
  • Gameplay: 10/10 (based off crafting)
  • Music: 10/10 (sound effects are great, music is pretty good, the transitions keep you "in the mood")
  • Combat: 5/10 (it's actually pretty bad, action focused combat but the monsters are super easy to kill even on the highest difficulty. Also Rose Bomb (a 2nd tier bomb) is overtuned and does more damage than any other bomb in the game. If you min/max a bomb, you can 1 hit KO all of the bosses as well).
  • Story: 6/10, it's not bad, but ultimately it's your average slice-of-life fare, while Ryza 1's story was a "growing up" deal, Ryza 2 is more of a "self-discovery, oh this is who I really am" which doesn't really have a huge climax or pay off. You do learn about some interesting history in the line of Alchemists though, which I'm not sure is canon to the remainder of the series.

    Final Score: 31/40. I recommend this game.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAtelier Ryza 2 by far is an upgraded version of the previous game. In terms of gameplay quality from the first few cut-scenes it felt like I was right back at home from Ryza 1. The battle system was improved adding multiple new features such as a guard option and individual cores. The boss battles didn't really take me too many tries because I was spending so much time in between story quests upgrading my weapons and armor.
All the new characters introduced had their own side-quests which really had me invested into their story. Although at times some quests were a grind in terms of gathering resources, these quests added pretty solid rewards. For example the cafe offers a bulletin board with daily quests that give money and sometimes gold coins which could be used in Romy's shop. The only cons I really noticed from this game include a few minor technical issues with the camera, and I had 1 movement bug during a cutscene where ryza's animation kept cutting and restarting in a spaz-like way. Overall very good game and highly recommended for anyone who has played previous Atelier games.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tStarting with a disclaimer: I've played most Atelier games in the franchise and love them all (except you, Lydie & Suelle). Now, Ryza 2 isn't a bad game at all. But if someone asked me, "Which Atelier game should I play?" I would never suggest Ryza 2. Even if that person described their preference for exactly Ryza 2's sort of gameplay, I would simply refer them to Ryza 1. That's how similar they are. But Ryza 1 had a better plot.

The biggest mistake in this game is the repetitiveness. From the get-go you are told that the plot will require you to investigate 5 sets of ruins. That's exactly what you do: go to ruins, get a cutscene, complete a very long-winded collectathon for glowing orbs, get a recipe out of it, go home, make it, go back to those ruins, get another cutscene, you're done. Next set of ruins. Rinse and repeat until the game ends.

Now, if you actually pay attention to the immense amount of lore written into those collectathon orbs, you'll find that there's tons of interesting, potential plot points! But, tragically, it's entirely irrelevant to the game. Characters never mention it. There's an entire history of a lost civilization written into the game, but the plot moves along as though all we know is that these ruins are old. We COULD have had a plot where we learn about the past and pick up where our ancestors left us. We could meet the Frozen Moon witch, do something with that special stone that holds human souls, find all those special weapons mentioned in the lore, learn the recipe for that ultimate medicine, visit the land of the dead they were searching for, learn about the old magic/origins of alchemy, the list goes on.

The crafting system was also really weak compared to other games in the franchise (except Ryza 1, which has the same exact system). You can't combine traits, and the traits we do have are really boring. The best ones just sort of boost your stats. Compare that to, say, Atelier Firis, where if I tried hard enough, I could create a set of items that effectively gives me infinite turns. I miss doing weird sh** like that. It's also boringly easy to get 999 item quality. In other Atelier games, that was something to be proud of.

Lastly, the fanservice was a little over-the-top. Atelier isn't known for its modesty, per se, but this time, every woman minus the 13-year-old had crazy proportions and clothes to show them off. This is a minor complaint. I know that Koei Techmo is just capitalizing on the hype over Ryza's body from the first Ryza game. but does detract from the immersion when I have to keep wondering, "Is the point of this scene what's in the dialogue here, or am I really just supposed to be showered in boobs?"\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAtelier Ryza 2 is a comfy JRPG about growing up and reunions, with relatable characters and relationships backed by fun, hectic ATB combat. If you're looking for a chill, cheerful experience that masterfully straddles the line between everyday adventures and world-ending threats, Ryza 2 is for you.

...and if you're a min-maxer like I am, whose idea of fun is optimizing every last 0.1% of damage from a build, and loves finding hidden interactions and loopholes in game systems, there's a completely different - and equally compelling - game present in the crafting system.

Similar to games like Disgaea or Path of Exile, this game allows you to become obscenely overpowered, both through raw numbers and by stacking effect after effect on every action you take. Armor and accessories that allow you to spam skills and items on demand? Go for it. Want gear that literally increases your stats by an order of magnitude? Why not? A potion that revives your entire team, cures all status effects, gives everyone a stat boost AND speeds up your entire party's next turn? Sure, you can do that! Elemental nuke that hits everything on the field twice, lifesteals for half your health and debuffs the enemies' speed and defense down to pathetically low levels? Feel free to combine it with an accessory that makes it cost basically nothing so you can chain a million damage off of one combo.

Unlike those other games, however, the path to doing so in Ryza 2 relies little on XP grinding or farming RNG loot for drops. Instead, it focuses more on how you plan out your crafting route and how you build interim crafting materials to make trait addition easier. The multiplicauldron and essence boosting systems unlocked about halfway through the game ensure that with some problem solving, you only need to find a trait once on any material in order to duplicate it infinitely and apply it to all of your equipment on demand. However, the path to doing so may not be straightforward; at one point I had to loop a material through five different interim crafting recipes, some with specific essence boost requirements, before I could get it onto the accessory I wanted to make! Due to the differences in each player's inventory, you have to figure out your own path for your specific materials and needs instead of relying on specific guides, which can pose some fun, rewarding puzzles.

All this combines to make an RPG experience that makes you feel like you've EARNED your overpowered nature, not just thrown in enough hours to make the system work. Through system mastery and abusing loopholes intentionally left in by the developer, you become an alchemical god incarnate and smite everything, including the post-game boss on the hardest difficulty, with extreme prejudice.

Which leads to my one real complaint about this game - there is no post-game of any real content. The game has a variety of difficulty modes, from 'I just want the story' difficulties that won't require you to craft anything, to ones that are brutally difficult until crafting really opens up in the midgame. But by the time endgame hits and you have the ability to craft anything you want, there really just isn't much need for it. There's the final boss, which is a pushover, and then there's the post-game superboss, who hits like a truck but needs about 5x the health to really push the combat system to where it shines. There's no infinite scaling or bonus dungeon to serve as a litmus test for how truly strong your OP builds and strategies are; there's just how fast you can obliterate the Lamb Roast and Weasel Roast challenges in your room.

Ryza 1 had the same problem that was eventually mostly solved by a endgame DLC and I suspect that will prove true in Ryza 2 as well, but we'll have to wait and see.

Now for a quick rundown of the actual game's systems in comparison to the first Atelier Ryza game:

The combat's an active time battle system that feels like a mix between FFXIII and the Tales series, of all things. There's always something to do, whether it's chaining skills, using timed blocks to reduce damage, swapping between characters, or setting up your item chain and watching things go boom. Unlike some similar systems (looking at you, XC2's earlygame!) there's very little downtime and you're constantly on your toes if you want to optimize your damage output. Compared to the first game, it's significantly flashier and faster-paced, with item use being much more common and strategic since you now earn CC per character during a fight. One of my favorite ATB systems so far.

In Ryza 1, hitting max quality relied heavily on crafting quality loops and was both tedious and unintuitive to reach. In Ryza 2, crafts will always go up in quality with more ingredients added, with higher quality items providing a bigger boost. This gets rid of the awkwardness in Ryza 1 where you generally wanted to use as FEW materials as possible to not waste 999 quality raw materials, so now it's feasible to hit max quality through conventional means. Some crafts will still heavily benefit off 999 quality intermediate materials, so there's still a benefit to optimizing in that regard.

The need for high quality materials also made shops, money, and even dungeon equipment chests(!) completely useless in Ryza 1. You'd never use shop materials because they'd probably lower your craft quality, buying armor was a joke when you could always craft better stuff by the time you unlocked it in the store, and the same applied to whatever gear a dungeon could provide you. This is no longer true in Ryza 2. Shops can now be developed by selling materials to Romy, which can allow you to unlock certain materials like Goldoterion and Septrim a full dungeon earlier than you normally would be able to farm them! They're usable too, because the absolute need for ingredient quality is gone. Dungeon chests follow a similar philosophy, allowing you to get low-stat gear with interesting traits earlier than you can craft them - a teaser of sorts, which incentivizes players to keep progressing so they can make their own versions for the rest of the party. In general, the whole economy and material system is significantly more fleshed out and improved compared to the first game.

The characterization and relationships between the characters is held pretty consistent from the first game, with all of the banter and jokes between the cast still as great as ever. The three years of separation between everyone is treated pretty realistically, and characters have developed reasonably during that time. If Ryza 1 was a coming-of-age story, this is closer to a bunch of high-school friends coming together for a reunion after university. (I can say this because of how damn relatable it was!)

The story itself is more fragmented than the first game. The game tells a significant amount of its backstory through text matching puzzles this time around, which can get boring for some. There's less of a motivation for Ryza to embark on her journey this time around, although she quickly develops more reasons to care. Some character introductions are also sloppily executed. Overall, it's a slightly weaker narrative than the first game, but the increased quantity and quality of random character interactions makes up for it somewhat.

In conclusion, Atelier Ryza 2 is one of my favorite JRPGs of all time. It's comfy, cute, and consistently interesting, AND it managed to scratch my number crunching itch without making me invest several hundred hours into RNG hell. Truly a worthy successor and an improvement to the first game.

This game came at a perfect time during the COVID lockdown, too. Definitely needed some comfy after another few months in relative isolation.\t\t\t\t\t
