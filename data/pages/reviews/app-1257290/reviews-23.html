\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI had a kind of odd experience with this game - got it last year, played for a bit, thought it was too similar to the first Ryza (which I'd just finished at the time), got bored, and ditched it before the main storyline really started. Now, I came back to it a year later, and it turns out I had quit right before all the new interesting stuff showed up! I've played it all the way through and am replaying again because it's fun. Some things that stood out to me in this title:

- Ruins exploration and fragment-collecting is something new to the series. It makes it more interesting to go around the material gathering areas, and the exploration journal gives insights into the behind-the-scenes lore (and possibly foreshadowing for the 3rd game?)

- The alchemy synthesis puzzle, which is the main reason I got so into Atelier games in the first place, is well-designed and I think would be pretty easy to learn for someone new to the genre. Personally, I prefer the more complicated grid-based system of the Mysterious series, but this one is more relaxing because it feels simpler. The recipe discovery mechanics are fun, and synthesis of weapons & gear feels satisfying because how well you make it will make a difference in combat.

- The action battle system is improved over the first Ryza. It's easier to see all the UI elements and learn the battling mechanics. The ability to switch characters quickly is good. I would have preferred more focus on item use, the way past turn-based games emphasized it. The points (CC) needed to use an item in battle take a while to add up, so I end up using character skills more than the items I synthesized. But then, I don't really play Atelier games for the combat, so as long as it's not too tedious or difficult, I'm good with it.

- The overall plot is interesting, but the dialogues in a lot of the cutscenes felt boring and simplistic. I'm clicking Skip a lot on my replay. Nonetheless, the story has good friendships and heartfelt moments, so if you are not put off by the banter, the cutscenes will be ok to watch.

- Gorgeous artwork of course. Character designs, environment designs, the alchemy and battle special effects - all just pure eye candy.

- The variety of exploration tools is awesome. This is such a big difference from early Ateliers, where you'd run across the gathering zone basically in a line (invisible walls everywhere) and have 1 way to collect a material from a gathering node. In Ryza 2 (and Sophie 2), you can, for example, use a staff, an axe, or a scythe on a tree, and each tool will give you a different set of possible materials from the same tree. It's fun to discover what each tool + gathering spot will result in. Also, there are abilities to explore underwater, jump across chasms, and other things that make the areas feel more open-world (with the use of special items available through the main plot progression).

Overall, I definitely recommend this game. It's a good starting point for an Atelier newbie, and works well for someone experienced with the series as well.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRyza 1 was a surprisingly good game that I thought punched above it's weight - it was definitely a niche game, but one that was fun and well-written enough to compete with the big boys of the JRPG genre. This entry, though, is on a whole other level. I liked Ryza 1, but I love Ryza 2.

The Ryza games do two things well that set them apart from the JRPG pack.

First of all, these games focus on an interesting form of crafting, Alchemy. At first, this was a put-off for me because I've grown used to the most JRPGs' terrible crafting systems and I couldn't imagine playing a whole game of that. But Ryza's crafting is not like those systems. For one, crafted items are central to combat because items are the source of >75% of a character's stats, usable items are the source of 50% of their abilities, and the items you find pre-made in dungeons are leagues worse than those you can make yourself. The crafting system is also very deep. Materials have a quality, an elemental value, and a set of additional traits that you have to manage while being able to mix only a limited set of items on a FFX-like sphere grid.

If that sounds overwhelming, don't worry. The game lets you auto-complete recipes if you want and on normal the experience is relatively easy. But the beauty of this system is how it is totally fine with you breaking the game if you're a good enough Alchemist. By the end of the game, I had crafted an Elixir healing item that was resource-cheap enough to be cast from the first turn of combat but could revive all dead party members, heal all party members to full health, add a strong regen to them, and apply a damage-reduction buff. And then I duplicated it and gave it to my entire party. How cool is that?

The second thing Ryza does well that separates it from the JRPG pack is the type of story it tells. To put it simply, the Ryza games are slice of life stories. Sure, both games have a central adventure that drives the plot, but the core of the experience is the relationships between the main characters and how they grow as people over the course of the story. Now I like my share of save-the-world plots, but it is definitely refreshing to play a game with a plot about something as relatable as "how do you grow up and find what you want to do with your life?" or "can friendship hold strong despite time and distance?"

So I've talked about what both of the Ryza games do well. But why do I like Ryza 2 so much more? Well, the answer comes down to polish. As much as I liked Ryza 1, there was a lot about that game that didn't quite hit. The way the map and exploration system worked felt awkward and cramped, as you didn't get the main world map and fast-travel system until a third into the game. Usable tems felt awkward because of the limited charge system and healing items were rendered moot by Klaudia's broken healing skill. And, worst of all, the slice-of-life elements felt awkwardly integrated into the game because a) they were required to advance the main plot, despite them not having much to do with it and b) they were so obtusely placed that you had to rely on a sub-menu to tell you where to go to advance the story.

Ryza 2 has none of those problems. Exploration is clean and simple, and you have the main world map and fast-travel system from ~20 minutes into the game. Combat has been refined, with a new tactics and item-charge system that encourages the player to use their resources rather than horde them. And the slice of life elements have been separated from the story into their own system and placed naturally into high-traffic locations like the game's shops, quest hub, and home base. Even areas where the last game was already good, like gathering, side questing and alchemy leveling, have seen refinements, along with improved visuals and a kick-ass soundtrack.

So, should you play Ryza 2 if you already played 1? Yes. Absolutely. This game is worth every cent.

Should you play Ryza 2 if you haven't played 1? Well, that's trickier. I certainly think the story and character development of the sequel hits a lot harder if you've seen these characters grow through the first game. But at the same time, it's a pretty big ask to tell someone to play a solid but flawed game for 30 hours so they can play the gem of the sequel for 40. I'd say, if possible, play Ryza 1 first. But if you're itching to get into Ryza 2 as soon as possible, playing it immediately wouldn't be the worst decision. Just keep in mind that these characters have a lot of history together that you won't know as much about.

Either way, Ryza 2 is a total JRPG powerhouse, and I'd recommend it to anyone who likes the genre.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSecond game of the Ryza series.

3 years later, invited by Tao, Ryza travels to an island across the sea, to investigate something that could be related to alchemy.

Atelier Ryza 2 takes it a notch further than its previous game. The combat system was massively improved, the Alchemy Level system was dropped for a Skill Tree, alchemy mechanics were refined or new ones added, new exploration tools were added on top of the previous ones and a development system was added that improved the NPC Shops. All of it without making it feel like a pain while playing the game. However, the difficulty and length are similar to the previous game and the english voices are still missing.

This time around, I found the combat system to be very enjoyable. You can combo your normal attacks into skills, which can then be comboed into higher level of the same skill once learned, different skills, or you can even use the new Switch Skill, which lets you swap your current character with the one that you chose to be in the reserve, giving you small burst of AP, on top of enabling you to extend a combo with their own skills or simply prepare for the next one. This time around, the Tactic Level system very nicely designed. By consuming AP, used when using skills, you passively increase your Tactic Level gauge, which will automatically level up your Tactic Level once a certain threshold is met. Additionally, the Core Charge system was changed so that instead of gaining a one time supply of CCs across the party, which could be renewed by temporarily disabling someone's item, CCs are gained by using skills with the individual character. A character's Core can even be upgraded further, using Core Shards, gained by using items in combat, which lets you improve a character's CC limit or their performances when using a skill or item of a specific element. Not a big fan of the camera during combat though.

The alchemy system got slightly better as well. Instead of taking the average quality of all the items used during a synthesis, it now takes a certain % of each item's quality and add into to the item being synthesized. However, while a nice feature, this also makes it so you can reach high values of quality very easily, making your consumables and equipments much stronger much faster, especially with Romy's development system. While the Alchemy Level system was dropped for a Skill Tree, it didn't feel that bad, since synthesizing, completing quests and exploring ruins granted plenty of SPs. A lot of the recipes are still obtained from the Merge Recipe mechanic. The Multiplicauldron can be unlocked fairly early on, making gearing up your character very easy, if you don't mind farming some gems to dupe, Rebuild system now scales depending on how many Rebuild nodes you've unlocked with a base of 25 levels, and a few more interesting features were added; which I will let you figure out yourself.

This game delivers a better exploration experience than the previous game by giving you 200 items basket, monster radar and gathering preview from the beginning. A few new tools were added, which is all fun and stuff while going through the story once. The main contender is the Spirit Beast Whistle, which lets you move faster than running speed and lets you find a few items slightly earlier than you normally would with other tools, but this comes at the cost of having to go through small loading screens when getting on or off of it, which makes it a bit of a hassle when you're trying to gather a variety of items when travelling, unless your goal is speed or items specifically obtained when Mining with the Spirit Beast.

I mentioned above about Romy's development system. In this game, you can only sell your items to Romy, and by doing so, it will grant you experience points into various item categories. When leveling these up, items in the shops will get improved, whether it's quality or trait level. Additionally, a lot of items have a one-time reward, which will increase the shops' stocks further or unlock new materials in the shops, which can let you earn certain materials MUCH sooner than you normally would. I found monsters in this game to give a fair amount of standard currency, Cole, and shop prices can even be reduced by leveling up your Town Reputations, which is gained by completing side quests or from the Request Board.

My main grip about the game was the Ruin Exploration system. I found the idea to be interesting, but even after forcing myself to try to get interested into the Ruins' histories, I just couldn't. I simply stopped caring after a few ruins and I would just cycle through the items until they fit into the slots.

The game is just as great as the previous game, if not better, depending on how you look at it. Outside of the Ruin Explorations, everything else was just so fun and enjoyable. I previously said in its own review that Atelier Ryza was my favorite Atelier by far, well this one takes the spot. I'm really happy with how the Ryza series is turning out so far and I'm looking forward the next one, if they decide to continue the series.

1. Mysterious series (Sophie +, Firis -, Lydie & Suelle +)
2. Arland series (Rorona +, Totori -, Meruru +)
3. Ryza series (Ryza +, Ryza 2 +)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is purely a gameplay review.

Atelier Ryza 2: Lost Legends is a decent JRPG with turn based combat based on a individual turn timer which you can manipulate with hastening and slow effects.

Main points:
Unlike JRPG that focuses on skill trees per individual character, you as an alchemist, craft items by expanding a smaller skill tree to enable more advanced crafts of items, accessories and trinkets for use in combat.

Atelier ryza 2 has the traditional aspect to a JRPG where you as the player can decide if you want to push story by focusing on tactics, or by simply just grind materials to make weapons that'll make you oneshot current content.

The game builds to a fairly average extend upon a system where you build relationships with branches of the city to unlock quick material gathering methods or skill tree points.

The world is divided into smaller instances that can be reset by re-entering the same zone by instantly teleporting or manually walking out and in again.

The game itself has a Pedia that makes for easy browsing of gathering materials and where to collect them however it can at times be very vague.

The game is in ♥♥♥ voice with ENG subs however it lacks subs for the quirky lines the characters says during combat or during crafting processes which just makes it bothersome noises for those who can't understand the language.

The characters are locked into a specific weapon archetype like most JRPG's are.

A short diversity of actions to execute during combat and you quickly find yourself using the same rotation or weaponized items.

The parties consist of 3 characters during combat with a total of 7 characters to choose from however the AI insist on using buffs for their special action skills before using attacks on most ecations even on encounters that would be finished in 1 hit.

The NEW GAME+ feature is pointless.
The NEW GAME+ feature allows you to start the story anew seemingly only to add a higher difficulty without additional content, however you are reset to level 5 (start level) while allowed to keep the items you've crafting during your first run, which basically negates the whole higher difficulty setting for the first 60+ levels of encounters as your gear does not have a level requirement, which begs the question, why not allow the highest difficulty from the beginning?


TL;DR
Atelier Ryza 2 is a decent JRPG for those seeking a quick JRPG fix, but if you just finished a Persona or a Final fantasy game, don't pick this up as you will be disappointed.\t\t\t\t\t
