\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Good
It's got all the crafting goodness of the crafting game. The graphics are a slight improvement and some of the scenery is very pretty.
Combat is pretty sparkly but no challenge

The Bad
- But now with extra gem grinding until you can get the materials to exploit for infinite gems.
- Way too easy even on hard if you hit the crafting with any dedication at all. I by no means min-maxed, --
- Didn't even bother with the final level of gear. Just too much gem farming.
- It fails to explain several mechanics at all, for example I only discovered that you could power up how many CC you started a fight with in the last dungeon. So much stuff is easy to miss. I guess that's a bit of a JRPG trope but it's particularly bad in this game.

The Utterly Awful
The characters and their interactions. At least in the first game her friends gave Ryza some ♥♥♥♥ so there was some vestige of character. But this is just one long stream of saccharine nausea which achieved peak at the ending where I nearly sicked up in my mouth. There is NO moment in the game where the script isn't comprised of wholesome Hallmark dialogue. My Little Pony is edgy by comparison.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe sequel to the experimental Atelier Ryza, Atelier Ryza 2 did a lot of polishing of mechanics and improves on what worked in the first Ryza and drops a lot of the things that didn't work. Story is still a weak part of both games.

Combat is quite improved in Ryza 2. The ATB-style combat remains, but the crafted item system changes. As you do basic attacks, you build up AP, which can be spent on skills when it's your turn. The "tactics" level goes up automatically with skill usage, this time it doesn't cost AP to increase the tactics level which removes a bit of resource management. Like in the first Ryza, higher tactics levels improves the power of your skills. Your AI controlled allies have action triggers just like in Ryza 2, but these are easier to hit, triggering off either physical or magical damage (abilities, certain items, and support attacks). New in this game is a 4th combat character called a support character. This character can be hotswapped in and out of combat at almost all times (there is a cooldown), and when swapped will do a bonus attack that is usually counted as a magical skill. Swapping characters in and out is essential for more difficult fights.

The item system is quite different this time around. Items cost CC points, which are built up by using skills in combat. By default, you start with 0 CC points, which reduces the usefulness and power of items in the early game which is quite different from the usual Atelier game where early game items are your most important resource. You will eventually be able to unlock CC upgrades, including starting with a certain number of points allowing you to use items immediately. Each character can eventually equip up to 4 items, what they can equip is dependent on their Dexterity stat just like in Ryza 1, and you can use as many items in one turn as you have CC points for. In addition, there are "ultimate" attacks that can be triggered by using certain combinations of items once per combat.

Exploration and gathering are good, the areas in this game are much larger and more exploratory than anything before. In addition to your different harvesting tools, you have a lot of new exploration items such as a grapple beam like tool, the ability to explore underwater, and more. Unlike in Ryza 1, there are no decisions to be made about what to bring, you have slots for everything. The effects of the monster radar and the preview of what materials can be found with each tool are always present, you no longer need to craft this ability using rare and powerful materials and no longer have to waste a tool slot for it.

That said, since the areas are so much larger it takes a bit longer to traverse. Some of the areas have pretty complex paths with a lot more verticality so it does take more time to get to places. Due to the much larger size of the areas, you also get absolutely flooded with materials. You'll run into basket limits very often, and Ryza 2 is the first Atelier game I've EVER managed to run out of space in the atelier's container! You will be doing a lot of selling and material deconstruction into gems. Unfortunately both of these systems are lacking in quality of life features. When deconstructing items into gems, the filter resets between uses which is unlike how Ryza 1 worked. This makes deconstructing items into gems take way longer if you filter one item at a time (so you can deconstruct the weaker and keep the stronger or ones with high level traits). Selling is done through "shop development", and you can only select ONE item at a time, and you'll get popups regularly during the process interrupting you. You cannot select large groups of items to sell so it's a very frustrating, click intensive, and time consuming thing. It's also important to have shops be any sort of value.

The story, however, is pretty lacking even by Atelier Ryza standards. While it's nice to have the return of the character events, the main story revolves around a mascot character to the detriment of everything else. You have a bit of "tomb raiding" feel during a big portion of the story, where you do minor puzzles, craft items to restore the ruins (as part of the storyline), and collect clues which can be put together into a story of the past. Overall it's a bit more interesting than the main story but held back by a pretty bad "research" system where you put together all the clues.

Crafting is one of the main draws of the Atelier series, and this game improves upon Ryza's new node system. It's even easier now to build up massively powerful equipment than Ryza 1. Shockingly, this game removes the crafting level. Instead, as you craft you earn points (you also earn points from doing ruins research), and these points can be spent on a skill tree/sphere grid type of system. The skill tree unlocks new recipes (including for more gathering tools), skills, upgrades to your crafting prowess, and new crafting options (like duplication or combat core upgrading). It makes crafting a bit more freeform which is interesting, but it also lets you get overpowered really fast. Just like with Ryza 1, crafting can be material overload, where a large complex craft could be built out of 50-100 base materials. Keeping a supply of gems and materials ready to do item rebuilds is quite important for attack items and equipment.

The high power level of the items you can craft and the ease at which to get it is probably why there are some pretty stiff jumps in difficulty. The bosses and some late game forced trash fights are a huge jump in difficulty and you really need to do some heavy crafting to make through them without much difficulty. Don't neglect your crafting as it's easy to get stonewalled by the difficulty cliffs.

Artwork improves even more, areas are very detailed and very beautiful, though in some areas the bloom and post processing are a bit much and it can make it hard to see sometimes. This port is much improved over previous Koei Tecmo ports, with a lot more graphical settings and all of the settings accessible in game rather than by a launcher. Sound and music are standard excellent Atelier fare. The boss theme for the bastion bosses is my favorite of the bunch, and probably my third favorite theme in all of Atelier after Ayesha's final boss theme and map screen themes. Like with all other Atelier games, it runs well on Linux but has regular stutters every few seconds if it's not locked to 60FPS. I do that with this launch option: DXVK_FRAME_RATE=60 %command%

There's a lot of fanservice, Ryza's new outfit is a bit more revealing, and you get a lot of scenes where the camera fixes on her butt or chest. There's also some equal opportunity fanservice with Clifford's design. Otherwise, the character designs are great with a lot of variety. There are no hot springs/bathing suit scenes like some of the earlier games, so while the character designs have fanservice, the story doesn't take it into any creepy aspects.

Overall, this is a great Atelier game and I quite enjoyed it. I was mixed on Ryza 1, while I liked some aspects there are others I didn't like. Ryza 2 just improved so much aside from story that I can't not recommend it. Definitely worth it on sale, maybe even full price. That said, this is a sequel and you are expected to know how the crafting works in general and the characters and story events from Ryza 1. There are not always summaries or explanations for those who might be coming into Ryza 2 without playing the first game. If you're going to play it, definitely play Ryza 1 first (and on sale).\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've enjoyed the Atelier series of games. Not all of them, gods no. There's too many. However, the one I remember best and enjoyed most was Atelier Rorona. Don't know why, I liked the cast. Atelier Ryza really recaptured much of what I liked about Rorona, but with an incredibly expanded crafting system and a much better world component. So, sequel time. Do I simp for the sequel just because it contains my favorite lesbian couple in gaming? Yeah, but in this case I still think the game deserves it. It's an improvement over the original in many ways, including the world building. Overall it's just more interesting and the RPG more tightly built. There are some quality of life improvements to the crafting that don't invalidate the charm and challenge, and just like the first one you can abuse the hell out of it to make incredible things. It feels good to craft and again shows how lazy, uninspired and vestigial crafting is in most games.

If you are going to build your game around anime and crafting, this is how you do it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs of writing this review, I have 100% completed this game and unlocked every achievement.
After doing the same for the first game, I was super excited to jump into the sequel, hoping it would somehow be even better than the original. With 59.7hrs under my best I can safely say, yes. Atelier Ryza 2 is an *even better* experience than its predecessor.

If you enjoyed the first game, this game is more of that, but taken to the next logical evolution.
Environments are even bigger than before, with exciting and adventurous new ways to navigated them, the scenery is stunning, dare I say breathtaking. The alchemy system also returns with plenty of new recipes, expansions, new methods of crafting and more.

The cutscenes and storytelling are dramatically improved, more coherent, more impactful. I genuinely found myself emotional on a number of occasions. Seeing Ryza reunite with her old friends – especially Klaudia – was just heartwarming, and their relationship dynamic is shining brighter here than ever before.
Speaking of the characters, their new designs are superb, and the new additions to the cast like Serri & Patricia are just a joy see on screen. The roster of characters, both playable and non-playable, for this sequel is leagues above the previous game. I became so immersed in the world surrounding the capital city, it all felt so alive.

One could hardly talk about immersion without mentioning the soundtrack. *Somehow* it’s on par with the first games incredible OST, and in honesty most of my favourite tracks from the Ryza series come from this game over the first. Hours of beautiful, orchestral, relaxing music to listen too in this game. One of the few games I’d gladly purchase the soundtrack to.

The combat however, is where the game truly shines. The original system from Ryza 1 is discarded in place of a new, faster, more energetic, more thrilling fighting style. While the Tactics & WT features make a return, they function slightly differently. Veteran players of Ryza 1 may take some time adjusting, but I found this combat system to be much much more enjoyable in almost every facet after I’d gotten my head around it.

I could go on and on singing this games praises, but I hardly think I need to.
Atelier Ryza 2 is a masterclass in capturing a relaxing, feel-good experience.
This series as a whole has had such an impact on me, I’m so thankful I played both games, and I really truly hope you’ll give the series a shot too.
It is something truly beautiful, and well worth your time\t\t\t\t\t
