\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAtelier Ryza 1 was my introduction to the Atelier series, and overall this feels like a successful sequel, though there were some disappointments. To anyone who hasn't played an Atelier game, if you like a more slice of life story with many character interactions, as well as a very addictive alchemy (crafting) system to create game breaking items, and a real time combat system that is turn base-esque this is definitely a game to look into.

I enjoyed every minute of this game, they made major improvements on the game mechanics and quality of life improvements, such as no more invisible walls when there is a 1 foot drop, you can swim/dive now, you can see what items you get pop up in a bubble if you were to gather from something, etc. Each sub story and character interaction is just as good as the first one, and the combat system had a huge improvement with the core item drives and upgrades (except that the game pauses now when you use an item). So I would like to say overall, i still really like this game

Some negatives. The overarching story was very very very bland. Fi as a plot tool was not used very well at all except for interactions with Clifford. And being "invited over to explore 5 ruins" is a bit too on the nose for me and made it all laid out right from the get-go. What's the biggest shame though is that the ruin puzzles contained some pretty cool lore if you took the time to read them all (i didn't but the ones I did read were cool) and those could've made a great story to explore (maybe it'll be dlc i hope or be used in the 3rd game) but the overall story just felt so lazy.

Combat system for the most part got improved but I really liked in the first game, the game would not pause when you were selecting an item. The tension of real time combat really evolves the turn based system and that is ruined a bit with items pausing the game now.

The crafting system definitely got super dumbed down. In Ryza 1 I never go above a 400 quality item but this game I accidentally made multiple 999 quality items. Made crafting feel less accomplishing.

idk how but the game is even easier than Ryza 1. The only time I felt in danger was when I was at the final boss and I went in with my party at half health.

Personal gripe, I just really like Kurken island. It felt organic for there to be mysteries to explore and such. In this game we get thrown into the big city, and there just happen to be 5 humongous ruins nearby to explore.

Overall: gameplay, music, graphics, side quests, and crafting system are still top notch and super fun. All the negatives don't ruin the game but I hope they get fixed in the 3rd iteration\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAtelier Ryza 2 builds on the success of the first game in several ways. Almost everything has had a light dusting of quality of life tweaks but the headliners are the new gameplay and traversal mechanics.

Being the first Atelier game to retain a protagonist makes a huge difference, as less development time was needed to create new characters, personalities and models. Instead, that time could be focused on bringing gameplay aspects that have stagnated within the series up in line with other RPGs. Simply adding climbing, swimming, a mount and rope swinging makes the game feel much more alive, even if they aren't particularly new or innovative additions (when compared to contemporary RPGs).

Changes to the synthesising mechanics, specifically taking elements from the original Ryza game and mixing them with the enhancement mechanics from the Mysterious Arc, results in a system that works really well. In my opinion this is the most refined and well-balanced version of synthesis I've seen in an Atelier game to date, and rewards bulk grinding and specifically targeting certain materials and monsters in equal measure.

Despite those improvements, the combat still feels like it's at a bit of a weird crossroads. If pressed to describe its current form, it would be a hybrid of "turn-based, on-rails auto-battling" that feels more at home in a mobile game than a console or PC RPG, but it's not bad by any stretch. I just personally prefer a straight-up turn-based system. I'd like to see this further built upon in a future Atelier game by either jumping in full to real-time action-based combat, or stepping into the ATB turn-based system. Either would work and allow for more meaningful gear/trait and character building choices.

Overall, Ryza 2 improves upon the previous game in a lot of key GAMEPLAY ways, but falls a little short in the story department by rolling out the same big "looming" force as the first game's. The pacing also falls apart a little towards the end, and there's no real justification for a lot of the late-game choices the characters make outside of "to progress the plot."

I really hope there's a third instalment so we can see just how much the series can improve, but for anyone looking to jump into the series I'd still recommend Ryza's original adventure over this one.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRyza 2 Builds on what the first game established. The alchemy system is more refined and intuitive and combat feels just as smooth, if not even more with how clean the new UI and moves feel.

Exploration is still a joy to go through, and the maps look stunning.

The music is delightful and I strongly recommend playing it without any background noise around you, so that you can fully enjoy the tracks.

Overall, if you enjoyed the first game, get this one and you won't regret it. If you're new to the series, this is a good title to start with. Knowledge of the first game helps but doesn't feel mandatory though you lose on some of the nostalgia that way.

Very solid RPG with a very nice mix of combat, exploration, music and visual treats.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCharming (despite the character designs) and wonderful soundtrack.

Likes:

1. The soundtrack is amazing. It is such an improvement over its predecessor and I listen to it outside of playing. The battle themes are fun and energetic.

2. The writing is simplistic and predictable, but charming because of it. It's a good pace for casual players like me since I can play irregularly but still understand the basic mechanics (except synthesizing).

3. Combat system is dynamic and creative. Turn-based and real-time hybrid. I haven't seen anything like it and I hope they continue to improve upon this formula. The battle animations are fun to see and execute.

Dislikes:

1. Everyone has their own preferences and boundaries. For me, the design choices for the female characters is too off-putting at times. It seems wrong to have overtly suggestive clothing on underaged girls.

2. There is too many "fixed" dialogue scenes where I am just pressing A to skim through the mindless text. I wish that dialogue didn't halt gameplay. I had to put down the game after spending close to 2 hours watching slow cutscene after cutscene. The pacing is off and I'd rather play while I listen.

3. Synthesis should be its own game. I cannot understand it at all. Thank God there is a shortcut button that automatically fills the synthesis ingredients for me.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNot as good as Ryza 1, but still solid.

Battle system is much improved. All the awkwardness of CC and skills vs items is gone. It's a bit button spammy, and I'd still prefer to go back to the old turn-based style, but this works well enough.

Gathering and exploration have also been improved. Adventuring items and gathering items now have separate slots, so you don't run out of space and don't need to craft hybrid items. You can now upgrade the gathering abilities of your staff and hands, so they now match the abilities of the other gathering methods. Exploration is a bit more varied as well, though honestly nothing major was added - you get a rope, you can swim, you get a mount (that I kinda forgot to ever use), and there are more narrow passages (mostly for pervy screenshot purposes, but hey).

Alchemy is the same for the most part. There were a couple changes that help keep alchemy from being quite so easily abused in the very early game (more required materials to craft, item level limits), but the full range of abuse becomes available much earlier since you gain access to gem reduction and duplication much sooner than the previous game. On top of the normal crafting, you have other systems layered on top, like core drive, and a couple other things. These are completely unnecessary, I never touched them and don't really know what they do.

Story and characters... honestly this is where the game kinda fell flat for me. I think the first game actually benefitted from its smaller cast. The larger cast in Ryza 2 brings with it a proportionally larger number of cutscenes and character events. It all gets rather tedious after a while. The story was just kinda there for most of the game - explore some ruins and solve the mysteries of your strange new pokemon friend. Then at the end there's something of a surprise twist that's not really all that surprising, but comes out of nowhere with no foreshadowing. The first game had a much better pacing in its plot.

Unfortunately, there's also no postgame to speak of. I tore apart the final boss in maybe a minute. Apparently there's an extra boss after that to hunt down, but that's nothing compared to the 5-6 in the previous game. Maybe they'll add some in an update, but for now there's really no reason to craft super powerful gear.\t\t\t\t\t
