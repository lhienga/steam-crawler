\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA rather unique tower defence game, tasking you with defending your house from zombie hordes using various plants.


Plants vs. Zombies’ main game mode is a campaign featuring 50 levels, each of which presents you with a grid split into multiple lanes on which to place your plants. Zombies will soon start to approach these lanes, trying to eat all of your plants and make their way beyond them into your house. As the level progresses, the number of zombies steadily increases culminating in a couple of huge waves, in which a coordinated horde tries to overrun your defences all at once.

As the campaign progresses, you’re introduced to different kinds of zombies with varying health pools, abilities and behaviours to make your live more difficult and generally shaking up the required strategy. To counteract this, you also unlock a new plant with a usually quite unique ability for almost each level beat, giving you counters to the different zombie types and enabling different options for dealing with them overall. The stages’ setting also changes every 10 levels, which introduces different environmental hazards for you to adapt to, further mixing up the gameplay.

Beyond this basic mode, Plants vs. Zombies also features various mini games, puzzle challenges, endless versions of the different stages and even a sort-of idle game in which you tend to a garden and collect the various plants featured in the game. These different modes along with a harder version of the campaign unlocked after beating it mean you’ll have plenty to do in the game and give Plants vs. Zombies a lot of replay value.

This game oozes charm, being supported by some decently witty writing and puns slotting right in with the weird premise. Plants vs. Zombies’ cartoonish art style holds up more than well and is further supported by a fantastic soundtrack which always manages to make me hum along in no time.

Sadly of note for this version is the fact that it never received the Full HD and Widescreen upgrades featured in other releases of the game. Personally, I found that this hardly matters given the game’s look and the honestly addictive gameplay, which will quickly draw your mind away from the fact that things aren’t looking quite as sharp as they could be.


All in all, I can’t recommend this game enough – it’s a bunch of fun and way more in-depth than you might expect from the first look.\t\t\t\t\t
