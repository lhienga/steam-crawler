\r\n\t\t\t\t\tImagine if Doom Eternal and Cyberpunk 2077 had a baby and that baby was raised by Platinum games. That's the kind of experience you can expect from Turbo Overkill.

You play as Johnny Turbo, a cyborg who is more machine now than man, who works for TeraTek, a futuristic corporate conglomerate that has developed an AI named Syn that has gone haywire. You set out on a journey to put Syn down for good in the hopes of reclaiming your humanity. The premise is nice and simple, and gives the developers an excuse to deliver all kinds of hyper violent mayhem into your eyeballs. While it definitely takes a backseat to the action, it's developed enough that the cut scenes (which sometimes become even more ludicrous than the gameplay) entertained me quite a bit.

Like the plot and many, many references to action movies in the achievements, TO is nothing you haven't seen before, gameplay wise. You zip around incredibly fast, using the environment to leap through the air in shooter arenas that vary in size while taking down hundreds (and I do mean hundreds) of enemies per level. Most of the time you're hunting for a key or a switch or something, but the huge, sprawling level design is rarely confusing like it can be in older FPS's. Despite how complex and vast the environments are, I rarely found myself confused or at a loss for what to do, which is a testament to the tight recursive nature of their design.

Meanwhile, you eventually get nine different guns, each with upgradeable alternative firing modes, and chainsaw arms that will grind most things into mulch. This is in addition to a double jump (which can be turned into a triple jump if you're feeling saucy (which you should be)), two midair dashes, and a ground dash where a chainsaw in your leg literally mows down anything in front of you. Each gun is definitely fun to use even though I found myself favoring some over others, and upgrading them feels worthwhile and distinct since it can completely change how you use the weapon. For instance, you get a double barreled super shotgun early on that can be upgraded to launch grenades. But what if the buckshot WAS the grenade? Its third upgrade makes any buckshot you missed turn into a time bomb that'll deal chip damage. As I said, the variety is astounding and insanely fun even though you'll definitely find your preferred firearm.

But the game does a great job of giving you excuses to use all of these weapons. The hordes it throws at you are massive, and by the end you're basically one cyborg vs entire armies. I'm 95 years old, so I find I can often become overwhelmed if there's too much happening on screen, but I found myself always able to read the situation thanks to visually distinct enemy designs. Like the recent DOOM games, each enemy fills a very specific role, and looking at them can tell you what you need to do at a glance. It's such a fine line between the beautiful chaos that Turbo Overkill presents and just being a mess, but it nails the frenetic pace right from the first level and never lets up.

Special mention should be given to the level design. Much like the recent boomer shooters DUSK and Amid Evil, the story isn't the main focus, but the levels do a surprisingly good job of creating a cohesive, believable (if over the top) setting. Lots of little details like how the voice in the vending machines will complain that they have a family to feed if you don't buy anything go a long way to building the atmosphere, and the variety on offer is incredible. Turbo Overkill knows exactly when to break things up with a vehicle based level or have a more linear but awesome concept, such as an Act 1 level that sees Johnny jumping across moving hover cars while blasting bad guys. You could argue the game goes on a bit too long, but it's very difficult to complain when each level feels so distinct from the last.

Turbo Overkill is an extremely impressive package. Between its story that starts at 60 and only accelerates from there, the powerful arsenal, and the diverse level design, it feels like a true, complete package for what it offers. There are so, so many boomer shooters on Steam these days, and that's because it's fun to zip around blasting bad guys in spectacularly gory fashion. TO goes above and beyond in its presentation and design, and delivers a fast paced, tremendously satisfying FPS that should be played by any fan of the genre.\t\t\t\t\t
