\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDon't be fooled. This is called a boomer shooter or a retro shooter, but it's closer to Doom Eternal than anything else. Doom Eternal meets Blade Runner. Minus Doom Eternal's ammo management and the weapon-to-enemy pairing. Instead, this game is a greatest hits of all the best FPS mechanics. Dash, double jump, wall run, grapple hook, ground pound, slide, slowmo, weapon upgrade, body augmentations, jump pads, it's all here and it all feels useful and worth engaging with.

The combat arenas just keep coming. They are always good, always getting crazier. By the third episode, I wasn't sure how the game could get more intense and frantic, but it does. You know those games that allow you to enter a kind of flow state? Turbo Overkill does that. Turn the FOV way up, turn the volume way up, and let the subconscious parts of your mind take over. Every night this week I've felt like I was in a trance.

What are AAA devs even doing? Seriously, who else is out here providing FPS campaigns this good? Id? Anyone else? The retro shooter scene can no longer be considered a gimmick, it's no longer just a fun little romp in the nostalgic hay. It can no longer be ignored or downplayed: this is where the real talent is currently located.

On top of excellent combat arenas, solid guns, smart upgrades, and incredible music, it also offers a pretty neat story. It's not exactly Baldur's Gate, but it's also not as simple as something like Duke Nukem 3D...even though Jon St. John is in it.

No gun reloading, no puzzles, no annoying recourse management, no downtime, no data entries to read, no cover system, no stamina system, no useless weapons. It's the perfect pepperoni pizza of a game that hits the spot in a way few games can. If you're a fan of fast paced FPS titles, then this is an must play.

Oh, and it never does that thing where you arbitrarily lose all your weapons and have to get them back. The game ought to be nominated for an award just for that decision alone.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTurbo Overkill is well deserving of its success, being a true bargain for the amount of content it provides.
Paying homage to the sci-fi/cyberpunk genre with several on-the-nose references to films like Robocop and The Terminator, It does not shy away from its inspirations. Respectfully and masterfully, it delivers an experience that is a cut above the rest in a long list of retro style shooters sweeping the market.

Yet despite all this, I must stress one important criticism I have, in that it suffers from a lack in moderation. The game runs a bit too long, culminating in a finale that left me exhausted when it was all over. I think after the 10 hour mark, my brain felt like it was just spinning a bare-tread tire in place in an attempt to cross the finish line.

In summary, Turbo Overkill is a testament to what indie developers are capable of, but ultimately lets their passion for game design get a bit carried away.

Still, I highly recommend.\t\t\t\t\t

There are times where a weapon may be tucked away or neglected for a bit, but it will always have a use at some point. Barring a couple enemy types, the game has no "this weapon counters this enemy" - allowing you to play the game however you wish.
