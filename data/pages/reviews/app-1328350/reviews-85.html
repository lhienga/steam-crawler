Turbo Overkill: A Riveting FPS Masterpiece That Breaks the Mold

I've recently delved into the electrifying world of "Turbo Overkill," and I can hardly contain my excitement to share my experience. This game is an extraordinary blend of exhilarating gameplay, retro-futuristic aesthetics, and a soundtrack that resonates deep within. Here's why "Turbo Overkill" has captured my heart and should be celebrated as a groundbreaking FPS gem.

1. Mesmerizing Soundtrack - A Harmonious Connection:
The game's soundtrack isn't just an auditory accompaniment; it's a symphony that flawlessly syncs with the gameplay and mood. From the first note, I felt an undeniable connection that only deepened as I played. It's like the music and I clicked instantly, intensifying every moment and enhancing the overall experience. Words fail to fully express the awe-inspiring musical journey "Turbo Overkill" provides.

2. Masterful Map Design - A Navigational Triumph:
Navigating through "Turbo Overkill's" meticulously crafted maps is an experience in itself. Unlike other games where you simply progress from point A to point B, here you're required to traverse the map in ways that defy convention. The circular design ensures that revisiting areas isn't a chore but a delight. This unique approach fosters a sense of place, allowing me to absorb the environment's nuances and intricacies.

3. Ingenious Enemy Design - A Kaleidoscope of Challenges:
The enemies in "Turbo Overkill" boast a captivating blend of silliness and menace. Each encounter poses a different challenge, ranging from mildly tricky to outright nerve-wracking. Learning their weaknesses and devising strategies to dismantle them efficiently is incredibly satisfying. As my proficiency grew, even the most fearsome adversaries became mere obstacles, with the thrill of overcoming them eclipsing any initial fright.

4. Enthralling Cyberpunk Aesthetics - A Visual Delight:
The cyberpunk aesthetic in "Turbo Overkill" is a visual feast that marries neon-infused retro vibes with cutting-edge design. While some elements are intentionally pixelated, they coexist seamlessly with realistic counterparts, creating a captivating and cohesive visual universe. This union of styles enriches the game's world and bolsters the immersive experience.

5. Embracing Imperfections - A Bug's Perspective:
Though "Turbo Overkill" isn't devoid of bugs and glitches, I found myself embracing these quirks with a grin. Falling into unexpected places and exploring off-limits zones due to unforeseen map changes became part of the adventure. It's a testament to the game's magnetic appeal that even minor setbacks transformed into sources of amusement, adding an unexpected layer of fun to my playthrough.

In Conclusion: A Paradigm Shift in FPS Gaming:
I was initially drawn to "Turbo Overkill" as a possible alternative to my beloved "Doom Eternal." However, it didn't take long for me to realize that this game is in a league of its own. It defies comparisons and stands tall as a new standard in the FPS genre. With its unique gameplay mechanics, the awe-inspiring chainsaw-leg, and an immersive world, "Turbo Overkill" has left an indelible mark on my gaming journey.

In the realm of gaming, finding a title that not only offers unparalleled entertainment but also reshapes your expectations is a rarity. "Turbo Overkill" has achieved this feat, earning my utmost respect and appreciation. To all the gamers seeking an adrenaline-fueled adventure that defies conventions, I wholeheartedly recommend diving into "Turbo Overkill." Prepare to embark on a journey that redefines what an FPS game can be.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR - I feel this game is overall, objectively, better than Doom Eternal with some caveats, those being music and graphics for obvious reasons. I think this game deserves to stand proud just behind the three greats boomer shooters that started this whole revival of Dusk, Amid Evil and Ion Fury.


This is a modern game in a retro inspired shell. It owns it and while using modern graphical techniques, the retro-tag lets it focus on gameplay and level design instead of spending loads of money for them textures. Story is one of the reasons I compare it to Doom Eternal, with the difference that unlike Eternal, the story and characters are campy and cheesy from the beginning in a Duke Nuke'm 3D kind of way without murdering the main characters origin like Eternal did with the 'Doomguy' after the great 2016 reboot.

Levels are great, fun to navigate and jump around and break. Movement only helps, it is incredibly smooth and fast paced. You start with two dashes and a double jump, and it only gets better. Each level has secret secrets, less secret secrets for plot and world building, and some collectables for side grades. I'm bad at finding them so I guess I can only say they were hidden pretty well.

The OST is great, starts cyberpunky and keeps the pace throughout the game. Can't say much than that really. It's not Andrew Hulshult like we're used to from almost every single other boomer shooter out there, but it does the job well and fits the game very much.

The way the Acts change the scope and type of story the game shows and tells is also great (though I do wish the second and third act would go into a bit of a horror theme for story reasons)

Guns I felt were a bit of a mixed bag for me. They all felt good bar the missile launcher (it needs a big boom oomph sound when rockets explode I think) but didn't feel like one is much better than the other, with some actually feeling weaker, like the double barrel, with its incredibly long reload time and weaker enemies taking more than 1 shot to kill. Alt-fire and weapon upgrades on the other hand was satisfactory, and even allowed me to fight the final boss with the starting pistols for efficiency, actually!

That said, augments are neat though I don't think there's enough of them to actually let you pick anything but the most obvious ones (healing, jumping, damage). In short I think they do not change up the gameplay enough.

The biggest negative for me was the silent protagonist (Jon St. John is great, but I felt he was underused for only voicing the AI companion because it was only scriptet) and you do need one-liners for this type of game.

Other thing were enemies - they were alright in design and such but barely made a sound, I had the game sound cranked at 10 for all of the options, and I barely heard a grunt or a moan from them, felt a bit dissatisfying in fact.

The last thing I found was the difficulty playing on hard for the first try - I found the game to be very hard, with dying only once or twice to the final boss due to not paying attention after my reflexes went to bed. Slide is incredibly powerful, and with the healing augment I never really needed to look for slightly hidden armor or health pickups at all. Just slide into the conveniently placed onehit trash mobs. Can't say it isn't satisfying though.

All in all, this is a great game worth its price and time to play.\t\t\t\t\t
