\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is like one of those fake games you see people play on a TV show, and I mean that in the best way possible

This game combines tons of elements from classic shooters like Doom,Quake and Duke Nukem and mix and evolve them with elements from new games like Ultrakill,Doom Eternal,Titanfall,Bioshock,etc

The movement is the most fun I think I have ever had in a shooter game. There is dashing,sliiding with chainsaw legs,wall running,wall jumping,b hopping,grapple hooking,etc

The guns all are pretty much viable and much like Eternal these guns have secondary fire modes you can purchase with money collected throughout the game. Some really cool stuff here like an orbital laser, a sniper where your bullets can make you teleport inside of enemies, a chaingun that doubles up as a flamethrower, chainsaw hands,etc

The story isn't anything mind blowing but it get's the job done. There is even some lore via audio logs/monster descriptions if you are into to that type of thing(think how Doom Eternal did its lore). However,This game is still a step above almost all of the other games in this genre when it comes to cinematic events and level design. Yeah, alot of levels are still key hunts but it is executed in such a way that it doesn't feel tedious or repetitive. The progression is excellent, and does a great job of easing the player into new mechanics/weapons and augments(basically perks) while providing a pretty good challenge(don't let the first few levels make you think you are gonna be able to just chainsaw leg every enemy you see)

The enemy selection has plenty of variety and just plenty of enemies on screen at any given time. There are certain enemies immune to certain elements(Such as fire, or electric shock from your shotgun) and some enemies that require you to hit them in a certain spot to kill them.

The only real complaint I have of this game is regarding a few specific boss fights, specifically the bosses that are the same size as you being really hard to hit(and see) however they are not to hard if you equip some specific augments and learn there patterns.


i think this and Tears Of The Kingdom are easily my 2 favorite games of the year and anyone who loves fast paced shooting should give this a try. For $25 this game is a steal\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tStrengths:
* Fun Gameplay with Options: The gameplay in Turbo Overkill is enjoyable and provides players with a variety of options to engage with, offering flexibility in their approach.
* Fluid Movement Mechanics: The game excels in providing fluid and responsive movement mechanics, allowing for omnidirectional movement and intense combat experiences.
* Engaging Violence: Turbo Overkill offers a gratifyingly violent experience that adds to the game's overall appeal.
* Stability and Performance: While the game isn't visually impressive, I think it is a nice step-back from other modern releases as the game runs smoothly and relatively bug-free, providing a stable and enjoyable experience, even on my lower-end POS.

Flaws:
* Wide Weapon Variety; Little Call for Versatility: Despite offering numerous weapons, many of them felt situational. Out of the, what, 9 or so guns the game gave me, I only used maybe half of them. If even. Probably 70% of my experience, I was just swapping between the two shotguns and to the dual sub-machine guns whenever I ran out of shotgun ammo.
* Underutilized Modifications: While the game features many different ways to build Turbo into an Overkilling machine, a majority of the options felt lackluster, leaving me to wonder why they couldn't have just made them into permanent upgrades, like what weapons have.
* Millennial Writing: The game's story, while easy to follow, is kind of all over the place. The dialogue is best listened to on mute. Especially in that final cutscene. Also, sequel baiting.

In conclusion, Turbo Overkill offers an enjoyable gaming experience with its strong points, including fun gameplay and smooth, tactile mechanics, leading to a potentially replayable experience. Despite its flaws, I believe that it remains a worthwhile purchase.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI came, I saw, and I kept sawing – and to think I almost skipped Turbo Overkill, figuring it was just one of the dozens of retro shooters that seemingly get released every single month these days... It is not! Instead, Turbo Overkill is possibly the single best action-focused high-mobility shooter I’ve played, and it should get way more attention than it’s getting at the moment! If you give this game a chance, it will utterly steamroll you with its countless awesome gameplay ideas, intricately designed lengthy missions, beautiful set-pieces and even a proper story that ties everything together without detracting from the gameplay side of things. Turbo Overkill is relentlessly fun and a clear contender for the all-time retro shooter throne.

Turbo Overkill follows the high-mobility shooter style you may know from games like Shadow Warrior or, more recently, Doom Eternal. In stark contrast to id Software’s most recent release, however, all of Turbo Overkill’s gameplay systems are geared towards maximising your fun with the game. No forcing the player to use insta-kill buttons because of a ridiculously limited ammo capacity, for example – Turbo Overkill gives you all the tools you need, and then some!

In fact, I think that ‘excess’ is the defining term I have in mind every time I think about Turbo Overkill. And I mean ‘excess’ in the most positive way possible! From boomsticks to orbital lasers, from grappling hooks to micro-missiles and from slow-motion to double-chainsaw berserk mode, Turbo Overkill offers a plethora of weapons, active abilities, and passive skills, all of which are just utterly fun to use and get introduced so gradually over the course of the game’s 3 long episodes that the game never ends up feeling stagnant in the slightest. It even throws some vehicle missions at you – and manages to make those feel awesome instead of limiting, to boot!

Level design is another area of Turbo Overkill best described with the word ‘excess.’ Sure, 25 missions spread across 3 episodes may sound like a pretty standard number, but every last one of these long and meticulously designed maps positively erupts with amazing gameplay ideas and beautiful set-pieces, making me feel like I played double that number – with not a single dull second to be found. And I’m saying that as someone who tends to get a little tired of non-stop action after a while. Turbo Overkill held my attention throughout by continuously escalating EVERYTHING over the course of its 25 missions. And while I felt that some of the later levels were just shy of having a bit too many arenas with waves of enemies teleporting in, the fact that I never ran into a wall of frustration or boredom speaks volumes, as far as I’m concerned. And on top of its campaign, the game also has secret challenge maps and a feature for downloading user-created maps. Once again: ‘excess’, but in the most positive way possible!

Perhaps surprisingly, another thing that kept me fully engaged with Turbo Overkill throughout were its story and the deliberately garish 'Blade Runner meets Tokyo Ghost' setting. Sure, the main plot about stopping a megalomaniacal AI isn’t exactly Shakespeare, but the story, worldbuilding and characterisation are way more effective at tying the action together and keeping players motivated than I would’ve anticipated from a retro shooter – a genre where many devs notoriously just handwave all of that away. Turbo Overkill manages to have its cake and eat it too, as the story hits exactly the right balance to keep you engaged without imposing itself too much on the gameplay.

At the end of the day, I’d be seriously surprised if Turbo Overkill doesn’t become a true shooter classic in time. If you have any interest in first-person shooters, you owe it to yourself to give this one a try! \r\n\t\t\t\t\tI think this game came out of Early Access a bit too soon...

The Good:

1. The game feels fluid and fun to play. Especially when you unlock all upgrades. It’s like Doom Eternal, which is like an FPS fused with a character-action game like Devil May Cry.

2. The music is mostly good.

3. The graphics look good enough, very bright and colorful.

The Bad:

1. Horrific level design. Rooftops was a level that was buggy and nearly impossible to finish. I had to glitch my way into finishing it. The level design is confusing and it’s very hard to know where to go, even with the game telling you where to go. As it points to areas that make no sense.

2. Awful selection of weapons. Most of the weapons in the game range from redundant to worthless. The SMGs and Chaingun share the same ammo type, and the Chaingun is way better, so you never use the SMGs. The magnum's main fire mode is useless, so you only use the secondary because it’s the only good thing about it. The super shotgun and the sniper rifle are nearly completely useless. The plasma gun’s secondary is too slow to be useful in such a fast paced game. The rocket launcher is slow and weak. Just horrific. The Ion gun is fine, basically the BFG of this game. The chainsaw arms are good, but they’re pretty much a clone of an in-game power up that does the same exact thing.

3. Terrible game balance. You will often just die instantly for no reason at all, even with 200 health and 200 armor. The beginning of the game is far too easy, and you can complete most levels in episode 1 without even firing a gun due to how overpowered the chainsaw legs are. You just dive-kick and slide everywhere and things will just die and give you health and armor on top of it. Later on, you just die nearly instantly with no indication of how or why.

4. Glitches and bugs galore. As mentioned before, Rooftops level was nearly impossible to complete without glitches. You will get stuck on walls or under the floor constantly as you dive-kick and slide around. I picked up a hidden item during a boss fight and then I died. When the game reloaded, the boss wouldn’t respawn, causing me to redo the entire level from the beginning.

5. Lame “secret levels”. You collect 3 tapes hidden in every level, and when you do, you unlock a secret level. These aren’t like your typical secret levels though. They’re just lazily slapped together challenge arenas. Disappointing and it makes collecting those tapes a waste of time if my reward is a short level where I have to run from a fire wall or some arena level where you must survive several waves of enemies. Quality over quantity. I’d happily take one good secret level over all of these lazy ones.

6. Bland story with Borderlands-level cringe writing. Normally I wouldn’t care if a story is bad in an FPS, but this game absolutely insists on interrupting the action with cutscene after cutscene, so in this case, it does bother me that it's this bad.

---

That's enough of that. Go play HROT or Zortch instead.\t\t\t\t\t
