\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTurbo Overkill is rad.

Featuring a diverse selection of monstrosities eager to tear you apart and an equally diverse range of weaponry to put them down with Turbo Overkill is not only a great arena shooter, it's one of the best games in it's category.

Every level throws in something new, whether it's a fresh face to blast apart, a new toy to assist with the blasting, some new traversal puzzle, the occasional vehicle level to keep the ante as high as it'll go capping each section off with a climactic boss battle that will put your skills to the test.

Three episodes split up into a myriad of levels from water logged docks and winding canyons of scrap metal to the insides of a super sized flesh beast and the besieged capital ship of a mercenary outfit.

No good cyberpunk dystopia would be complete without cybernetic augmentations and Turbo Overkill has a plethora of options, most augment your abilities; reduced cool-downs, extra damage and tweaks to the functionality of related abilities, all customisable to your preference.

If you're into movement shooters, arena shooters, boomer shooters or any variation on that style of game, heck even if you're just getting into first person shooters in general I can't recommend Turbo Overkill enough.

10/10\t\t\t\t\t

++ Amazing visuals
++ Amazing levels and setpieces
++ Banger soundtrack
++ Insane cutscenes
+ Chainsaw Leg
+ Cool vehicle sections
+ Many weapons and augments
+ Good voice acting

* OK story

-- Game was completely broken on first start up
-- HORRENDOUS audio mixing in some cutscenes

This game was a blast to play through, for the entire time. The gameplay and flow in combat are addicting and a lot of fun. On the audio-visual front the game is pretty insane as well, combining unique and memorable visuals with a banger soundtrack. The chainsaw leg is one of my all-time favourite melee weapons and the vehicle sections were a nice change of pace as well.
The negatives are related to stuff which should be easy to fix (but for some reason are not):
The first start up is pretty broken, with the wrong language selected, after which I had to look up a guide to fix the game. The second is the HORRENDOUS audio mixing in some cutscenes, where the music deleted my ears, while everything else was drowned out.
All in all the game is very worth it's asking price, just maybe wait for a patch to address the issues first.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game start off simple - short levels and basic weapons that you know from all other shooters. The only gimmick you have is the chainsaw leg which is very useful because you can replenish health and armor with it. Given all that you might think that this is just another retro FPS... But then it just start giving you more, and more, and more...

Weapons have secondary modes which can be completely different from the main mode (electro-burst on shotgun, grenade launcher on sawed-off, flamethrower on minigun, gravity gun on plasma gun etc; reminds of Painkiller) or you can unlock upgrades that add something new to the gun.
When you get used to the game's basics it opens up, starting with a flying car section. Levels become bigger but not a chore to traverse because you also become faster (as a player and as a character).
And it doesn't stop there. E.g. you get to jump on speedy cars and trains, ride through the whole city on the bike with miniguns and chainsaws, jump around the space from one ship to another and then ride a robot.

The level and arena design is great, looks like new Doom games. It's hard to get lost, the game always show you where to go without relying too much on objective markers.

Visually it's gory techo-body-horror stuff. The graphics are pixelated but it's noticeable only in static. The music is also great and fitting, hard stuff during the fights and more ambient when you just run around.

All in all, this is the best FPS of 2023.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t"Turbo Overkill", ah yes, one of those boisterous, exaggerated retro shooter titles, right? Wrong. This game's title isn't kidding. Everything about it is revved up, lightning-fast and taken to gratuitous excess. The title fits like a glove; you play Johnny Turbo, a cybernetically augmented exterminator tasked with blasting his way through a gritty cyberpunk metropolis to root out a massive infestation of cyborg monstrosities and their ascending singularity of a leader/god. Sounds like simple, dumb fun? Again, not entirely correct.

See, this game keeps changing things up. From the get-go, you have a chainsaw leg. This is a strange idea but gameplay-wise, it works; you can charge forward with it, damaging or instantly splattering enemies into a shower of chunks and gore. But you can also propel yourself to the side, while still attacking in that direction, or behind you. Someone sneaking up, popping their little pea shooter at your shiny metal heinie? Give 'em the old backward chainsaw surprise, without even bothering to look at them. The "chegg" as it's called, also propels you a good distance across the ground while letting you duck under a lot of ranged attacks. It can even recover health and armor as you take out weaker enemies with it! This is all very useful for keeping yourself alive while never stopping your assault on the game's sometimes satisfyingly squishy, but other times chonky and murderous enemies. More on them later.

You start with dual magnum pistols and soon find some more weapons; then, later, you unlock alternate attacks or entirely new usage modes for them. The guns are all pretty neat and enjoyable to use, with a few caveats - for one, I found that the minigun-flamethrower hybrid quickly loses utility in almost any situation, as its damage output simply isn't high enough to justify its ridiculously wide spread which, even if you use the spread reduction augment you unlock around the game's mid-point, renders the dang thing next to useless even at short to medium range. Unless you're fighting a barn door at point blank, you use this thing largely for the sake of hearing it roar. Here's hoping they fix that at some point.

Contrastingly, the assault rifle mode for the dual uzis tips the scales of weapon balance to the other extreme; it's so powerful, accurate and effective at essentially any range that you use anything else, again, largely for the sake of it. It fires slower than uzi mode or the minigun, both of which use the same ammo, but that just means it's that much more ammo-efficient. Its projectiles fly crazy fast, which often edges out even the late-game plasma rifle for overall utility at moderately long range. The rocket launcher, meanwhile, is mostly for people who want to run around for seven seconds every time they'd like to maybe fire enough boom in a single shot to take out a larger enemy. That's with the alternate fire upgrade. All in all, the weapon balance could use some work. My personal take: Get every other weapon up to the level of utility that the assault rifle uzi mode currently has. Tune upward, no nerfing. This game is hard enough already, and giving back some of the joy of using whatever weapon you like to the player? That's the way forward, the way to greater glory. Do it, devs, you know you wanna!

Bet you didn't expect a game like this to have a story, eh? Well, it does. More of a story than a lot of triple-A stuff out there, with some characters, a bit of intrigue, and a whole lot of curve balls. Also you get to listen to John St. Jon voicing the protagonist's dad in audio logs - as well as the protagonist's AI assistant, who is awesome by the way. Besides the story snippets that tie the world together beautifully, there are also some gratuitously violent cutscenes where Johnny goes to town on enemies in some of the most over-the-top ways imaginable. Did I mention this game's title is not a coincidence?

But what is a shooter without monsters? A walking simulator, I suppose, or possibly one of those "realistic" hunting games. Never mind, that was supposed to be a rhetorical question. The monsters range from "trash mobs" who rush you in large numbers and soon realize that was a mistake, to a variety of larger, tougher enemies who often have some really nasty attacks of their own that you'll need to zip around and avoid while you're whittling them down. To be honest, this kind of gets old. The game is admirably long, especially for something made by a small team, and it's cram-packed with all manner of bad, bad cyborg monstrosities for you to grind your chegg against.

The tiresome part mainly comes from area-denial enemies, I found. One enemy electrifies areas of the floor, limiting your ability to dodge around and making chainsaw charges a riskier option. Another enemy explodes into both a damaging burst, and a lingering puddle of goo that somehow harms you even when you're zipping through the air above it. Then there are the damn one-shotters. These enemies, at least at the Street Cleaner difficulty which I opted for, will frequently kill you in one hit with very fast-moving projectiles from across whatever cavernous room you're currently in, even if you have full health and most of your armor remaining. This ferments a frustrating sense of "what even killed me?" That feeling gets old. They're not kidding when they call this difficulty setting "Hard". In retrospect, I'd sooner recommend the second difficulty, or switching down to it once things get too annoying. You can power through, but are you really having fun? I suppose that's a matter of taste.

The greatest failing of this overall awesome game is, I feel, that the area denial and one-shot enemies actively counteract the wonderful feeling of speed, momentum, power and recklessness that you have for much of Episode 1 but which gets rarer after that, as more and more of these obstructive enemies appear; a prime example is the frigging Mortar enemy which commits both sins - nearly instant death flamers at close range, one-shot mortar projectiles at a distance, AND its mortars leave goo puddles, plus it has way too much health. There are also two separate enemies that employ one-shot projectile attacks, and though the game, to its credit, doesn't seem to have hitscan weapons, a lot of the projectiles, including these very nasty ones, have such fast flight speed that the difference is almost irrelevant. A lesser but still recurring concern is that because the game is extremely "busy" visually, with gorgeous colors and bright lights all over the place, it can be difficult to see the little yellow trails that indicate a potentially one-shot-kill rocket cluster is planning a surprise visit to your face. They don't stand out much relative to how damaging they are, is what I'm getting at.

The bosses are a lot, too. I don't even know what else to say, other than that, particularly toward the end, they are... a lot. You will, without a doubt, get your money's worth of running around dodging and shooting stuff, from this game. And then some!

All in all: A very, very intense, largely solid, fast-paced, varied, fleshed-out, gorgeous, in places somewhat flawed but overall impressive piece of gaming history that sets some very high bars. Recommended... for the brave or the foolhardy.\t\t\t\t\t
