\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a difficult one. Did I regret reading this? No. But I had my own reasons for sticking with it, like practising my Japanese, and an academic interest in Japan in general, and visual novels as a form of Japanese popular culture in particular. Did I like it? On balance, yes, but the same caveat applies, with the addition that some of what I liked probably did not survive the translation. Did I buy the soundtrack? Another yes. But the question is, would I recommend this, and I honestly don't know to whom I would.

If you're new to visual novels, surely there are better and more beginner-friendly options.
If you have a few under your belt, chances are you'll find it mediocre, and who has time for that?
If you're a veteran you've no need of my recommendation, one way or the other.

FWIW, a lot of what I liked -- the soundtrack, art, setting, and world-building -- can be fully experienced in the free trial. If that captivates you, even knowing that it doesn't get better than that, and considerably worse in some aspects, go for it.

P.S.: There is a free patch with additional content to be had on NekoNyan's website. Though honestly, if you're on the fence about these things, read without, you're not missing anything.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhile Senren * Banka isn't the longest VN I've played, it's certainly the one I played the longest, from February to June(!?). The main story was engaging enough, and each character's route diverged quite a bit rather than "same event but different person." The artwork was cute, colorful, and attractive, and although the girls tended toward large chests it wasn't as pronounced as for example Wagamama High Spec.

The voice acting was a delight. The voices were well-done and expressive. Each of the characters had unique speech patterns and affectations, from Mako's "choppiri" and Murasame's "wagahai" to Lena's "tatami-gami." The completely adorable voice failures were superbly bookmark-able as well.

The writing was mostly good. While there were parts I wish they'd done differently such as the infrequent occasions where main character Masaomi was a jerk, overall it didn't leave much room to gripe.

That leaves the ever-looming elephant in the room, Localization. The localization was solidly mediocre: I've seen better, I've seen worse, and most of the time it got the job done, but it was also inconsistent, ranging from a faithful re-telling to creative fan-fiction. The localization lacked the feeling of overall consistency and direction characteristic of a top-tier localization, with the same phrases used in the same contexts localized differently at different times. Lena's route provides perhaps the best and worst examples of this, where some of her catastrophic Japanese failures are explained clearly while other renderings are completely head-scratching. In other places the translator didn't even try, for example turning "It's my first time enveloped in such soft fluffiness" into "I'm getting a lap pillow."

Fortunately, the game engine provides bilingual subs, so when I was dissatisfied I could read the original myself especially in the R-18 scenes unlocked by the publisher patch, which tended into the head-scratching realm.

Senren * Banka is not the deepest or most emotionally moving or twisted or surprising or beautiful or captivating but the content is good and there is a lot of it. The pacing worked perfectly for shorter reading sessions when I'm home from work or between doing other things. I see no reason to discourage buying it, and if you have any questions the free demo should let you know if you'd like it or not.

I was looking forward to completing the game so I could move on to others, but the feeling of completion is bitter-sweet:
It's been a long time since I wasn't playing Senren * Banka, and I'm going to miss hearing everyone's voices now that I'm all done.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tVery very solid VN from the makers of Sabbat of the Witch. Compared to Sabbat, which is a very stock-standard school club romance VN, this one adds a bit of spice by focusing on more fantastical elements. Still a highschool setting, but the school itself is really on the periphery--the focus is more on the rich lore of the small village our main character finds himself in. Each route helps build a more complete picture of just what happened in the past of this town, and no one heroine really takes the center stage like Nene in Sabbat did.

What results from this change in focus is that Senren Banka feels a bit more unfocused than Sabbat, with a story that feels fractured between all the routes--there is no "true" route that pulls all the elements together into a cohesive whole. Each route ends with valuable context missing for why things happen. That said, it's ALSO nice that no heroine feels particularly lesser in importance, Pick a heroine without fear that she's the one that got added in just to check a box off.

That said, Murasame was far and away my favorite of the girls. 10/10 would hand feed again.\t\t\t\t\t
