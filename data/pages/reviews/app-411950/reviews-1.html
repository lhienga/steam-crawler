\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis was my first full-fidelity module for DCS. I haven't tried any other full-fidelity modules (notably, the f16), so do bear that in mind. That said, I am very satisfied with my purchase and I am glad that I chose the hornet. The biggest advantage of this module is not the plane itself or any of its characteristics (although it is pretty easy to fly), but rather the huge amount of free content that people have put out on the internet to help new hornet pilots. There is also a ton of singleplayer content for the hornet if you're into that.

Some things to note: the hornet is extremely versatile, but this can also be something of a trap. For example, as a new player, I wouldn't factor the Hornet's ability to do carrier landings into my purchase—carrier landings are quite difficult and you're not going to be comfortable with carrier operations for quite some time. More generally, it's easy to get carried away trying to learn all of the various capabilities of the hornet and to feel somewhat overwhelmed. I'd recommend focusing on learning one thing at a time instead of trying to teach yourself all of its systems at once. If this is a concern—and you want a plane with a more specialized mission profile—I would tentatively recommend the f16 instead of the hornet. Again, I've never tried the f16 myself, but the f18 is a bit more of a bomb truck (although it's still competitive in air to air) while the f16 excels at BFM and dogfighting.\t\t\t\t\t

Looks like a life-long relationship with it, with enough complexity yet handed over in a streamlined fashion.
Might not be king of BVR, or dogfights, or ground attack, but it does all of it well enough to make it enjoyable for all those aspects. Perfect for a night ride, or during the day, from the very bottom of the South Atlantic, through the deserts of the Middle East, and running across the mountains of Caucasus.

PROS:
Perhaps one of the most versatile aircraft we have in DCS, rivaling the F-15E, and the F-16C, if not surpassing both in versatility. Good avionics, which are very straight-forward, easy to learn, and only somewhat hard to master. I am pretty sure that many people often have bias and swear by a type of aircraft / DCS module. I swear by the Hornet. It is not the perfect fighter, or the perfect strike aircraft, but it's the perfect multi-role aircraft. Lots of campaigns. Carrier operations. Sexy cockpit.

CONS:
High fuel consumption, and a relatively low fuel amount for that.
Addictive.
Carrier operations.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis was my first full fidelity module, which I bought about five years ago and I’ve more than got my money’s worth. It’s easily the most versatile aircraft in the game and it’s fairly forgiving on new pilots. The systems, while complex, are relatively easy to use with a moderate level of study and effort. This jet is a Swiss army knife, a jack of all trade’s striker with a dizzying array of weapons to use It’s not the best at everything but it will do just about anything that you want it to. That said, there are a few things you should know about this module before you drop your hard-earned cash.

Air to Air
It has the full range of modern radar and IR guided missiles. The radar, while good, does take a bit practice to fully understand and utilize. It tends to spot fighter sized targets around the 50-mile mark, so your competitive at beyond visual range. The helmet mounted sight allows for easier target tracking and high off bore site missile shots, thanks to the X variant of the sidewinder missile.

It’s not the best and can be slightly out classed in areas like long range fights and speed by the F-15 or F-16 but it’s up there and can absolutely hold its own in a dog fight. The gun sight is clear and at low speeds you can get your nose to point just about anywhere you want it to. It has problems regaining speed and is a bit of a fuel hog but it’s good enough to get you to and from your target.

Air to Ground
This is where the Hornet really shines, particularly in the strike and SAM suppression mission sets. The list of things that you can drop on stuff is mind blowing. Laser and GPS guided weapons to small cruise and anti-radiation missiles the “bug’ has got whatever tool you need to put warheads on foreheads. The two targeting pods that are available are more than enough to find targets and even put dumb general purpose or cluster bombs down with a good amount of accuracy.

That said, it is also not perfect. It is gets held back but it’s limited carrying capacity and fuel consumption. No where is this more apparent than when doing Close Air Support (CAS) missions, for which the A-10 is much better suited. Generally requiring a bit more loiter time, it can be frustrating to keep hitting a low fuel state while looking for targets and once you do find them you can usually only drop a few bombs before your out of ordinance. There are external fuel tanks to help increase the range but it comes at the expense of bomb load so it’s a constant trade off. Either way, you’re going to have to get really good a fuel management (thank you FPAS) and or air to air refueling.

General Systems
In terms of what’s in the Hornet, it’s a 4th generation multi role fighter so it’s got all the bells and whistles. Air and ground radars, targeting pods, countermeasures suite with a jammer, flares and chaff, as well as modern radios. As far as navigation is concerned it has an INS/GPS which uses a way point system for navigation and targeting. Its got systems to help locate airfields, the carrier and radio beacons and in pinch you can enter your own way points on the fly. Seriously, if you get lost in a Hornet, it’s because you wanted to.

Carrier Ops
Along with all the aforementioned capabilities, this aircraft can take off from and land on a carrier. This is a completely optional game play element but one that is an experience unto itself and totally worth the effort and sometimes aggravation of taking the time to learn. The game does come with the free Stennis carrier model so it doesn’t cost anything extra. There is the Super Carrier module, which does add a ridiculous amount of immersion to the game play experience but is not required and does cost extra. I have it, I like it but I wouldn’t recommend buying it until you know DCS is going to be your thing. Even then I would recommend waiting for one of the many sales.

Commitment
Be warned, this is a “study level simulation of a complex fighter jet. It will take time to learn much less “get good”. That said, the Hornet is easy to learn but tough to master. The “push button” nature of everything in the cockpit makes doing most things fairly easy. There are a large number of systems to learn from the radar to the radios, all of which will require practice. These are on top of learning basic piloting skills such as takeoff, landing, navigation, in-flight refueling and basic tactics. Depending on how much free time you have, it could take weeks or months to feel comfortable with it.

I don’t say this to put anyone off buying it. The Hornet is a fantastic module, worth the money but does require an investment of time and energy to get the most out of. This has been my module of choice for the last 5 years and I would just now consider myself a competent virtual pilot with it. The good news is that the DCS community has taken it upon itself to develop a ton material to help new people learn a particular module and the F-18 is no different. Everything from manuals to videos are available there is something for everyone to use.

Being a visual learning, I used a lot of videos to get information. I found the Grim Reapers, in particular, to be the most helpful when starting out. The videos are easy to follow and specialize in helping someone with no experience gain a rudimentary knowledge of a particular system. Thy teach you how to put the thing on the thing to blow up the thing. Being able to quickly learn how to do things really helped me to start enjoying the Hornet rather than just being frustrated with it.

Summary
So, would I recommend this module? Yes, I would but with a caveat. I wouldn’t buy this module if you were just trying out DCS. In that event I would suggest the flaming cliffs 3 module which comes with a lot low fidelity aircraft on which you can practice basic pilot skills with a lower end flight stick and a minimum of key bindings. That said, if you’ve decided DCS is for you and if you don’t know what module your interested in, I would humbly submit the Hornet for your consideration. Its systems make it easy to fly for new pilots, it can do just about anything you need it to do and it opens the full experience of DCS in one aircraft.
