\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFor thousands of years game creators have attempted to create strategy games that can replicate the battlefield experience. From pushing Pushing Blocks with Sticks (TM) to computer simulated grand strategy games, none have succeded.

Neither has this game, does that hurt the game? Not in the least.

While there are some factors that are gamey such as limiting the amount of armies that can stay at a post to 3 regardless of the size of the army, it's clear they mean to insentivise the player to focus on building up holdings (Playing tall) as opposed to simply expanding their borders (Playing wide).

Also the game runs great, my laptop is kinda old and ran this game like butter.

The game also has 100% customizable officers and Daimyos, so if you're like me you may feel entitled to create a clan based on an island filled entirely with Sonic the Hedgehog OC's and laugh evily as ColdSteel the Edgehog topples the Ashikaga Shogunate.

There are problems I had with the game however, it can get kind of dull with diplomatic missions often lasting entire seasons and trying to manuever the complex web of alliances of sengoku era Japan when all you want to do is smash castles can really damper your will to play the game. The game is also really slow time-wise and if you want to follow a historical path, good luck with that because once you finish a bookmark's quest-line thats where history ends and the game becomes purely sandbox.

**READ BEFORE BUYING**
Although I really like this game and whole heartedly recommend it for history buffs and casual strategy gamers, there is not enough to this game to warrant a SIXTY dollar price tag. If the game is on sale for about $20 then you should definitley consider it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a blast from the past, at least my past, mixed with a little new. Takes me back to my old Sega days with this and Romance of the Three Kingdoms. I still enjoy this as much as I ever did. Its not an easy game particularly, there can be a heavy amount to grasp, there are lots of intricacies, some a little complex to learn, but the quests and tutorials do help a lot with understanding much of it. The little cut scenes break up the gameplay a little as well as teach history. Creation of officers, is always fun but even without doing so just picking a random small clan and setting out to unite the country is a fun, if daunting, endeavor. The replay is limitless as things are random, from events to maidens being born, not to mention different ways to win and the created characters you can add. If you like strategy or management games, or history, or even putting your battlefield tactics to the test these are all elements of this game. Be the powerful general, and the silver tongued statemen, marry into other clans to give long term alliances or demand others weaker than you to become vassals. Protect your lands, form coalitions to fight others, wipe out other rivals, capture their generals or princes then hire, free or execute them. It's all here and all have their ramifications.This game is and always was a treasure.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThat this game doesn't review better is a sign of the times. Despite numerous setbacks over the years, Koei has still managed to bring us a fantastic experience that, in some ways, other key strategy games cannot match.

Only the proliferation of games these days has caused gamers to expect more and more.

Yes, it is not as deep and complex as Europa Universalis.

Yes, combat is simpler than Total War.

Yes, diplomacy is odd and easily exploitable.

However, let's consider what this game does extremely well, that other games don't.

AI and challenge! How many times have you spent countless hours in games like Galactic Civilizations and Civ5 only to find the AI has just been dinking around the whole time, leaving your expectations for an epic late game confrontation completely unfulfilled? For me, even an advanced game such as EU4 has done this to me repeatedly.

In this game, Enemy armies maneuver in a way that will take a careless player by sunrise. You thought you were heading to a face to face clash and suddenly the enemy god tier officer flanks you and wrecks you. Or on a larger scale, enemy clans form coalitions against you if you start to steamroll the map. Granted by that point it's not impossible to overcome, but I just spent the good part of a day fighting EPIC HOJO. It wasn't as one sided as it would have been in EU4 or Civ5. Hojo means business. Powerful officers and lots of resources all of which were brought to bear.

Thank you Koei for actually providing AI that makes all your preparation worth it.

Note: you can make officers that are so powerful that the game becomes trivial. I recommend against it - make some flawed and varied officers if you're into created clans. Also, playing as Nobunaga, Hojo, or Shimazu will result in an easy game no matter what. Good for beginners though.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs someone who was raised on the old Koei series, and loves just about any micromanaging strategy game out there, this game is pure perfection.

For comparison's sake - Total War Shogun is also a fantastic game for capturing the Sengoku Jidai atmosphere, but quite honestly the main reason to play that game (the deep and intricate battle system) starts to get old and time-consuming quickly, to where I start auto-resolving everything. Sengoku, on the other hand, is so extreme with the political and economic management that it starts feeling more like work and less like a game. Nobunaga's Ambition hits the perfect sweet spot between the two.

Even though the outcome will obviously be different than it was in history, I find myself constantly thinking that the flow of the game and the decision-making is as close to representative of what Nobunaga, Mori, Shimazu, Date, etc, went through in their day. Watching the armies move during an allied or a coalition war is a sight to behold. Development of the towns/castles is just detailed enough, and almost feels like a mini-game on its own. Diplomacy is simple, lightweight, and extremely important to pay attention to on every turn.

Possibly the best feature in the game, and one of the most historically accurate, are the provinces. You can only directly control so many castles, and the rest you must assign to separate and autonomous provinces. Once again this system is simple and lightweight, but extremely deep on a strategic level. You may find yourself completely re-arranging everything in order to keep control of the castles whose armies you wish to control directly, or even to take the load off of how many development/army commands you end up making each turn.

I hope this game does well enough to bring Gemfire back. They just don't make games like Koei did anymore.
