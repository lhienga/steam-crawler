\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOne of the more unique and fun strategy games I've played. I've always been a fan of Koei's work in the Samurai and Dynasty Warriors series. While I've wanted to get into the series, I've made the mistake of using the older games as sorta my original starting ground and couldn't make sense of it.

Now I'm not saying that this game is casual, but it's definitely a lot easier to get used to how controls work now. Visuals are pretty, and it's pretty fun to slowly watch your towns grow as time passes and you become more powerful.

As for battles, granted I must admit I've not quite gotten the hang of it yet, but it's fairly complex battle tactics far as I can tell. And sometimes you do get lucky and overcome a bigger opponent and feel like the king of the world.

For the history buffs such as myself, this game really places historical knowledge more at the forefront unlike the Warriors series. The events telling the lives of different warriors and lords is always enjoyable to learn. And as for the CG movies and events, they're rather well done.

While it's not perfect, I feel comfortable recommending this game to near anyone who wants to try a strategy in general.

9/10\t\t\t\t\t

If you like the turn based strategy genre then I guarentee you'll like this game, if not then unless you're willing to take on the steep learning curve (that the tutorial does little to help) you probably won't enjoy yourself too much. All and all I give it an 8/10\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI regret paying full price for this game for the following reasons:

First, a minor detail: the game never saves your display preferences. I need to go into the options to make it full screen with my monitors resolution every time I start playing.

This game is a real time / turn based hybrid. Once a month, you go into council. If you are doing something important, council interrupts. It can even interrupt battles you are currently leading. When this happens, you are given a set of menus. Each month I find myself checking every menu. I start with development, then move down the list in a very repetitive manner. It gets to be a grind.

Battles are determined mostly by army size and officer. That is because there are only three types of units, spears, horseman, and musketeers (eventually). The quantities of each unit are based on the total army size, not any personal decision. There are buildings that can make them better, but it really doesn't matter, because they all play the same. Every army is a rectangle composed of people if you zoom in far enough, and they move towards other rectangles on a plain battle map. Then the officers use abilities and one side wins. You could use tactics, but most of the time I just let the AI handle it, because the sphere of the battle rarely ever encompasses all the units participating in it (a unit of 1000 with a unit of 10000 behind it could get slaughtered, and the battle mechanics won't always acknowledge the 10,000 until the 1000 is gone).

Historical events are a farse. The game has no adaptability built in whatsoever, which is strange, based on how the events play out. If you make one mistake (accidently take something you aren't supposed to, make an alliance with someone you are supposed to fight, etc.), the historical questline for the clan is completely derailed. The longer the chain, the more difficult it is to prevent derailment, because even if you don't screw it up, the AI can, and often will. Somehow you have to know what the entire questline requires before the questline starts. An example: the name of the game is based on Oda Nobunaga. This questline can be derailed in about 1000 different ways. Fastest way to derail it: enticing a nearby officer who is spectacular to join you before you finish the first two quests. If he isn't a part of his starting clan, your events can't work. Also, this quest chain is very long, and you can't expand at all near the beginning. Any expansion (including building fortresses) derails it. The AI derails it on their own. Events that are supposed to fire won't because a clan got wiped out, or it isn't holding the right province. That would all be forgiveable, if the historical events didn't show that they can just manipulate the game to whatever shape they want. In one Oda event, the Imagawa just take one of your fortresses, no fighting involved. In others, clans spring up from nothing. If they can do either of those things, why couldn't they adapt to what the player has done?

The building system is difficult to understand. I only just figured out last night that castle facilities (which are labeled as equipment in the help menu), require specific development levels to work. You can't know this easily because the help menu doesn't explain the build requirements outright (no other castle structure requires development), and you can't select the pulldown menu to see the choices (and the requirements), until you can afford one. I found out last night that you can hover over a castle or fortress, and sometimes, if they might be able to make one, a list of possible facilities appears.

I could go on about how disappointing this game is, but I'll stop here. There is very little community support for this game so far. You can't even easily google things to find answers. If you want a grand strategy that is very challenging, far more diverse, and has a Library of Congress level of community support, check out Europa Universalis 4. 800 hours in and I still don't feel bored with it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis latest installment in Nobunaga's Ambition is quite good. The historical cut scenes and flavor are a nice touch and add a lot of depth and character to the game. The combat system is a bit interesting and reminds me of the Dynasty Warriors Tatics system with unit commander skills and traits.

The City customization is also nice and fairly easy to grasp, but difficult to master in order to maximize your cities growth.

Some of the translation issues from the Manual to the ingame tool tips can be a bit confusing, but this is to be expected frankly.

While it certainly does not have quite the depth of say Europa Universalis 4, it makes up for this in the robust and more personalized battle system. All in all, its a great title and worthy of its "Very Positive" Steam User rating.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNobunaga's ambition: Sphere of influence is a masterpiece, essentially a product of over 30 years of development. There are so many wonderful things about the game, but for me, the biggest success it's how I actually learnt quite a bit (and started to become more interested) in this period of Japanese history and the key characters in it.

One of the most brilliant parts of the game is how it is designed to allow players to effectively set up automation. On the surface, many new players might wonder why certain things (the resource model, battle system, generation of troops etc) seem so different to other similar games. But play longer, and you'll realise the incredible design philosophy of the game. Many similar games suffer from ''big empire syndrome'', while Nobunaga's ambition is incredibly fun both for small and big empires - due to the ability to set up effective delegation. The game shifts from micromanagement of individual units and bases to overall management of provinces and grand strategy. This includes coordinating huge attacks (eg: 100,000 soldiers) from multiple bases and allies.

Another astonishing idea in the game is the design of alliances. Unlike many games, in this game a player would often truly help allies (instead of taking advantage of them or planning a backstep). Marriage alliances are permanent and coalitions can be formed, where allies help each other to take out larger empires. It is almost like a movie or real history, where an alliance of desperate armies unite to defeat the enemy. You can win the game with having active allies (instead of having to betray them and conquer their lands)

Similar to the Romance of the Three Kingdoms series, the other thing I really like is the ability to pick any clan in the game to play (instead of being forced to play one side). It's awesome trying to defy history, such as stopping Nobunaga from trying to conquer Japan. There are also many other features such as the ability to create custom officers, clans and change BG music settings.

Overall, I totally recommend the game - I would rank it probably among the top three games I've ever played.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game is great, and there's a lot of reviews already, but I thought I'd give it a go , from a point of view of a CK2 player.

1/ Choosing your clan / Start Date
- Compared to the centuries spanning CK2, NA:SoI focuses on a sort period of time, the Sengoku free for all following the fall of the Shogunate.
- Like in CK2, Historic positions are hugely imbalanced. You CAN start with a small clan surrounded by huge ones, and you'll be crushed, as is logical.

2/ Economy / Demesne
Similar to CK2 demesne limit, you can't rule everything directly, but the game is very generous, allowing you to manage everything in a given radius from your homebase. It is not until you reach a huge size that you need to resort to regents.
However, you also lose controls of the armies of your regents, being only able to give them general directives like "Stay put"/"Attack this clan"/ "Support this province"

The economical management itself is pretty straightforward: develop a mix between crops/cash/soldiers and improve your infrastructure. The "Auto council" does a good job if you feel lazy.

3/ Officers
Like CK2, Officers are at the heart of the game, but in a different way. Almost all actions require an officer to perform it, so you need a pool of retainers to conduct your armies and develop your realm.
Each officers has stats (War vs Economy) and a loyalty rating. In theory, low loyalty officers can betray you if bought by a rival clan (up to defecting with their castle, if there were alone in there), but in practice, it's merely an annoyance.
Don't be afraid, no retainer is going to try to plot to assassinate your heir or launch a full fledged rebellion.

You can play a marriage game too, , in order to secure permanent alliances or make a promising officer you kin. But you will never inherit land that way

4/ Rule of Law
Japan is in the middle of a civil war so might makes right. You can attack anyone without any casus belli.
The only thing is that the Emperor can force a 6 months truce if someone bribed him enough.
Likewise, you can abdicate and choose your successor anytime you like.

5/ Combat
No surprise, NA:SoI is much more detailed and tactical than CK2. Even without going into Real Time mode, the mechanics of NA makes war very interesting.
- Armies can only travel on the roadnetwork, and a maximum of 3 armies can fight at a single node:
This "simple" mechanics is enough to demonstrate the importance of chokepoints and an efficient road network. A strong fortress at the right place can defend access to a whole region.
- Supplies. Soldiers leave base with 120 days worth of supplies and refill at each friendly base. This makes it very difficult to rush behind enemy lines and attackers may have to cancel a siege if it's progressing too slow.

6/ Events/Quests
in constrast with CK2 dynamic generic events, events in NA:SoI are both historical and tightly scripted.
Playing to recreate history makes it very awkward, since a moderatly competent player will significantly outperform history and will then need to wait for the "correct" date while refraining from expanding too much.

Some quests are also "unnattural" in the sense that they make you do things that would be suicidal.
e.g. an Historical quest with Kenshin Uesugi asks you to save a castle by destroying a 6000 strong army before they complete a siege, and it's set up in a way that you probably have no more than 1000 survivors under Kenshin unit (I had 58). If you try to muster more units from your lands, the castle will fall before you reach it, but if you charge to certain death with Kenshin, an event will trigger, with Kenshin going full "God of War" and annihilating the 6k stack by himself.

Still, any fan of Japanese history will want to trigger Honnoji's Incident and others shattering events.

7 / User Interface
I'd never tought I'd say that, but NA:SoI makes me long for Paradox's UI. :)
For a game that relies so much on retainers, the interface for sorting through them is a nightmare, with information inconsistently split on several tabs.

e.g. You conquered a new fortress and want to assign a Lord.
You have three buttons (move officer, summon officers, assign lord) that can potentially do the same thing.
let's use Assign Lord that shows all available retainers (i.e. not the one currently leading troops).
You can sort them by Leadership (useful stat for a Lord), but the listing includes retainers that are already Lords of existing castles, so you need to switch to another tab to know if the guy is a Lord, a Retainer or a Regent.
Once you found a retainer with high Leadership , you need to switch again to another tab to see if the guy is not also an Overseer, or the loyalty tab to check that the man doesn't hate your guts.

Long story short, the game needs an officer search engine ...\t\t\t\t\t
