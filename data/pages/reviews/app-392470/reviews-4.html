\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs much as I want to give this game a thumbs up, I simply cannot.
I LOVE historical games like this, and Romance of the Three KIngdoms (also made my Koei Tecmo) is one that I enjoyed playing during high school.
Sadly this game, possibly due to its source material (which ACTUALLY happened compared to RotTK which is a semi-fictional tale) is bogged down with trying to create a (too) perfect recreation of the Sengoku Jidai period 1545-1600.
The game all but forces the player to play within a very strict set of parameters, and to its detriment won't tell the player that taking certain actions will result in an a-historical timeline, aka, getting stuck.
There an in game "edit" feature which might mitigate this, but I didn't attempt to use it because to me it seems like egregious meta gaming. What's the point in giving a player this "open world" type of mechanic ala Total War or SM Civ, when if the player takes the yoke and runs with it, it could devolve into a boring cycle of Council>Develop>Diplomacy>War rinse and repeat.
If someone is fine with playing a game like RotTK minus the story, then this game is right up your alley. If you just want a game where you don't care about recreating the glorious Sekigahara or anything in between and just wanna conquer Japan for the lols, then go ahead and get this game.
But if you value history and want to feel like you're a part of something big and world shaping, pass on this old game or get it on sale.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo here's a review from a guy who's played this game longer offline.

What I found good:
(1) The game's music.
- The game's music is really great and I just loved listening to them even in the BGM gallery. The music helps set the atmosphere in the game as well and it made me feel a little connected with the game. I liked in particular "Winds of Ambition" when you play as the Oda Clan and "Fragrant Wind" when your clan grows to a large size.
(2) Character portraits.
- The characters all have individual portraits and I liked how well they were done and great they turned out in bringing more imagery to a certain general or character.
(3) The Game Scenarios.
- The game is features parts of Japanese History and I like how the game scenarios try to tell them through stories and quests. From "Birth of Nobunaga" until "The Battle of Sekigahara", I've enjoyed knowing more and more about Japanese History.

What I found lacking:
(1) The battles.
- So the battles in Sphere of Influence is pretty bland in my opinion. The army with the higher number of soldiers win most of the time. An army with a smaller number of soldiers can defeat a larger force but will need a general with better stats and skills than the enemy general. Oda Nobunaga's 3000 can take on a 5000 enemy force led by a weaker enemy general with ease as your general's skills and stats usually become a factor but the battles will become quite repetitive too.
(2) The AI.
- So there are times where the AI knows when to hit you hard and fast. There are times where it feels like the AI isn't trying to defeat you at all. There are settings that can change how an AI behaves inside the game but it really varies per CPU clan.
(3) The diplomacy options.
- In a game about conquering, diplomacy does play a hand in it too. The game offers limited options when it comes to diplomacy unless you're playing a historical quest in a historical scenario. The limited options are like; marriage, alliance, vassalage and submit. You can form coalitions against large clans however which can be another way of gathering allies other than making direct alliances. These coalitions however will most likely be used against you in the late game though depending on how strong you are which could probably be something that can entertain you for a while until you get annoyed.

Should you get the game?
- Well, I suggest you get the game on sale if you're interested in the game itself and would like to experience the game yourself. Though I found it unlikely for me play the game everyday, I see this game as something to get back into every once in a while and helps me pass the time.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tJump right in to Sengoku era Japan and create your own clan that is led by a cat, make an anime girl your top-dog generalissimo and turn all your generals into Adan Sandler, just because you could. Type your army and town layouts on spreadsheets because the game provides you only with vague information regarding everything! Have your mouse pointer disappear several times within single hour just to boot up the game to make it reappear! Enjoy a localized translation where every character's name is frakked up and their name is read the wrong way around: Are you looking for "Oda Nobunaga", the man himself? TOO BAD, IT'S "Nobunaga Oda!", just because the localization team decided that because the game is being translated into english, it's THE WEST, right, so the character's family name should come after their given name! just. WHY?! It gets really frustrating. Want to move all twelve Date-clan family members to your newly established province? Just browse through the list of 300+ officers, viewing only 4 names at a time, and hope you didn't miss a single one. Also, no, there is no option switch the names around.

Overall, it's a neat strategy game, entertaining enough to keep my attention for several days after re-installing it. A few glimpses of visual novel-style scenes play during the campaigns that establish interesting historical events. Figure out the basics on normal, then crank up the difficulty. The character naming frak-up with the localization/translation just bugs the crap out of me.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHaving played every single Nobunaga's ambition except Taishi, it's one of the better ones. But like all Koei entries, some areas are a step back, some forward (why can't they just retain elements that work well).

The good--city building, planning and development is probably the most interesting part and affects your progress the most, moreso than officer stats compared to previous games. Policies alone will greatly affect how quickly you can expand, which adds a lot of depth and variety to playstyles compared to previous games. Amass big armies by creating thriving populous cities, or create a spartan regime where you forcibly rule with an iron fist.

Unlock other Koei games where you get nickel and dimed, this comes with the Power-up kit as part of the base game, enabling for officer customization and other editing features (castles, land etc). A huge plus, which gives it more bang for buck than previous entries.

The biggest downside to the game is the combat sucks. Don't be fooled by the screenshots, most of the time you'll be controlling the unit from a very ugly top-down view, as zooming in doesn't help maneuver or micro your units. Compared to entries like Rise to Power it just feels so clunky, and I end up letting the computer handle the battles. Part of the reason is the castle storming makes you loose too many troops, so you end up parking the troops outside, where the enemy surrenders.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn my ongoing and likely futile effort to write a Steam review for every game in my library (#127 out of 555)... it's time for Nobunaga's Ambition.

Okay, I'm a bit biased here: I love history, I especially love ancient Japanese history, and I especially especially love the sengoku period. Koei's Nobunaga's Ambition series is one of the longest-running strategy series out there, and for good reason. Think complex mechanics and readable UIs and a strong emphasis on individual characters and diplomacy. Spheres of Influence represents the Nobunaga's Ambition series at its best, and I'd even go so far as to call it one of the best grand strategy games on Steam.\t\t\t\t\t
