It has supply, building management. Charector base military and polical exercises.

I want to give it a good review for the spying system alone as it's both realistic and often overlooked.
Logistics and Supply are generally where I start differentiating between a casual wargame and a serious one and this one lets starvation fight your battles for you.

The problem with the game comes from its lack of depth in anyone of these feilds.

For example battles can be commanded in real time like total war, but only like 3 units with abilities based on the commander.

Generally when I see vassals I assume the game had put some thought into diplomacy. Unfortunatly, I think the diplomacy feels abitrary and the politics with the emporer lack options.

Production and management have options, although the effect of choices are not always clear to the player.

I actually could see a game in the series being one of my favorit games of all time. If the company flesh out the game a bit more. But, at this time the game sits selved in my library.

Clean up the interface and get some inspiration from Total War Battles, Crusader King Politics and Hegimony Logistics and Decisive Campaigns Briefing and this will be 50/10. The frame work is all there, unfortionally frameworks do not get good reviews. This game is not worth the $50 so I am forced to give it a negative review.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI did this review after conquering Japan with Oda Clan in 1566 AD. In brief, this is an officer-centric war simulator. You head a clan of your choice and try to unite Japan as what Nobunaga, Toyotomi Hideyoshi and Ieyashu Tokugawa would have done in the historical events. I have played Romance of Three Kingdom IV on PS and I can also say this game has alot of the game concepts similar to ROTK IV with updated graphics, more content and set in the Sengoku period. If you have played th Koei's ROTK series, this game should be a familiar face and a must buy. Comparing to modern good 4X or historical sim, this game lacks some of the info-rich interface features (mouse-over info, proper summary tabs), in-depth battles and complex diplomancy. The good news is that the sum of all parts is robust enough to provide a satisfying game-play without bogging down with too much info or excessive manual control. Here are some of the pros and cons.

+Pros+
-Fantasic artwork of the officers, staring hours into the screen is less tedious when you have your leaders and officers looking hansome and inspiring. Officer-centric approach make the game more human and helps in the immersion.
-Story Cut scenes are beautifully drawn and historical events presented in an appealing fashion
-Simple interface - easy to intepret once you overcome the learning curve which is not very steep.
-Simple but robust battle and diplomancy mechanics, dont be fooled by its simplicity.
-Provide a fair deal of automation and delegation of mundane tasks especially during med-end game if so the player choose to. (formation of province and auto-council)
-There are unlockables to lengthen the game-replayablilty, new scenarios, movie and officers.
- Create custom officers.

-Cons-
-Lack of in-game info like those in modern 4x games.
-Simple interface.
-Generic voice-over for officers, too few variations.
-English translation errors but it is better than no translation at all.

My recommendation:
One of the strengths of this game is the immersion into Japanese History during the Sengoku Period. Some of the officers and clans have rich back-stories which helps in making the game more fun and interesting. I suggest reading up on history to enhance your gaming experience. For a quick summary of the events during the Sengoku period, you can search "Extra History" on YouTube, you can wiki more after that. These guys on Youtube made an interesting 5-part series to aid you in understanding the back story, you would look at some of the officers in a different way after that. (like Akechi Mitsuhide)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI was hesitant in buying this game but I needed something different from the constant 3-D player shooting games and this is it! Here's why:
1) It's like Civilization from the 90's (haven't played Civilization since then) but you don't develop technologies. It's not as hard as Total War: Shogun for some strange reason. This game is easy to pick up.
2) You'll have to consider a lot of things to keep you on your toes: 2a) season (I got to wait until September to harvest then I can sell it since that price is high, etc), 2b) placing officers (need to place him there in order to use all the available soldiers, need to place them there in order to develop agriculture, 2c) overall strategy (develop less soldiers within and more on the outskirts of my territory?), 2d) goals set for you to achieve based on history (hey, it provides an incentive to play this game on and on).
3) doesn't eat up computer power so you can play on your less-powered laptops

In sum, I didn't think I would spend hours on it but I did. I'm middle-age and used to love thinking strategy when the jobs I had long ago involved strategic thinking and did make a difference in a society I was in; but now I'm just a desk monkey. This game is simple to learn and not as complicated as people make out to be, and at the price it is now, I think it's worth it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOnce I found it, this game became my go-to for the turn-based strategy genre. I have always loved this era/setting, but I can't just play Samurai Warriors forever. This game does a lot of things right: blending turn-based strategy with real-time combat, including diplomacy elements in a military conquest game, and allowing for a great deal of development of your land holdings. Not to mention that the game is very friendly to new or casual players, as you can choose to have the game AI make many of the decisions for you, and even have it 'demo play' for you to see how the game is played!

But lets get into the finer details. I'll start with the turn-based + real-time blending element. Each turn starts with a 'council' phase, where you make all your development and diplomatic decisions. During the council phase, time is paused. You then progress to the 'war' phase, where the real-time element begins, and you can move your units to attack your foes and claim new territory. You can still pause during this phase, and do some military-based diplomatic interactions as well. I love this approach, because it allows you to have more control over each and every battle rather than just taking turns hitting your foe and rolling a die. It doesn't end there, however, as this game takes things one step further and allows you to actually go to the field of these battles if you like, and directly maneuver your units and decide when to activate their skills! This means that you can often win battles where the leaders of your units are not as strong as your foes, or if your army is not as large. In the end, strategy prevails over luck.

Another aspect of the game that is done well is the diplomatic interactions. Each clan will have a different perpsective on you, depending on your size and distance relative to them, as well as being somewhat influenced by historical factors (or, at least, KOEI's depiction of the history of this era). Some allies may be harder to gain than others, but with the right amount of work you can turn any foe into a friend and do a great deal of manipulating the various factions in the game to act in your favor. You can even take this one step further, and form a 'Coalition' of forces to fight a larger foe, allowing you to direct your allies' attacks and time your moves so that no foe can stand before you for long.

Lastly, but certainly not least importantly, there is a deep system of development of your territory. You can improve the system of roads in your territory to speed your army along, build small forts to defend key chokepoints, or even build new settlements to artificially increase your own holdings. You also can develop both the 'city' and the 'castle' of each territory you control, and this is where the system truly shines. It is more than just building one of this and one of that, as each plot of land you build in a city can be upgraded along several different paths to provide different benefits. Not only that, but some of these upgrades will interact with other plots of land and improve their efficiency, so it is not just WHAT you build but also WHERE you build it! The castles have a similar variety of upgrades, each with their own effect, though they do not 'interact' with each other like the city upgrades do. This means that you can take several different approaches with each settlement, whether you desire them to each serve a separate purpose, or even if you want to change their purpose as the game progresses!

Overall this game is really very entertaining. There are a number of different historical scenarios already in the game, and the DLC scenarios can add to the fun as well. The one downside to this game is that it does stick pretty closely to KOEI's version of this era, so there's rarely a standard game where Nobunaga doesn't rise to power. Though I suppose the game is named after him. However, if you do happen to (somehow) tire of rising to power as any of the various factions across the various time periods, there is also an 'edit mode' in this game which provides literally unlimited replayability. The main aspect of this that provides replayability is that you can 'shuffle' the starting positions of the clans in the game, completely changing the flow of any era. On top of that, there are many more specific edits you can do to the game to re-balance it any way you like or to simply punish the last foe who bested you.

One other little note to make is that there are also 'quests' in this game, and many of them follow a more historical route. If you want to get to know the characters in the game a little better, or to know some extra bits of information about the events that took place, you can follow these quests to guide you through the game (and to get some achievements as well). Or, you can ignore all of that and follow your own path to victory as you see fit.


If you prefer medieval Europe to medieval Japan, then perhaps this game is not for you. Or, if you are not as famliar with this time period, or you don't enjoy KOEI's version of history, then you may be turned away by this game. But the variety of player-friendly options in the game make it accessible to players of any skill level, and even if you consider yourself a 'pro' at strategy games you can still set the pre-game options to make things much more difficult for you if you so desire. All things said, this game greatly stands out among its peers in the genre, and I'm going to be playing it at least until the next generation of strategy games around, and will continue to come back to it even beyond that time.\t\t\t\t\t
