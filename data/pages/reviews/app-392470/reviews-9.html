\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI remember playing Nobunaga's Ambition all the way back on the SNES (which I was also addicted to). The game has evolved quite a bit in graphics and gameplay complexity since then but the core gameplay is still nostalgically similar and quite addicting.

Graphics:
Awesome, I love it. The picturesque style of the portrait of the generals and gameplay footage fits in really well with the game.

Gameplay:
Good macro gameplay with interesting military and diplomatic options (a bit simplified compared to Civiliation series but has a low learning curve that even non-strategy fans can appreciate). On the extreme difficulty the AI can quickly expand out of your control and simply crush you if you aren't aggressive enough or build massive coalition alliances.

The micro gameplay (where you choose to take over the troops for micro-management during combat) is a bit disappointing and a step backward for the game compared to its SNES roots. The main issue is that the troops move incredibly slowly and you are always on a VERY short timer for each round/year. You can barely get anything done before time runs out and your combat options are very limited and simplistic (Total War series beats this game hands down in how well its implements the dual macro/micro gameplay). This is why I basically ignore the micro-battle options and just play the game at the macro level.

Replay Value:
High. You can play as any of 20+ factions starting in 7+ different time periods and scenarios.

Other:
I may be a bit biased for the game because I have a soft spot for Japanese Sengoku history that was started by the original Nobunaga's Ambition on SNES.

Overall: I really enjoyed the game and would recommend it to strategy fans, especially those who are interested in Japanese Sengoku history. I do find the micro battles to be disappointing. Don't come into this game expecting Total War or Civization and you'll be fine.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWarning: this is my first time playing a Nobunaga’s Ambition title. I realize Nobunaga’s Ambition as a series precedes Europa Universalis, Total War, and Civilization.

Sitting between the tactically focused Total War series and the grand strategy behemoths of Civilization and Europa Universalis, Nobunaga's Ambition: Sphere of Influence manages to blend some of the highlights of both games into well realized strategy game with great production values. You will miss the scale of the battles of Total War and the complexity of planning trade/technology/resources from a grand strategy title, but this is my first time playing a game where I can see them both combined into an engaging experience.

Nobunaga's Ambition is partially turn based, partially real-time. The turn-based portion is at the start of every month. You have a “war council” and you get to plan all of the infrastructure/diplomacy/finances. This is much more substantial than the Total War franchise with an order of magnitude more lords, cities, things to develop etc. It really highlights the amazing map they have created of Japan. It's size and complexity makes it more akin to the grand strategy games like Europa Universalis, Crusader King II, etc. You don't have quite the number of buildings, resources, systems of a game like Civilization, but it is so close you can taste it. And unlike the Paradox titles, the buildings, cities, districts are beautifully laid out in the map, giving you some feeling of accomplishment.

Once you are done setting your projects, plotting, buying/selling treasures, the month will play out in a real-time fashion. Here, your focus is moving armies around. The system is basic, but integrates well with the empire building. Essentially, each city will have conscripts that you can call to field (a number you increase as you develop your city) and they will be led by a lord that you assigned there. You will need your army to siege enemy cities/break enemy sieges. Once summoned onto the map, they will eat through their provisions which they can only refill by being next to your own city. The further your army needs to move or the longer it takes your army to achieve its goals, the more expensive the war is for you. If you can’t feed your army, be prepared to lose your holdings. It’s an elegant system which ties together a lot of mechanics in the game.

Onto the battles. On its own, it’s not anything to write home about. You are basically turning and moving giant crosses in hilly areas/areas with some defensive terrain. Each commander does have some ability to call to field, but this isn't really deep. I immediately thought the battle system could be better, more massive. What it does do though, is tie well with the strategic layer. Armies you sent to back up your frontline will join the battle you are controlling mid command. It’s really cool. And if you have enough units in the same area, you can engage in a “mass battle.” Basically, an all-in affair with every lord/army in the area. What a ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ awesome idea! If only the battles LOOKED as massive as the numbers keep telling me. You can, of course, zoom in to see some detail, but it’s just not detailed enough.

And the diplomacy is great too. Unlike a lot of other strategy titles, here it is tied to lords and gifts. Lords do a lot of things, like lead armies, build roads, build infrastructure. But you can also use lords to help create alliances, convince other lords to defect to your side (or just agree to not to defend against your next conquest). Lords themselves are important, almost everything you need to do in the game needs to be carried out by the many lords/nobles you have in your clan, so convincing other’s lords is good way to weaken your opposition. And keeping your relations happy by giving expensive gifts, marriage, or employing policies they would like is part of your challenge as a leader. It is a refreshing take on the frankly abstract nature of diplomacy in most strategy games.

To top it off, this is one of the best-looking strategy games out there. The production value is the highest I have seen in the genre. The heart of it is the strategic map which is absolutely bursting with details between the towns, the minor factions, the roads, the terrain. But on top of that, cutscenes keep you updated with the politics of the time. Fantastically drawn and filled with betrayals, plotting, and men of great ambition, they do a great job to immerse yourself in Japan’s history and gives more life to the lords you command. It's a nice change of pace from the spreadsheets of the Paradox titles. But the problem is that I failed a "story" quest (sometimes, you can be too agressive), I felt that something had went wrong. It doesn't handle alternate paths very well. Perhaps there is a way to integrate this more into the system itself.

Now, there are downsides. There is a repetitive nature to it, eventually a lot of the towns/cities start blending together. Some of the quests they put in do not tie well into the game (IE, an invasion force that you cannot see). I wish resources were a bigger deal in the game, it would be cool to have a particularly rich mining field to defend or to target and then develop. Trawling through factions after factions to find a couple of guys you can convert to your side each turn eventually gets dull. There are also some ridiculously powerful factions in the game. I understand that they wanted to create some challenge for the player, but it creates a wonky piece of Japanese history. The fact that you know EVERYTHING about your empire (down to 100+ point stats for every lord) does mean that the computer will never be able to sabotage you if you are paying attention to everything. This can get tedious. So it’s not perfect. But for all its faults, this is a well-made and well produced strategy game, and you will have fun with it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a pretty interesting strategy game. It's not Civilization, and it's not quite Crusader Kings II either. There are aspects of both, but it incorporates unique gameplay elements to make it appealing to fans of those series. You will fall victim to "one more turn" syndrome as you manage your army, forge alliances, and think about if you want to spend ten thousand bucks on an old pot. The game is very logistics focused. Wars can be won or lost depending on if you sent out your troops at the right time. A lot of the fun came not from sending in a doom stack, but from navigating the terrain and predicting the what the AI would do. There is also a fairly diverse diplomacy system, as the game is character focused instead of nation focused. You can marry, capture, or execute officers each with their own unique skills and attributes. Crusader Kings II fans should enjoy that feature, and you can even create your own officers. If you are interested in the sengoku era of Japanese history you will probably find a lot to enjoy as well. If not, it may seem a bit dense and strange, but I still recommend it to anyone.\t\t\t\t\t

Nobunaga's Ambition is always a fun and addicting series

I've been following the Nobunaga's Ambition series since PlayStation 2 and I still loving it today.

Nobunaga Ambition: Sphere of Influence really do what I want since the first time I play the series, and that is to add some details in it. You can manage your troops when in battle and I love how much they make it so detail. Also, I love the cinematic scene and stuff. Managing the city is always fun and there is a lot of things you can do, like performing diplomacy with other Daimyo to increase their trust, upgrading your castle, develop your base, and much much more.

Well, there is however the downside of the game. First, you can get bored if you waited too long for an event to kicked in and second is that it's hard to know when an event is going to start and finished. I have to go through the steam community and find someone who write all of the event list. And the last but not least, the Hojo Clan is over-powerful in this game, so better watch out with them. They can destroy a clan so easily so that you can't triggered an event.

It's fun to play, I've played 25 hours in total when I write this review, and you can easily lost in time when playing this game. Like you didn't realize it's already in the noon, and you like "whatever" and continue playing.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNA:SoI is one of those games where you start playing, the next time you look at the clock its 10 hours later and you honestly wonder if you've been abducted by aliens and lost half a day, because there is no way it's been 10 hours.

If you enjoy Total War or Paradox's grand strategy titles I definitely recommend at the very least checking out a let's play, because this game exists as a kind of a middle road between the two.

Pros:
  • Strategy Map combat allows for flanking, interception, choke points, etc. (like EU4, but more in depth and much more satisfying)
  • Events add immersion, personality, and can help keep a game somewhat more historical, making the game overall more than just paint the map one color (Events are optional)
  • While you can only make civil development and diplomatic choices at the beginning of each month, the months themselves, and therefore combat, plays out in pausable real time
  • Spheres of Influence add a sense of fuedalism, speeds up gameplay, and are a satisfying display of power (Spheres of Influence can be set to unlimited for those who want full control of every aspect of their realm)
  • Much of the development can be automated to speed up gameplay and make event hunting less strenuous
  • The music is really, really good
  • After 150 hours, not a single crash; can't say that about most of my game library

Cons:
  • Battle Map is simple, feels like an after-though, and will be a dissapointment for most Total War players (Tactical Battles are mostly optional, with the exception of famous Event Battles like Okehazama or Nagashino)
  • In game achievements "Feats" exist but are not linked to steam, so no achievements for you achievement hunters
  • No mp3s for the amazing soundtrack, not even as dlc

It should be pointed out that there is now an updated version on Steam, "NA:SoI Ascension", which is probably the better version to get. But, since I don't own that version I can't really comment on it one way or another.\t\t\t\t\t
