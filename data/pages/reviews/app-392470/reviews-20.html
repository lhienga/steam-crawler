\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOverall Rating - 9/10

Graphics - 8/10 : There is nothing stand-up amazing about the graphics and there is nothing bad about them. They are more than solid. They do the job but there is enough there to actually make you think the game is pretty. The artwork in cutscenes is splendid to look at. The best part for me is the castles and how detailed districts look.

Sound - 6/10 : Meh. The songs are a little repetative and loud personally. They do not really provide any ambience and I found myself turning off the music itself. Voice acting is brilliantly done and there is a good range of voices that make characters feel different, which is good.

Gameplay - 10/10 : Halfway between EU4 and Total War. It doesn't have as in depth a battle mode and it doesn't have as in depth a grand strategy mode but it is a brilliant balance. It doesn't feel "light" on mechanics. It has a nice spectrum of things to keep you busy each turn. The battle mechanics are a little clunky but it doesn't subtract from it.

Replayability - 8/10 : Much like EU4 and Total War, there is a lot of replayability in so much that you have access to all of the different nations from a variety of different starting points but it isn't that different an experience. It is repetative but if you play games like EU4 and TW it is the same situation as those games. There are feats to accomplish but these aren't linked to steam achievements which is a little annoying.

Price - 7/10 : Considering games like EU4 and TW, the price point is a little higher than you would normally expect but it isn't overpriced per-say. If you like these types of games you will most definitely put more than enough hours in to make it worth it and if you compare it to a game like EU4, if you include all of the expansions, then it is a pretty good deal.

I would really like to see it get some proper expansions or mods possibly in the future. This type of game in the European setting would be amazing.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you enjoy Total War series but find the neverending battles eventually bog things down, this is the game for you. I played it on a "budget" PC with integrated graphics and it ran silky smooth.

The character system seems deep and kingdom organization is very addicting. The kingdom management succeeds because it's focused on scarcity. You have limited money, limited vassals, and limited "labor" (action points). Population helps build up action points and is required for a strong military. Investing in the economy is important, even investing in roads, building new castles, fortifying key points. There are so many choices but they are structured in a simple way that is not overwhelming. Your characters increase in stats as time goes on and gain new traits.

The battles are also great. They are smaller in scale than Total War, and they play quicker. However, this also means the computational demands are a fraction of Total War series. You lose some of the interesting decisions of Total War, such as specific units to produce, precise formations to employ, etc, but I found myself usually auto-rolling those battles as they drag on forever. In Nobunaga, I am excited for the battles.

The menu UI is very logically laid out. There are a few options that could be confusing at first, like transferring vassals, but the game walks you through what each action does. Speaking of options, the game has a great set of options both in terms of difficulty (even specificying how you want the AI to be more difficult) and for graphics/what not.

The translation is very thorough, with some good localization here and there, like the dialogue in the Tutorial. There was only one or two points where I felt the wording could be improved significantly.

I paid $50 for it at launch but, knowing what I do now, I would have easily paid $70-80 for this game and been happy.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI played the NES version as a Kid and I put more hours into the Romance games than anyone should ever admit to - so when I saw this I knew I had to get it. While I didn't set my expectations very high.... the tutorial pretty much doomed my opinion.

I don't believe I've ever played a tutorial which did a better job of turning me off from a game than this one did. The tutorial shows you how to do about 5 basic acts within the game, so that you can have some idea how the game's interface works. Sounds good right? If that's all it did, that'd be fine. But it also includes dialog between your character and someone advising them. If you want to actually read the descriptions of what it is that you are doing - you click your way through conversations that take dozens of clicks.... click click click click click... Otherwise you have the option of hitting the skip conversation button which fast forwards through it all - revealing just how ridiculously long the conversations are without telling you much.

After forcing myself through the tutorial - I decided to give the game a shot. To be honest, it's not bad. In fact it would probably be just fine if it weren't for the fact that there are so many other games out there. As it is, the game feels as though I have to go through far too many menus and clicks just to accomplish basic tasks. Nothing is so encombersome that I can point to it and say "AH HA!", but it still feels ..... Heavy.

TLDR: I found the game too much of a Clickfest to truly enjoy the game that exists here. 4/10\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you have only ever played the Total War series of games the first thing you need to do is wash all expectations you have away based on your experience with TW and make a vow not to compare them. It's apples and oranges and both can be equally awesome in their own right.

The strengths of Nobunaga's Ambition: Sphere of Influence as I see them are in its kingdom management gameplay, its fidelity in relaying historical events of this period in Japan, and the level of detail put into characters that inhabit the game. When managing your kingdom (or Daimyo-dom?) your various castles and towns have districts within them that you can upgrade to improve their perfromance at generating a resource (gold, provisions or conscripts) for your clan. There may be special locations in the area that if found further increase a given districts production. You can also add more districts to most castle/town areas after certain requirements are met. Add to this other infrastructure options such as road upgrades, fortifications, fortresses (not a typo, both are a thing) and you have many options on the way your clans lands look and play.

If you have never played a KOEI game and are a history fan, shame on you. Once again KOEI brings to the table a level of detail and polish on relaying historical events in the prescribed setting at a high level. If you like to read and enjoy story it is very likely you will find this aspects of the game entertaining. Unless you are a scholar in Japanese history of this period you will most certainly learn a few things. They also explain why certain characters did what they did in the context of the period.

Speaking of characters, they are what drive your clan. They have four abilities (Leadership, Valor, Intelligence and Politics) which are used to complete various actions in the game. They also have (up to 20) traits that give various benefits based on the action taken. Characters can gain traits through events while playing and you can also create your own characters and assign traits to them as well.

Lastly, combat can be handled at the macro and micro levels where in the former you joint point your forces at a target and let them handle it and in the latter you can direct units individually on the field, use leader special abilities and make other tactical decisions in a "real-time pauseable" environment.

Very happy that KOEI decided to release this game in english. I hope they continue to release updated versions of their other non-Dynasty Warriors catalogue as well. If done at this game's level of detail and polish they will be well worth the money.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a really good strategy game. There are many aspects to the game. It runs in monthly turns. Military orders may be given during the turn, as the month passes by. Deploying and moving troops is possible. Of course, during the Council phase which occurs in between turns, military deployments and movement orders may also be given.

The economy aspect of the game is pretty neat. You can do all sorts of economic things. These include increasing your economy (agriculture for provisions, crafts for gold, and conscripts for troop increases), construct special buildings in city districts, change city district types (food, cash, troops), build new districts once city population increases, improve defenses, increase road size (impacts troop movements and city populations). There are a few more things as well, such as building a new Fortress (small city).

Diplomacy is cool. You can improve your rating with another clan, persuade minor Tribes to your side (they give troops in battle or special income or you can even have them join a nearby city, increasing that city's population), give gifts to Clans (makes them friendly for a few months) or Officers (officers improve their loyalty), place a Lord at a castle/fortress, or even marry people to gain loyalty or alliances. If anoth Clan is at 40+ diplomacy you can request troops from them. Neat.

Military battles play out on the strategic map. Optionally if there are enough troops engaged you can control the troops directly, similar to the Totwl War series. Personally I use the strategic battles as I prefer them. When you surround a castle/fortress you need as many troops as that location's defense has in order to lay siege and capture.

A Castle has a larger town than a Fortress fyi.

There are a few more things you can do, such as hire ronin as leaders. This game has many things to do.

Anyway, this is a pretty neat wargame. Good game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you have been a fan of the NA and/or ROTK series by Koei, much like me, you will most likely enjoy spending countless hours trying to unify feudal Japan under one clan's banner in Nobunaga's Ambition: Sphere of Influence. I've been a fan of NA for almost a decade now. Having spent hundreds of hours playing Lord of Darkness, Rise to Power, and Iron Triangle. I patiently waited for the day Koei would release another one of these titles in North America. The wait was really worth it. Sphere of Influence surpass previous titles of the series in many way, although it falls short in some areas that made the series good in the past like certain feature they added or remove that I did not like (i.e. removing officer stipend, Naval battles, etc...), and city/fief building along with some other aspects of gameplay has been simplify. Sphere of Influence however kept the core aspect of what fans love about the previous titles and is a solid good title to reintroduce fans of the series in West to Koei's strategy/historical simulation games as it delivers feature rich engrossing gameplay, taking and/or enhancing certain good aspects from its previous two english titles, such as improving on and adding more depth to diplomacy, along with adding a few new features that greatly improve the game like clan quest events. Although the battle mechanics and tactics seem good but lacking sometimes, it does not take away from the satisfying strategic gameplay element that made this series fun to play in the past, If you have enjoyed the previous Nobunaga's Ambition and /or Romance of the Three Kingdoms titles you'll likely find yourself enjoying spending hours on end planning, building, scheming, and taking city after city as you struggle and fight other clans to unify the land under your rule. Nobunaga's Ambition: Sphere of Influence is a great installment to the titled series and a good game to get new and old fans interested in the genre for possible future titles. If you are interested in historical strategy simulation games then you should definitely buy this game as it is easily a 8.5/10 game. Well that's all the time I have to tell you about my thoughts on Nobunaga's Ambition: Sphere of Influence as I will now be spending most of my waking hours playing this game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOk this is the first game I've refunded. Why?

Well to start, every time I set the game up to my preferences - fullscreen, 1600 x 900 and such, it reverts back to it's default settings.
Secondly, I knew the game was going to be akin to the ROTK series. I knew that, BUT it also looked like a mix between that series and Kessen.
That was a bit of misrepresentation in my eyes, as all this game is, is a ROTK clone. Using the same art as they always used.

Let's speak of graphics. On the surface, it looks BETTER than all the games that came before it, but let me tell you...same exact style. Just little tweeks here an there.
I felt insulted as a consumer when the people of your town, zoomed in, only look like ants moving about an ant farm. Even less detailed really. More like pencil shavings lightly wafting across a piece of paper.

Just ugh.

they want 60 dollars for THIS???
no

Even at 50....no

There are better games out there, AND if you own an ROTK game from the PS2 era -- you have this game already. Only, set in China.

My advice. If you want something of the Warring States era, get Kessen 3. If you want something like ROTK, just pop back in an old ROTK game. And if you're looking for a new strategy game...well, wait till Warhammer comes out with it's new one, and just play Civ 5 till then
