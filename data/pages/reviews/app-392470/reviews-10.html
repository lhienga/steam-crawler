\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNobunaga's Ambition is a light grand strategy game with combat focus that succeeds thanks to it's extremely clever design bypassing many of the genre's flaws. Micro and macro-management are balanced perfectly from the early game to the endgame.
Especially clever is the addition of mass battles, which makes controlling a lot of units and sending them into battle a breeze. A key to victory is to strategically move your battalions across the set paths on the map, catch the enemy in pincer attacks and attack their flanks. This leads to you commanding several units. As the game progresses there will be huge amounts of troops on the map, which would normally lead to a lot of annoying unit management. Yet, with enough units in one sector of the map, mass battles can be triggered. These put your troops in a Total War-esque real time battlefield, with your units placed in relation to their placement on the map. This not only eliminates the annyoing management of every single unit but also makes the outcame dependant on your battlefield skill, not only the size of your army.
Warfare is clearly the focus of the game, with much of the endgame often comprised of huge battles on several fronts. Thankfully this aspect is more fleshed out in NA than most other grand strategy titles. In this phase you will also delegate all micromanagement of your cities to the AI, whose course of pursued development you can easily adjust.
Another feature setting NA apart from are the quests which have you following the historical course of events with lots of cutscenes and dialogues. You will certainly learn a lot about the Warring States Era if you decide to follow these. You can of course veer off course any time you want and just ignore all quests.

Nobunaga's Ambition:SOI is a unique game in the grand strategy genre and any fan should certainly give it a try. It does not have the depth of the games by Paradox, like EU or CK, but it excels in other areas and does it's own style of strategy extremely well.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGrand Strategy is one of my favorite genres. And this game has quickly become one of my favorites games in the genre. While this series may be new to western audience, Koei have been making these games for decades, long before Total War or Crusader Kings etc. Thanks to Koei's strange aversion to making money, we have only received 2 games of this series localized for PC in the past 15 years.

Overall, this game, is one of two the most realistic and in-depth "ruler simulators" I have had the pleasure of playing. The other one is Romance of the Three Kingdoms XI from the same series, available in gamersgate. It may lack the court and dynastic politics of Crusader Kings, or the detailed strategic warfare of AGEOD wargames, or the graphical fidelity of Total War, but all gameplay areas combined, it trumps all other games.

Pros -
1. Character focus - Characters are portrayed with great detail, with RPG-like attribute and skills. Most of them are historical characters. Every bit of gameplay revolves around them, from development to diplomacy to warfare. Beautiful hand drawn portraits bring them to life. Large part of the gameplay revolves around making sure you assign the right man for the right job. Much like history, the fate of the realms is based on the abilities of its lords and officers. You can even create your own officers with similar details.

2. Provinces - You can distribute your realm into provinces and let your officers handle things. You can further set guidelines for the provinces, such as military targets, production priorities, assisting and supplying other provinces etc. This is both realistic, and prevents unnecessary micromanagement.

3. Realistic Command and Control - As said before, the game is a proper ruler sim. It does way with most of the "gamey" micromanagement as seen in games like Total War, and is more reliant on broad decisions that a ruler is supposed to make.

4. Multi-layer Development - You develop your realm in multiple layers - castles, their surrounding settlement (villages / districts), the roads connecting them, and strongholds in road junctions. You also set policies for your realm. There are five main resources that you need to manage - gold, manpower, supplies (food+), muskets and horses, that are used in development and warfare.

5. Warfare - Tactical battles are nowhere as "detailed" as Total War. But they are a lot more realistic. It plays out in real-time. Troop numbers are authentic. The army structure is true to feudal warfare, much like Crusader Kings. You also need to consider supply and equipment (though this part is not as detailed as Romance of the Three Kingdoms XI, where you have to consider food, morale and each individual weapon types). The pacing of the battles IMO is perfect for a game like this. The diversity and details of the character system spills into the battles, with your generals having access to multiple passive and active tactical options during battles, leading to very dynamic situations. Best of all though, as with everything else, you can choose to let your officers lead their battles completely instead of micromanaging everything.

6. Councils - The council system adds to the authenticity. Every month you hold a council, and decide on the development of your realm, diplomacy, scouting, exploration etc. At any point you are lost, your officers will suggest actions that you can take. And again, you can be totally hands-off and let your castle lords decide what they want to do.

7. Diplomacy and politics - This part is not as detailed as Crusader Kings, but with so much else to do in the game, I think its detailed enough. You can interact with other factions as well as the Emperor who is still the nominal head of the country. You can have marriage alliances, timed alliances. vassals as well as form coalitions against other overpowered factions. The diplomatic stance (friendly, in awe, wary, hostile etc.) and rating (% number, used for diplomatic actions) are independent of each other, which may lead to funny situations however. You can call for your allies and vassals to aid you in battle with specific objectives.

8. Tenets - Japan during this time was conflicted between the old ways and new. That conflict is represented here as well. All the characters, as well as your realm as whole, can be progressive, conservative or neutral. This effects how well your officers get along with you, as well as the development of your realm.

9. Customizable difficulty and complexity - There is a plethora of difficulty settings at the beginning of the game, such as how aggressive the AI factions are, or how harvests, manpower, troop strength etc. are balanced between you and AI factions. You can learn the game easily, then take on more difficult challenges. The massive degree of possible automation will help you cope with the great amount of things to do in this game.

10. Events / Historicity - Thanks to the game portraying a relatively short time span in history, it can play out in a lot more handcrafted way compared to the generic campaigns of Total War or Crusader Kings. There are many historical events, accompanied by text + image based cut scenes. You are even given a bunch of progressive historical objectives for your ruler to re-trace his/her historical path.

11. Graphics - This is a tricky one. The 3d graphics is old fashioned, like something out of 2005-06, but fully functional. The 2d artwork however, is exquisite.

12. Music - Last but not the least, is the epic soundtrack. Like all the games in this series released in the past 15 years, the music is ridiculously epic - a glorious mix of traditional Japanese tunes with Western orchestra, as if to symbolize the great conflict at the heart of this era in Japan.


Cons -

1. UI and intuitiveness - The UI somehow gets lost in translations. You may need to spend some time going through manuals and in-game help screens to understand all of it, because the UI can often be unintuitive and esoteric. The game is not meant for the simple-minded: P

2. Politics (especially internal) - Faction politics can be a bit random, with diplomatic rating and stance contradicting. Also lacks complex diplomatic deals as seen in many grand strategy games. Internal politics are completely lacking, except managing the loyalty of your officers.

3. Family / Dynasty - Family and dynasty are barebones. Characters can be "kin" and thus more loyal. You can also marry talented officers into your family to achieve that effect. But that is all there is. No treacherous vassals plot against you, no ambitious cousins or brothers to usurp your rule, unless through pre-defined historical events. Characters can give birth and adopt, but you do not have much say in their bringing up, as most of them are pre-defined characters of the era.

4. Logistics - I prefer the logistical depth of ROTK XI, where you have to manufacture and transport individual equipment types and then equip your soldiers with them. Here it’s much more "streamlined" for better or worse (worse in my case).

5. Tactics - I sometimes missed the tactical control I had over units in ROTK XI, or even in Total War. But as said above, I do however still believe the available pacing and depth of the battles is more suitable for this type of game, which allows you to focus on being a ruler instead.

6. Duels and Debates - In ROTKXI, your characters can engage in duels or debates to settle various issues. This is missing in Nobunaga's Ambition.

Well, this is already long enough. I will end it here. I may revise this later, to make it shorter and add in any other pros and cons I may have missed this time. Overall, if you are a fan of grand strategy and prefer your historical games to be high on history and realism, or have an interest in the Sengoku era, you owe it yourself to give this a shot.\t\t\t\t\t
