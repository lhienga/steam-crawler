\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really appreciate tis game and the nostalgia it brings having played the original one 30 years ago. Much of it is very similar in feel to the more recent Romance of the Three Kingdoms games. I have always loved KOEI games for their attention to historical detail, with a very good balance of fictional flexibility. This game is a bit more rigid in this context. If you choose to play a clan in historical mode, you need to intentionally hold yourself from expanding like a normal strategy game and stick to the actual historical expansion of the clan. Otherwise, you can break the historical quests.

It is very poorly programed in this sense and there needs to be more information in advance to prevent you from breaking the storyline. The game should have been programed so that historical mode was not set in full sandbox mode. I have learned a lot of history about the characters of the era because I had to follow the wikipedia history of the clans in order to not break from the historical timeline. It should be more simple to follow this within the flow of the game (especially for non-Japanese languaage users). One thing that helped with this was to play on extreme difficulty, so as to slow expansion more generally.

I love the historical character editor and fictional mode. That is what feels right as a global conquest oriented playthrough. I made the mistake of playing on normal the first playthrough. This game is too easy when you hit a certain point (very early on at that). If you are an experienced grand strategy player, start off on at least hard or extreme (whih also get too easy when you reach a critical mass (also early on). However, it has sufficient depth to enjoy for a long time and many replays.

For grand strategy lovers, Europa Universalis IV is way better for deep campaign. The combat is also laclustre compared to Shogun Total War. Overall though it is an exciting and unique game. Experienced KOEI gamers will love it, but it has a lot to offer new strategy gamers as well.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPros:

* Largest map in the history of any KOEI game. Larger than the global map of Uncharted Waters. Larger than the map of China in ROTK. It's absolutely glorious.

* The building system is a fun little boardgame, and with randomized resources and tribe placement every game would play differently.

* The combat system catering to both grand strategy players who likes abstracted battles like those in Europa Universalis, or those who likes tactical battles like those in Total War. It's amazing that it makes both modes work well enough.

* Three distinct alignments on the progressive-conservative axis for your country to pursue. Both extremes and the very middle offer great and game-changing abilities.

* If you are into that kind of thing, female samurais! Even if you are not into that kind of thing, at least that's a way to gain randomly generated figures. I would like if they add the ability to have unhistorical male descendants for those who happens to live longer than in history, but ok daughters make do too.

Cons:

* When you have like 150 fortresses under control, you suddenly realize that all the fun mini construction game does not actually matter. But the "optimal" strategy still requires you to fine control them.

* Or you can give up and just rely on regents. That's actually a good idea to reduce the tediousness of the game. But there seems to be too much autonomy and the different types of regents are too stereotypical.

* Some tasks that can obviously be automated are not. Can't I have a "Lord queue" that automatically appoints lords to empty castles? With that system, I can still appoint if I want, but if I don't do, the AI will pick one from the queue. All I need is just to refill the queue from time to time. Also, what about I pre-design how a town to be built and the lord works towards it when fund and labor is available? I find I spend 2/3 of my game time clicking stuff that I don't really need to make decisions with.

* The policies are a bit poorly presented. For such a game-changing system, it should really use a better UI than a list of everything lumped together.

* A game is usually done within 15-20 years in-game years. Nothing wrong with that, but the pace is a bit unnatural if you start in the earlier scenarios.

A final note, KOEI is one of the weird companies that sell different language versions separately. Make sure you buy the version with your desired language. I should have bought the Traditional Chinese version (since names in Kanji look much better), though I do not regret grabbing the English version either.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSummary: 7 out of 10. Game offers a strong campaign and overall strategy options, but don't buy it for the battle map. Just don't.

I bought the game on Steam, the PC version. Bear in mind the game was made for console and ported to PC.

The good things: The campaign map is huge and features dozens of clans that you can play. There's no "fake" difficulty, so if you select a small clan with few resources, be prepared for a very diffuclt fight against stronger clans. Overall depth of design places it lower than most Paradox games (so less depth than CKII and EU4 - games which I have hundreds of hours in) but slightly above Total War games (the same). The map graphics look good and the camera has some nice controls.

The campaign or strategy (council) play is fulfilling. You can do a number of things each turn and you're limited by your resources. You have to priorize and sometimes make tough decisions. It feels like a solid strategy game. If you like this era of history, it's a worthwhile purchase for that alone. But before you buy, consider the bad:

The bad: The battles are pretty awful. They're not weak, they're just terrible. I've probably watched over 40 hours of YouTube videos of people playing the game and either just admitting the battles suck or offering lame excuses for why they function the way they do.

So what's the problem? First, you have no unit selection (other than a general guns v. horses option when you raise a force). In this sense, it feels less like Total War and more like CKII. Your retainers will raise forces at castles to fight your battles. It's just a group of warriors, all mixed together.

To be clear: this is NOT a Total War game where you recruit different types of units and build up an army that way. You just a mob and you move it over the map with other mobs. And honestly, I don't mind this. It feels realistic. But you need to know that before you buy the game.

Next, the battle maps quickly start to look and feel identical - as in little to no variation at all. Maye it was just the campaign I played (the Oda campaign) but after a few hours of gameplay all my maps were similar.

I've not find a way to siege castles and fortresses on the battle map. I've seen YouTube players actually fighting battles on fortresses, but when I tried to do it, the battle ends because I've defeated the enemy outside the castle and all my options take me back to the campaign map where I siege the fortress on that map.

I could go on but overall the battle map is a disappointment. The armies begin looking like Tetras pieces. You can zoom in but still it's only okay. You have one basic strategy: flank your opponent in a pincer movement because units will only fire at the unit in front of them. They will literally die letting another unit shoot them in the back. Yeah.

To summarize: This game has some Total War elements but is not a Total War game at all. That said, it you like this era of Japanese history and you can get over the limitations on the battle map, there's a lot to be enjoyed here. You can create your own characters and roleplay and/or immersion options are pretty solid.

Overall Rating: 7/10 (losing points for that battle map functions).\t\t\t\t\t

Sphere of Influence is much like its Koei predecessors- a game that rewards those who micromanage number clicks. (IE. Rice production increases from 580 to 590.) It plays much more like a board game than a real time strategy title, and while it is easy to learn the nuances, the game can be complex to master. I had not played a Nobunaga's title since the days of Super Nintendo, but overall I enjoyed it more than its cousin Total War, because of the personabilty of the leaders and the depth of retainers. The game is time consuming, but rewards you with cut scenes that give you background (albeit loosely and sometimes comically) about the history of Japan during the warring states period. The true question here comes down to value: Is Nobunaga's Ambition Sphere of Influence worth $59.99? To me? No- but at the $29,99 Steam Sale price, it was just right- especially with trading cards. The only real disappointment here is there is no real achievement system that applies to Steam.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFirst of all, this is probably the first grand strategy game I've ever played. The user interface was very daunting and the tutorial level did little to get me ready for the real thing (I lost / died in the tutorial and felt ashamed!). However because of its complexity, I took the time to learn everything so I played Nobunaga's first mission and followed the hints.

In the end it was rewarding to finally get used to things. I had lots of fun doing stuff like managing castles, sending out troops and staring at handsome warrior portraits (I'm looking at you Masamune-sama 😘).

Overall, it's beautifully crafted game, great for fans of the Sengoku Jidai.
Nobunaga, Masamune, Yukimura and Hideyoshi are my favourite historical figures from that period. Gotta love these real-life badasses!\t\t\t\t\t
