\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHaving played a bunch of Banished, this game hits exactly the same and had a bunch of things added.
After playing 50+ hours and reaching 3000+ population after 200 years, I can say I enjoy it greatly, but the game still feels really rough around the edges, and I feel it needs balancing.

Here are some considerations:

It has a bunch more processing pipelines.
For example, Settlement Survival adds water as a resource that's needed both by your citizens and some processing recipes. Wheat can be turned into malt which can be turned into sugar or whiskey. Animal pastures produce fat and dried dung in addition to food and textile items.
This can mean that production can become very unbalanced. If you want to make enough meat to produce fancy foods for your population, You'll end up with a huge surplus of fat. I make so much fat that I have a couple of hundred citizens dedicated to processing it into lamp oil and medicine for trading. And I still have to trade away thousands of units of fat per year just to stop it from clogging my warehouses.

It has a tech tree.
This lets you gradually unlock new buildings and buffs, however you are able to max out relatively quickly and after about 100 years I was able to demolish all my research academies as research points become useless after that. The whole paper/books processing buildings can be demolished too.

It has an expanded trading system.
Compared to Banished, you can only have one Dock, but you can have multiple land and water Trading Posts. In Banished, there are several distinct merchants that visit you periodically at your Docks, and you can make standing orders so that you'll get a consistent supply of the resources you need. You can stash your production surplus at the Docks to trade it away for the items you ordered.
In Settlement Survival, you can still stash items at the Dock, but the merchant always comes with random items. you can "order" 5000 weight units of specific items at a premium per year, but you can only pay with coins, and the prices are much higher than with the Trading Posts system.
The Trading Posts, instead, let you trade with better prices, plus you can trade both your surplus items or plain cash for the items you want. The only limit you have is how many labourers you can assign to each caravan. You can set up recurring caravans to "sink" your excess production and buy items you don't produce enough of.
Each caravan you set up needs to be assigned to a Faction. Each Faction has slightly different prices for each item (determined, I think, by how much of that item you have traded in that faction compared to other factions) and also give you faction reputation points. Faction Reputation points let you unlock extra buildings and recipes, including "Wonder" buildings which are welcome but a bit OP in my opinion.
In the late game, you end up with basically unlimited money than you can spend (thanks to bottomless silver mines, mints, and the Bank), and citizens become scarce as you can't expand your population beyond a certain point (more on that later). So, Trading becomes a much more efficient way of getting some resources (especially food and raw materials), in terms of items per citizen per year. So realistically you'll demolish many of your farms and production building to reallocate those citizens to caravans for trading.

It has a "population stress" citizen parameter
This is, arguably, to keep the population in check. The game struggles to run smoothly (on my 7 year old gaming laptop) after reaching about 2000 citizens. the "Population stress" metric increases linearly with population. You can push the population limit by upgrading all housing to garden villas with dogs, cats and luxury furniture/Toilets but that only gives you so much extra population. You can also build the Great Temple, which increase happiness in exchange for a couple dozen citizens dying per month, but you have to turn it off periodically or you'll run out of workers. I wish I could at least tweak the sacrifice rate, or the threshold for sacrifices, so the population can be stable.

All in all, it's a really enjoyable game. I'm looking forward to when it will be more polished and balanced.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm definitely the sort of person who enjoys simulation games (believe me, it's a good thing mid-2000s Sims doesn't have a 'total hours played' counter or else I'd be ashamed), and there's something about city simulators that is just really appealing. Perhaps it's seeing your little townies being happy, or maybe it's the God aspect, but hey ho.

Settlement Survival definitely scratches the itch. The graphics are really nice, the music really relaxing, house designs can be toggled through a small selection which means you don't have 100 identical houses which is novel, you can see little animals on the map (which can then be caught and raised by your townies) and there's a number of different unique aspects I haven't seen in others games; factions and trading, immigrants, random events, a technology unlocking system that enhances a wide range of things etc. The in-game water is also really nice, and I'm a sucker for lovely-looking water.

Various building instructions are very clear (eg. You need X to build this, you get X from THIS building) so it's easy to track how to get things.

However, there are a few things that are... a bit difficult; there's a gauge for how 'happy' your townies are. However it's not always overly clear what contributes towards them being happy, and it fluctuates wildly. Buildings that can be constructed to help increase happiness (eg. church, mosque, theatre), but the selection is rather limited, and they have an 'area of effect', this area is a bit difficult to manage because you have to build houses within the area of effect of a marketplace, so if you build your Happiness Building in the middle so it captures all of your houses you're sacrificing housing space, and if you build it outside that space you're only capturing a portion of the houses and the townies that live there. Having to build houses near a marketplace is another downside, as once you run out of space you need to build another marketplace or have people moaning about not having a house (which, to be fair, is a valid moan).

Early-game resource management is a bit dicey, but if you plonk down as many fishing docks as possible, as well as Foresters Huts, it does help! Some achievements are also a bit janky to unlock, unfortunately.

You also can't move a building once it's constructed. Honestly, if you could do that then that would make the game absolutely Top Tier.

Overall, I would absolutely recommend this game! Whilst it does have some minor irksome things I don't find it takes away from the overall enjoyment derived from this game, and I look forward to future updates!\t\t\t\t\t
