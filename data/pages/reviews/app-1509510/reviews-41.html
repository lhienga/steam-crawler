\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've seen quite a few reviews thumbing this game down with only a few hours played. Now that I've got 47 hours in it, I can tell you it's almost impossible to fairly rate this game with just a few hours played due to the massive amount of content it provides. If you want the short and sweet, it's almost everything I wished Banished would become. It's an amazing game with some criticisms here and there that I'll describe.

GRAPHICS/LOOKS/STYLE
Instead of polished graphics focused on realism, it leans more towards a cartoonish look and style that I'm not a big fan of. However, within just an hour of game play, I completely got past it's looks and found myself enjoying the city-building survival aspect that it's designed for. Another reviewer mentioned the UI gives it a FB feel when navigating through various info-panels in game-play and I somewhat agree. However, again, I found myself completely forgetting this once I realized the information provided in these panels were more than sufficient, albeit confusing at times. Overall, I would prefer a graphics overhaul that leaned more towards a realistic approach (as zooming in on your town isn't very satisfying due to it having that "mobile-game" look to it that just puts me off). Others might like the style and it might be a dev limitation, but it's just not my taste.

Some noted that the game maxed their CPU/GPU despite not having demanding graphics, however I only experienced this once my population started getting past the 1,000 mark. At this point, I had to lower the graphics (which really doesn't change much) and it never happened again. I think this is more of a limitation of the game engine (due to the thousands of micro-actions the population is doing) and less of how powerful your system is.

GAMEPLAY

-- THE GOOD --

You have two modes: Standard and Sandbox. Standard plays more like a traditional city-building survival game where you unlock buildings as you progress. I only played Standard and I ended up building a town with 2.2k population after about 42 hours.

Starting out it plays nearly identical to Banished, with some refreshing changes. You start with a small population, a few houses, and you start gathering resources to expand your town. Overtime, you will gain tech points that allows you to unlock additional production buildings and/or buffs. This will allow you to strengthen your population by giving them new resources to gather and make higher quality equipment. Equipment can have effects on individual citizens increasing or decreasing their health, happiness, and stamina based on what shoes, clothes, and tools they are wearing (or what buildings are around them). In addition, you can have them carry more by manufacturing straw baskets, pocket books, back packs, or wagons. It's all these small details that I really enjoyed as they made nearly every choice you make to produce something feel way more meaningful - instead of producing a better quality item that has little-to-no effect on individual citizens. For example, in Banished making better quality food from a mod was just for the aesthetics. However, in SS if you make a better quality food, it will fill them up more, make them happier, healthier, and reduce their stress levels.

Every time you progress with a new item or more people roaming around your town, there's a natural flow to the game that gives you a new goal to achieve to grow and improve your city. I saw some reviews complain that the tech tree was game-breaking due to them choosing the wrong path, however I didn't experience this. I guess I could see this being done, if you're playing it like a City Skylines game choosing to build what you WANT and not choosing what your town NEEDS. This is a survival city-building game, not a city-builder. So while there's no external threats or war in the game, the threat you're combating is dying from starvation, thirst, weather, sickness, etc. Thus, if my town was struggling for food, I would advance my tech into food. If my town was struggling with tools, I would tech into tools. This did not lead to linear game-play (as some claim it does) as once you solved that issue, you could then go back to choosing how you want to better your town (i.e. better healthcare, produce better tools, make a new food, etc.)

-- THE BAD --

Some production buildings seem a bit unbalanced, which makes it difficult (and sometimes near impossible) to play the game like the devs intended. An example of this is the Bathhouse. It consumes Medical Soap to boost it's base health effect. While you can turn on/off it's consumption , it consumes an insane amount of Medical Soap making it an end-game goal despite me unlocking it in early-to-mid game.

Another example is Fine Food. Once I was able to produce Feast (the highest quality food item in game), it spits out only 300ish Feast per year. This made it incredibly underwhelming as it's simply no where close enough to feed your large town. Meaning you'll need a ridiculous amount of production buildings to supply/make the raw materials to make more production buildings to make more Feast. So much so, it sort of just makes you not even care to try - because at this point in the game, your population is so large 300 Feast/yr is like spitting into the ocean.

-- THE UGLY --

POPULATION STRESS!!! While I LOVE the citizen stress mechanic, there's one stress level you will NEVER EVER solve no matter WHAT you do: Population Stress. It's NEARLY game-breaking. As more people are born or you accept immigrants, your citizen's population stress will increase. If your citizen's stress levels increase, they will procreate less. See the issue? No amount of healthcare centers, tools, churches, food, wine, etc. can bring it down to reasonable levels. PERIOD. Early game it's not an issue. Mid-game it's a HUGE issue. Late game it's not an issue. Really late game it becomes a HUGE issue again.

Early game, there's not enough population that it matters. Mid-game, your citizen's population stress gets high enough, that they are not procreating enough to replace the older dying off. Thus, you're basically relying on immigrants for population for laborer/workers, which is really high risk due to diseases they can bring in (so you have have multiple saves in case they wipe out your town). Late game, your rich and have enough healthcare to accept every immigrant group and ship-in/trade the best high-quality foods/wines to make your citizens max'd happy/healthy (though their population stress will be even higher now). Really late game, population stress gets to a point where even MAXED population happiness and health, they STILL have SUPER high population stress and they're not procreating faster than the immigrants you get and people dying off. Meaning your city just equalizes to immigrants and births equaling the deaths.

The last thing I really hated was the limitation of being forced to build a house around a small radius of Market Square building. This sounds good in theory, but it's so frustrating considering that so many other buildings have a radius that are NEEDED around your houses due to their bonus effects. You'll find yourself wanting to expand new houses, but you can't build a house because you need a Market first. Once you build your new market, you need a Church for those new houses, a Hospital, a Barber Shop, a Pooper Scooper building, and a Theater. All of these buildings also have a radius. So once you build those buildings that gives NEEDED buffs, you can only fit a few houses around the market! This could easily be fixed by greatly expanding the Market radius, removing it all together, or greatly expanding the radius of all the other buff buildings. This limitation just feels very unnecessary and frustrating when you're trying to logistically plan your city. It really forces you to build your town in a very specific way.\t\t\t\t\t
