\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBackground: I have about 700 hours in the main game, and have finished vortex campaigns with all of the 4 VC lords, so I have a pretty solid grasp of the content here.

Overview: This dlc adds an entirely new race, cobbled together from various table top and lore fluff sources (VC was not playable in warhammer fantasy). As a purely creative endeavour this is CA's best work. The units are beautifully modelled and animated, they have a distinct playstyle, somewhat reminiscent of skaven but with meaningful differences.

Campaign/Lords/Mechanics: There are 4 lords to play as, all playable in both mortal empires and vortex. Like a lot of game 2 factions however, 2/4 lords are much more fun to play in Vortex, as ME squashes their start positions, making (especially Cylostra's) them frustrating. Like base game 2 factions, all VC lords engage in a unique set of win conditions, thankfully it does not involve any rituals this time. Instead the VC factions acquire a resource called infamy, which is tracked by a leaderboard, featuring the other VC lords and a number of rogue armies. Infamy is acquired through typical gameplay, with an emphasis on sacking and razing as opposed to empire building. Top the leaderboard, beat a number of quest based foes, win the final battle, campaign complete. It is simple and elegant.

As I mentioned, The VC do not do well as conquest factions, their economy is bad, their garrisons are bad, it is much smarter to find a convenient province (The Dreadfleet start with control of the perfect one) and turtle there, whilst your armies go out and raid. This is thematic and pretty fun. Your main army and later others via tech upgrade, function as hordes, with growth and building options, consisting of buffs and recruitment. Focus on horde growth early, it is the key to success.

There are some smaller mechanics that all 4 lords get access to: treasure maps are acquired and heroes or armies can dig up buried treasure using them, regiments of renown are unlocked by beating specific rogue armies (other pirates, often on the leaderboard).

Current state: Whilst an excellent dlc at launch, a number of problems have cropped up since, I will briefly go through them.

1. Replenishment. Armies can only replenish in friendly waters, many VC units have large health pools and are very squishy, meaning that needing to replenish is common. This incentivises you to have easy access to friendly waters, meaning conquering territory, something that the faction is actively designed to avoid doing. This is really confusing. VC need a replenishment stance.

2. Quest design/campaign progression. VC campaigns have missions that push you towards being at war constantly, often with strong factions, this is usually more trouble than it is worth, as taking landlocked settlements as a pirate faction is pointless. Campaign line of sight has also been nerfed, making it harder to react to oncoming enemies, making defending already vulnerable settlements tedious.

3. Pirate Coves/Economy: VC have access to a mechanic based on the Skaven undercity mechanic, after conquering a coastal settlement, VC have the option to establish a pirate cove, instead of one of the normal options (occupy, raze, sack etc), these coves are single building slot, undetectable spy outposts, they remain even when the settlement is captured by a different faction. A number of buildings can be built in the cove, but they are all variations of two types, buildings that provide income or vampiric corruption. The problem is: they are not an efficient source of money, Vampiric corruption is useless for a faction that does not care about expansion. They need some work.

4. Multiplayer: upon release VC were arguably the strongest faction the series has ever seen. The multiplayer meta heavily favours shooting and single entity monsters. VC have an abundance of both. They have been nerfed somewhat since and the meta has evolved to favour shooting complimented by swarm tarpits or kiting. The VC artillery and ranged still remain extremely strong and they have access to good tarpit units, however their monsters are on the squishy side and their frontline units do not stand up to other factions best infantry or monsters. Good players will have a lot of fun with them, but they are in a somehwhat strange place in the meta.

Verdict: If you are heavily invested in the series, you likely have this dlc already. So for those who instead like to pick and choose which dlc to buy, I would say this is probably the best or second best of the race pack DLC (other contender being the equally excellent Tomb Kings) and one of the best DLC for Warhammer, period.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDid you like the pirates of the carribean movies? Are you a fan of guns? Do you like zombies that are a bit different? Do you like guns? Are you not afraid of water? Are you absolutely sure you like guns? Do you want to sail the seas and plunder every little bit of gold from the world? Do you adore guns? If yes then this is the dlc for you.

TLDR;
This is a faction all about gunplay. If your not a fan of that you will not like this faction if you do, you'll love it. Its the second race pack for Warhammer II and its just as high on quality and content as the Tomb Kings. The gun units are backed up by very powerfull artillery, some very good melee infantry and a varied assortment of undead sea monsters to break lines. Most powerfull multiplayer faction. The campaign is a very unique hybrid of horde gameplay and standard settlements that works very well. The loyalty mechanic for generals can be annoying for newer players or those that like to play more turtle-y. The new lore of magic and vampire coast mechanics are meh, not bad not good either. The recent mixed review score is due to a 3 Kingdoms review bomb, not indicative of the actual quality.

The Army and its Aesthetics
The Vampire Coast is a rather unique faction in that its mostly based from White Dwarf articles rather than the mainstream army books. It was a pleasant suprise. It was the most fan requested faction besides the Southern Realms/Dogs of War. Regarding aesthetics its pretty simple its loads of drowned zombies with guns, all sorts and manner of guns. To back them up you've got some vampires, a load of undead sea creatures dragged up on land (lots of undead monster crabs) and an ungodly amount of artillery. They are a very nice contrast to the Vampire Counts that they came from. Unlike the Counts who have no ranged whatsoever the Coast is all about ranged. Two of the four legendary lords are vampires, one is a vengeful ghost and the fourth is a human mutant who wants to stop the vampires. Aranessa is the mortal daughter of the sea god and the good guy/girl here. The Coast plotline is loosely based on the Dreadfleet board game. Alas there is no naval combat in this game. The campaign and army really give you the drowned zombie pirate feeling so its very well done.

The Good
The army and campaign gameplay really sell you on the faction fantasy. You truly feel like a pirate playing it. The coast is a hybrid faction in terms of settlements. You can play them as a regular faction conquering cities or keep your starting city and use your legendary lord as a horde and go out plundering the seas. Two of the four lords also start on an island making this a very viable strategy. Normal lords can't play as a horde except for a couple special ones you unlock via research. Lords can also go out digging for treasure.

The units of this faction are very, very versatile and enable you to be more then the shoot and bomb faction. You have living bomb units, shooting flyers and even possesed shipwrecks with flamethrowers (I mean really!). But it doesn't stop there you have a whole lot of thirsting vampires and angry ghosts and wraiths.

Like the Tomb Kings the Vampire Coast is not a participant in the race for the vortex so you can ignore that part of the campaign making for some nice variety.

The Bad
This pack doesn't really have bad points just weaker points. Firstly the lore of magic that is unique to the coast isn't great, not bad but not great. Realistically you will not use it since you've got access to lore of vampires and death which are better.

The Coast has the same general loyalty system as the Skaven and Dark Elves do so if you did not enjoy that in those factions you have the same problem here. The four unique lords don't have this loyalty system though.

The vampire cove system is a bit lackluster, the Skaven undercity system that came after it is better. Grab Lothern and Naggarond and then you can forget about it really.

Price
The Vampire Coast is of just a high a quality as the Tomb Kings dlc was and is a worthy addition to Warhammer II. It has a gorgeous thematic army roster and campaign that is fun to play. That said the price of 20 Euro's is quite steep especially given that the base game is 4 races for 60 Euro's. 15-17,5 Euro's would be more appropriate in my book. But 20 Euro's is not unreasonable either. Don't expect discounts above 50% though (they do come often though).


A final beware note. The faction looks great and is very thematic but if you don't enjoy using guns (I personally don't so I don't play them much) you likely will not enjoy the faction. So beware if gun gameplay is not your thing maybe give it a pass.

Overall one of the best thematic races that I can recommend as long as you like gun gameplay.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe sequel to the best strategy game ever made. Which is also the best strategy game ever made. Also the best use of the Warhammer licence in gaming.

Each race plays differently and each has a massive amount of character and charisma. The graphics and sound are lovingly crafted, nothing in this game has been skimped. Each of the factions have fully fleshed out graphics, sound and story. It all gels into an immersive atmosphere which can suck you in for hours.

The Battles are exciting to play and watch. With Dragons and giants charging into phalanxes, vampirates fighting lizardmen the game is both a tactical and spectacle masterpiece. This is a polished game. It was clearly crafted by a team that loves Warhammer and the result shows. A truly great game, worthy of soaking up endless hours.

The Total War: Warhammer games are similar to the Lord of the Rings franchise in structure. While each can be played separately, they truly transcend the individual product when played together. All the factions you own in the first game can be played in the second. Both in multiplayer battles and in a grand campaign mode called Mortal Empires. This game is brilliant. This series is a masterpiece.

I whole wholeheartedly recommend 100/10\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAn incredibly fun faction to play especially if you like gunpowder units. The vampire coast was didn't really exist in tabletop Warhammer and CA did a an amazing job creating them into a fully fledged faction including the creation of Cylostra which was their first go at making an entirely homebrew character. The units are very powder heavy with gunner zombies and artillery and at the higher end you have giant ghouls, crabs and the dreaded necrofex which is a great late game unit. It can be a hard faction to play if you choose to use the gun route as you need to make sure you position your zombies well so that they can get good shots (Tip: Always get a gunnery wight if you do this).

If you want to play competitive multiplayer they can be great in the hands of a specialist but have fallen a little behind in recent times and are due a buff to some of their units. For the multiplayer side I definitely watching content creators like Enticity and Turin to see on their channels great use of this faction by various pro players.\t\t\t\t\t
