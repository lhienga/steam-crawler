\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI genuinely believe it improves on the vanilla game.

Improvements and additions:

- Another hub area which is superior to Astera for the sole reason that the Gathering Hub is actually worth using - you can access most things of worth from it. The vibe is also nice, too.

- A new locale, which is the biggest(?) of the bunch, and remains as interesting and well laid-out as all the rest. On top of this, a post-game locale which allows for infinite hunting of 90% of the monsters in the game, which I overall enjoy because it's a great time to just hunt with buddies or hunters around the world with much fewer repercussions for failure.

- New monsters and quests, of course! Iceborne doubles the monsters from the basegame, and even though some are variants (AKA reskinned monsters from vanilla), they are sufficiently unique in their new ways and patterns of attacking that it doesn't feel overly unoriginal.

- A postgame that, while rather grindy, does have some genuinely great content in it that doesn't fall into the trap of assuming that players just want to play the game forever getting tiny incremental increases to their stats and gear after the story is done. It finishes with a hard but satisfying fight that gives you a set of gear that is easily the best in the game and says "Go on now. You are free to play other games. We won't keep you any longer. You won.". But if you want to stay, you can then continue to do hard fights and challenges with your gear and still have a good time. I sure do.

Negative points:

- The clutch claw is an unpopular addition because it's a tricky mechanic for a player to pull off in a game where timing your attacks in the space between a monster's is the key to combat. That's an incredibly obvious point about combat in most games, but here, the barrage of not only mild shakes that a monster can make but also roars that come out somewhat randomly and environmental factors like other monsters coming in to fight, makes it difficult to consistently use it. And the purpose of using it is to allow you to do more damage to a specific part of a monster, or to create large openings to attack. I differ from the mainstream opinion a little here as I don't think it's THAT bad, but even then, I still can't say I like it.

- In practice, the post-game infinite hunting locale will not offer better general rewards than just doing investigations. The purpose of the area (beyond the fun of it) is to gather materials that are unique to each monster in this specific locale and allow you to upgrade your gear even further. But it is rather grindy. I mean, the whole game is technically grindy, but because ALL you do there is hunt with no loading screens or breaks to go back to the hub, it's very frontloaded. And it can be a bit frustrating to get some of the special monsters unique to that area to actually appear, as it requires quite a few hunts in a row unless you go for faster and yet somehow even more repetitive strategies.

- The monsters in this game are generally regarded to be on some kind of steroid mixture that grants them great mobility, speed, and attacks, to the point of obnoxiousness for some of the slower weapons (and even the fast ones to be honest). It is Master Rank, it's supposed to be difficult, and this is at least better than just outright stat bloat on the exact same monsters (which, to be fair, also happens for the vanilla ones - but most get a new attack so at least there's something there to enjoy again), but there's only so many times I can miss a True Charged Slash or have to chase a wyvern around in circles before it gets a bit unfun. This problem is especially exacerbated in multiplayer, where if you're not being focussed then you have to run after them, and if you are being focussed, the damage of the monster is high enough that you'll wanna run away and dodge.

- The final fight is Fatalis (I hope by now this isn't a spoiler) and he is the hardest fight in the game (outside of some of the weird ones like ExBehemoth or Ancient Leshen), and he represents an uptick of the difficulty that you are only vaguely prepared for prior to that point. I ultimately believe that most people can beat him, but doing it the first time is the hardest time and can take several hours if you've been coasting by a little up to this point. Even when you do beat him and get his armor, some aspects remain pretty unfair - ranged characters straight-up get oneshot by some of his attacks even with max health, very good resistances, the best armor in the game (his) maxed out in defense, which is a frustrating mechanic - it requires half an hour of something approaching perfection until you get used to it. To be clear, as a Bow main, I've beaten him ~40 times and on any given run, I wouldn't be able to guarantee that I could beat him 75% of the time.

Overall:

I like the game. Not much more to say at this point. Is it better than Rise? In my opinion a tentative "yes", although there is so much to love about Rise that Sunbreak might push it over the edge to the top spot of best Monster Hunter for me. I feel a mixture of shame and pride in the number of hours I have (though they might only show on the base game, not this specific DLC), and it's the most played game I currently have in my library.\t\t\t\t\t
