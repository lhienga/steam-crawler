\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHear me out when I say that I enjoyed MHW Iceborne but don't actively recommend it. Though it adds a ton of new content it comes at a cost of being antithetical to what the base MHW set out to do, which was to bring the MH series to a larger audience and to retain said audience.

TL;DR: If I had to give Iceborne a numerical rating, it'd be 7 / 10, strongly. I enjoyed it a little less than the base game, but I really wouldn't recommend it to people who aren't super duper into Monster Hunter, as Iceborne cranks the difficulty up (mostly in a good way) while introducing a clunky but mandatory mechanic that can be frustrating when you first start out with it. Additionally, post end-game monsters spike massively in difficulty, feeling more frustrating than challenging, while the Guiding Lands (the end-game loop where you collect materials to augment and enhance gear as well as fashion) is so poorly designed it really detracts from the overall experience since it is mandatory to hang out in there.

The Good:
-Massively improved hub-world. Everything you need in a centralized location, no more shuffling through levels of Astera.
-Stunning environment that's wonderfully alive in all the right ways, without being overwhelming to look at or a chore to explore.
-New skills add more combat options and better utility / offensive combinations allowing players more freedom when it comes to what they build.
-Clutch claw combos really open up more options in combat, which is really nice if your weapon has a good clutch claw combo. If you've got Slinger Burst instead, I don't know what to tell you as a CB main, cause I've never used it once.

The Neutral:
-Once again, the story is incomprehensible. Though it's much easier to follow along than the base game, it really doesn't make too too much sense.
-There's a lot of grinding to do, and it's really not super duper intuitive. Your progression is super staggered compared to the base game and it upsets the pacing of the story and introduction of monsters. Armor spheres for instance are nigh mandatory due to the fact that un-upgraded gear doesn't protect all that well, so one mistake may cost you your life or push you to spend the next 20 seconds healing. This point goes in the neutral section, I understand that grinding is Monster Hunter, but I also understand how it can feel like a chore / alienate potential players.

The Bad:
-If you were hoping for decoration grind improvements, be disappointed. There's more of them now to further clog the pool.
-Clutch Claw (though an interesting mechanic) is clunky and flawed in its implementation. The damage and knockdown it gives you is incredible, and it does require skill to use effectively, but it's implementation and over-reliance on mantles can make it frustrating to use both for new and returnee players. Only being able to claw in cardinal directions to knock down monsters makes it harder to use and more dependent on monster position, all the while forcing you to wait for a good opportunity to use it should your mantles be on cooldown. It's tough, and I still flub it here and there.
-Tenderizing is again, a super powerful mechanic that's great in theory and super poor in implementation. In essence you whack the monster a bit after clutch clawing to it, and the specific part you tenderize will take increased damage and increase your crit-rate when you hit that part. It once again, is really reliant on mantles to consistently use, and this is primarily due to the different speeds at which different weapons tenderize.
-Monster balance is much worse than the base game, every monster hits way harder and has more HP, and the developers balanced the game with the mindset that you're a returnee player with oodles of experience and can rise to the challenge. If you haven't left, hear me out, because I'm not complaining about the difficulty for most monsters. The real issue is that if you're not a returnee player, and you're 100% new to the game, the developers have given you a specialty set of armor and weapon that breezes you so fast through the base game it genuinely doesn't make sense. There have been countless threads wondering why Iceborne is so hard compared to the base game coming from newer players, and 9 times out of 10, it's because they used this armor and weapon set and didn't learn any of the necessary skills to survive. They get dumped into Iceborne, get smashed, complain, and it just alienates people from what could've been a good experience. Don't use the Defender set or weapons.
-Back to monster balance, late game challenges feel difficult for the sake of being difficult. Safi'Jiiva, Raging Brachydios, Alatreon, and Fatalis are all either super mobile, super aggressive, hard-hitting, or a combination of all three. The difficulty spikes up strangely on all these monsters so it's not a surprise people got hung up on them, not to mention the fact that because this review comes in 2022, most of the players trying these monsters are new, so they don't quite know what they're doing either. All monsters fall when the player devotes time to practice them, learn their moves, and get gradually better at the game, but the amount of time required for these monsters while fighting with sub-par gear that can't fully protect you feels more akin to banging your head against a wall rather than a challenging climb to victory. Though eventually I triumphed, I can't help but look back at the time spent and wonder if there was something else I could've been doing to have fun rather than feeling frustrated by my lack of progress.
-The Guiding Lands are awful. It's a neat area, but like many other things, its implementation is poor. It's supposed to be your end-game area where you collect materials to further level up and enhance your gear. Yet it's designed more akin to a time sink rather than a rewarding experience. There's multiple areas that you level up, and when you do, the materials gathered become higher quality for higher ranked gear, and the variety of monsters (and their difficulty and drops) increases. But when you level up one, the others decrease. So then you'll have to go back and re-level up the different areas as you need them, which means again, the others will decrease. You cannot lock individual regions either, you can only lock the levels as a whole, forcing you to lose levels on areas you've maxed out in order to level up a different area. It's just not well done or intuitive.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tClearly a fantastic extension that add A LOT of content for the original game. I'm really glad that Capcom stopped to release "Ultimate" versions from Monster Hunter games and actually focused on an extension instead which is way more adequate.

The only issue that I have with the extension is the existence of the Clutch Claw mecanic who went Monster Hunter World from a 10/10 game to a 9/10:
Yes, the Clutch Claw (CC) added some interesting changes, gameplay wise, for certain weapons. But with other one it's just a pain to use it: mainly the Insect Glaive where you now are a "time-slave" who constantly needs to recolt Kininsect buffs and use the CC every 3 minutes...
It would have been a such fine mecanic if the gameplay didn't focused that much on it, which was clearly what I thought with trailers that Capcom showed to us before the release. Instead you get a tool that significantly lower the time of your hunt by forcing you to weaken certain parts every x minutes. Sure they added some QoL improvement by the time by realasing the shaver jewel and by extanding the time of a weakened part but it still feels weird to use it with some weapons. Also the monster animation when he drools completely destroy the flow of a hunt.

I would recommend the extension to anyone who liked the original Monster Hunter World, but I was really disgusted by the Clutch Claw mecanic and I wish that you will like it instead of me.\t\t\t\t\t
