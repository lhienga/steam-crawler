Of course, you may be looking at that "Mixed" score and wondering what's going on there. See, while there are a few things here and there that are valid complaints, I want to say that the vast amount of these negative reviews are likely out-of-date or plain foolish in the first place.

Let me explain.

First, Iceborne on PC was kind of marred by technical and performance issues at its launch. Of course, this was the MH team's first foray into a PC expansion, so it's kind of understandable, but some of those issues were REALLY rough. HOWEVER, virtually all of the technical issues that you see other people giving this game negative reviews over have been resolved by this point, many of which being fixed fairly soon after launch at that. So, plainly, those reviews are pretty out of date, and I'd even go so far as to say that the people who wrote them should fix or remove said reviews at that.

The second problem was a little endgame post-launch monster known as Alatreon. To put it simply, when this monster was added, it got on a lot of people's nerves due to a mechanic that perhaps could have been explained a touch better. I should know, I was one of those people who's nerves got rumbled (though these days I think that monster is a good fight once you actually understand that special mechanic), but I didn't go so far as to review bomb the game like some people did. To be honest, bad reviews of the expansion that solely exist because of that monster are ENTIRELY ridiculous; It plain makes no sense to tank the score of an entire expansion this good because you disliked one monster they gave you for free post launch that honestly has pretty little bearing on the rest of the game as a whole outside of its own quests.

Just... do yourself a favor with Alatreon if you get Iceborne: Don't go to fight him entirely blind. Look up the mechanics around his ultimate attack. Trust me.

The third issue isn't even remotely the fault of the game or its developers. It's Paul W Anderson's. Yeah, I'm being completely serious, the guy who directed the Monster Hunter Movie (which has loads of problems as a piece of MH media in and of itself) got this game review bombed. How? Well, I won't repeat much of it here, but let's just say that the initial version of that film that aired in China before global audiences literally opened with a 'joke' that those audiences immediately (and not unreasonably) took as a reference something pretty racist that ultimately led to a review bombing from Chinese Steam users, among many other consequences. Now, I certainly don't blame them for their outrage, but this expansion didn't do a damn thing to deserve that kind of backlash, and it can easily be argued that the MH team didn't either.

So, yeah, there's some pretty dumb reasons for Iceborne being at mixed rather than positive, and none of them actually reflect on the quality of this expansion as a whole. If you liked Monster Hunter World and want more of it, I'd readily recommend you grab Iceborne and have a time, regardless of that ugly little 'mixed' indicator.\t\t\t\t\t

What You Get:

-2 new areas: a snowy, tundra-like locale called the Hoarfrost Reach + a post-story area called the Guiding Lands.

-1 new move/mechanic per weapon, often slinger related. Greatsword gets the ability to speed up the process of getting to a TCS by firing a burst of slinger ammo, IG can give their bug slinger ammo to power it up, etc.

-1 new story, just as bad as World’s base story.

-35 new large monsters; 19 are subspecies/variants of either basegame monsters or monsters added in MHW:IB, 16 are completely new.

-15 new endemic life (do note that 7 are variations on one creature, though)

-3 new fish

-1 new house, with enhanced decoration and pet placement options

-Four slot decorations that can combine two skills or double down to hold two skill points from one skill

-New weapons and armor

-The Clutch Claw (it gets its own section)

Improvements to the basegame:

-New and improved endgame:
instead of farming threat level three tempered monsters for individual weapon augment materials and decorations, your endgame time is split three ways. First, there are the Guiding Lands, a new area available for expeditions where monsters drop unique materials needed for the updated augmentation and layered weapon/armor systems. There are five regions in the area, each modeled after one of the game’s other maps, and spawns specific monsters from those maps. Fighting and tracking monsters in each region levels the region up, getting stronger monsters to spawn. Then, as before, there is the threat level 3 tempered monster investigations for decoration farming. Finally, there are a suite of monsters which function as endgame bosses and which are more challenging than even threat level 3 tempered monsters. Their equipment is the best in the game, and they are also a very reasonable source of good decos.

-Vastly improved weapon customization through Safi’jiva weapons:
Safi’jiva is a multiplayer-focused post-story boss who drops weapons and upgrade materials for them. These weapons start out very weak for their rarity, but you can upgrade their stats. You choose which stats you enhance and focus on; sharpness, damage, affinity…it’s up to you.

-Mantle upgrades:
You can now upgrade your mantles to have decoration slots.

-The Steamworks:
A new machine has been added that will produce basic hunting resources (tranq bombs, mega/max/ancient potions, mega barrel bombs, etc) essentially for free, massively cutting down on the farm micromanagement side of the game. It is an excellent QOL update.

Points of Contention:

The Clutch Claw:
The Clutch Claw is a new ability added in MHW:IB for all weapons. It is a new attack that can be aimed with your weapon either drawn or sheathed. Using it shoots out a grappling hook with medium range; when it hits a part of a monster, it rapidly pulls you through the air and latches you onto the point you grabbed. Once attached to a part of a monster, you can use an attack to soften that part, raising its hitzone value and increasing the affinity gained from Weakness Exploit. Alternatively, one can (if attached to the head) use slinger ammo to launch the monster into an obstacle. This deals a substantial amount of damage, partbreak damage to its head and any softened part, and topples the monster. Doing this almost invariably causes the monster to enrage.

The Clutch Claw has both good and bad aspects. It gives players more agency, lets them reach and damage higher parts, and can be used as an additional option for avoiding damage.

On the bad side, it’s a very janky mechanic. The Clutch Claw itself has a very wide hitbox and significant travel time, which combined with how the game determines which parts of a monster are one clutch zone makes it very easy to grapple to the wrong part. Additionally, many people find the new necessity of constantly softening body parts to achieve optimal damage frustrating. Finally, the ability to knock monsters over by slamming them into walls is both somewhat overpowered and breaks the flow of combat; if you’re playing optimally, monsters are enraged for like 90% of the fight.

I think it’s ultimately not a dealbreaker; the mechanic’s been significantly improved since launch and shouldn’t be too much of a nuisance anymore.

Alatreon:
You may have heard about some review bombing that happened when Alatreon was added to the expansion. To make a long story short, Alatreon was given a new mechanic requiring the use of elemental weapons. It isn’t very well implemented, and it can be very frustrating (especially in multiplayer; never ever EVER try to SOS Alatreon), but the blowback was rather out of proportion. The mechanic can be brute forced through or rendered at least palatable with enough grinding, and overall Alatreon is one of the best fights in the entire expansion. Don’t let the hate worry you; you don’t even have to fight him if you don’t want to because most of his gear was made irrelevant by Fatalis’s existence anyways.

General Thoughts:

Meta Problems:
The meta is rather limited. Several sets, namely Fatalis and AT Velkhana, make virtually everything else irrelevant by virtue of being so much better.

The Guiding Lands Grind:
Somebody saw the great reception MHW got when it streamlined item gathering and said, “hey, what if we did like the exact opposite?” Now, weapon augmentation and layered armor is tied to collecting from bonepiles and ores in the Guiding Lands; unless you cheat in the items, expect to spend literal hours of your life running around hitting rocks with a pickaxe to get all the materials you need.

The Deco Grind:
It’s less ♥♥♥♥ than it was in base World, but only because there’s a wider variety of quests that give decent amounts of the best decos. It’s still an ungodly long slog to try and get decos (and sometimes multiple decos, with how many slots the armors have now) with like 0.22% drop chances; again, be prepared to go hundreds of hours without being able to complete your build unless you cheat the decos in.

Monster Quality & Difficulty:
Iceborne is an entirely Master Rank expansion, which is the equivalent difficulty of the old G rank. Monsters are faster, more aggressive, and hit harder than they did in High Rank; in endgame content, it is commonplace to get two or even oneshot in appropriate gear.

Additionally, monsters have more complex movesets on the whole, and in the post-story content, an effort was clearly made to massively ramp up the difficulty of the endgame bosses in a fair way. The four big endgame fights (Arch Tempered Velkhana and Namielle, along with Fatalis and Alatreon) are the most difficult content seen in either the basegame or expansion, and rival even such hair-pulling nightmares as the old Deviant monsters in how brutal their fights are. If the basegame felt too easy and too simple, Iceborne is what you are looking for.

Conclusion & TLDR:
Iceborne has some issues, but it fixes base World’s lackluster endgame, improves its monster count to reasonable levels by more than doubling the roster, introduces QOL features and new mechanics, and has given us some of the best fights in the entire franchise. I strongly recommend MHW:IB to anyone who enjoyed the basegame, and especially to those seeking a greater challenge than was provided there.

Final Note:
Not really part of the review, but just as a word of advice for those who end up getting Iceborne: please, FOR THE LOVE OF GOD, do NOT go in with a four man group against any of the four big endgame bosses (AT Velk, AT Nami, Fatalis, Alatreon). It makes them MASSIVELY harder because of more random AI and many more successful dodges having to be performed on the same faint limit. If you must get help for them, go in with one other player, and leave your cats at home.\t\t\t\t\t
