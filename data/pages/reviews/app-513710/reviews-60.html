\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI purchased this game when it first hit Steam EA back in 2018.

It looked promising, a unique twist on the whole survival genre with some cool mechanics that set it apart. On top of that it was developed by a Croatian company and I wanted to contribute in supporting my fellow Croats and even got a few of my friends to purchase the game.

At launch it was pretty bare bones but I gave it a pass. It is Early Access after all. I played until I felt I've seen the majority of what the game has to offer and left it aside, waiting for the full release. Over the years I would return to the game just to see the progress. There were some major updates which peeked my interest such as vehicles, base building and map expansions, but all in all nothing that would keep me playing for more than a couple of hours. Then I saw the cosmetic packs.

Honestly, an EA game should not be allowed to sell additional content. Yes, it's cool that Danny Trejo lent his likeness to a character in the game, along with Luis Moncada, but those are the things that the devs should focus on AFTER the game is actually released. And yes, I do understand those are just cosmetic items, but someone had to spend time putting those characters into the game which means less time developing the game towards a full release.

I want to see this game succeed, but, This is NOT the way.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWelcome to Scum, where survival is the name of the game... unless, of course, you find yourself inexplicably attached to the zombie buffet line. Here’s a rundown of my rollercoaster ride through this zany world:

Character Creation:

Creating my character felt like assembling a contestant for a ‘Survivor’-meets-‘American Ninja Warrior’ mashup. I could customize everything from muscles to, well, uh, some rather personal attributes. Needless to say, my Scum character ended up looking like a cross between Rambo and a professional sumo wrestler with a penchant for bright orange sneakers.

Survival Tactics:

Apparently, Scum wants to remind you that life is all about the simple things – like foraging for food, crafting weapons, and, oh yeah, dodging everything that wants to turn you into a chew toy. After desperately hunting for sustenance, I finally found a can of beans. Victory! Until I realized I didn’t have a can opener. Lesson learned: Hunger can be more lethal than any zombie horde.

The Wildlife:

Encountering the wildlife was like stumbling into a National Geographic documentary directed by a mad scientist. The majestic sight of a deer gracefully prancing through the woods was quickly overshadowed by the chaos of watching said deer charge headfirst into a pack of zombies. Survival of the fittest, indeed.
Friendship in Scum:

Teaming up with fellow players led to both hilarious antics and desperate attempts not to become zombie chow together. Communication was key, but it turns out that yelling "Don’t eat that mushroom!" is surprisingly ineffective when your teammate is already mid-chew.

Death:

Death in Scum is like being handed a participation trophy... for dying. When I inevitably met my demise (let’s just say mistaking a landmine for a pet rock wasn’t my finest moment), I respawned – minus my hard-earned loot. It’s like the game said, “Welcome back! Here’s a pat on the back for your spectacular failure. Try not to explode this time!”

In conclusion, Scum is a wild ride that’ll have you laughing, crying, and possibly yelling at your screen in equal measure. It’s a survival sandbox where even the best-laid plans can end up as comical disasters. But isn’t that the beauty of it all? Embrace the chaos, enjoy the absurdity, and remember: in Scum, being a little "scummy" is all part of the fun.

Hope that brings a smile to your face!\t\t\t\t\t
