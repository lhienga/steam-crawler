\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPeaks of Yore is a very QWOP-like climber with fairly simple controls, but presenting a huge wall of difficulty as you progress. The first few climbs are challenging but not too tough, and you will learn a great deal by completing them.
As you explore the various peaks you will meet characters who sometimes give you advice or crucial new gear, as well as find collectibles to display in your cabin. Collect enough and you get some other helpful rewards.

It is a frustrating game for sure, but while the graphics are very simple and somewhat stylized, the action itself is extremely immersive, and when you finally figure out how to pull off some dangerous moves that you thought were impossible until the second you actually do it, it is quite thrilling. If you like climbing games, of which there are few (this is not parkour!) this is a no-brainer.

This is absolutely a sleeper indie gem so far, with a ton of content especially for the price. I am a sale hunter but I feel very pleased with the price-point on this one. It's going to keep me busy for a while. Just when you think you are nearing the end of what seems like a generous amount of challenges, it opens up a few more times with even greater difficulties but also broader variety, it's actually astonishing.

You will also unlock a number of tools as you progress to help you advance, as well as find extra gear such as (critically) more ropes, by exploring alternate routes on the peaks. The non-linear way in which these bonus rewards are introduced means you can either perfect certain climbs to get the most extra benefit going forward, or forge ahead and perhaps unlock a tool which will simplify previous challenges. Regardless of how you proceed, you will feel yourself gaining reflex skills that will contribute to your next summit. Remarkable design balance.

The graphics options let you get rid of the rather quaint filters including dithering and monochrome; while they are charming for a time, they don't help with the readability of the levels. The keybindings may take some experimentation as well to find something that works for you.
I said earlier that the controls were simple, but they are also somewhat in-depth especially after you acquire some equipment. You can immersively grab tools from off your belt with a free hand, but there are also hotkeys to bring them up so as not to overtax your already-strained concentration, and as your options open up, so do the controls, eventually providing you with a selection of options to handle various hazardous situations.

The colorful dialogue and little bits of information you receive during your journeys are also well-written and voiced, often delightful like an aged cheese. This is not an amateur attempt at game making at all, it is fully-fleshed, perhaps even a masterpiece?

I will say now that I have put some time into the game, that the difficulty ramps up very abruptly after the first 20 "fundamental" mountains. Nerves of steel are required, while my own nerves are more like, well, pudding. Quick and precise actions need to be chained together with increasing perfection, and just when you think you've gotten the hang of what the game can throw at you, it pitches you another doozy, often way into the climb. There are situations later in the game where you cannot rely on a rope to save you, you must perform actions correctly in one try, or fall to the bottom. You will sometimes have to walk away for a bit and rethink your life.
It should also be mentioned, that if you have an RSI, the mouse controls are probably not an option due to the constant clicking and holding required. The controller support works fine, but it may be a little harder to achieve the same precision that mouse aim affords, especially in the situations later in the game, noted above.

Also, ♥♥♥♥ seagulls, man.\t\t\t\t\t
