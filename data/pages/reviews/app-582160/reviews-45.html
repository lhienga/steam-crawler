\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNothing like a round of AC VALHALLA to make this feel like a masterpiece.

It's impressive how your perception changes, in the face of experiences so weak that even the minimum expected is enough to please.

It's much easier to accept AC ORIGINS today, with the benefit of knowing how deep the franchise could sink. Even though the game itself shares practically the same problems as its successors, composing its entirety essentially of a large open world full of generic and redundant content and a huge amount of shallow, grindy, spongy and undynamic combat (and which becomes stagnant in the first 30 minutes) and a narrative that, although not offensively bad, makes as many mistakes as it gets right.

In other words: The same infamous industrial and soulless formula from Ubisoft, which fails to generate content beyond average quality and punctuates, very rarely, with provocations that could indicate a prosperous and richly artistic path, which the franchise, regrettably, was never able to blossom.

Sometimes I believe that the Assassin's Creed franchise is the worst franchise of all time. Not because they are the worst games I've ever played, but because it is without a doubt the franchise with the greatest wasted potential.

One would imagine that by making practically the same game within the same formula for more than a decade, Ubisoft would have refined the formula and transformed it into something unique and tasty. But no, each game just seems to get bigger and worse, lacking polish, not even coming close to reaching previous standards.

And as always, they never say anything about anything. They never use the historical setting in a meaningful way to enrich the narrative or articulate some larger, more sophisticated comment about something. They are empty theme parks, with practically no educational or philosophical value. Reducing the scenario to practically a pile of Wikipedia factoids.

They use very important historical figures as film cameos, even though these same figures are directly connected with the most important events in the sociopolitical reality that you are part of.

Without much commitment to developing characters beyond their nicknames, the villains are evil, just for the sake of being evil (And even in that they are not very convincing). The heroes are charismatic maniacs who like to scream to compensate for their lack of personality.

And when they decide to touch on more sensitive subjects, like the pain of parents who lost their children or something like that? Imagine how tasteless, artificial and lacking in humanity they approached these topics.

It's much worse than trying to be melodramatic and sounding ridiculous. They miss the mark so much trying to be profound that it makes me want to laugh just remembering some parts.

They don't construct anything, they don't tie anything together, they don't articulate any complex arguments. No deeper explanation. No elaboration that avoids empty and anachronistic political platitudes like "We are against corruption and tyranny, bro". It's so unbelievably stupid.

It seems like they didn't even try. It often feels like a game written by George Lucas.

You're left wondering whether or not you should care about anything, as the story they want to tell fails every attempt to build some sense of dramatic conflict. There is no narrative tension. There are no climaxes. Everything falls flat, every time.

Even explanations that no one asked for are delivered in the laziest way possible, probably to hide the fact that they were never given much thought in the first place.

You don't even want to know the origin of the symbol of the Assassin Order. I think I missed the deep symbolism of the scene because I was laughing out loud at how stupid that scene seemed to me.

It's really sad to see the art team's majestic work being wasted on such an average, dumb and derivative game.

It was supposed to be better. It wasn't supposed to be so forgettable.

In this sense, AC Origins, like most games in the franchise, are a letdown.

They captivate and emerge in their epic and immersive, ridiculously detailed settings, and disappoint with their artificiality and truckload of repetitive and banal activities.

Ubisoft's formula is so ingenious that it makes open-ended games with the potential for a lot of emergent gameplay feel so locked and linear. Static rather than vibrant.

But as a game in its own right, if you can acclimatize to the game's mediocre content and find a groove, it's not all bad. Especially if you've recently gone through a round of the worst version of this type of content.

It's much easier to accept because it's a game that does the bare minimum. The gameplay is responsive, it's not such an absurdly large game, it's a little more focused and once you avoid engaging with the game's zigzagging features and the grind of generic objectives, it's a truly immersive and functional experience. Especially if you focus on free and organic exploration, without being guided by markers.

It's a much more complete, detailed and eye-catching setting than the wilds of England. The art direction wins by a considerable margin. And I'm sure the graphics are better.

And because of that, I believe it to be a much more enjoyable game. And it deserves a positive recommendation.

In addition, you have the Discovery Tour. Something that currently interests me much more than the game itself, even if the informative content is quite weak. You can at least explore the world freely, absorbing and breathing in every detail without the artificial constrictions and intrusions of the game's general content, and as a bonus it has some of those factoids, accurate or not, presented to you through audio guides.

You'd be surprised how much better the game feels when most of it has been removed.

Furthermore, AC Origins is not a bad game, but it is not a very good one.

For someone, like me, who has been playing open world games for over 20 years, there is nothing here that I haven't seen better yet elsewhere (and even within the franchise itself), beyond the setting. It's below the industry standard at the moment when it comes to open world games. It doesn't have many memorable moments and lacks a bit of polish and genuine creativity.

But its alright. It's not the worst thing in the world. It still deserves to be visited, if only to contemplate Alexandria and the pyramids.

If all else fails, you can, like me, dive into the wave of cringe nostalgia, remembering that this game was building a new, more solid foundation, on which new games in the franchise would be worked on. At the time, there was a certain hope that a new game in the Assassin's Creed series would reach new heights.

Unfortunately it never happened.\t\t\t\t\t
