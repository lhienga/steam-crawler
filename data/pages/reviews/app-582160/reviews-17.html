Worth Playing - But Overstays Its Welcome

Assassin's Creed Origins is the tenth installment in the mainline series, and finally the developers figured out what is fun: Running around an historical setting. Almost everything else takes a backseat to having the player just explore the final years of Ptolemaic Egypt. The world is absolutely beautiful, with impressive detail and draw distances that let you see just how expansive the world is. The only trouble is that after a while, the illusion breaks and you realize just how empty the world actually is. Ubisoft created a beautiful open world with nothing of real substance in it, which only starts to show around the 30-hour mark. Unfortunately, completing this game takes 80 hours, and the combat and gameplay do not carry it through.

The Open World

The franchise's strength is dropping the player into an historic setting and letting them run around. Unfortunately, in 10 mainline entries that's about all they are still getting right. Ubisoft consistently mistakes content for substance. In Origins, the Ubisoft formula remains the same: You get a map, and points on a map, and you go to those points and do stuff. The core problems with this approach are:

1. Copy/Paste Activities
There are really only a handful of activities to do in the world:
- Camps/Outposts/Forts/Hideouts - These all include the same objectives: Kill captain(s) and/or commander(s), loot treasure.
- Locations where you just need to find 1-3 treasure chests
- Find papyrus, which leads you on a treasure hunt.
- Kill animal - You get extra crafting materials…
- Tomb raiding - Usually you have to find something and you get an ability point.

Now this is enough for a while. But after about 20 hours it wears thin.

This is actually a significant step down from the previous entry, Syndicate, where the open world was tied back to the story. Unlike Syndicate, you get nothing from completing all of these side objectives and quests. The world does not respond in any way. Take down a fort? You just get a check mark on your map! If you go back any time, the fort will be restaffed and restocked.

2. The open world does not serve the story

A fundamental problem most open world games have is that the world and the story are almost entirely disconnected. While there are moments in the story where you have multiple targets to assassinate, the story itself is mostly linear. Once the story starts coming to a head, you are more or less locked into a series of events where the open world is closed off to you as you follow along.

Most, if not all of the side quests are a distraction from the story and do nothing to support it in any way. Side quests mainly just net you some new gear, but gear is so abundant, it really doesn't matter. While the open world is pretty, it does lose its luster in the 80-hour experience. I'm sure there are things I missed, but I didn't feel I was missing out.

The Gameplay

The core gameplay is an action-RPG with minor exploration elements. It's mostly mindless fun, and like with the open world, it works for a while, but eventually the cracks start to show and the dam breaks...

Combat
The combat is fluid, but a bit clunky. You get access to a decent range of weapons, being able to equip 2 ranged and 2 melee weapons at a time. I found this to be perhaps too limiting. Button mapping in this entry is weird, and it took me a while to get used to. Buttons are over-mapped and it's really easy to make a mistake. Just about every button can be pressed or held to perform different actions, and oftentimes the hold is not registered. Point in case: Once you acquire the shield charge, you have the same button used to shield bash (press), as you have to charge (hold). 50% of the time when I would hold to charge, Bayek would just bash, which is a useless action, because unless you are within kissing range of an enemy, you will miss your shield bashes.

I stuck with a regular sword, heavy blade, warrior bow, and hunter bow for my load out. It was too burdensome to have to switch to anything else, and this was mostly balanced and let me get through just about every scenario without fail.

RPG Elements
The main vehicle for progression is gaining experience by completing quests and killing enemies. Experience nets you levels, and each level buffs your health and damage and gives you an ability point. You have a skill tree that’s divided into 3 main paths: Melee, Stealth, and Tools. Tools are useless (with the exception of a smoke bomb).

Skills, too, are mostly useless. You get some skills and passives that make things more convenient, but they are never very satisfying to use. Throwing an enemy weapon after an overpower kill takes an entirely too long animation, and is only good for killing one other enemy - if the throw lands.

There is also crafting. There should not be crafting. It’s basic, and eventually, you get to a point where you just special materials, and upgrading your gear has diminishing returns anyway. By the time I was at the endgame and was finally getting star shards for final upgrades, they made no noticeable difference.

Stealth is Dead

You can hide and assassinate people from hiding spots, but getting discovered isn't really punishing because combat is so easy. It's not much to take on an entire army, so why bother sneaking around?

The Story

This is by far the weakest element of this game, which is unsurprising as the story of this series has never been great. Like with previous entries, the story is split into the modern day and the past.

The Main Story
You play as Bayek of Siwa, and medjay the pharaoh, who goes on to become a founding member of the Hidden Ones - later to become The Assassin Brotherhood. Surprise, surprise, in true AC fashion, it's yet another revenge story. The story begins in medias res with Bayek already hunting down and killing one of his targets - a masked man, part of The Order of the Ancients (who, presumably go on to become the Templars), a group that murdered Bayek's son in pursuit of the contents of the vault of Siwa. If you're new to the series and starting here, then you will be absolutely lost. The game does not try, at all, to explain the significance of anything. You must come in with knowledge of previous games, otherwise, this is a mess of things just happening. Occasionally, you play as Bayek’s wife, Aya, so that Ubisoft could get some feminist creds in there. But here’s the thing: Every time I played as Aya, she was weaker than Bayek, kind of killing the whole point they were trying to make.

The Present
In Unity and Syndicate, there really wasn't a modern day story, which was nice, actually. Here, for some reason, they decided to shoehorn the modern day story back in. I cannot stress enough how pointless this is. At seemingly random points, you are pulled out of the Animus to play as Layla, a rogue Abstergo employee who is constantly overlooked at Abstergo, and so she decides to invent her own Animus (with blackjack and hookers!), to prove herself more than she apparently already has, all so she can get a job with people who clearly don't like her and steal her work.

Nothing comes of Layla's story and given how threads from the modern day story are consistently abandoned, I expect this too, to go nowhere in future entries. Ubisoft should have stuck with the trend and nixed the modern day story completely. It was dumb from the get-go and only gets dumber as it goes.

Overall

Assassin's Creed Origins is not a bad game by any stretch. Everything works. Its competently made, and the world is beautiful enough to distract you from all of its flaws. It's worth playing, but not worth completing, which is evident by the drop off rate seen in the achievement stats.

6/10\t\t\t\t\t
