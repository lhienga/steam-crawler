But for the price tag, and despite the room for the game to be more, it's quite satisfactory for what it is.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's an "active idle" game, and quite addictive.

Some things feel a little unpolished. A few times I thought "I wish there was a hotkey for upgrading buildings", "I wish the building menu was easier to navigate", "I can't find my cursor" and "Oh wait scrolling doesn't zoom"

But that barely distracts from how engaging and satisfying the gameplay is.
It throws new challenges at you at a steady pace, but also continuously makes you re-explore things you've unlocked earlier.

As others have noted, this game is NOT a "The Factory Must Grow" type of game. It's an optimization puzzle -- Exponentially scaling costs means you effectively can only afford a limited amount of machines, and if you utilize them poorly, your progress will slow down drastically.

This game might not be for everyone, but if you enjoy optimization puzzles, definitely try it out!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tthis game has been....frustrating. i think it has so much potential, but needs some big rebalancing and quality of life changes, and i hope the developer makes those changes because this could be so much more fun.

other reviews already address a lot of the balance problems, with how much micromanaging is required and the painful exponential growth that makes it impossible to really play around with factory structures. but i've played for over 24 hours now and around the 24 hour mark for me i finally unlocked proper automation so i could stop micromanaging. however, there are some really frustrating quality of life problems that make all the previously mentioned problems even more painful, and drag down the game even with automation. if these quality of life issues were addressed, i think it would be much easier to stick with the game for a lot longer and even make it through some of the grindy micromanagement (though the dev should still rebalance it to not be such a grind).

my QOL complaints:

1. you can't act on multiple buildings at once -- everything happens one building at a time. want to delete 15 buildings? click 15 separate times. want to build 15 buildings? click 15 separate times. you cannot drag through buildings while holding the mouse button, to delete them all or build multiple in a line. in the early game that's not much of a problem, but wow does it get frustrating quick, but particularly when paired with issue #2.

2. it's too easy to cancel the tool/mode your cursor is in. trying to delete 15 buildings? not only do you have to click 15 times, as mentioned above, but if you accidentally click on empty ground where there's no building, it will cancel the delete mode. same for placing buildings. accidentally click where there's already a building? cancels build mode. this can be especially annoying because you can't even enter build mode for a particular building unless you have enough resources for it, but by the time you go to click you might have run out of those resources because they were snagged by something else, and so you click and it cancels the build. you need to play this annoying timing game of waiting until the build button is unlocked and immediately zooming to where you want to put it and clicking before you run out of resources.

3. the Q key should not do 2 things. if your cursor is not over a building, pressing Q puts you into delete mode. but if your cursor is on a building, it tries to copy the building. so i constantly get into the situation of trying to delete buildings, accidentally clicking on nothing and canceling delete mode (per #2 above), then clicking Q to re-enter delete mode, but my cursor was accidentally on a building in that moment so i actually copy the building instead. it's infuriating. please just let me have build and delete modes that are permanent until i press something to leave them ;-;

4. because of the exponential growth, i'm often at the limits of being able to build just one more of a particular building. so if i want to rearrange things i need to delete the existing buildings to reclaim their resources so i can build them somewhere else. that's not the end of the world. except......some resources naturally degrade over time, and quickly. have a building with a high chromalit cost? if you refund the building you have to absolutely run to place it down in the new place before the chromalit degrades and you no longer have enough, so you basically just lost that building. when you had to grind to get the building in the first place, it's extremely frustrating to lose it because of this mechanic. you're being punished for trying to experiment with factory layout. i really wish you could do something like freeze time or enter a build mode that let you sell and rebuild without losing the recycled resources until you exit that build mode.

5. more like a #4 part 2, but the problem described in #4 would be a little less bad if you could just move buildings rather than having to delete and rebuild. the combination of degrading resources and no move mechanism, just punishes experimentation.

anyway, i'm still playing. was this worth the $5 i paid for it? probably? at least now that i got to full automation. but i'm super frustrated and would be able to recommend the game way more if these problems were fixed.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tUpdate: Latest update seems to address quite a lot of the complaints below, buffing silos and reducing the cost multiplier for some key machinery. I'll give it another try later and see if the changes make a practical difference, but that's promising!

I'd love to change this review to positive, but some massive progression issues have to be addressed first.

The premise of the game is combining an incremental game with a factory builder: you infinitely dig up non-descript cubes and process them through a series of (frankly beautiful looking) machines, so that you can build more and better machines. All coupled with bits of funny text message dialogue.

Many people already noted the lack of automation (it'll be anywhere between 10 and 15 hours before you unlock full automation) forcing you to constantly click your silos to keep the game running, and they're right. This is made significantly worse by both the nature of the factory and quick decay of some resources: if your machines stop, you might not be able to restart them, so better pay attention!

But I think where it truly falls apart is bringing the cost multiplier over from idle games into the factory. Like buying upgrades in Cookie Clicker and such, every next building will cost you extra, usually 1.1x the previous cost. The costs grow exponentially, completely killing horizontal scaling of the factory. Depending on your favorite layout, expanding from 2 to 3 or from 3 to 4 channels can easily cost you 10-15x the full cost of your factory. Here's some of the other ridiculous roadblocks I ran into:

* I could not build my seventh hell vault without idling for 2 hours. There is no way around it either: hollow stone production has a hard cap, so you will have to wait for enough to accumulate.
* Due to both the above, and some of the later-game hell gem mechanics, I could not build anything costing hell gems without risking my factory stopping dead for at least a minute, hoping the decay of another resource will be enough to restock my hell gems.
* I attempted to expand my factory from 2 to 3 excavating channels while waiting on a certain machine with a total cost of 80m black stone. I had to give up: adding a single hell destabilizer to the third channel would cost 15m black stone.
* One of the main parameters of any late-game build is how optimal your wire usage is: wires naturally cost 1/1 of two late-game resources, but are subject to the multiplier. I had to re-do a build entirely to optimize 6 wires out of the layout because those wires would cost between 2000/2000 and 3500/3500. EACH.

I think this game could be an amazing one-off factory builder, but for the love of all, drop the 1.1x multiplier down to something more reasonable and add some kind of lower-tier inverse chasm for early automation. The saying is "the factory must grow" not "the factory must wait at a fixed production rate for 3 hours because expansion costs have run off into cosmic proportions". Hell, even in an idle game proper, I'd be looking for an ascension/prestige mechanic to save me from that about 1 hour in.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game was a really interesting experience for me overall, but it may not be the one you think.

While the joy of discovering new systems and having to find out solutions to problems is definitely the core of why I enjoyed Sixty Four, it has to be noted that the game does not start as an idle game and will require some maintenance, sometimes for long stretches of time without changing much. For reference, I have played for about 25 hours before the game was entirely idle.

The rewards of new story beats, new systems, and a clear next objective was enough for me to keep going, but it may not be for everyone. A couple moments gave me pause as I was unsure of how to proceed, but discovery of the unknown is a big topic of the game, and solving those was the most fun of all.

The art, sound, and story have to be commended as they support the gameplay really well, although the latter was definitely the main appeal for myself. I also enjoyed achievment hunting even though some of them can be complicated or long to grab.

It's also to be noted that the developer is active on the official Discord and Steam forums, which promises good support and updates.

Give this game a shot if this sounds interesting to you.\t\t\t\t\t
