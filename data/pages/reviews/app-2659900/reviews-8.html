\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI purchased Sixty Four a few days ago and completed it in just under 40 hours.

I enjoyed the game and felt it was worth the money I spent ($5.99). The graphics, effects, and user interface are excellent for a game in this price range. However, the design and mechanics are all fairly simple.

Most of the negative reviews I read complained that the game was neither a clicker nor a factory game. In full disclosure, I love factory games (Satisfactory, Dyson Sphere Program, Factorio, etc.) but I don't play clicker games of the Cookie Clicker variety. Sixty Four definitely falls between a clicker and a factory game, and I find that to be a pro over a pure clicker game. As with factory games, some strategy is needed to establish goals and find the shortest path. But unlike factory games, Sixty Four demands continuous interaction. You achieve the highest throughput by building the best machines in the best configurations and then monitoring and resupplying them as needed.

Although it begins with a click mechanic, that quickly progresses into a hold mechanic, so repetitive motion isn't a serious concern. In fact, if you prefer, you can play the game by simply holding the mouse button and panning the scene with WASD.

The story is shallow. However, I found the progression to be surprisingly good. There are a wide variety of machines and each stage of the game introduces a twist on what came before. There's very little instruction beyond the short description provided with each machine, but the mechanics are easily discovered which adds to the sense of achievement.

There were a few occasions where I felt I was beginning to grind. However, when I thought through what was slowing me down, I realized there were optimizations I could make. In every case, this paid off and my progress rapidly accelerated.

That isn't to say that time isn't required. There are points where an hour or two are required to unlock the next resource or machine. But the arc I took never felt punishing or unreasonably slow.

If you enjoy variety and are looking for a quick fix over the weekend, I recommend giving Sixty Four a shot. And if you beat my time (39.2 hours), please leave a comment. :)\t\t\t\t\t

The only suggestions I have are some improvements with the UI.

3. There are certain times when I wished for the ability to queue up a build/upgrade that would execute when the resource requirements get met. (Due to the mechanics, certain resources will spike and it got a bit ridiculous that the build option would be flashing due to it meeting the requirements for a split second and I would have to click repeatedly hoping to coincide. 4. Might be nice to add the ability to pause and still build; that could also address the resource timing issue.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI see a lot of comments talking about how it's basically a glorified idle game. Yes, there are aspects of waiting around for materials to come, but there's definitely a consistent need for redesigning your setup, and later materials have very interesting and very unique strategies required to gather or store them, and there's stages of the game where you realize your setup suddenly sucks and you have to redo a major portion of it to optimize.

There's shifts in material focus, one hour you're pressed to get a ton of chromatids, and then suddenly you figure out how to start mining them en masse, but to do so means sacrificing another kind of material. Or you unlock a building that beefs up that production, but that building is really hungry for a different kind of material. It's a pretty well balanced game. Through the whole thing up to "Reality" I felt engaged and actively required to problem solve. I get that factorio-esque satisfaction when I solve a shortage issue or bulk requirement, and then it paces right into the next issue needing attention.

If the devs are reading these comments, I have a few QOL suggestions.

1. Give us a settings page for key bindings. I appreciate that a lot of the game is left to discovery, but it took me 5 total hours of gameplay before I realized I could zoom out, to alt-mouse over buildings to see their description (particularly useful for "only-one" buildings as they disappear from the building list after construction), etc.

2. Tell us the refund amount when recycling a building. Sometimes I buffer materials such as chromatids by building structures that are hungry for it, so I can delete them when I'm ready to build something bigger to avoid decay. Sometimes I'm close to an expensive building but it's guesswork whether recycling another building will give me enough to push me over the edge to afford it. Knowing how much I'll get back upon recycling would be great.

3. Give me a toggle to view previously-built "one-only" buildings in the right-hand build menu. Sometimes I forget what a specific building does and I have to destroy it to get the "level 1" description of the building. E.g. I forgot what oscillators did at one point when I got the third iteration, and neither the second nor the third level description described what an oscillator does. I had to destroy it to remember it helps me break blocks faster by hand.

4. Consider clarifying some of the building descriptions in general. The toggle suggestion in #3 might be pointless if the descriptions-- especially of level 2 and level 3 buildings-- are a little more clear on what they do. Main offenders include upgrade one-only buildings which don't carry over the original description (like the oscillator), the inverse chasm, and the inverse channels. I enjoy the trial and error to learn myself what they do, but sometimes it feels like an unnecessary struggle. This suggestion is far more subjective, I understand

5. Provide an upgrade and downgrade buildings feature. Three use cases that would immediately be improved is: a) new extraction channel builds where I have enough materials to upgrade to level 3 buildings on a lot of the components, but I have to hunt-and-peck through the list, and b) downgrading from Hell Gem Destabilizers to Industrial destabilizers when I get an extraction channel beyond the hell gem depth, and c) going from a level 2 entropy resonator to either a capacitor or a void resonator, it would be nice where doing an upgrade action here gave you the choice to pick one rather than seeking it out on the right-hand list.

6. Click and drag to build conductors, sim city street style. Since I have to lay some pretty long lines to reach some of the parts of my setup, it would be nice to be able to click and drag instead of trying to tap one at a time.

7. Add a UI component or maybe a "counter building" of sorts that will give me the output of a given building, or even a line-graph of changes over time for my materials, with the ability to slice by input and output source. For example, I'd like to know exactly how many beta-pylene blocks I get from a celestial reactor, but with chromatid decay and excavation making that number go crazy, it's hard to tell how many blocks I get from one reactor. If I knew how many, I could weigh the pros and cons of either investing in a new extractor setup, or if it's better to build an array of reactors.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo i've tried it for a bit now and first of all: I would refund it, if i could.

I thought that this is a combination of idle/automation game... well.. it's not. You hit several "walls" every 1-2 hours that are there to slow you down heavily (basically everytime you get access to a new "resource", because you basically replace the previous one). You most certainly don't have enough resources to build new "miners" everytime (because the cost scale in a very crazy rate).. so you have to figure out a way to get the resource you previously had more than enough but you will never have enough of at least one resource.

Overall it's not an automation game because there still is a heavy amount of manuell steps involved in a process (at least in the first 6 hours)... nor is it an idle game because, well... you have to be there every 1-2min so everything actually runs. And the whole process is unbelievable tedious and really.. really really slow.

Long story; short: It's not a factory builder nor an idle game. You have to look at it every 1-2 min to click at some stuff, the resource scaling is insane and sometimes prevents progress from being made. It's tedious, boring, unbalanced, frustraing, don't buy it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWonderful game, incredible game design.

So many little aspects of this game were done extremely well and make it pretty clear that the developer put a lot of thought into it. The design of the Red-Blue Rock and 64 kmph down achievements and how they dovetail with the rest of the game's design to make them JUSTTTT barely possible with specific strategies really highlight the game's real intent as a puzzle-solving game about engineering and efficiency.

It seems a bit unfortunate that many of the people who know about the game so far seem to expect it to be more of an idle game than it is, or more of a factory building game than it is. While, sure, those are valid tastes to have in games - this game really isn't either one, fundamentally. However, I think it does borrow some of the things that are fun about both of those genres and combine them into a fantastic optimization game.

After finishing the game, I do have one thought about a potential improvement:

I think the rate of hollow cube spawns once you fully upgrade the mysterious cube is too low, and/or the way the hollow flower cube production rate is gated to the natural cube spawns is unfortunate. You seem to max out at about six hollow flowers allowing residual occasional cube spawns and seven fully preventing them, and further flowers do nothing. This ended up pretty much forcing me to run the game overnight, which I don't feel like is in the spirit of the game (but if that was intended, or if i missed something, fair enough!)

I think if a single hollow flower prevented all cube spawns, but you could build as many as you want and they'd all produce 100% of the time, forcing you to decide how many cubes you want to allow to spawn before turning them into flowers, that would be pretty great. Tbh, that's how I expected it to work!

Alternatively, a simple increase in the rate of cube spawning post full upgrade would help, but I think it's a less interesting solution.
