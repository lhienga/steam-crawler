\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSixty Four presents itself as a mixture between a clicker game and a factory/automation game. it fails at both.
Regarding clicker games, most if not all of them have the player set up a system to automatically accumulate points as one of the first things they do. Sixty Four's micromanagement is nauseatingly overdone to the point you'll seldom be able to sit back and watch the resources build for more than just a couple minutes at a time. Even with the right setups and all the proper machines, you'll find yourself still manually clicking to reach the next milestone. This bring me into the second half, Regarding automation games:
With each machine you make, the cost of the next one increases heavily. This means you'll have to deconstruct old networks to make new ones. This makes it very difficult to experiment with more efficient designs while letting your factory run, because building each new machine costs 3x-4x what they should be. Beyond that the most optimisation you can get out of it is deciding what machines get placed next to others. There's no 'wiring', no 'timers', not even 'power' (the machines do need an upkeep though, and that's the crux of the micromanagement problem).
I'm basing this 'evaluation' off Universal Paperclips and Factorio, an incremental game and factory/automation game respectively, both of which I've completed multiple times. It's not fair to compare any of them to this, but they share a lot of mechanics and ideals I think this game could learn from.
It's a very interesting blend of genres, it has a nice graphical style, and I haven't encountered any bugs, but it's just not fun enough for me. I won't refund because this game was cheap, and it has lots of potential. I look forward to updates which may rectify these issues\t\t\t\t\t
