\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is _almost_ good, but it suffers from an identity crisis, and the conflicting goals harm the overall experience.

It wants to be a factory builder, with a variety of resource extraction devices that interact in interesting ways that encourage experimentation in design. You might wake up in the middle of the night with some idea for how to better arrange your factory for improved efficiency. That's fun!

The game wants to be an idler. Resource requirements scale up to absolutely insane heights, and there's no way you can proceed through the technology and resource tiers in a timely fashion. In isolation, that's fine!

The game also is also actively engaging, and unfortunately, this directly conflicts with the prior goal. For most of the game's runtime, your primary tool for automation is refueling stations that feed other buildings, and they must be manually reset on a regular basis. A given refueling station might need to be reset once every one to three minutes, and given that you might grow to have (say) 16 refueling stations, you're almost constantly tending to one of them. Step away for more than a few minutes, and your factory grinds to a halt. Full automation isn't available until nearly the end of the game.

The game could be a good idler if it allowed for full automation; or it could be actively engaging if it dramatically dropped resource requirements; but the experience is significantly harmed by trying to do both.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSuch a unique and interesting game that consistently surprised me the whole way through. I'm not even a fan of "idle" games and have only a passing interest in Satisfactory but this one really pulled me in. Unless you're specifically looking for an idle game or a Factorio clone, I highly recommend giving it a try and see if it hooks you.

This is definitely not an idle game where you set things up and leave it running in the background. There are a couple of times where you have to do so, but for the most part your machines require your constant attention. I had a huge grin while desperately trying to keep everything running to chase the next milestone on my not-quite optimized machines which can't be fully automated. There are some clever and unexpected interactions with and between materials that you find that allow the game designer to put obstacles in your way that you won't see coming, and you'll have to do some experimenting to figure out how all of the mechanics work. \r\n\t\t\t\t\tI wasn't sure about this game when I started: coming from games like Factorio, the price scaling meant that I could never actually manage to finish a build, because I'd always run up against one shortage or another.

However, somewhere in the discussions, I read that the developer wanted the game to focus on small, efficient builds. For whatever reason, knowing this made this game ten times more enjoyable; once I understood that the price scaling was a deliberate design choice, and a deliberate constraint on my builds, I started to optimise for it.

The key to enjoying Sixty Four, for me, was to stop treating it like other idle or factory sim games. You can't just press a button and come back a day later to find that you've moved onto the next plot point. Especially earlier on (no spoilers!), it pays to re-evaluate your builds regularly. What gives me X resource most quickly? What can I scale without spending Y resource? How can I design Z so I don't have to check back every thirty seconds?

If you're looking for something very different to other factory sim / idle games, both in its mechanics and story, I can't recommend this game enough. It wasn't love at first sight, but I've grown to enjoy this game tremendously.\t\t\t\t\t
