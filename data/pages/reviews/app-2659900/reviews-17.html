\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAn interesting take on a factory game with much more emphasis on efficiency and puzzle, than on automation and scale. The art style is great (although the isometric angle-locked view and great differences in building heights does present challenges at some points).

Before you buy, be aware that this game is not an idle game until the very end and makes significant departures from the standard factory game style in that it does force you to click a lot in the start to perform manual actions; the point being that you precisely don't want to be clicking all the time and therefore should want and need to solve various efficiency and resource requirement puzzles to reduce manual interactions to a bare minimum.

Even despite some annoyance from requiring a lot of manual action initially, it's a great game.

A very impressive achievement for a solo developer.

PS: Having an auto-clicker or infinite click mouse macro greatly reduces the manual clicking aspect and gets you to a semi-automated state much, much quicker.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFirst things first: It's not an automation game nor it's an idle game. Almost any building you place will stop working after a while, even if you place silos next to every building. The game presentation on Steam page is misleading in that way and If that's what hooks you, then don't buy this game. It's the main reason behind all negative reviews of this game.

I'm writing this from a perspective of a person that has not yet finished the game, but played it for ~12h, still have not seen any actual automation. I had to play the game by manually refilling a building (mosty just silos) every 15-30 seconds. There might be some form of actial automation unlocked later, but if that's the case then why put player through such a grindy chore for so long?

When it comes to being just an incremental clicker game that you need to actively play, Sixty Four is very solid up until getting Hollow Stones. At that point the game becomes very unbalanced when it comes to costs of things.

The imbalance comes mostly in form of inability to produce Elmerine or Qanetite on a large scale. The game really needs to give you better tools of producing those starting at the Hollow Stone tier. Having 40/50 refining buildings boosted by preheaters is definitely not enough. The simplest solution would be to add tiers 2 for refining buildings that already exist in the game that work at 300% of the basic rate. Alternatively being able to actually fully automate refinement would help too. Another issue producing those might be caused by cost of constructing multiple of a building scaling exponentially and after a while you just can't scale up the production any more due to every building costing millions.

The artstyle and story are quite interesting as they present abstract machines that sort of make some sense, but are there just to convey an idea. The dialogues in the game serve a similar role as two characters try to make sense of the very simple, abstract world created from the AI-generated-like explanations of game mechanics. It's a bit meta and feels like the writer themself is not sure how to explain those things. This kind of writing can be quite enjoyable for some people.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAn interesting premise. A sort of factory-like game mixed with an idle clicker. The problem is it fails at both.

The resource management of the "factory" you build is far too simple. There's a few interesting mechanics that pop up in how the different resources interact, such as resources decaying back in to others, but these mechanics mainly serve to frustrate rather than give a sense of accomplishment.

For example, there is a moment when the game suddenly transports you to a completely new space (without giving too much away). But there's very little to do here. Just manually gather some uninteresting resources that slowly regenerate. It takes quite a few minutes to do this. There's literally nothing else to interact with, and you can't go back until a threshold is reached.

But the real issue is with this being an idle game. It isn't really. You have to save up to buy auto-clickers, and even they only have limited charges before they need to be clicked to be reactivated, so in the end you're just sat there clicking them still. It is an faux-idle game that forces you to be present.

You can't expand your base as one might expect with a factory game to speed things up, because you'll hit a brick wall in the exponentially increasing costs.

Unfortunately the resource cost to progress scales so aggressively that really there is nothing to do but sit and click to recharge. And click again. And click again. Every 30s you'll need to click everything again, with no sense of accomplishment. When you finally do get a new unlock, the impact is typically minimal.

The game is just so slow that I quickly turned to the community to copy one of the more optimal factory layouts. Even with that "cheat" the progress is glacial, and I lose the sense of discovery.

18 hours in and apparently I've just unlocked something that does finally allow a true idle experience. But the cost is astronomical, and the forums say that this will just incur several hours of true idling. I can't face the prospect of just sitting for several more hours waiting for something to happen.\t\t\t\t\t
