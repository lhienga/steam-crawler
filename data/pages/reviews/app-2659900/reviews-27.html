\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFun factory-themed incremental puzzler. Note it's not a factory game, bring that expectation and you'll be disappointed. You click stuff, you unlock stuff, you put things next to other things to click them, you use other things to convert between resource types... it's more about resource management within the constraints that you're given than building products.

The puzzler aspect comes in from the fact that very little is explained, but is there for you to figure out by experimentation. This is one the game's strengths and weaknesses: some of the mechanics are opaque, some are gated behind triggers that you won't know about until you hit them, and others are just there for you to stumble on from vague hints and almost no feedback.

Personally I enjoyed this experience, but I can see it being extremely frustrating for some, and leading to people getting stuck without progress. Even though I enjoyed it there was one point where I was stalled for 5-6 hours just because I thought I'd followed the hints, but because of the lack of feedback I was unaware that although I'd tried one avenue I hadn't tried it ENOUGH, and so I was just sat around fiddling with my layouts and assuming I'd hit another unlock eventually at a depth milestone, completely unaware that I was 10 seconds of effort away from the hidden next step all that time.

If you view it as a game about advancement and building a factory and so on, that will be incredibly irritating; if you view it as a puzzle game then being stuck is part of the fun, so your expectations will shape the enjoyability greatly.

For the price, I've had hours of fun and it's well worth it. It comes with the caveat that if it were a £12 indie game, I'd be a lot more on the fence given the pacing issues and chances of getting yourself stuck.

Oh, ignore the people complaining about using autoclickers and getting stuck, they thought they were playing a game they could just brute-force through, and so missed out on learning the techniques they need to advance. You CANNOT complete this game with a single strategy, you need to adapt along the way as the rules change and "what used to work" will stop working at several points. The game doesn't make this very clear, so people just call it broken when it's their assumptions that are broken.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt looks like an idle game, or a clicker game, but it's the worst merging of those two. Even things that 'automate' your product still need you to click on them occasionally to refill their fuel, and the graphics are in the way of indicators and you can't change the view, so the game demands you to constantly watch the machines run and check to see which ones need more fuel. And then as you progress you stop getting the early resources while the need for them increases too fast to continue building, so your options are either to build conversion machines (which don't convert later goods, so they run out eventually) or tear everything down and rebuild it all one space to the left so that you start mining all over again. It's a kludgy, frustrating take on the idle genre.\t\t\t\t\t

While it is a factory game, it is not the kind of factory game where you keep expanding further and further, instead, you will be rebuilding the same one/couple factories over and over. The moment you think that you have an efficient design, the game will throw a new concept at you, like a wrench in your plans.
All in all, if you are a masochist that enjoys managing machines to make funny numbers go up? Perfect, this game is for you!
The first time Celestial Foam showed up, I had to restart my 9 hours of active progress. This game is painful, but sticking with it is fun. \t\t\t\t\t
