\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNow I know what you're thinking: "What did you expect? Of course the game is difficult to play in battles, it's ww1!" and that would be forgivable. However, every single battle is not decided by "oh dumbass you charged human waves into enemies in trenches", it's decided by "hahahaha you forgot to micro your one artillery strike 2 pixels to the right so it would suppress the right unit, now your entire army is gone and the enemy has taken 4 points". A ww1 game should not be this micro intensive. It's also worth pointing out that the AI cheats pretty blatantly, and they basically always have more numbers than you even if you outnumbered them on the strategic map.

At the moment, the best strategy is to just auto-resolve every battle. In the 1914 start as either side you can set up one or two battles that are great victories, which give the enemy so much war exhaustion that even if they take a few provinces from you, the result is still that the enemy gets closer to 0 points faster than you do. The battle system is mediocre, and there isn't even a real gameplay reason to play it. Auto-resolves will basically always be as good or better than what you could have done in the RTS. To win this game on its default difficulty all you have to do is position equal troops wherever the enemy puts some, and then auto-resolve every battle. The game seems to actively discourage you from actually playing the game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs of now, the game is on the cusp of greatness. However, I do have one major concern regarding the tactical battles - the AI seems to possess an unfair advantage with map hacks and a surplus of supplies. This invariably leads to their ability to bring their full force against you and fortify their defenses far more effectively than you ever could. Additionally, the pace of research seems to be a tad slow, and the strategic layer of the game fails to truly captivate. Although the idea of persistent battlefields and fighting for the same hex is innovative, it can become tedious to constantly push through, even after decimating entire infantry cores. With some fine-tuning, this game could truly shine, but for now, it still has a ways to go.

The game would benefit greatly from enhancing the longevity of units, as they seem to perish far too quickly. Introducing the ability to attach snipers, officers, and mobile machine guns during the mid-game onwards could be a game-changer. Such a feature could provide various advantages, such as moral boosts, suppression bonuses, and an unwavering resolve to keep fighting.
