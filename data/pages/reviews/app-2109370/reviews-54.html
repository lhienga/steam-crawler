The game is just unplayable. I have had bugs where my soldiers have disappeared into the map, my soldiers have also refused to fire back for no reason and a whole lot of other issues. I have lost battles for no apparent reason except for my men refusing to fire back. The AI also doesn’t seem to follow the game rules at all. The AI will just know where your troops are and blast them into pieces while they are clearly in the fog of war. The game is great in almost every other aspect and very fun when it does work. However, I WOULD NOT recommend this game at its current state. I strongly recommend anyone looking to pick this game up to wait for some serious patches.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGood RTS. I've been waiting for a Total War-esque WW1 game for a long time. Petroglyph did really good.

Pros:
- Very fun battles with many different strategies to take on the enemy
- It really feels like trench warfare when its a straight slugfest to even get to the enemy's trench line.
- Nice (But limited) maps.
- Central Powers and Entente actually feel and play differently somewhat but are still balanced and function the same.
- The overworld gameplay is really fun and intuitive.

Cons:
-AI feels like its cheating and has nearly limitless supply because they can just spam artillery.
-Trench combat is buggy and looks like garbage because I'll pass an enemy in a communication trench and they will not fight. The enemy will tend to just skitter around endlessly until they find a trench to maybe settle down in but sometimes they get stuck and I have to use artillery to kill them despite having troops adjacent to them.
-Limited maps, Soisson looks the same as Ypres and looks the same as Chatilon du Marne, etc.
- Bodies disappear along with battlefield damage.


Overall a good game but not a cinematic as I thought it would be.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBeen following this game since it was first announced. I've been looking for another WW1 era strategy/tactical game since the flashgame days of Warfare 1917. It's quite a struggle to make the back and forth of trench warfare into something enjoyable but this game succeeds at that.

Pros
- A relatively intelligent tactical AI that will recognize strongpoints and switch up their attacks on occasion. Based on my initial defensive battles, the AI appeared to pick a single part of the trenchline that they repeatedly mass assaults. I started leaving more forces in reserve to reduce casualties from heavy artillery bombardment and then pushing troops up to counter the infantry attack. The AI recognized this and launched its next attack on the opposite side of the map. I also like the way the AI has memory for static emplacements like machine guns and will bombard them continuously to soften up the defense for its next attack.

-Supply and Gold Reserves are an excellent counterbalance to the fighting capability of the player and the AI. You receive gold each turn, which is used to buy global supply. As infantry companies and corps are not destroyed by casualties, only replaced at the expense of gold reserves, the theoretical offensive power of the units on the field never diminishes (until you run out of gold, but you've already lost the war at that point). However, they are limited by the availability of supply, of which there is a certain amount allocated to each corps before it has to start being pulled from the global pool (accessible only by construction of a supply depot). Every company, fortification, and artillery barrage has to be purchased with supply. Without sufficient supply, your offensive has ended before it even started.

The AI is constrained by the same supply rules as the player. This can be exploited if you have scouted the enemy logistical situation and can see which areas do not have access to the global pool. Strike those points and the AI may not have enough points to deploy their full force or support it with artillery. More than once have I overrun enemy positions because a lack of supply meant that their heavy artillery could no longer whittle my troops down. The same situation can be reversed though, which resulted in quick surrenders when I realized my troops had no chance of holding their ground with the little supply I had left.

-The persistence of trench lines functions well when battles are repeated on the same section of the strategic map. It's fun to look back at prior battles and think about what trench designs worked well and what did not. Fortifications other than trenches are not persistent so keep that in mind. I have not yet unlocked siege artillery or undermining that would allow for destruction of trench lines so I cannot comment on how that is recorded by the game. Other things like the bodies in no man's land when deploying troops and the gradual destruction of villages over time sell the persistence and make the battlefield seem more alive, as if there were attacks and bombardments off-screen when you weren't commanding the troops directly.

Cons
- The strategic AI tends to overcompensate for breakthroughs too much. I broke through the British lines at Arras and the AI drew far too many forces from around Rethel, allowing another offensive with the moderate forces I had left there. Rinse and repeat results in rapid games that end in 1916 before much of the tech tree can be researched.

-The tactical AI is far too eager to recapture lost territory instead of reinforcing its next line of defense. Even when greatly outnumbered, the AI will throw troops into a war of attrition that it cannot possibly win to recapture a control point that is meaningless in the greater scope of the battle. This makes offensive battles a little bit too easy once you capture the first point's trenches and are shooting the reinforcements trying to cross open ground.

-Tech tree advancement is a mixed bag primarily due to the limited research points. There are increases to research point income over time but I feel like 1915 research is much too slow. This leads to the intel tree being massively underused because it takes too long to invest into even though it has some techs that could greatly affect the strategic level of the game. The effects of the other trees have immediate effects on the tactical map, which is more enticing to many players.

-Pathfinding of troops in trenches can be finicky. While it is mostly my fault for not being careful in clicking, it is a little difficult to tell troops to move through the trenches to the next line instead of going over the top and getting massacred by rifle fire. Opposing companies will often unrealistically move past each other in communications trenches because only firing trenches can have melee combat. Companies can also get stuck in loops in the communications trenches or between firing trenches, though this usually resolves itself in time.

Cheating
In my experience and contrary to what many other reviews are claiming, the AI does not appear to cheat at all. Most instances of the AI being able to see and bombard your troop positions are either due to the presence of observation balloons or enemy troops in nearby forests. The AI can remember the location of static emplacements, which makes up for most other cases of seemingly blind bombardments. The player can do the same as machine guns, artillery, and other fortifications can be seen through the fog of war and are marked on the minimap once they have been spotted.

I tested this by placing primary trenches with machine gun emplacements far back from the frontline, outside of balloon visibility, and then a few forward trenches just barely inside of machine gun range. The AI will pound the sacrificial forward trenches as its infantry attacks but the machine guns will be safe from artillery because their range is slightly further than the infantry's line of sight. Only when the infantry breaks through the first trenches and comes closer to the machine guns will the artillery begin to prioritize the machine guns.

The large forces the AI throws at the player in the tactical map are due to its higher efficiency in commanding units. If the player uses the pause button to issue commands before attacking, they too can achieve similar mass and synchronicity in attacks.

Conclusions
The main features the developers promised are all present and very functional. The core tactical gameplay loop is enjoyable, if a bit repetitive. The autoresolve alleviates this somewhat, as the cost of an autoresolve is fairly similar to what a player would incur. The layered strategic level, with the unique supply-gold reserve management recreates the back and forth slog of the WW1 battlefield convincingly. Some abstractions had to be made, with the star-system and the machine guns being prime examples, but it maintains enough realism. Some issues in the AI's responses to player offensives are the main problem holding this game back from excellence but I am sure those can be tweaked. Overall, a great WW1 strategy game that is well worth your time.\t\t\t\t\t
