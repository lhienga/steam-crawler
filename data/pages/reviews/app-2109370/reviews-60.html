\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA solid idea and a solid foundation. And TGW:WF has thrilling features that begin to deliver on the promise of a truly immersive World War I experience. But for me, a few small changes stand in the way of it being truly stellar.

Positive:

+Fantastic sound design that easily puts TGW:WF on the level of the best Total War titles, and even reminds one of memorable classics like Sid Meier's Gettysburg. The noises of battle are excellent (though *more* battle noise might be needed) and the music is first rate. Win a battle and hear a scratchy old record of a WWI-era song on the victory screen, laid over original photos of the time. Perfect!
+Art direction. The use of original photos is classy. The painterly style so popular nowadays is well-done in cutscenes and not excessive. Unit portraits remember the style of WWI propaganda posters - vertical flags and illustrated soldiers. It's an authentic look and feel.

Negative:

-Poor graphics. As others have pointed out, the animations are weird and bad, and the figures of soldiers swarming over contours on the map look more like germs or something than troops. The photorealistic quality of the interface builds immersion, only to kill it when you zoom in. You really do get the sense that you're looking at *representations* of armies instead of, you know, real armies.
-Truly persistent battlefields would be nice. Aside from baked-in features like mud textures and craters, there's no sense of the true horror of a Great War battlefield, and most of what you do to the land (and the soldiers struggling and dying on it) disappears pretty quickly.
-The hex-based system and limited actions during turns provides a sense of powerlessness on the strategic level, and not in a good way. You're provided with a lot of ways to counter the opponent - through espionage, military action, and in other ways - but I never got the feeling that I could use all the resources available to me to adequately meet threats. I just watched stuff happen to me, powerless to do much.

I've been waiting for a good World War I strategy game for a *long* time. And TGW:WF answers that need in a lot of awesome, immersive ways. Unfortunately, I find that it struggles to deliver a smooth command experience, often fettered by its own design.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you're a WW1aboo, this is worthwhile at full price simply because there aren't many other WW1 games. If you aren't interested in WW1 specifically, maybe get it at half price.

Pros:
-Core gameplay of attacking and defending trenches is solid. It may get a bit repetitive over 4 years of campaign, but WW1 was a bit repetitive in real life.
-Captures the problems that made breakthroughs so difficult to achieve and forced both sides into static attritional warfare.
-Number of troops and casualties is a pretty good approximation of historical battles.

Cons:
-They didn't really deliver on "persistent battlefields" - only the trenches are persistent, not any visual traces of previous battles. This is, in my opinion, a huge missed opportunity considering there's not many different maps. Bodies disappear in seconds and shell craters don't last much longer, which is ridiculous given how low-detail the gfx are to begin with.
-The animations on infantry and vehicles look bad. Moving infantry have this silly running animation which stands out even at max zoom. It's not a big deal, but it totally fails to convey the impression of a desperate bayonet rush across the killing ground.
-Corollary to the above, very little effort was put into the models. Most notably, despite the low resolution, every soldier's rifle is clearly a British No. III Lee-Enfield (as are the silhouettes of rifles on the unit icons). This is striking because the SMLE in only really recognizable by its distinctive muzzle shape and magazine box, so it would have been trivial to delete those features and give the French/Germans a generic rifle that more closely resembles the Lebel or Mauser.
-The game starts in December 1914, skipping the Battle of the Frontiers, 1st Marne, and Race to the Sea entirely. There's no cavalry, armored cars, or early-war uniforms as a result. I can understand why they did this, but it may affect your interest in buying it.\t\t\t\t\t

Petroglyph i understand you wanted to make a fairly realistic WW1 strategy game BUT THAT DOESNT MEAN YOU GOTTA KILL MY FUN, thats what this system does, your fun starts to build up, you start winning which makes you wanna play it more and more and then boof, game over.

Please give us the ability to turn off the National will system so we can keep playing the campaign until one side takes the others capital, no matter if it takes 10-20-30 years, this is an A-Historical game, let us have A-historical fun\t\t\t\t\t
