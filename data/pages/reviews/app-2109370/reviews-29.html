Battle of the Somme's Skinner Box

The basic premise of The Great War: Western Front is fascinating. The developers intend to take one of the most brutal conflicts in recent human memory and turn it into a real time strategy game. It is especially bold when you consider that this particular war is known for its particular lack of both maneuver and grand strategy (when considered on a theater scale, and disregarding battalion-level movements). Petroglyph does not shy away from acknowledging that the Great European War was one of miniature change and massive loss.

The overview, while phrased as complex, is remarkably simple. As the general of either the Entente or the Central powers, you must balance three resources: gold, supply, and national will. Gold is gained regardless of the map. Supply is purchased by gold and used to influence battles. Battles affect your national will. This resource management is more chutes and ladders than the typical rock-paper-scissors that triangular resource games typically emulate. To "capture" a tile and change the world map, you must win a pre-determined amount of Great Victories, with a number of stars assigned to that tile. Think of stars as that tiles "health". These stars regenerate if you do not continually attack the tile. So, while you may have scratched one with a brutal Great Victory in Turn One, you must attack again and again to keep that progress, even if the following battles do not achieve another Great Victory. The player is forced to decide whether the drain of gold and supply is worth attempting to move the front line by one mile.

Victory can technically occur by either capturing the opponent's capital city or draining their faction's national will to zero. In reality, you will win by draining national will before you even step close to the enemy capital. Even when dropping the game to the easiest setting and cheesing the AI to just try and press into their territory, I won before even advancing three tiles into their territory. The analogies of the game mechanics are obvious. War is hell. The Great European War was pointless. No one truly won in the end, as the cost was so high for both sides.

Pros

-Tactical Intensity
Tactical battles carry an expected ebb-and-flow of humanwave warfare. This builds with an intensity during both attack and defense. On attack, you must careful plan artillery in lockstep with your vulnerable infantry. Without the cover of bombardment, foot soldiers are caught in no man's land and quickly decimated by enemies firing from the safety of trenches and fortifications. Your riflemen rely on artillery to both soften and silence the enemy, allowing soldiers to live long enough to jump into the enemy trench and fight face to face. Close quarters combat, while costing anywhere from a quarter to three quarters of a unit's manpower, is an attacker's best hope of gaining ground. What little foothold you can carve out for yourself, you barely earn a respite as the AI rallies to counter-attack. If you are lucky, they charge over the tops of their back up trenches and your weary troops can fire upon them from freshly-captured defenses. If you are unlucky, they can weave their way up through various communication trenches and runs to engage you in hand-to-hand. This is all while trying to safely bring reinforcements up through no man's land and preserve enough supply to continue the attack. On defense, you find yourself under wave after wave of the AI's entire force. You barely have enough time to breath and count the wounded before you'll find yourself again under bombardment and rushed by infantry. This intensity can be very enjoyable, as after a particularly rough battle you will find that your perseverance has paid off and cost the enemy dearly. It gives a small (small) sense of accomplishment.

-Tech Tree Possibilities

Just as the Great War is historically known for its wide-leaps in technology, this game also seeks to provide major changes in the course of your war. You are drip-fed research points that you can slot into various technologies. Some will give you deadly chemical weapons to scare the enemy off with. Others give you reign of the sky with fighters and bombers. Still others will give you access to some tanks and impressive fortifications. However present these possibilities are, you will not be able to field them all in one campaign and must choose wisely.

Cons

-Achievement Catharsis (Lack of)

While the end of a tactical battle may give you a small rush and feeling of accomplishment (after all, it is remarkable to hold off thousands upon thousands of men with only a few hundred and a couple machine guns), the strategic layer of the game does very little to reward this further. After a loading screen, you are ushered into a viewing of a small slider scale telling you just how effective this battle was. A small sound bite is played, with its intensity and tone communicating how good or bad you've done. And, if you managed to scrape out a Great Victory, you get to watch a small animation of a tile's star being deleted. And that is all. While that may not seem like much, you will continue to grind until you finally capture a tile... only to be met with the same sound bite and a sudden flurry of movement as the enemy pawns are shuffled backwards and yours pushed forward. A little textbox tells you that you've gained ground. And nothing else rewards you. Given the amount of work needed to get to this point, you are given very little positive feedback. It feels like you come out of the slog of one battle, only for the game developers to light up a cigarette, slap you on the shoulder, and push you back in with indifference.

-Tech Tree Execution

While the possibilities for rewriting history may appear bountiful, you truthfully do not get much done in an average campaign. Tech points come slowly. One tech field is useless, as strategic buildings do little to sway the tide of battle. Another entire field is wholly necessary to remain competitive, and so you will find most of your tech points sunk into that one rather than having the Central Powers field tanks years earlier than in reality or the Entente dropping deadly gas during the dawn of the war. Tech progression becomes another checklist, another chore for the player that must be done according to the developers' wishes.


-Historical Dependence

Here, the developers try to have you play alongside history while also trying to change it. This is not well-executed. You may capture Verdun early on and march past it. Still, in 1916 you will see headlines about the battle of Verdun. The developers wished for both historical accuracy and player agency, and here they failed at both.

-Lost Promises
Originally, this game was advertised as a war that evolved with you. Trenches, craters, destroyed tanks, and downed aircraft were to remain as you warp Mother Earth under the boot of war. In reality, only trenches remain. Everything else disappears. Each map has a set progression, with pre-destined craters appearing as the war goes on. Even the huge craters from undermining disappear between each match (though, they were permanent during the beta test).

Petroglyph leans too far into the drabness of the war. Historical games must strike a delicate balance between realism and gameplay. In TGW:WF, the developers erred on the side of realism. With that decision, they give the player a bleak message on the true state of war with little to thank the player for their time invested. I find myself occasionally trying to return to the game to really, truly make some alternative history fun with it. I am immediately reminded how little options I am truly given as I fight over the same patch of ground again and again, buying supplies again and again, making sad wojak at the national will bar again and again. This is not a game. This is a skinner box, and a poorly made one at that.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI want to like this game, but right now I really can't recommend it.
In short, the RTS part becomes tedious and repetitive very quickly and the strategic map part lacks any depth.

The game fails on the strategic map part because it is incredibly simplistic and boring, and essentials boils down to just grinding down the national will of your opponent once you are ahead in national will. Almost no interesting strategic decisions are made in this part of the game. Other games such as strategic command do this part much better.

The research is fun though.

The second big part of the game is the RTS side. At first this is fun and interesting and makes the game unique as a WWI strategy game.
However, this side suffers from several severe problems.

First, once you understand how to take enemy trenches without too many losses (by suppressing the enemy trenches and MGs with light artillery and clearing out their trenches in melee combat) the RTS part becomes incredibly tedious, boring and repetitive.
Once you figure out how to take trenches cost effectively, you can even trade favorable as the attacker. Afterwards every battle plays the same, capture three control points (usually in the same order) with the exact same tactics.

Second, if you are the defender, the RTS part completely falls apart. If you set up your trenches at least somewhat competently, the AI just sends wave after wave of infantry that barely reach your trenches and just gets gunned down by your infantry without ever getting dangerous (no MGs or artillery required). After trading on a ~6:1 ratio in losses, the AI eventually has enough and concedes resulting in a major win for you the defender.
No tactics or interesting decisions required besides thinking a bit about setting up your trenches. Just watch the AI fail.

This makes the strategic map part even more boring, because you know that the AI will never be able to take any of your regions because you can always play the defending battle manually and win, even if the AI has five times more troups.
The offense also becomes annoying and frustrating because on the campaign map with just auto resolve you will never be able to meaningfully advance anywhere, but if you play the exact same battles manually you will win a great victory even when you have barely more troupes than your opponent.
So once you are ahead in national will from manual battles, you just grind down your opponent's national will in auto resolved battles because you can't be bothered to play the same tedious battle over and over again in manual mode.

The evolving battlefield is basically completely irrelevant. In an entire campaign, you will maybe play one or two maps with already set up trenches from a previous battle. The battlefield devastation does not change either (at least not that I noticed).

As said above, I want to like the game, I like what it tries to do, but this ain't it.

There's a lot of solid ideas here that just didn't pan out and a few questionable gameplay decisions which, while I understand what they were TRYING to do, lead to boring repetitive frustrating gameplay. I get it, that's how WWI was and that might be ok except for one glaring issue: the AI simply does not know how to play this game. On the campaign map and the field battles, on attack and defense, and in resource management it just plains sucks. In my several playthroughs I did not see them make even one intelligent move and once you learn the game mechanics they do not pose a threat at all.

Also, the persistent environmental destruction things was a bald faced lie. I can forgive bugs, glitches, and mechanics that didn't quite work out but this was one of the major selling points game and feels like straight up false advertising. Without it, the battles feel disconnected and weightless.

I'll keep an eye on it for future patches and I bet modders could do wonders here but for now I'm shelving it and recommend you wait for it to go on sale before trying it out.\t\t\t\t\t
