\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've been waiting for a proper WW1 strategy game, and this is half of that equation. It's the first and only RTS to properly implement the attrition and artillery-heavy infantry tactics of WW1. Not perfect but it captures the spirit and scale that makes it a fairly unique RTS and much-needed game to represent the WW1 era.

The negatives I have are the missed opportunities and AI jank that is inherent with a lot of strategy games, there's no MP campaign and no early-war uniforms/campaigns. I understand you could easily make an entire game based off of August and September 1914, having a highly mobile early game with riflemen and cavalry devolving into static trench warfare could've had really interesting gameplay potential. But as of now, starting in Dec. 1914 feels like you're just grinding and waiting until 1916 when the interesting techs and units unlock and the game really starts. I would love to see an update that adds Early War uniforms or even a Battle of the Frontiers DLC, but I bought this game at launch because WW1 is a highly underused era in video games and we need more WW1 RTS/strategy games.

I hope the devs keep developing this game which is why I bought the Victory Edition, but as of now, it's a solid game with some jank and missing details/polish (I'd love an Early War update/DLC, a playable French historical battle, and giving the French/German world map models a rifle that's not an SMLE). Buy it if you love the time period or want a unique RTS experience.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tReally, really good. Only weakness is that the soundtrack that does not quite match the epic scope of losing 10 thousand men over a trench. I suggest you listen Battlefield 1 OST while you play for the optimum experience. Also, graphics could be slightly prettier and grittier, but that does not subtract from the experience.

Pros:
- Epic evolving war (from the beginnings where you only have basic trenches to when you start developing chemical weapons and such, you do feel like certain strategies simply do not work. Per example, scout balloons as an early tech clearly become obsolete once the enemy has airplanes - that is, if you don't have planes yourself). It is quite an unique experience how you learn to build and acquire tech to develop more sophisticated trench networks.

- Consistent battlefields that change with season. Issues like snow, mud and rain do affect the battles consistently. The trenches that you build from previous battles and that do not get ravaged are still there.

- Battles are realistic mass slaughters. You do feel the despair when the encroaching enemy has so many men that they fill your entire screen only for you to attempt at destroying them with desperate artillery barrages, machine guns and pulling men from the reserves to man up the lost trenches. The battles can get nerve wrecking at times, which makes every cease fire feel earned.

- Impressive strategic depth campaign + battle integration. When it comes to planning, it is Total War on steroids. From how many troops you use in pre-deployment, to choosing if you should invest your always tight supply pool doing more artillery strikes or call in more reinforcements; or if you should risk losing entire battallions in order to win as much territory in a battle as possible rather than waiting for the maximum amount of artillery barrages... The game has an outstanding variety of strategies. It will be a while before you find your own optimum way to win.

- Easy to learn, very hard to master. This game can get very hard on Normal difficulty.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA gruelling and brutal RTS certainly fitting for such a horrific war.

I am very passionate about the history of the First World War, so I was overjoyed to see that Petroglyph (of Empire at War fame!) were working on a title set in the period. Finally! A *definitive* Great War RTS that isn't decades old or a half-abandoned mod! I am very happy to say that GW:WF delivers on much of the tactics and feel of how a WW1 Strategy game should be. Its translation of trench warfare, defence in depth, and storm tactics is fun, rewarding, and mostly authentic. If you are curious about the First World War, or are looking for a challenging RTS with a unique edge - I would recommend buying GW:WF as soon as possible. Support the developers!

My issues with GW:WF are nitpicks - mostly historical paraphernalia and the occasionally bizarre nature of the AI. I should note that the latter has already been identified and appears to be getting worked on. I am somewhat disappointed at the, ironically, static nature of the factions available. Due to the sheer scale of each tactical map, the individual soldiers (of which there may be thousands on screen) lack much of the personality and distinctiveness of each army which participated on the Western Front. Uniforms do not change; even in late 1914, the armies are dressed in 1918 uniform - helmets and all. I have noticed that all models, regardless of allegiance, carry British SMLE rifles. Light Machineguns, which massively altered the way in which the armies fought due to the wildly different takes on what the concept meant (e.g. the Lewis gun vs MG08/15) do not appear to be present. Said weapons were proliferated massively from 1916 onwards.

Finally, I do find it disappointing that the selection of maps is so limited. There are some which certainly reflect areas such as Passchendaele, the Verdun region, etc, but the French frontier maps will very quickly repeat. Perhaps that is the point, but it extracts the sense of fighting a war which extended from the Alps to the Yser. Thankfully, they all provide a unique challenge and the robust trench-building system allows you to rapidly redefine areas.

Overall, even if you do share my trivial concerns with the above, I would highly recommend The Great War: Western Front. In addition to highlighting an increasingly dismissed part of modern history, as well as its ruthless lessons, we need new RTS games which do things differently.\t\t\t\t\t
