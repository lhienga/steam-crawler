\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA Major Letdown

As someone who has been following this since before release, I waited as I saw the game released with some bugs and initial hiccups. This is not the reason why I do not recommend this game.

A year after release, this game does not capture the time period it seeks to replicate in its core gameplay loop. To summarize, the main loop is this: attack a bordering sector, capture all 3 points to win the battle. Each victory decreases the sector's "stars" by 1 until there are none left and you capture the sector. Some sectors have more stars than others.

The most frustrating thing about this, and one of the only reason reasons I don't recommend the game, is that if you don't capture all 3 points, you lose *all* progress. The game reminds you in loading screens, something to the effect of, "victory was measured in yards, not miles". Laughable considering one can capture 2/3 objectives just to have to start all over again.

You will load into the same persistent map again, keeping all trenches you built, but you lose the vast areas of control you worked so hard to gain the previous turn. There's no building on previous success, no connecting trench lines, and no slow crawl to victory that I was hoping for.

That all being said, the game has an intricate research web, interesting unit design, and compelling level design. I was so engrossed in the environment when learning the game. Maybe that explains the depth of my disappointment after realizing the gameplay simply does not match the incremental gains that were iconic in the Great War. I so hope that this is on the Dev's radar, because if not, this aspect of the game should give any prospective buyer pause.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's frustrating how much potential this game has. i really tried to like it.

The core mechanics are all there, the overall feel is there.
The grinding, the endless trenchlines, the brutal charges, the attrition, fighting for the same little spot again and again.

But there's so little content, the optimization is terrible, the graphic is extremely outdated, the balance is off, battles are clunky with abysmal pathfinding, the AI is dumb as but also sees every inch of the map, everything gets so frustrating so soon.

If this game was released in 2003 it would be a best-seller and set an important milestone for the strategic genre, but it was released in 2023 therefore it feels like a genius idea, but half-assed, left bare bones and unrefined. I'll keep it installed in the hope that someone creates a huge overhaul mod or the devs release substantial updates.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR: too many RTS and not enough wargaming elements. I am voting Not Recommend because I don't see myself spending more time with this title. But that doesn't mean it's not worth trying. Especially if you pick it up on sale.

TGW toes the line between a more casual RTS game and a proper wargame. The devs have put a lot of love into this game. This is evident in the event system and the variety of units, especially on the Allied side (French and British colonial troops, etc.). The game has character and atmosphere. Historical events, news headlines, and period music add to the sense of time and place.

TGW's core gameplay loop does a good job approximating the nature of WWI warfare. To be effective on the defense and the attack you will use the same strategies the belligerents developed historically: defense in depth and combined arms warfare. Researching new technologies, deploying them on the battlefield and building up trench networks and supply stockpiles is appropriately satisfying.

At first I enjoyed the tactical battles. Over time, I found them increasingly tedious. This is due to the system TGW uses to prevent you blitzing across the strategic map. To capture a region on the strategic map, you need to win multiple overwhelming tactical victories. This means fighting the same tactical battle on the same map. Again. And again. And again. It wouldn't feel so tedious if you didn't always start the tactical battles from the same jumping-off points, with you and the enemy holding the same control points, regardless of any territory you took in any previous tactical battles on that map. You can route the enemy one turn and if you don't clear the region on the same turn you will find your guys back at their original start lines on the next turn.

The constant deja vu is exacerbated by the repetitive nature of fighting tactical battles against the AI. On the defense, you build out a defense in depth and then jam reserves into any part of the line the AI comes close to cracking. On the offense, you concentrate on one flank or the other, break the initial line and then roll the enemy up. Sometimes you get it all done in time to force a surrender (and win that all-important Great Victory). Sometimes you don't. The tactical battles all end up feeling the same.

Likewise, there is no order of battle. You have infinite manpower reserves. The limiting factor is gold reserves, which you spend to replace losses. I understand why the devs chose this system. This is not War in the East 2. However, TGW's handling of replacements further strips the tactical battles of weight and meaning. I didn't end up feeling like I was feeding precious lives into a meat grinder to inch forward across Western Europe. Rather, it felt like contrived systems were preventing me from making progress commensurate with what I was achieving at the tactical level.

There are interesting strategic level gameplay elements here, and some interesting tactical level elements as well. They just don't come together in a game I want to sink more hours into.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Great War Western Front isn't exactly unfun, but the difficulty is through the roof for this game in a way that I didn't expect. I played through the tutorial and got a grasp of the controls. I rebinded my keys. I was ready to play, and I selected the average difficulty the game says is the recommended experience for players. Once I got into the main campaign I was shocked with how the core gameplay loop plays out. You fight, which is 90% of the game. It's not too hard to turn likely defeats into victories if you play carefully and use your units well, which was fairly quick and intuitive to learn. However, if you win a battle, it means next-to-nothing because your victory is graded by its intensity. It isn't enough to win a battle, you have to crush the enemy 100%, but even that isn't enough because you have to do it 2-5 times per territory just to budge one hex on the map. Fighting a battle manually could take easily over 20 minutes if you want to be meticulous enough to crush the enemy entirely. I had my timer set at 25 minutes and found myself rushing to the point where careless mistakes were easy to make. A longer timer may be needed, which makes doing a single battle take forever in a game that is nothing but battle after battle after battle. They need to be snappy in order to be worth doing, but they need to be substantive wins in order to justify even fighting a battle manually rather than auto-resolving so it becomes difficult to justify engaging in a manual battle at all. Doing manual battles multiple times in a row explains how I wound up playing for about 15 hours, yet I had made next-to-no progress as a result. I was so dumbfounded that I became determined to make a breakthrough. Other people may finish the game in 10-15 hours because they'll auto-resolve almost everything which is probably how the bulk of the game is meant to be played. But that ain't me, it's not how I roll, so this game really isn't for me or anyone like me who approaches this like it's a game of Total War where you do it all yourself to turn a bad situation around. I do not want to play Western Front any more, and removed it from my steam library. I ended up playing a different kind of strategy game than I was hoping for. It's historically accurate in that it's a herculean task to make the slightest progress on the western front. Accurate to a fault for some, including myself. If you want to go for the win, the game makes you smash your head against a rock to get anywhere and the rock wins more often than your head.\t\t\t\t\t
