The game is way too basic for something being developed in 2020s. If it was 1995 this game would be the ♥♥♥♥. I digress.

Cons:
Very simple battle field maps that are on constant repeat. I think I've seen a grand total of 4 maps. 8 because you get to flop sides! Detailed maps are a absolute must for a game like this. Please take a look at Ultimate General and Grand Tactitian Civil War. These are some great examples to pull from as they play very simular. I'm sure there are old maps floating about that document what the terrain looked like during the 1910s.

No multiple attack options. You can only attack from one region to another. Eg. Verdun has 4 german tiles connected to it. So instead of pooling resources to get a resource advantage(I'd also love to see a detailed map where they are essential surrounded if you attack from multiple regions), you have to attack Verdun 1 region at a time...
I'm sorry Devs, but what were you thinking on this one.

Not much is explained. Be prepared to not have a single clue about anything for a while. But since it's a simple game you'll pick it up.

I hope this helps the Devs understand where they definitely need improvement. I desperately wanted to refund the game at 2 hours. However I kept at it hoping it would open up. I was disappointed in the end.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI very rarely write reviews but this game is insanely good.

As a Military Historian and TBS/RTS fanatic, it scratches all the itches. It doesn't rely on familiar mechanics or recognizable themes, it has a fresh take on a unique war. The game is not about 'winning' both in battles and campaign, its about sucking your opponent dry of resources so the do not have the ability or will to continue the fight. Its not often you get a game where you can win by breaking the morale of the Home Front and this is perfect for it.

Saying that, I would like to see further development of the game, it has such fantastic potential.
- Italy as a tangible faction with a spawn point in SE France.
- The integration of railways that would allow the enemy to intersect rail lines to prevent troop movements and resupply.
- The ability to see enemy artillery when they fire to initiate counter-battery fire.
- Ability to move infantry through pre-dug mine craters (that's what they were for)
- Night Battles

Looking forward to continued development of this fantastic game.\t\t\t\t\t

Learning to Play
*Long review*
The game is a classic strategy game with an overhead campaign map in a modern visual WW1 style, where you manage resources in a turn-based fashion and then attack and defend regions, leading to real-time battles. The game has a large variety of tactics you can use, which may take some getting used to... as I found out playing as the Allies.

Battle 1, attempt #1
Organising my units on the campaign map, I unlocked research favouring infantry warfare. I wanted to take Lille with 4 Belgian units stationed at Ypres. Germany only had 3 units there and breaking through the coastal area seemed a great idea to get started. The battle commenced and I focused my units on the left flank, intending to overrun the Germans with numbers.

However, I didn't have the hang of timing my artillery barrages. My troops faced unsuppressed German units and were annihilated by just a few of theirs. I royally messed this up. Lesson learned about timing my artillery.

Battle 1, attempt #2
Decided to retry. Same battle, same plan. This time my soldiers touched the enemy trenches exactly when my last shells exploded. A melee fight ensued and with superior numbers I overcame the German defense. However, the sneaky AI countercharged my right flank... which I left without units and trenches. I hastily called in reinforcements and put them at the treeline. Luckily, without trenches the enemy had no cover either and my volley hit them first. It proved crucial, as despite taking losses I held ground... and learned a second lesson: keeping defensive units around.

Battle 2
Feeling experienced. I dared have a go at Lille again with just two combat-ready units stationed at Hazebrouck. Success meant the liberation of Lille.

I did, however, choose to not build a Supply Station (lesson three: it makes a huge difference). As the battle started, I realized I had no supplies for spare barrages or units, everything had to go perfectly. Again I massed on the left and positioned some trenches on my right, with one placed slightly ahead, so that attacking Germans would be compelled to attack it first. Four of my infantry units manned this side.

The offense went well, the Germans had few units in their trenches. However, I spotted a massive attack, launched at my right flank. Some dozens of German units attacked. However, my units picked off their early units and by the time some bloodied Germans reached that forward trench, much of their units were wiped out. With my units in good health, I spotted an equally big second wave following behind it. They didn't fare any better.

As I took the command post and final victory point, all guns fell silent. Lille, was liberated, at the cost of ~1.6K men lost from the garrison of Hazebrouck... and leaving 7K German losses.

This, would only be the beginning of the long war to come.


Positives
+ The visuals are nice and set a good WW1 vibe. Visuals and gameplay perfectly blend: an expertise you will seldomly see done so well. The Campaign map and UI is a blast from the past if you are used to Star Wars: Empire at War (it's Petroglyph).

+ Campaign map mechanics are very straightforward. Do your research, balance your income and supplies. Then divide troops between frontline sectors and decide where you will do battle. In real time battles it all comes down to timing your artillery barrages and infantry pushing while keeping an eye on your flank. Or, use other equipment like tanks, planes and undermines (that can blow up entire trenches).

+ The AI is competent in trying many tactics in real-time battles. For example, while on the defense, they can randomly decide to go on a counterattack.

+ No battle really feels quite the same.


Negatives
Note: none of these are distractions from gameplay, they are minor grievances (borderline nitpicking).

- Loading a singleplayer Campaign game, the menu may freeze if you click "Load game" and allows you to select another savegame and press "Load game" again. At times this leads to some weird errors as if you try to connect to a multiplayer lobby. Probably a UI bug. Also, creating a new savegame when having an existing savegame selected overwrites the old one even if you gave it a new name.

- Every unit is one entity, so it's not like every individual infantryman fires at an enemy unit. This means if you barely tickle the shooting range of an enemy unit, the entire unit hits you and it can lead to very high casualties, very quickly. I'd rather see that a tiny overlap means taking tiny bits of damage, but to be fair, it does make sense in a WW1 game.

- At times you select a unit and tell it to move somewhere and they just refuse to do it. I think pathing/garrisoning trenches commands can disrupt other units. Also I noticed that tanks sometimes keep facing a random direction even if you point it elsewhere.


Verdun...ehr...Verdict
This is a superb game. Don't expect a Total War experience or dynamic automatic frontline combat like in HoI IV. You will need to make rational, tactical decisions on and off the battlefield.

The strategical Campaign map management and UI are very remniscent of old good turn-based Strategy games (with real-time battles) like Star Wars: Empire at War. It has lots of tactical options (artillery, tanks, planes, undermines, defenses, flanking, resource usage, using intelligence to spot enemy entities and so on), neatly packaged away in a research tree that helps evolve frontline trench combat "across years of war", suitable to the player's choices.

With the choice to play as Allies or Germany, there is a lot of replay value, but otherwise you can always give Skirmish/Multiplayer a go. It also has a nice training mission that you can continue into a campaign. I love how you can use undermines to completely blow up trenches and leave a massive crater, most prominently referring to the the "Lochnagar Crater" (near La Boisselle, Somme region) and the "Messines mines".

I also think this is a great game for people who loved the concept of (but were disappointed by) games like Ultimate General: Gettysburg (confusing terrain height and unit veterancy mechanics) or RIOT: Civil Unrest (completely broken game, units rarely follow orders). The Great War: Western Front gives you a ton of units to throw at the enemy, but the enemy has the same. You have a lot to fight with, but must still think rationally how you are going to tactically make use of it... or else you are just throwing away men like in... WW1.

The lack of good WW1 Strategy games has been a thorn in the sides of PC gamers since forever. Well, we've finally got a good one: a really good one! It's very well made, it's a BLAST. I rarely ever buy games full price these days, but I bought this at full price. It did not disappoint. Thank you for this gem, Petroglyph!

Now excuse me, I have to liberate Europe.\t\t\t\t\t
