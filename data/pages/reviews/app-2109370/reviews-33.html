\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA tentative recommendation. I really do see the good in the game, but there are clearly a few things that would enhance the experience:

- Allow a non-historical war. maybe as a different mode. Enforcing an end date is kind of irritating. I would love to be able to play a 10, 15, 20 year war, having a proper chance to sweep the map and make up my own head-canon of the apocalyptic conditions. The pre-defined events are also a bit off - I was told that the British had created mysterious fortresses on tracks, and we should destroy a few to show them how they had wasted their time on making these lumbering useless things...meanwhile I've spent months capturing their positions with German tanks.

- Enhance AI play. A lot of game time is spent moving your corps to face the enemy corps, and then they don't attack because it would be too costly, so they move their corps, so you move yours to face theirs AGAIN...repeat. The AI never seems to do anything more interesting than "create doom stack" and "cancel doom stack attack, enemy stack also quite doomish".

- Enhance AI play in battles too. The early battles especially are pretty poor - spam unit spawns on to threatened control point -> control point lost, spam units towards lost control point. The moment you get tanks this becomes dangerously stupid, but they keep doing it. Only when AI has access to tanks, raiders, flamethrowers, and planes, does it really get interesting.

- Enhance espionage. Related to the above, if both player and AI could pull tricks like making a force look larger in a certain area (or smaller than it really is), it could produce some exciting and unexpected battles.

I really do enjoy the game, it scratches a certain strategic itch around positioning forces and ensuring they have the right supply and support...then playing as those forces in real time, very nice. I just get a little disheartened after 10 turns moving pieces around without any action, then find the battles themselves a bit lackluster, especially early on when supply is short.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSome up front context: I am the resident "Great War nerd" in my friend group (wrote my undergrad thesis on the Great War in sub-Saharan Africa), so I do know a great deal about the Great War (but I do not claim to be a professional historian on the subject).

EDIT 9 November: Petroglyph has made a couple updates since this review, notably adding air recon and additional corps (Bavarians for the Germans and French Colonial and Army Corps of Africa), which add some great flavor to the French and Germans. I hope that next Petroglyph is looking at adding the US Marines next, as their omission is glaring now that we have the FFL and Bavarians. Also an update to the in-game encyclopedia with entries for these new units would be appreciated. Game is still an 8/10 for me.

What this game does well is create the feeling of the Great War, and gives a basic idea of why trench warfare became the M.O. for most of the war (whole companies getting mowed down by rifle and MG fire in the open brings to mind the Battle of the Frontiers in 1914). What this game is NOT, and was not meant to be, is a 100% accurate Great War simulation, nor is is meant to be a CoH or Steel Division style highly detailed tactical combat game, but in the trenches. IMHO any reviewer who was expecting such misinterpreted what Petroglyph set out to do. Authenticity and atmosphere are leading here, not accuracy and detail.

I pick up some heavy Empire at War notes in the game design (capture point focused tactical maps, lack of individual unit promotion or experience gain) but that isn't a bad thing. In addition I get the feel of playing a board game when on the campaign map, which I actually enjoy. For me the sliding victory / defeat level scale actually makes achieving a Great Victory more satisfying. Pathfinding could use some work, and trench traffic jams can cause issues and eat up battle time. Air recon is sorely lacking, and I think is a needed addition. Furthermore, while the game is more focused on tone vs detail and accuracy, I would appreciate some historical details being added to create more depth, such as upgrading fighters from early war models to better performing late war models rather that just having the generic "fighter" represented in game by the Sopwith Camel and Fokker Dr. 1.

The soundtrack is a definite winner. All glory to Frank Klepacki.

For me the game is a definite 8/10. Again not accurate but it isn't supposed to be. Would recommend along with some reading to get a full understanding of the Great War.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPut it back in... it's not done yet. Why this isn't an early access game, I'm not sure. Promising, but broken.

The strangest thing about the game is not the wonky clicking or strange supply system, it's that the number of men killed is entirely abstracted away. As long as you win enough, your side has literally an unlimited supply of men, and the number killed has no effect whatsoever on any part of the game. All men are replenished after every battle, without loss of experience, changes in position, or any other consequence of any type. It has a strange way of encouraging the player to simply meat grind their way to victory, throwing away their best troops as if pouring grains of sand over an anthill... which, um, isn't how WWI was actually won... and is famously how it was lost, and nearly lost, by several of the combatants.

It's very strange for a WWI game to care nothing about casualties. In actual history, the military collapse (due to manpower being swallowed up faster than it could be replaced) of one side led to a revolution (Russian Empire); the casualty lists of another led to mass hangings of fighting men and a near societal collapse (France); and total exhaustion of a third led to one of the most complete surrenders of all time and a failed communist revolution (Germany). Victories were not lacking in WWI, and didn't prolong it as such. It was the swallowing up of literally millions of men which was the hallmark of the then "Great War".

P.S. It was won not by "dying for your country, but making the other bastard die for his."\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI enjoy this game. The trench mechanics are interesting, and the core gameplay loop seems to have several possible tactics that will lead to victory. For example, in one campaign I prioritised gas attacks and effective infantry while in another campaign I prioritised massed armor.

That said, I'm going to go into greater detail on the less good parts, because I have a vain hope they might be improved in future!

First, supply costs are all over the place, and kind of break the dynamic, especially with the defensive emplacements. Machine guns cost as much as 6 standard infantry companies to set up during the initial phase, which is reasonable enough for 1914 (well, for the allies), but absurd for 1916. You get better and better defensive emplacements, but I kept finding that they cost too much to be worth using. For the costs they run, they probably need to be persistent (and maybe capturable?)

The other big issue is that the AI is not that strong.

It is fairly effective at massed infantry attacks supported by light artillery, but those attacks are expensive and the AI often fails to keep a sufficient reserve to defend itself after attacking. Weirdly, this is especially true on defensive battles, where it's fairly common to see an outnumbered AI throw its entire complement of soldiers at less defended parts of the player line. Even on offense, if the player turtles around the command trench, a massively outnumbered force can hold out and the AI will throw away enough soldiers that the battle still comes out as a loss or a stalemate for the AI. This is despite the AI having captured all of the victory points except the command trench. This allows the player to dramatically underman most of the front, with only 2-3 companies each, and to pull everything else into a doomstack with more supply than you could want.

The AI also overdevelops its trench lines, eventually connecting the entire territory with them if a territory is fought over enough. Counter-intuitively, this is quite bad strategy. As you get into the late game, sweeps actually become easier, as once you are into their trench network the cost of attack moves from needing about 5 to 1 advantage along with artillery to even get a foothold to needing closer to a 1 to 1 ratio, even without artillery. If you still have artillery (and especially gas) that advantage swings heavily in your favor. The AI needs to build more self contained bastions.

It also really shows weakness when tanks come onto the scene. The AI sends 1 tank at a time. This means that they are generally not decisive when the AI uses them. When the player uses them, however, they can mass a solid group of 6 or so, allowing a very easy full sweep. The most catastrophic example of this in my play so far saw the AI take 17k casualties attacking a trench line supported by 6 tanks. At the same time, the tanks can be killed with enough rifle fire, which seems odd. I would suggest that the tanks need to be more protected against rifles, but much less reliable. Taking notes from how the vehicles in EUGEN games work, with ammunition and breakdowns, would probably be good here.

Finally, it doesn't feel like the AI matches it's research to what the player is deploying in any way. This became really clear in a game where I went all in on gas attacks, and the AI never developed gas masks. The whole "Tear gas causes them to run from trench where we can now shoot them" thing is great... but it needs to stop working pretty quickly or it becomes a easy total campaign victory.

All that said, overall, I quite liked the game! It manages to make trench warfare more interesting than I would have expected, and I like the way the tactical and strategic maps play.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game is fun, but there are glaring issues.

Problems:

1: Artillery suppression is all over the place, when you call down an arty barrage it suppresses any units, expect tanks, within the area of effect. If so much as one soldier is inside this AoE circle the whole unit is suppressed. Sometimes units outside the AoE circle, and I mean noticeably outside the circle, will be suppressed and other times units in the middle of the circle won't be suppressed, I've noticed this with Machine Gun nests the most.

2: Units will bug out when leaving a trench sometimes, running out just to moonwalk back in just to run back out, and sometimes units just don't respond to orders. I've encountered this bug mainly while trying to attack an enemy trench. Planes sometimes won't shoot other planes directly in front of it. Tanks sometimes won't attack units out in the open. Notice how I say sometimes, for the most part your soldiers do what you want, but when they don't it's noticeable and frustrating. Whole units will be wiped out because they refuse to move into an enemy trench.

3: The game ends too quickly. Every turn is a month and you start in December 1914 and I think the game ends in 1919. By the time you have your tanks and bombers and all the bells and whistles the game is over. I understand the devs wanting historical accuracy but there should be a game mode where you're not restrained by the clock.

4: The animations just plain suck. The same death animations are used, same rifle motions, and it doesn't look good. It doesn't look "bad" but it's seriously underwhelming for a game released in 2023.

Positives:

1: The game beautifully captures the brutality of WW1. Thousands of soldiers die every battle to achieve relatively little. Do not be attached to your soldiers, they will die brutally in hand-to-hand combat or otherwise be shot or blown up. The trench warfare is fun, the perpetual battlefield of ever-evolving trench lines is fun to see.

2: The game gives the player a decent amount of choices in how they want to brutalize the enemy. Do you want to go heavy air? Maybe you want to rain down thousands of artillery shells on the French? Or maybe you prefer to roll over your enemies in tanks and decimate whole trench lines in seconds? There's also the tried and true method of sending 10s of thousands of conscripts directly into machine gun fire to make way for your flamethrowers and other elite infantry. The game gives the player different ways to conduct warfare and they're all viable, if used right.


Notes:

1: Supply is probably the most important factor on any given battlefield. Supply dictates how many soldiers you can call in, as long as you have the unit cap to do so, and how much artillery you can call in as well as how many trenches you can place and how many machine guns/barbed wire/ mortars/mines you can place. If you ignore your supply, you will lose.

2: You will NOT be able to research everything in one playthrough, so be careful what you research.

3: The game is hard, expect to lose a couple battles, especially early on. Unless you're playing on literally the easiest difficulty you will inevitably lose a battle.\t\t\t\t\t
