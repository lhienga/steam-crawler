\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a really difficult review for me to write. Because on one hand, I'm having a lot of fun with this game and want to see it grow and develop and maybe push a sequel one day, but on the other hand, I can't help but be incredibly disappointed with the finished product. There's a lot of potential here, but its current state just feels so hollow.

Cons:
- They lied about battlefield persistence. Although you do see your trench lines develop over time, which is super fun to see, the rest of the battlefield will always reset back to default. Craters, destroyed houses, burnt trees, corpses, once the battle is over, that all gets reset.
- Underwhelming audio. I'm not an audiophile, but man does this game sound mid. The score is almost whimsical and happy? Not something you'd expect to hear when you're sending tens of thousands of teenagers to their deaths in the trenches. Guns and artillery sound dated, and there is a serious lack of trench whistle. Sure you hear some cheers and a whistle when you have 2 units next to each other engage in melee. But when I'm sending 20 companies over the top, the least I should expect is some cheering and whistles. Just hearing "Yes sir" as you watch 7500+ men get mowed down by machine guns is kinda lame.
- Graphics - It's a strategy game, I wasn't expecting a visual marvel, but ffs I thought we left corpses disappearing after 3 seconds back in the 90s. I want to see the carnage after a huge battle, everything looks so clean and sterile currently. At least let us configure how long the corpses should persist for.
- The lack of multiplayer campaign is enraging. You can't just build an awesome campaign map then tell us it's for singleplayer only.
- Battle limits and campaign time limit. Who thought it was a good idea to cap battles at 20 minutes, and force the campaign to be over in 1919 at the latest? That doesn't even give us enough time to research all our technology let alone play an involved campaign. Why limit your players with how much you can play your game? We're not in an arcade playing with quarters ffs.
- I know the game is called the western front, but the complete cutting out of Austria-Hungary, Italy, The Ottomans, and Russia is really disappointing.
- DRM in 2023? Wtf were you thinking?

Pros:
- They did a very good job of making you have to get creative with WW1 tactics. Infantry is squishy, if you don't properly plan your assaults with support and follow-ups, you're going to see your boys get slaughtered (just like in real life!)
- Trench building and seeing the battlefield evolve over time with your ever advancing networks is satisfying
- It's an actually functioning WW1 RTS

TLDR: If you're hardcore jonesing for a WW1 grand strategy game, pick this game up on sale. If this isn't you, skip on this unless they improve the game significantly, its current state is not worth it.

Finally, Dear Devs, if you are reading this, please, I beg of you, take our complaints into consideration. I REALLY want to like this game, I have been waiting years for this exact type of game. I just can't in good faith honestly say it's all we wanted and more. Although I'm having fun, I see myself playing a few more hours before it gets uninstalled and goes into my never touch again library for good, I really don't want that to be the case :(\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRecommended with caveats. It's a perfectly fine light RTS for anyone to get into and enjoy, up to 2-3 playthroughs.

At first it seems to capture the spirit of ww1 and be designed as such well, however after playing further and watching other campaigns, there's some nagging issues. Some easily fixed, and some that require a bit more thought but with plenty of room for improvement to make a truly excellent experience.

BUGS - There's some:
-Infantry that gets stuck on trenches, or even becomes invincible in the process.
-Tanks that randomly stops firing.
-AI Tanks that sits around and sleeps all game until attacked.
-Perhaps not a bug, but there's issues with pathfinding, leading infantry to decide that leaving trenches to get gunned down is preferable to just using the connected trenches to get to a target safely.

- Strategic Map AI is poor, regardless of difficulty. It will leave several territories with 1-3 corps, making for easy great victories/sweeps even at higher difficulty, and this ties into another flaw of the map:
- The Strategic Map is at points just 2-3 tiles from the "map edge" from the starting positions, with 2-3 star map tiles that are easy to take (eastern france/southern germany). Combined with the poor strategic AI, that does not prioritize beefing up these VITAL tiles you can easily cut off large parts of the map which leads to basically winning the campaign from the massive loss of corps and national will.

- Units cannot attack if they are encircled, you do not even need to guard them and can leave any tile unguarded that doesn't border a connected enemy tile. So the moment you complete that eastern encirclement, you can move 4-5 tiles worth of corps elsewhere, ensuring the AI cannot break out their encircled units.
Solution: Make those southern tiles that are connected to france/germany (but you can't enter) have alternative supply modifiers that prevents encirclement.

- You can strategically redeploy an unlimited number of corps from anywhere on the map to any other territory that you hold (which isn't encircled).

- National Will is fixed and annot be modified, with big losses/gains from great and majority victories. This makes it very hard to lose to the AI, and makes it exceedingly difficult to go for say: Paris, unless you soley focus on that because the game will end from national will losses to the AI before you even get close.

- NATIONAL WILL: At 1918/1919 the game starts subtracting -100 national will *per turn* from both sides. The sides are unlikely to have more than 400 national will at this time. This is a very clumsy solution, and a smaller 10-20 NW loss per turn would be more suitable (perhaps tying into some other stat than just time passed - like total losses of men or falling under a certain level of national will in the first place). Randomly starting to lose like 25% of NW per turn just because you cross one date limit is a bit dull design.

- Limited Campaign Options and replayability: There's only 2 campaigns, with no settings aside for difficulty. You can't adjust starting tech or national will etc. There's no "what if" scenarios (various German offensive paths not-through-Belgium for example).

Problems with Tech design:
- In 1914 start you have way too little tech points available, and you'll spend a year just unlocking basic logistics and income techs, nevermind basics like barbed wire, aircraft (you don't even start with basic ♥♥♥♥♥♥ ww1 scout planes) etc.
While the "espionage" part of the tech tree might appear interesting, a lot of techs are "nice to have" rather than vital, which leads to rather samey tech unlocks.
Solution: I'd like to see you start with more points overall, and make add more bonuses to less interesting techs. Do we really need to spend tech points on making tanks 25% faster in mud (a condition that only appears during specific circumstances)?
- The lack of starting points also means that if you (or the AI) does NOT go for certain techs, they'll be at a big disadvantage for months or even years. Certain techs are just game winning if you have them and the enemy doesn't have defenses for it.

- Some tech and strats are unbalanced/overpowered. Gas that forces enemies out of their trenches, aircraft that give +100 supplies and stack without drawbacks or diminishreturns, strafing aircraft swarms that can pick off any unit you force out of trenches, tanks without any of the ww1 drawbacks.
- Artillery has no direct fire mode vs tanks for example, so driving up to artillery is perfectly safe (it's only dangerous to stop and sit through indirect artillery shelling).

UNLIMITED AMMO/STAMINA:
-Units have unlimited ammo, tanks and infantry alike. While a nice simplification, it does lead to some very un-ww1 like scenarios - driving your tanks around like ww2 tanks and taking the entire map, holding off dozens of companies of infantry because you can't run out of ammo for your tanks. No tanks breaking down etc.

- 1914-1915: Early game is much too easy to capture territory with, partially because of the lack of basic tech like barbed wire, expensive machinegun nests and (more on this below) very modern artillery mechanics.
Units can simply waltz through artillery fire zones to get into trenches, unless you drop several batteries worth right on the infantry company. But all this is connected with a bigger design issue...

UNLIMITED COMMUNICATIONS:
- A very un-WW1 design, where you have perfect UAV modern RTS command vision and communication with your troops across the map. Yes, fog of war is there to an extent (balloons, and from men), but calling in artillery strikes is both near instant and *very* accurate. There's no drift or scatter mechanic, shells falling short etc.
You can perfectly micro your men, pause time etc.
There's no tech or mechanic to represent the *wired* communications of the time.
In 1914 and 1915 (and 1916) it would be exceedingly difficulty for a high level commander like yourself to manage troops in this way. Both assaults and artillery would have to be pre-planned, and once it gets going it would just get harder to communicate with frontline units (relying on runners where you can't pull wire communication forwards).
- The only representation of this is the on map command post, which *if taken* prevents reinforcements from being called in. But you can still command your entire battlefield force just fine!

What I'd like to see is techs and mechanics to represent communication and logistic issues. So starting out you're limited to pre-planning waypoints for assaults and timed artillery (and things like the pre-battle artillery bombardment option that only becomes available later, currently!),
- Harsher fog of war, once shells start landing and melees start you should be losing direct command and LOS to the frontline (unless you have intact wires/com posts).
-Longer call in times for unplanned artillery strikes, improved through both tech and com structures.
-Early infantry and units having delays to their orders, again- influenced to how close they are to your command posts/control points.
-Tanks to function more like a call in ability (or pre-placed and waypointed at the setup phase pre-battle). Early ww1 tank tactics were by necessity and lack of communication techs limited to lining up tanks hours before a battle and then giving flag commands, pointing them in the right direction and hoping a few % of them got to the other end. Changing objectives mid-battle wasn't easy at all.
-Add Battlefield communication posts similar to other placeable structures helping give an aura of direct command, or cutting down on delayed orders. This would also allow you to target these wires and posts, hindering enemy communication and vision further (or the AI to do that to you).
Tl; dr, by 1917 and onwards you can have the current perfect RTS control, and tech gradually helps you get there as the game progresses.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe idea is great, but the execution is lacking. The game's systems are just not working very well.

Everything in this game costs supply and this is the main limiting factor in every battle. But then what is the point of paying 60 supply points for a bombing run if I can also get 6 extra squads of infantry instead? Why would I bombard the enemy trench if every artillery attack costs more than the infantry it destroys?

The game is like a "do the most with the least amount of resources". And the vast majority of the things in the game seem not to be worth the supply cost.

This seems to be the main reason why the game feels so boring. Every battle is just using the same few tricks. Spending research on new things is just pointless as they are too expensive to use anyway. After a few hours I dreaded having to play yet another repetitve battle and wished I could just hit end turn instead.

Then the AI is also problematic. The AI isn't very good at playing either the strategic layer or the tactical layer. Encirclements of large amounts of enemy troops are easy to do on the strategic map as the AI doesn't reinforce weak areas enough. Frequently a large portion of their forces will be trapped and auto defeated.

The AI unfortunately fails on the tactical level as well. All you have to do is supress with light artillery and charge with infantry. Yet the AI often only supresses half of your trenches and machine guns. The other half can just effortlessly mow down the charging troops, leading to huge pointless slaughters. Even when massively outnumbering you, the AI still doesn't win.

On defending the AI does not create multiple lines of defense and connects everything together. This allows you to easily walk from trench to trench and makes them lose their defensive advantage almost entirely. I often found that the AI loses more soldiers than it kills when defending.

I feel there is a need for a good total war like WW1 simulator, but sadly, this game just is not it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game is, overall, quite fun. Unfortunately it feels much more like an early access title than a full release and for roughly 35 bucks it's an absolute shame how many things are missing while limiting the few aspects that are there.
Probably my biggest gripe with the unnecessary restrictions? In the literal GREAT WAR you are apparently limited to having 30 units on the field at a time and artillery batteries count towards this cap. Ridiculous.

tl;dr up front: Solid foundation, nice premise, really good soundtrack, but otherwise utterly bland, missing QoL improvements left, right and center and an overall letdown when compared to what was promised. For now it's a 6/10 for me.

Time limits
Why so many 'strategic' games insist on having time gates I'll never understand, but this game has them both during the battles as well as on the campaign map and the latter is even worse than the first. You have almost no chance of capturing the enemy's capital before they run out of 'national will' - a currency so arbitrary it's not even funny anymore. And if you don't - well, game's over in 1919 anyway, because the game sure needed a third time restriction, didn't it.

Tech tree
An incredible waste of opportunities and stupidly back loaded. Since you barely get any tech points (1-2 per month) you will only start unlocking really meaningful, impactful and not to mention fun techs in the late/end game phases at which point it's more or less irrelevant. There's also no diversity between Central Powers and Allies in regards to their available perks with the one exception being tanks and even that's marginal. Entire branches of the tree alongside their corresponding game mechanics are utterly useless, 'Intelligence' being the most prominent example. You simply do not need it, ever, rendering 1/6 of the entire tech tree worthless.

Balancing
You always do the same. Get supply perks, then gold perks and then bum rush tanks or planes and if you somehow manage to unlock them before you already won the game (thanks to the aforementioned 'national will' currency) you can then spam the ever-loving crap out of them and win every single encounter from then on with a 'great victory'. Tanks are insanely fast, take very little damage and wipe out everything. And you can apparently have hundreds of them, even as the Central Powers, despite the fact that only a whooping 20 of the German A7Vs actually got built during WWI.
The encirclement mechanic is equally broken. You are able to cut off entire regions of enemy territory by conquering like two hexes with 2 stars and you've basically won and there's nothing the AI can do about it. Not that they actually try to do anything, but still.

AI
Enemy artillery strikes are insanely accurate, the enemy apparently still has no fog of war or weather based debuffs and thus has constant knowledge of where your emplacements and troops are - even newly spawned ones just barely walking into the map from the side are instantly targeted by artillery.
That being said the AI is still very dumb, even while obviously cheating. It telegraphs its every upcoming move on the campaign map with troop stacking and spies and can be easily exploited by using doomstacks and plane/tank spam.
(Un)fortunately the AI being dumb isn't restricted to the enemy, your own guys are perfectly capable of breathing through their ears. Units will randomly leave trenches when ordered to move to a different trench line even when connected to it, will get stuck on enemy or other friendly units and other pleasantries.

Historical events
They're neat, but don't make any sense. They seem to be tied to the corresponding year in which they historically took place, but then ignore the current state of the campaign. I had an event take place regarding the Battle of Verdun lowering my national will..all while I had already captured that entire region many turns before.

UI, UX & general gameplay
No planning tools whatsoever, no chaining orders, no possible micro-management of troops, extremely basic formations.
Furthermore a complete letdown regarding the whole 'battlefields are persistent' mechanic the devs promised. They are not. Trenches are and that's it. There's no scarring of the landscape from artillery, bomb runs or even undermining explosions which leave huge craters in the landscape - but only for the current battle and will revert back to smooth surfaces right after. No corpses, no burned out tanks, no plane wrecks. Even destroyed buildings revert back to their undamaged state after a single battle. An incredible let down.
Hell, even troop movements on the campaign map are lacking animations, they just..glide.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhen this game launched I gave it a negative review, there were serious issues with the AI on both a tactical and strategic level, but to give total credit to the developers, they have GREATLY improved the AI.

So to review the game now, it is VERY good. It is ultimately a WW1 RTS, so the game is slow and tiresome, it is not the kind of game you can binge play for days on end, Its the kind of game that you play for a few hours, put down, do something else, and then come back to once you've essentially refreshed yourself, but frankly it wouldn't be a WW1 game if it felt anything different. You are in control of a slow, grinding war of attrition, and its a good idea to remember that.

In my second campaign, the first with the bettered AI, I played the central powers. I made my first order of business to try and take Dunkirk and Calais to try and essentially knock britian out of the war. I fell short thanks to my treasury collapsing, turns out that throwing millions of men away in a less than 6 month period takes a severe impact on your finances.

I had won a long series of tactical battles, but I had essentially thrown away the strategic war attempting to blitz my way to Calais.

And I really like that even as your winning battles, you have to keep very careful consideration of your resources.

There is still some AI weirdness, although infinitely better than at launch. The enemy strategic AI seems to be near flawless, and the tactical commander AI is much improved. There are still certainly moments of weirdness, for example I've had a few battles where I was desperately holding my command trench with 2 units, completely out of reinforcements, when the AI offered me a ceasefire (essentially calling it a draw), when a tiny advance would have given them a total victory, and the Unit AI still seems to get stuck every now and then, but its much less prevalent than at launch.

Ultimately the game is not perfect, but the developers seem to have dedicated themselves to improving it, making it MUCH better, and I imagine there's more to come.

If you were looking at a game right now, I'd say its certainly worth it if bought on sale, and if you are at all interested in WW1, I wouldn't hesitate to recommend at full price.
