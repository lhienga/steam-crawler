\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR: It's a gem but it needs some serious polishing for it to shine. If you don't already own it, I'd recommend waiting until at least May to see how they tweak it in the first month.

The bad: I am a huge fan of a lot of Petroglyph/Westwood games (I grew up on C&C, loved SW:EAW, Grey Goo, etc.). This is a great entry from them. That being said, it needs a fair bit of additional QA just to iron out what should hopefully be minor things but are game-breaking.

Solid example: Just played a defensive battle where AI troops just hugged the edge of the map and walked past a trenchline full of my guys & a few MG nests who apparently couldn't even see them because they were on/?over? the map edge. They just walked right into my rear-most trenchline without taking a single hit. Map edge stuff is a pretty common human tactic too, so if you want to avoid multiplayer tomfoolery and singleplayer frustration it'd be good to either make those guys shootable or have a more rigid edge to the map that troops can't cross. Maybe have an offmap "neighboring units" line or deployable which can shoot enemy units near the map edge, falling back as the enemy takes points but never moving forward past its original location?

Some graphical glitch stuff too (example: fast-blinking post-battle screens gave me a headache). It's not horrible but it does distract.

The good: It's WW1, and it feels like WW1. You will lose thousands of guys trying to take a map and you need to do it multiple times for it to stick. The star system that forces multiple battles in the same sector before capping it is reasonable given the static nature of the conflict. You start off with Crimean tactics with light/heavy artillery and infantry with rifles in basic unreinforced trenches, and you progress further. I think it's as good of a WW1 simulation as we're likely to see, and I love the mix of turn-based campaign and realtime battlefield tactics, which I think is a good match for the very basic C2 systems of the era (generals point at the map, men die, and any meaningful tactics are local).

The recommendation: I don't regret buying it but it's going to sit on the shelf for a bit to see how Petroglyph addresses bugs. I've seen some response from them already and they do good work so I'm happy to give them the time.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a beautiful, and unusual, strategy game. But it is so unusual that if you approach it the way you approach most strategy games, you could come out of it disliking this game.

This game seeks to put you in the trenches of world war one, and those trenches were unlike anything before or since. Machine guns control ground, not kill. Artillery doesn't kill infantry - it suppresses them. Tanks were invented literally yesterday, and strategies for coping with them - and for using them effectively - do not exist, making them powerful, expensive, and easy to misuse.

This game will challenge your perspective, in a good way if you let it. But it could also frustrate you immensely.

In the hopes of reducing frustration for other players, I wrote a guide with my thoughts after playing it all day for a couple of days pre-release - 'Tips For Avoiding Trenchfoot' - which I strongly recommend you look at if you find the game frustrating, or get confused.

I have never, ever, been put so thoroughly into a time and place by a strategy game before. The combination of elements, from the quirky supply system to the way in which trenches give infantry almost total protection from assaults (in the first months of the war, at least, before tanks and bombers and heavier artillery), the fact that the territory you fight over is a crater-blasted ruin that offers no economic advantage and all troops and supplies are shipped in from off-map, the way choking sound effects rise as you commit war crimes by using mustard gas shells to empty enemy trenches, all combine to make something that is more than the sum of its parts.

This game was clearly made with love, care, and attention.

Battles can turn into some of the most satisfying siege and assaults I've ever played - creeping my units along trenches and hiding in forests before springing out under the cover of artillery fire to assault, carefully planning my defence making use of multiple lines of trenches to watch over each other. It's an experience like no other I've ever had in wargaming.

Having completed a campaign, I don't know how often I'll return to this game - admittedly, I played it quite grindy, using the auto result for battles very rarely, and the pace can be slow - but there is replay value, and this is such a unique opportunity to experience a wargame covering a little explored war that I don't think any serious wargamer can pass up giving it a try. However, you will have to adapt - there is no such thing as a blitzkrieg for another twenty or thirty years. Air support in the way we understand it from other games is a fantasy of the far-flung future. You will have to learn how to fight in the trenches, and it will be different to any war you've ever fought before.\t\t\t\t\t
