\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThere is a lot of promise in this game, but at this point in time I cannot recommend it at full price.

First, there's some serious UI and control issues. Managing combat is hugely clunky. Only two units of your faction can occupy a trench at a time, which means you often have to do an awkward scuffle when maneuvering units around. This is frustrating enough when its in your own back-line, but when it happens during an attack it can be disastrous as troop decide to leave trenches in-front of machine-gun nests or secondary lines full of riflemen. Troops often do not respond quickly to orders, hanging up and freezing if issued too many too often. You also have to carefully click on enemy unit banners and not on the unit itself if you want to issue an attack order, something that I have found to be irritatingly precise. Finally troops often hangup in 'traffic jams' in communication trenches when too many companies are moving, which requires either a long wait or for a unit to be ordered out of the trench. This can really screw you when attacking or defending both, because it makes it impossible to reinforce through existing trench lines,

Second, there's no campaign customization other then difficulty. This is a huge problem, because tech is accumulated much to slowly and national will depletes much too quickly. I've completed both a German and Allies campaign, and both were forced to a conclusion around 1916 from the 1914 start. In both cases I had maybe a quarter (probably less) of the tech tree unlocked. If you actually play until 1917, the second option for in-game start date, you will have more tech then can be accumulated playing the 1914 start to that point. This renders entire trees effectively useless. Intel can pretty much be completely be ignored, aviation and tanks both require such significant investment to be useful that you will not unlock both in one run, gas-mask and gas attacks are in separate tech trees and far enough apart that you cannot unlock both in one run, and I've found no purpose whatsoever in the entrenchment tree other then unlocking the specific techs I need for tanks. Level one trench plus infantry is basically all you're going to need, troops under attack will not live long enough to benefit from the defensive buffs from artillery because the enemy is just going to attack you in melee and negate them.

Both of my campaign victories were from depleting National Will, and I don't think its actually possible to claim the VP points without a min-max approach because of the massive shifts in national will that happens when you actually move the lines- your opponent is going to hit 0 national will long before you approach their capitol, often by attacking you and refusing to leave when a victory is available.

On more then one occasion I was forced out of my two control points, but held onto the command trench. I then mowed down thousands of enemy infantry until the score bar shifted back from 'loss' to 'stalemate' or in one case I actually gained a victory. The AI seems programmed to go for every single control point, regardless of what it will cost it which becomes easy to exploit.

There's a few other minor problems, AI seems to only sometimes suffer from fog of war for example, but generally I think all of these problems will be fixed eventually. I'd wait to catch this game on sale in the meantime.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really enjoyed this game. I know the graphics aren't the best but the game play is good. I didn't have an issue with the time clock on the battlefield, it forced me to be more aggressive to sweep the field.

I liked the resource limitation during the battlefield. It meant that I had to be more strategic in what I placed down at the start of the battle.

I have been straggling to find a good WW1 strategy game. Beating the allies by September 1915 was fun. I wish the campaign would continue a bit longer and forced you to take Paris to complete the victory. The French loss at Verdun was unable to be enjoyed as the area fell on the same month that the game ended. Using artillery/infantry attacks was pretty fun but never really gave me a chance to explore the other technologies that you could research. This is were I feel that an extended campaign, maybe not one based on the will of the enemy but their complete collapse would be better. Overall, I feel that they did good job and would recommend this game.

I am hoping that they are planning on doing an Eastern front, involving the Russian expanse, the Italian front and the Balkans. I feel that that would be quite a challenge. It would be great if your victory on the Western front (month and year of victory) and the choices that you made would reflect in the campaign.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDon't be fooled, this is not a WW2 map painter where you will be blitzing through Western Europe with combined arms. This is a war of attrition, where you will be sending wave after wave of troops to the meat grinder, so that you can slowly bleed the enemy's own will to fight.

The strategic map is the usual board-game style map with hexes & little unit tokens you get to deploys along the front.

The battles are a mix of Total War (or Ultimate general) with a hint of Tower defense & there is a strong focus on money & supplies, over manpower; expanding trenches, placing units, or calling for artillery barrages costs supplies & replenishing losses costs money. For an RTS, the AI does a fairly good job & is reasonably convincing in how it behaves in both offensive & defensive situations ; there are obviously still ways to cheese it a bit, but overall, it behaves consistently with how generals would have commanded at the time (i.e. recklessly sending waves of soldiers to a certain death just to secure few miles of trenches).

By nature of the conflict, there can be some repetitiveness & don't expect huge breakthroughs or victories but this all culminates into a rather faithful representation & abstraction of the conflict.

If you are after a WW1 RTS, which replicates the grind, attrition and futility of trench warfare, look no further, this is IT & does a pretty good job at it too.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSolid S:

+Stable, Optimized (no crashes no performance issues on a rig that has problems with newest games). Sound non cheating AI on normal difficulty (this alone is woth an S, and yes you can easily beat it when you get the grasp, but is human vs ai). Solid strategic(tbs) and real-time battle AI. Good setting (ww1), approached and depicted very well. Terrain gives good tactical options in rts. Star system is somewhat new and gives strategic options on strategic level (this part can be improved though, especially AI needs help on strategic level).

-Music is meh; AI can and should improve even though its one of the best. No real multiplayer lobby. Tech tree is probably not balanced well. UI needs minor improvements like better hotkeys and responsiveness.

Recommended: yes. if you like wargames/strategy games.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is an exceptional war game. Most people would be forgiven for assuming that The Great War: Western Front was destined to be either repetitive or disrespectfully historically inaccurate. To be historically accurate it would have to be repetitive, as this is the reputation of the actual First World War, but if such repetition were sacrificed to the Gods of hyperactive modern gaming audiences (like was done in Battlefield I), it would disrespect the actual sacrifices made by those who actually fought in the war. This game however keeps the strategic repetitiveness of World War I, while keeping the player on their toes during tactical battles. The AI is extremely good at exploiting weaknesses in trench-lines and formations and is competent at using technological development to overcome defences the player believed to be 'perfect'.

Perhaps the most amazing element of this game however is its capacity to make you feel guilty for managing an effective defence. Watching literally hundreds of enemy soldiers getting cut down by your machine-guns and mortars while your entrenched men fire volley after volley of rifle fire into their lines is bad enough, but then witnessing the handful of men that survived dragging themselves over the first line of barbed-wire as you drop hundreds of 155mm shells onto their position can be heartbreaking. It is a game that truly encapsulates the slaughter that embodied the Great War. It has so far given me almost 20 hours of engaging game-play and I have not finished the Triple Entente Campaign quite yet. I've still got the Triple Alliance Campaign to go, not to mention historical battles and scenarios.

The Usual Story: A 16 mile march done in record time, then the devs sat down for a rest 100 meters from the finish line. WTAF.
- Multiplayer does not include the Campaign. Utter lunacy. They've got multiplayer for individual battles, so the more complicated elements of the net-code are sorted. They've got a turn-based campaign with non-simultaneous moves, so the conversion of this system to multiplayer now the net-code and server client architecture is complete would be an issue of weeks. So where is the multiplayer campaign? It's like the devs ran up a sixteen mile hill and stopped 100 meters from the finish-line. This should be added.
- Political pressure to attack. It is possible to play a game of The Great War without ever attacking. I have lost thousands while the enemy has lost millions. This however would be viewed as cowardice by the governments of the day, especially the nobility. This should be added.
- Corpses, blood, and disease. The bodies of fallen soldiers disappear about 10 seconds after death. There is no blood. There are no swarms of flies. There is no disease in the trenches. I know leaving bodies on the screen would result in performance loss but give the players a slider in the options menu. Regardless, the devs should find a way of representing this. This should be added.
- Tanks. These are improperly represented in the game and remove the World War I feel when they arrive. Firstly, all attacks have them following their development, which can occur quite early, which is both detrimental to the game experience and horribly inaccurate. I've destroyed 33 German tanks so far in my game and its only just ticked over to 1918. That is quite impressive considering that the Germans only ever developed 18 tanks during the entire war. While the Allies did have substantially more, they had a limited impact on most battles, which is not the case in this game, with tanks often causing entire flanks to collapse simply because they can't be destroyed by anything other than elite grenadier infantry or protracted rifle fire. Secondly, the tanks are too fast and effective. They move like greyhounds across no-mans-land, seldom break down, seldom get stuck in the mud and the most glaring error of all - they can stop and easily get going again. Their engines were never that good. This should be changed to represent how it actually was.
- Weather. This is an unusual omission. The greatest enemy of the average solider on the Western Front was not actually the soldiers on the other side of no-man's-land. It was trench-attrition as the result of weather. The game has weather but no real effects are felt during the game. Trenches don't flood, soldier's don't freeze during winter, there's no starvation or trench-foot. This needs to be added.

FINAL: A truly great single player experience that is worth the money. However, the devs, while clearly talented, are strange people. They do all the hard-stuff to make the experience great, but then inexplicably leave the easy stuff so as to avoid their game being fantastic. Maybe the issues listed above will be fixed in a patch? Who knows. But, never-the-less, a great game. Not fantastic, even though it so easily could be, but great.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game takes those old legendary flash games to their logical conclusion and includes large scale theater level decision making on top. This game very effectively demonstrates the scale and destruction of the war to end all wars, as well as its glacial pace.

The game takes a light hand on narrative and allows the player to reach his own conclusions. The game doesn't take either the mocking, cartoonish tone, or the overwhelmingly dismal grimdark tone that other games do. the game doesn't slap you on the wrist for deploying poison gas or for sacrificing tens of thousands in failed attacks. those are decisions that the player can make, and the game gains a strong authenticity for that. The war will not go on forever and while you see the casualty numbers roll in, you can decide for yourself what actions are worthwhile to achieve victory.

There are some things I dislike:
The AI is pretty bad. On the tactical map it leaves glaring weakspots and doesn't defend its artillery properly. On the strategic map it rarely masses enough troops to launch really effective attacks, and when it does it never follows up. over the course of a campaign it is trivially easy to set up field hospitals and supply depots and let the enemy launch fruitless attacks that bleed their national will, autoresolving stalemates all the way.

In a related note, suppression is too powerful, which makes attacking easier than it should be. "suppressed" units cannot shoot, and so a whole defensive network can be cut through with relatively little difficulty.

Tanks can be obtained at a reasonable time, If you rush you can get them by mid 1916. this is good, but what's bad is that they're too good, and once you get them then the game is pretty much over. in my next game I probably won't even research them because they just trivialize the game.

TL;DR the game is very good, and gives the player a very authentic feeling while playing, and a very enjoyable experience overall, but it's pretty easy once you get the hang of it and some systems are just tuned in such a way that decision making ends up being both brain-dead and gamey. I'd like to see some balance and AI changes to fix this.
As it is, I recommend it if you're starved for a good WW1 RTS, which is a pretty rare commodity.\t\t\t\t\t
