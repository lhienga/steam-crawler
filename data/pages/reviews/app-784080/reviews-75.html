I am also not a longtime Battletech fan, so I'm coming at this more from the perspective of someone who just likes mech games.

Mechwarrior 5 has you as the leader of a mercenary company, traveling around the galaxy to take on jobs from a host of different factions. The game has a campaign and a "career mode". The campaign takes you through a set of specific missions on top of the travel and job-taking, Career Mode takes out the campaign and lets you just roam around. While aboard your ship you perform all the administration of running your mech company - repairing your machines, changing loadouts, travel, and choosing contracts/missions. Once you've picked what you want to do, you'll deploy with your "Lance", a squad of four mechs.

You'll then have to lead them through the mission, designating targets and pulling them around for different maneuvers, while fulfilling whatever the objective of your contract was. Objectives vary; you may end up destroying a base, chasing down a convoy, dueling another lance, really all the basic sort of things you could imagine doing if you had a bunch of mechs to screw around with. Contracts come with a brief negotiation phase, where you can through your reputation with a faction determine how much money you'll get paid, how much salvage you can claim after the mission is done, damage coverage, and/or air support.

In battle, mechs handle a lot like tanks. If you're on a controller, you use the left stick to increase/decrease throttle, moving always in the direction your legs are pointed. The right stick controls your torso, and different mechs have different aptitude for turning and looking around (there is one, for example, which can rotate 360 degrees while moving). Your weapons are assigned to weapon groups, and through a panel for that you can configure which keys fire what, as well as setting up "chainfire", which fires each weapon once in succession. There are a few types of weapons. Ballistic weapons consume ammo, generate little heat, and do all of their damage upon a successful hit. Energy weapons have infinite use, but generate much more heat, and for the most part they fire for a set period of time, meaning your damage potential can go down if you can't keep a lock. Missile launchers, depending on the type, may lock on or may be fired blind, and come in different-range variants.

At first it feels very slow - even with a very quick mech, it plods and stomps and takes forever to turn around. In comparison to other mech games, this is much closer to an Armored Core than a Gundam. You won't be flying around chopping up zakus, there's no Nineball defying the laws of physics. In Mechwarrior you're going to take a ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ of punishment and have to trust that your machine will hold together while you get ♥♥♥♥ done. You might come out of a mission missing both your arms and hobbling, and the more damaged you are the more expensive it's going to be to get fixed back up. This gets pretty detailed too in a way I appreciate - for example, ammunition is stored in the mech and you choose where it goes. If that section of your mech takes a gnarly hit, your ammunition can catch fire and explode, which could end you then and there.

Designing your mech's loadout is fairly simple and mostly intuitive - there's a mechlab screen where you choose where to slot different weapons and accessories. At first these options are very limited, but as the game progresses/time passes, you find more varied options of stuff you're familiar with and whole new things. There's more to it than the beginning of the game lets on.

The game has a timeline and sits within the Battletech universe's big timeline. I'm not very familiar with it, but in checking out different bits and pieces, if you're into lore and having a big context for what you're doing, seems like Battletech's got that for sure. For someone who isn't familiar (like me), it's pretty cool to stumble into such a detailed and meaty setup for a game. There is a *ton* of material if this is your thing.

One thing I did not like was the soundtrack. It's extremely generic, very "autozone commercial". I ended up turning it off until I found some replacements for it. It's not badly put together, it doesn't sound horrible for what it is. It's just not very interesting. That said, with the music turned off, you can hear more of the sounds of the game and that actually can be pretty cool. Definitely cool when all there is are environment sounds and the thud, thud, thud of your squad's footfalls.

The game had several pieces of DLC which I think were integrated in a pretty neat way. They come up as optional quests, big contracts that entail long periods of time in service to a faction/person/etc. You're given plenty of notice they are available and the game will remind you when the time comes to make a decision, which I appreciate. It is really enjoyable, once you get your footing and a nice roster of mechs, to just roam around finding what kind of ♥♥♥♥ you can get into. There's a decent variety of mission types and lots of little bonus things come up along the way - rumors of a long lost mech derelict somewhere, people contacting you because they knew your family, setting up deals and bigger mission chains. The generic missions sometimes exist as multi-mission operations, with big payouts and lots of opportunity for salvage/capture.

There is a form of balance to these missions, the tonnage limit. Your mechs have weight, and missions will have a specific weight total you need to either be at or below. if you exceed the tonnage limit you take penalties to your payout and salvage, increasing the more over the limit you get (you can, depending on what you're facing, choose to exceed the limit and just take the penalty). There's also a difficulty rating and reputation recommendation for different missions/areas, to help you gauge what you can and can't do.

As you go along, time passes (literally, days go by and eventually, months, then years). As the years roll on, you start to encounter new and different things, which I'm assuming lines up at least loosely with how things progress in the greater Battletech timeline. New mechs, weapons, and accessories will start appearing, you'll start salvaging new things, it's got a cool way of bringing those things into play without really announcing it to you. There is a news feed that gives you information about them, sometimes there are actual product announcements and such.

All in all, I've had a wonderful time with this game, especially the Career Mode. It's a ton of fun to jump into it, figure out along the way how different stuff works, and then start checking out Battletech stuff to see how it all fits together in the world. There's also a modding community, and because this ultimately draws from a tabletop game that means a *ton* of content and mechs derived from those materials. There are mods to make the mechlab more complex and closer to the tabletop game; others generate new mission types and expand the possibility space with respect to things like allied forces being sent to help, enemy merc bands staging an ambush, so on and so forth.

If you're playing on a steam deck, the game works great as it is. There are mods which massively help out performance without changing the visual fidelity, too. Mods are very easy to install, they mostly go into a single folder and you can enable them in a menu in-game. Very much recommended, especially if you're a fan of games that play around with war machines - if you're into tanks, I think you'll really like this. But too, if you're more of a general fan of mechs like me, this one provides a unique vision/approach and backs it up with a healthy bit of management/strategy on top.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is taking my free time away like it's a free-loading scumbag!

What to say about Mechwarrior 5? Ever wanted to manage your own mercenary company in outer space? This is your game. Ever wanted to pilot giant metal behemoths into battle and stomp your way to victory? This is your game. Ever wanted to feel the uncertainty of a man-inside-machine military fight against enemies uncertain, then change up your strategy from long range shelling to guns blazing at the turn of a moment? This is your game. Ever thought to yourself "What I need to win this is bigger, louder, angrier weapon!" and then wished you could materialize your idea in a workshop that customizes your bigger, louder, angrier death robot? This is your game.

I've played the Mechwarrior franchise since Mechwarrior 2 was released and I obtained a copy. Mechwarrior 5 is definitely a step in the right direction, but with knowledge of Mechwarrior and Battletech in mind, you should know that it's not perfect. It lacks enough customization to really make a 'Mech feel like it's your own creation, and instead makes you adapt to what a particular mech chasis is built around doing. In that sense, the game will make you strategize where you have been meeting defeat in order to overcome, so that can be said to be intentional: there is no one 'Mech that can do it all (unless you use mods, and even then it depends on a number of factors). Double-edged sword, perhaps, on behalf of the creators and programmers of Mechwarrior 5.

The "world" of Mechwarrior 5 is more of a map of choices, presented in the form of the Inner Sphere, humanity's expansion into the Milky Way after around 10 centuries of exploration thanks to Jump technology and HPG communications. On said Inner Sphere, shown to you as a map of stars where you choose your travel destination, you'll find mercenary contracts that you may choose to accept. There are several mission types as well as difficulties, from straight base defense and warzone attrition, to establishing a landing zone (Beachhead) for planetary invasions as well as new mission types provided you purchase the expansions, such as the new infiltration missions. Mission requirements will necessarily reflect on which Battlemechs and 'Mech loadouts you'd do well to choose, pertaining to the objective or objectives of a contracted mission. This not only affects your personal 'Mech that you will pilot into battle, but also those of your "Lancemates", fellow Mechwarriors that will accompany into battle in a group of four 'Mechs maximum per mission. These lancemates are AI-controlled, but if you have friends that own and play Mechwarrior 5, they can enjoy a game session with you piloting your 'Mechs in place of AI-controlled lancemates duringh a mission, which is a favorite among other Co-Op features of this game.

Returning to my prior mention of mods: you SHOULD mod this game! No two-ways about it, this game's modding community is doing a superb job of improving upon surrounding game mechanics and even in some of the main game mechanics, resulting in a completely enhanced experience in playing Mechwarrior 5. Personally, I can't play without TTRulez AI for lancemates as well as the VonBiomes packs of mission scenery and play areas. I also enjoy the addition of Clan weaponry ("the Clans" being a race of exiles that return to invade the Inner Sphere) options that can enhance my designs here and there, but I will always prefer Inner Sphere weapons in high tier categories for most of my Mechs. As such, returning to a "vanilla" experience is now game-breaking for me, and I now thus dread any time the developers of Mechwarrior 5 release an update, because it breaks the mods I have installed in my system.

Mechwarrior 5 is a fine addition to any game library and it has returned Battletech to the front of the fray of stompy robot carnage options in PC gaming. I honestly regret not playing it when it came out due to life commitments keeping me busy, because now I feel like I have to make up the year-or-so I did not spend playing this game with 6-hour-long sessions just to feel personally satisfied. My body needs more sleep as a result.\t\t\t\t\t
