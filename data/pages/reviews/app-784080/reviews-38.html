\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTl;dr, MechWarrior 5 is a enjoyable and likeable game, although it takes a bit of skill to master. While the writing and the visual effects are rather lackluster, they are carried by a deep and complex system of game mechanics that make you feel like a true MechWarrior. Overall, a fantastic addition to any hardcore mech fan's library, 8/10

Introduction
I was first introduced to the BattleTech universe by the 2018 strategy game of the same name. Before this point, I had heard of the board game, having been part of the tabletop gaming community for some years, but it was BattleTech (2018) that got me into the world of game. So when I found out that MechWarrior, another games series I had vaguely heard of in the past, was set in the same universe, I immediately put this game on my wishlist.

Gameplay
MechWarrior 5 is, first and foremost, a first person mech sim where you pilot one of the awe-inspiring BattleMechs that are the bread and butter of the game. While I won't go into the specifics of the gameplay, the movement, shooting, and damage mechanics of this game will take some getting used to for new players. It's not overly challenging to learn, but it does take a while to master the gameplay mechanics, and there is a fair amount of depth to the tactical aspects of the game.

Mission and level design are fairly decent, although I do have to criticise the frankly glutenous number of armoured vehicles, aircraft, and enemy mechs that you will be destroying in every mission. One mission in particular, one of the DLC missions where you are tasked with retrieving an enemy nuke before it can be used in a act of terror, is frankly absurd, with your lance of up to 400 tons of mechs are pitted against nearly ten thousand tons of enemy armour. This, combined with the tendency of the enemy AI to target whichever pilot is doing the most damage overall to them, means that you will be downed relatively quickly while your own lance spread their damage out among too many different foes.

Visuals & FX
In terms of graphics, this game is fairly decent for a 2019 video game, although a lot of textures can look pretty muddy. The environmental graphics in particular are pretty lackluster, with not a lot of particularly striking locations, and with rather poor damage modelling for structures and destructible objects. Particle effects are a mixed bag, although there aren't any bad examples to speak of. Lighting and fog effects are decent, if a bit dated. The best part of the effects though are the SFX, with a fantastic range of voice actors, great visual feedback during missions, and a diverse and rich soundscape.

Story
Unfortunately, MechWarrior 5 has one of the most lackluster stories I've seen in gaming, with great voice acting, but a poor overall structure. The campaign mostly acts as a tutorial for the gameplay, but is ultimately way too handholdy, and doesn't really have many standout moments. This extends to the expansions, which have some pretty nice setups, but ultimately fall flat. The fact that anyone remotely familiar with the BattleTech setting will see the twist midway through coming from a mile away is alone a reason to skip the story and jump into the career mode as soon as you're familiar with the mechanics of the game.

Conclusion
MechWarrior 5 is a game which I would recommend, but which isn't anything grand. It does its job well, and it has enough staying power to remain fun for dozens of hours. Ultimately, I feel this is a game that, while by no means a masterpiece, is a great game for anyone with a interest in bipedal war machines. 8/10, recommended.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tMy initial impressions of this game on it's release was 'Why is mech armor paper thin?' Combat was blistering fast, and I was losing arms or weapons less than 15s into engagements. I hated it. I wanted bit tanky stompy mechs, and I was getting early game Armored Core twitch shooting.

I hated it.

I revisited it with a friend recently, working some PS5/PC co-op and starting a new campaign. Playing the game in co-op mode made me realize some of the problems I had been running into. My mech was getting ripped to shreds because I was leading my AI lance into short range combat, but was not piloting mechs intended for that role. With a co-op partner, suddenly the attention was not immediately on me as the lance leader and I could also play more into the Centurion's role as a mid-range fighter using the LRMs and AC/10 where they were intended.

This bled over into my single player sessions as well. Better use of commands for ordering my lance into action, or switching to mechs that were better suited for brawler roles as the lead made all the difference. No longer was every mech covered in tissue paper. Battles could still be brutal on the repairs but I felt like I had a better handle on it. The little time I spent at base game launch hating the game has turned into really liking it with all the DLC.

I haven't looked seriously over all the patch notes since launch, so I don't know how much rebalancing was done, other than the rework of the Campaign story opening. The game is certainly better for the passage of time and support from the developer and leaves me with a positive look towards MW5: Clans in the future.\t\t\t\t\t
