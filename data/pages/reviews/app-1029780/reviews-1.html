The Good
It's similar to Banished, but a small clan of 5-15 people instead of a village of hundreds, and the simulations of weather, food spoilage, and combat are very detailed. One feature I really like is that each settler has a skill level in each of the 20 or so skills, but also they will be "passionate" about a few skills, and they may start out only being passionate about the skills they're bad at! So you find yourself investing a few hundred iron on the guy who is passionate about Smithing, but starts out at level 0, just letting him make a bunch of poor quality swords until he gets better, and he'll spend the whole time in a good mood! The little details like that create bits of almost parental decision-making that really endear you to your settlers. You get to know their names and religious practices, and you remember what they're good at and why.

The game has simulation rules for things most games ignore, like the rate at which food spoils at warm temperatures, the rate at which crops grow when not in full sunlight, or people getting poorer quality sleep when forced to share bedrooms. In the beginning, you can ignore these without consequences, so the game is accessible, but as you go on and try to optimize more, this creates emergent decision-making that is really immersive. You build your cellar to keep food cold in storage, and salt is valuable to pickle vegetables to preserve them. You avoid planting crops adjacent to buildings because they'll get less sunlight and grow slower. You give the nicest and most spacious bedrooms in your house to the settlers whose skills you most depend on to be sharp. It is very cool when you realize you've unintentionally reinvented elements of medieval architecture and society, purely in response to the conditions under which you are trying to survive.

The Bad
The settler AI can be a bit annoying. They seem to process their daily schedule in time slices, and they'll sometimes randomly quit doing a task halfway through because they've reassigned themselves to a different task. If they were doing a task that involved a long walk to get to (like hunting a deer out in the woods) then this makes them waste huge amounts of time on pointless walking.

The combat is a little bit clunky. The game doesn't give you good tools to orchestrate tactics in melee fights, so by far the most effective strategy is to ignore melee entirely and just have every single one of your settlers stand on the roof of a building and shoot arrows at the enemies, even the settlers that are skilled at melee and suck at archery. This is not as boring as it sounds, but it's not super interesting either. Sometimes, the enemies are attacking you because you took in a runaway slave of theirs, which can be cool and immersive, but most of the time they're just random bandits who want to destroy your house for no reason.\t\t\t\t\t
