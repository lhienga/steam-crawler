\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDespite playing for a couple hours and rather enjoying myself, I can't comfortably recommend this game. It's not bad by any means and if you like a relatively calm and slow game, this may be for you. This review is written by someone that played games like Farthest Frontier, Oxygen Not Included and many tower defense games. With an initial look, this game seems perfect for someone like me.

Four things, however, killed the game for me : lack of depht, odd progression, micromanagement hell and bugs. Oh yes, bugs. Now, the last one is to be expected from a game on Early Access, so I'd be fine with starting a new game and trying it all over again (which I did plenty of times in Oxygen Not Included). But the first three were quite impactful.

First, the game is quite simple. You collect some resources, put together a couple buildings and then repeat. And then if the gods are feeling particularly spiteful that day, you may get a looter attack. That's it. Coming from games in which you have to establish gathering and production chains, this feels quite different. Not bad, but it's something to be noted. What more could be added? More variety in attacking forces, more natural events, resource nodes on the map that encourage building further from the initial point, and so on.

Second, the game lacks meaningful progression. In games like these, I feel like the idea of "ages" is a good concept. Make researches that are more meaningful, and that unlock more side researches. Add interesting names to them, and make it so that they actually unlock something new, rather than just making it so that you can craft yourself something that was already available. In short, it feels like the progression of the game just isn't there. You start with few citizens, and that barely changes (and depending on your choices it doesn't, which can be a good thing if you want a small village). You start with pretty much no weapons, and after many researches you can put together the same kind of weapons you get from looters from early days. Even after hunting for years, the same villager misses 90% of their attacks, and so on. The game needs to have more flashy progression. And having to recover "lost knowledge" is awful. I get it, but it's an awful mechanic.

And lastly, micromanagemnt. I understand that making settlers "smart" is hard and subjective. But they feel immensively helpless. Some improvements would be : get the closest material, not ANY available material. Please. There are always going to be many different materials sources, since settlers seem to not care about carrying the materials they just harvested to a centralized warehouse. And also, make hunters smarter. I quit the game after having to every day manually select which animal to hunt. It's painful. Add a system in which they search for prey. It's the bare minimum.

To finalize, the game's idea is wonderful. I don't want to make it seem like this is a bad game. It's lovely and it's something I'll keep an eye on. And I had fun, quite a bit. If the game improves on those aspects, I'll come back, and even change the review. But for now, I don't recommend the game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFirst off, I'd like to mention that I've been with GM since near the beginning. I've seen it from the start, and I feel like it's been going in a direction I didn't expect nor buy the game in "early access" for. Second, this is NOT a castle-building game. This is a sim game and is only becoming more and more of one. There aren't any Castle type of structures. You can *build* a Castle, but it won't really look like one. None of that has changed since the beginning; although, they did add ladders somewhat recently, but the main focus has been on quality of life stuff, and more ways to micro-manage processes and your villagers.

I've waited for long stints hoping the broken stuff would be fixed after the wait, or new things would be added, but it never was. It's been several years, and yes, they've added new things, but the original stuff is still broken. Settler pathing, building, hauling, and anything really is so broken that you can't just get to a point and sit back to watch a masterpiece or job well done. It requires constant over-watch and management, and only more of that has been added with stuff like the "quality of life" update where you have to pay attention to how the settlers feel about each other, themselves, or their work/living space. It's becoming so tedious and micro-managing that it's a chore. It takes so long to get anything substantial done because everything you do is a grind that takes even longer than it should because the AI is so broken.

Some problems with this non-strategy game is that the AI is still as broken as ever. They have to work so much on it, that you won't get a lot of new stuff except for every now and then. The AI will build themselves in when building layers of wall so badly that you have to be strategic with how you build whenever you want something fairly thick or more than one story off the ground. I've had settlers get stuck in the corner because they built the walls around it, and they can't travel diagonally like that; thus, you have to constantly oversee and micromanage, adding only a few blueprints at a time. Yeah, you can build some "pretty cool looking-stuff," but your options are disparagingly small. There is also a limit to how high you can go, so if you were wanting to replicate some tall buildings, you either can't, or you'll have to not build it to scale.

If you're into sims, you might like this, but like I said, the AI is broken. Furthermore, much of the player-base has gotten sentimental with the animals and everything else, so they want to have item after item for them, or they want to add more ways to micromanage. I really don't know what the devs want in the end, but it does seem like they're going for a full on sim. Either way, it will take a very long time for them to even get to the end of their "road-map" of things that they plan to add as main parts of the game.

The combat is another somewhat broken thing. I've heard people complain about settlers not moving when commanded, and that can get them killed. The enemy isn't overpowered at all, but they have "moving trebuchets", that wreck your hard work very easily, and they have no counter. Some of the player base don't want the player to have stuff either because then it would be even easier to cheese combat than it is now, but combat currently is just this really tedious thing where archers are supreme. By the way Foxy Voxel, trebuchets Don't move! Those are catapults. Thank you!

Bottom-line. If you want a castle-building game that isn't a sim, then don't buy the game. If you like sims, you may like this, but it's currently broken, so I'd recommend waiting or getting it on sale. I kind of wish I could recommend the game because the devs do seem to be working hard on it, but I can't because it's broken, you'll get bored fast, and it's not my cup of tea. I actually regret getting it because it wasn't actually fun.\t\t\t\t\t

Preface
Two house keeping items to take care of before getting into the review. First, the "early access" tag is not a valid excuse for the plethora of issues still plaguing the game. The point of the tag is to allow devs with a functional prototype (i.e. a working product in alpha or beta) to generate additional revenue to finish the initial full release of a game. See Baldur's Gate III for a good example of a valid "early access" title.

Second, the roadmap is not a valid excuse for lack of core mechanics. It's rather unfortunate and frustrating the trend developers seem to follow by including their core mechanics in a "roadmap" as a method for enticing players to stick around after making an initial purchase. A roadmap is intended to - as the name suggests - map out planned features that are not critical to the base value and functionality of the game.

The review will be in bullet format to properly express my thoughts without unnecessary fluff text. Keep in mind that as updates are released, aspects of this review may become obsolete and should only be referenced for the current major game version.

This review may be updated following major releases, but no guarantees are made

Missing Core Mechanics
This section is intended to outline base mechanics which are vital for gameplay in a colony sim that heavily utilizes AI storytelling.
  • Flammability (hazards in general)
  • POIs (i.e. tombs, broken towers, abandoned homes, etc.)
  • Workshop support
  • Combat counterplay (trebuchets still don't have a counter, and archers are still overpowered in most scenarios)
  • Colonist relationships
  • World Traversal
  • Imprisonment (necessary for accumulating extra pawns outside of random chance)
  • Debug mode (critical for fixing game breaking bugs, such as pawns disappearing)
  • Map configuration
  • Fluids (think of lakes as an example)


Current Issues
Going Medieval still hosts several core issues that make either make the game feel like a grind or infuriating to deal with.
  • Job/Priorities - Choosing to mine a tile further away than the one next to them. Hunting is even worse with pawns constantly needing to reacquire their target.
  • Combat - Pawns have ~0.5s delay between executing commands
  • Temperature - Multi-story buildings turn into saunas during the summer (noted by devs)
  • Pathing/Spawning - The game regularly fails to check whether there are valid paths from the edge of the map to the settlement before spawning events
  • Pathing/Jobs - Pawns will still routinely get stuck in holes if given a blanket mine command
  • Building/Priority - Pawns will build multi-story buildings in a seemingly random order rather than layer by layer despite sufficient stability (results in longer than average build times)
  • Building/Construction - Cannon replace already constructed components, the player must first deconstruct and then construct the new/upgraded component
  • Attributes Tab - Messy UI design that conveys critical information about pawns into a tiny window in one blob
  • Settings/Configuration - Lack of control over storytelling aspects alongside missing keybind options

Positives
Despite the overall critical review, Going Medieval is groundbreaking in other aspects.

  • Building System is implemented well and functions without any issues or frustration
  • Ability to change the aesthetic of already placed components
  • Seamless connections between build pieces
  • 16 layer build height with half steps to provide easier navigation between layers
  • 6 levels of job priorities that allow for precise pawn management
  • Complex attributes system - similar to Rimworld - that introduces a level of complexity to gameplay which feels natural
  • Control over pawns' attire, meals, and drug usage policies

Conclusion
Going Medieval started off as a clone of RimWorld with the z-levels mod, but lacked most of the features RimWorld has. However, it's both unfair and dishonest to make the claim that Going Medieval is a RimWorld clone as the game has progressed to a point where the two are artistically and functionally different. Some UI elements still exist that are direct rips from RimWorld (namely the job priorities tab), but core features seem to implement entirely unique approaches that are non-existent in base RimWorld.

Despite everything that has been implemented so far, Going Medieval is vastly overpriced for the amount of gameplay it offers with lack of any kind of modding support. After 2 or 3 playthroughs, most scenarios will start to feel repetitive as you repeat much of the same gameplay elements with almost no variation whatsoever. The harsh reality is that the publisher is selling a $15 USD game for $25 USD while hiding behind the "early access" title to shield themselves from proper criticisms.

The only time I can recommend buying this game is if it goes on sale for 50% off, at least until the current roadmap is filled out.\t\t\t\t\t
