Short Answer:
A two-player escape room puzzle game that was way more fun than my friend and I expected it to be. Aside from one puzzle that I don't feel was a good fit for this kind of game, the average quality of the rest of the puzzles was solid, offering just enough variety and complexity to keep things interesting until the very end. Around a 3-hour runtime, and well worth its low price tag for anyone looking for something new to play with their friend or partner.

Long Answer:
Unboxing the Cryptic Killer is an escape room puzzle game that follows detectives Ally and Old Dog as they attempt to solve the decade-old cold case of the Cryptic Killer. When an anonymous tip leads them into what appears to be one of the Cryptic Killer's traps, the two detectives are locked in adjacent puzzle rooms and need to work together in order to make it out alive. Before picking this game up, there are a couple things you should know:
  • It's a game for exactly two players‚Äîno more, no less‚Äîand both players must own the game to play.

  • There's no ability to search for active games with randoms online, you need to directly invite/join someone via an invite code.

  • There's no in-game voice/text chat function, so you'll need a third-party service like Discord to talk with your co-op partner.
And when I say you "need" a chat service to communicate, I mean it. No puzzle can be fully solved on your own because each player only holds half of every puzzle, so good communication is necessary in order to win. You're given the option to play as either Ally and Old Dog; and your choice determines which of the two rooms‚Äîand which half of the puzzle solutions‚Äîyou'll be able to see. Neither side is better or worse, so don't put too much thought into who you pick.

The rest of this review contains spoilers for some of the puzzle design you can expect from the game, so proceed with caution.

Each of the five levels contains a box with, among other things, a four-digit combination lock; and the code can be found piecemeal by solving the other puzzles found inside the box. There's a lot of variety to the mechanics and presentation of the puzzles, but almost all of them boil down to you uncovering a number you'll need for the main lock. The actual process of getting these numbers is what matters, though, because you'll find everything from logic puzzles to word puzzles and even other mini-sized puzzle games standing between you and your escape. Some puzzles will require information that isn't universally known (e.g. morse code), but thankfully it provides some quick reference charts for these kinds of puzzles so you can generally get by with no external assistance. They also took some simple but crucial steps to ensure accessibility for players that are colorblind or deaf/hard of hearing: the handful of color-based puzzles include separate visual indicators for each colored component, and the single audio-based puzzle also includes a small visual aid so it can be done without needing to actually hear it.

As for the game's difficulty, I guess it depends. Some players found it too easy, but I thought it offered a good, balanced difficulty overall. Again, what will make or break your playthrough is how well you and your co-op partner can communicate, namely when it comes to explaining the details of things you see on your respective screens. Sure, you could just use Discord's screen share feature and make things way easier for yourselves, but where's the fun in that? I shouldn't judge, though, because even though we did our best to avoid it, my friend and I ended up screen cheating on two separate occasions. The first one was just because we were being kinda dumb, but the other was due to a puzzle that I don't feel makes a good fit for the escape room format:
  1. A connect-the-dots puzzle in Level 1. I could argue that the solution from my (Old Dog) perspective wasn't as clear as it could've been; but I blame this one more on us not yet understanding the "language" of the puzzle design yet, because things went a lot more smoothly once we got past that point.

  2. A nonogram (also called picross) puzzle in Level 3. Personally, this is my absolute favorite type of logic puzzle ever, but my friend had never done one before and struggled to the point that I had to step in and help him solve it. Ignoring the lack of support features a dedicated picross game provides (like being able to "X" out squares or cross off numbers you've completed), the puzzle provided is in a 5x15 grid‚Äîfor reference, just about any picross game eases you into things with 5x5 grids. It's the only puzzle in the game that goes beyond providing a reference chart and just straight-up hands you a page of written instructions on how nonograms work. As much as I adore them on their own, I feel like if a puzzle requires that much on-the-spot explanation for the layperson to figure it out, it might be better to use a different type of puzzle entirely.
This one is more of a nitpick than anything, but I'm not the biggest fan of the achievements list. Unlocking them doesn't require require much effort, but most of them are locked to one character, meaning you'll need to play the game at least twice‚Äîonce as each character‚Äîin order to get 100% completion. The issue is that there's no good incentive to replay the game for missing achievements because the puzzle solutions don't change between runs. Sure, it's kinda neat going back to see what your co-op partner saw, but the novelty of that wears off pretty quick. On the flip side, it also means that more dedicated completionists can just pull up a guide and blitz through a final achievement hunt in a matter of minutes, which is what my friend and I did once we beat the game.

We finished our main playthrough in a little over 3 hours, and it was honestly a lot more fun than we anticipated. Sure, it wasn't perfect; but if the only problems big enough to point out are one or two puzzles and some slightly out-of-the-way achievements, I'd say it did a damn good job. Unboxing the Cryptic Killer was a surprisingly fun co-op experience, and given its low price, it's hard for me not to recommend it.
