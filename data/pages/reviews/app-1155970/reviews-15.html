\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSuper fun illustrated text based rpg. Exploring the peninsula feels exciting and dangerous, and dealing with the various factions WHILE uncovering the mysteries of the peninsula and its people is a blast. I loved all the interesting characters so much. Each person feels complex and historied. I think Dalit at the Pelt is my favorite cause she's badass AND funny. Its not an easy game, but also it doesn't punish too severely for your mistakes. As an out an proud save-scummer if the story isn't going my way, I usually fix it, but the "mistakes" I made felt so real and interesting that I never did. I'll probably go through a second time to see what I missed but yeah, the game is a blast and I honestly can't wait for a sequel or a even another game in the same setting (with the same mechanics). Overall 10/10, definitely play through this if you like TTRPGs or story-based adventures.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDisclaimer: This is my first time playing an RPG like Roadwarden, so if I'm saying the obvious about this genre then I apologize--this is just my perspective.

Roadwarden is, put very simply, a really wordy choose-your-own-adventure novel packaged as a video game. Which means it's not for everyone, especially if you hate reading; this can be seen in the achievement stats, which shows that about 70% of everyone who bought the game actually opened and played the game a bit, but less than half of those players progressed much after that. The game describes its characters and locations with very detailed (but not overly flowery) prose, and I can see how these walls of text can be off-putting to a first-time player.

That being said, anyone who can get past this hurdle is in for a very fun game. While the story is technically linear, in the sense that the locations and characters you encounter remain the same in every playthrough, the game offers you so many choices that you never feel encumbered by the plot. Do you spy for a controlling mayor, telling her your friends' secrets behind their backs? Do you save a town from a deadly plague, or ignore it to gain another group's favor? What secrets will you pursue, and which will you leave buried? It's all up to you.

The health/armor/cleanliness/hunger stat system the game had also feels very clever. You don't die if your health hits zero (unless you are in specific combat encounters), but your sick-looking mug will make people less likely to trust you. You can skip staying at an inn and sleep in the wild to save money, but this will also make people less likely to trust you, because you'll look filthy. And if you can't gain people's trust, how can you achieve your goals? Juggling money, health, cleanliness etc. is both challenging and satisfying...but never frustrating, since you can easily savescum in the game and the game autosaves before every major combat encounter. You'd think that makes the game less fun, but it doesn't.

The combination of choices and challenge make the game very replayable. It's fun to find new options and story beats, or discover things that were overlooked in previous playthroughs.

Some people say a full or initial playthrough of Roadwarden takes about 11+ hours, but I think that's only if you really take the time to read everything in full. Since I partly skimmed some of the text my initial playthrough only took about 4-5 hours? It depends on how fast you read, really.

Anyway, Roadwarden is a gem. The walls of text might seem off-putting at first, and if you're the kind of person who prefers direct action over text heavy, choices-matter roleplay stories, this might not be for you. But if you enjoy story-heavy games then please--give Roadwarden a shot. It's lovely.\t\t\t\t\t
