\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRoadwarden is quite a wonderful game, a lot of the other positive reviews cover exactly what makes it great (to which I agree) so I won’t go into too much detail on that, I want to write more on the topic centring around the difficulty levels you're able to select before beginning considering it has a significant impact on how a play-through may unfold.

Firstly, I will just quickly cover the game overall by saying the story, characters, and writing in general are all remarkable from top to bottom. The writers did a great job at conveying the story in a way that really captures your imagination immersing you into the surrounding world. I can’t fault the game in those aspects.

But, onto reviewing my experience regarding the difficulty setting for anyone diving into this game for the first time like I just did. Casual setting has no time limit, standard gives you 40 in-game days to finish, and the hardest one gives you 30 days. If I could go back knowing what I know now, I might've settled on casual. I know it’s easier, but I’m pretty thorough when it comes to playing games in trying to complete all quests, explore the world, and cover all bases speaking to all characters to uncover any new information, and I didn’t feel like standard catered to that style of play-through.

I did read beforehand that the 40 day limit on standard was generous enough, but to me, it didn’t feel entirely accurate as in the end I didn’t have the luxury to take liberties on my travels – I had to be quite strategic throughout. Completing tasks and quests as a whole continually opened up new dialogue trees with various characters throughout the world and it’s extremely difficult trying to discover and cover them all with such a time limit.

I didn’t dilly-dally, I did think about where I had to move to next, and even when I approached 20-25 or so days remaining I occasionally started quick-saving before I travelled anywhere to trial run 2 or 3 different locations I needed to head to for the best outcome going forward. Even after this I only just managed to complete some important questlines at the death. After the 40 day limit came to an end, I still had some loose ends and it resulted in a small unfulfilling feeling that I hadn’t done as much as I’d have liked to.

But alas, it was probably all by design. Maybe the player isn't meant to be able to solve every problem and complete all the quests, and I was just too feeble-minded to carry on playing in an organic manner by not save-scumming to deliberately pick and choose between what lay ahead of me. But when I think about it, anyone playing on hard difficulty with the 30 day limit is going to have to make some very difficult choices in prioritising story and character related outcomes. So all in all, I likely ruined it for myself in a way as the inclusion of a time limit does lend itself to the fact the developers most likely aimed at trying to establish an uncompromising setting.

Overall, I highly recommend this game, I haven’t actually played anything in this type of style since a kid in the 90's (playing Where in the World Is Carmen Sandiego? and Wanted: Ned Kelly, for example), so I’m thankful I could relive those memories through Roadwarden as it's a very rich, rewarding adventure in the same way. It's very rare I return to games due to having such a large backlog of games I want to complete, but I would definitely revisit this to explore the different paths available in the story and play it more in the way the developers had intended (I regret the save-scumming). I just wanted to give my 2p in regards to the experience I had playing on standard difficulty in the hope it may be food for thought for those just starting out.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFine game, a bit too long for my taste. It was captivating at first, but the metaphors and descriptions become repetitive later on and often do not aid the narration of the story or the explication of the context (e.g. that of the villages) that largely require the players to visualize; however, they are consistent with the vibe of the game. Perhaps Scholar was a bad pick for someone who plays through the game for the first time, but I was not tempted to play through again to explore alternative endings - I am not sure if the game can be changed in any way to encourage that, for this might be the nature of a text-based story. If I am interested in the alternatives, I might as well just google it up online and read it.

As a game it is fine, since it really brought me back to being a child who's enthusiastic to imagine an adventure totally out of this world but, at the same time, also has threads attached to fantasies only born out of "a city within the walls". Maybe I would have had fun being an adventurer in the old time, enjoying exotic stories at a remote inn where travelers from all parts of the world gathered. Not sure if such remote inns still exist today, for they would have been in the major international hubs and are irrelevant to the review of this game anyways, lol. The game fails to continue capturing my focus after a few hours because of the grindy tasks and the lack of creative languages.

As a fiction, tho, I do not really find high literary values in its rhetoric. I probably would never have picked it up from a bookstore, but it is not a book, but a game, and a game that I spent a solid 6 hours on enjoying exploring the unknown. For 7 bucks? That's not too bad at all.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWithout a doubt, it's an insanely immersive adventure, and all the little graphical elements almost make you forget that this is a text game - with events vividly appearing in your mind. I can still see my character standing there, shirtless, boots half immersed in the swamp waters, axe in hand, facing some kind of mountain lion; bleeding, dirty, but determined.

And yet, Roadwarden suffers from a pretty major issue which holds it back greatly: there's a time limit, and it's rather tight. I hold this somewhat unpopular opinion that time limits aren't bad per se - they can add a lot of tension and additional strategic layer in which you need to consider your options very carefully; but they need to be well communicated and reasonable. Here, the limit is well communicated, but I don't think it's reasonable at all.

To keep it short: I played the game with the foreknowledge that the time limit is tight, tried my best to minimise pointless travel, always acted with intent, was mindful of my resources and chose my goals carefully. And even with all that the ending I got wasn't one I'd call good; I simply didn't have the time, or even visible means, to complete a pretty crucial quest.

Now, you could say, what's the problem? Just replay the game, go straight for the important goals, choose your options better, etc. And to that I say: no. And that's for a very simple reason.

There is only one playthrough: the first one. (the game allows you to replay it, that's not what I meant)

Think about it. For all the clamouring about replayability, whenever people talk about their most potent experiences with a game, they usually say "when I first saw X..." or "it blew my mind when I realised that Y". It's simply not the same when you're doing it again, it can even feel like you're being dishonest, since you - the player - have knowledge that the player character doesn't. This argument might feel nonsensical in the age of wikis, roguelikes and social media, but there still are many, many games that are straightforward stories - and Roadwarden is one of them.

And what a story it is. It really manages to suck you in; your dialogue choices matter, your decisions affect the world around you. Your resources are limited, and so is your power. You need to maintain your health and hunger, but also the state of your armour and even your hygiene. Wash yourself in the river, maybe brush your teeth if you bought a teeth cleaning set, or perhaps pay for a bath in the village? But it's so expensive. Anyway, you need to wash off the blood on your face, and your clothes are torn from getting through the thicket, better visit the tailor.

The worldbuilding manages to avoid being your generic fantasy with some cool twists, such as the fact that human bodies just kind of rise on their own after death, as if everybody had a dormant zombie virus from birth. Horses are a rarity, and your equine companion is often seen as a curiosity by almost everybody; the previous roadwarden used a big lizard as a mount instead. Also, you often get asked about otherwise unseen parts of the world, and whatever you answer will be treated as true for the rest of the playthrough, that's pretty cool.

But what's really golden about this whole game is that it really feels like an adventure through an unknown territory. The closest thing I'd compare it to is a pen-and-paper RPG session, where you truly feel like anything can happen, and everything is so viscerally real. The quality of the writing is excellent, too; it has this rare quality that comes from being written by a non-native English speaker who managed to attain the mastery of the language the hard way (the writer is Polish).

There's a lot more to say about Roadwarden, but I think I've said enough. The time limit is a grievous wound on an otherwise impeccable experience; luckily, you can select easy mode which disables it - and I suggest you do just that, pride be damned. Or simply be prepared to play more than once. Either way; if you don't mind reading in games, I can easily say that Roadwarden is very much worth playing.
