\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is just a magnificent piece of writing, and captures a sense of adventure and discovery I haven't felt since I was a young child reading every fantasy book I could get my grubby little hands on at the library, while carrying an unforgiving melancholy that resonates deeply with me as an adult. The picture Roadwarden paints is a harsh one, full of danger and catastrophic mistakes with steep consequences. The people of it's world are a wary, hardscrabble lot with well-deserved suspicion of some foreign agent coming in asking questions and doing a duty no-one really asked for, and the lands you find yourself exploring will gladly kill you and anyone else foolish enough to venture in unprepared, but if you stick it out and learn to survive it's dangers, and give people reasons to trust you, you will find an incredibly vibrant and believable world. The characters are complex and feel very human, and each settlement has a distinct culture, with it's own history, values, dialect, and goals. There are countless different leads on countless different problems to pursue, and most quests can be resolved in multiple ways, with multiple results, and while some people might find the 40 day time limit on standard difficulty polarizing, I think it really enhanced the experience. There simply isn't enough time to figure everything out and resolve every problem, and while this can lead to some very harsh outcomes, it makes the things you *did* take the time to dig into feel that much more significant and real. (Just like real-world roadwork, the final boss is ultimately the deadline.)

Across the board, this game is very tightly woven. Nothing feels like an afterthought, and progressing in one area of the story frequently and organically leads to new options and revelations about other parts of the story. Each settlement's history and goals are tied up in everyone else's, and even seemingly minor characters often wind up being tied into a whole bunch of other story threads. The worldbuilding is also very deliberate, and very well-researched, taking heavy inspiration from medieval Europe in a way that manages to feel fresh and grounded rather than cliche, with a really wonderful amount of attention paid to everyday life in the middle ages, whilst putting a fresh spin on classic fantasy monsters and carefully considering how their existence would realistically impact daily life. It also provides the opportunity for you to define the history and culture of the region your character comes from in a very organic way when characters in the game ask what your home is like, which I think is a brilliant choice for a game where the player character comes from a set location and exists in a fixed profession/role.

10/10, would improve the lives of those I touch in small ways and acquire a found family to the detriment of the world because I am a mere mortal with finite time and non-negotiable obligations that prevent me from saving the world from massive, complicated, systemic problems again.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tReally well-done text-based RPG. Both the story and the world it takes place in are engaging and take time to unravel, giving a sense of satisfaction and progression as details are uncovered.

One gripe I have with many recent games is that they are too easy; that is not the case for Roadwarden. Obtaining currency (dragon bones) and goodies was actually a challenge and so provided adequate incentive to complete quests and explore. I was barely able to complete my chosen personal goal of collecting 100 bones in the 40-day time span, achieving this with literally a single day to spare (there is also a 30-day option and a limitless time option!)

This game also successfully executes the "moral grey area" trope; sure, there are some situations in which there is clearly a "good" and a "bad" choice, but others left me genuinely lost in thought as to which path was the most morally justifiable. I think that even pulling this off a single time is an impressive feat, but it happens over and over here.

There is a LOT of reading (no voice-acting), so if that's not for you, maybe look elsewhere. That being said, I'm not a big reader and had no trouble staying engaged and processing everything, so don't let that scare you off, either. All in all, if you enjoy story-driven experiences, this is a must-try (even at full price!)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRoadwarden offers a world that is at once charming and merciless. Most of your time is spent as a political actor of sorts, trying to win the favor (or begrudging acceptance) of various villages and factions throughout the peninsula, which ends up being an excellent way to combine narrative and gameplay-centric sources of tension for the player. Instead of feeling like a book that was jammed into an .exe file to boost sales, as so many narrative games do, the decision-making and resource management feels very natural, like an homage to TTRPGs on the narrative side, or to old-school text adventures on the challenge side. Even if this game were more of an executable book than a game, the writing is strong enough that it still would have stood out. The world feels truly alive, and the various competing interests and hidden motives of each actor make for an intriguing experience in its own right.

If you like narrative-heavy games, and want something that invokes the feeling of curling up with a cozy fantasy novel, this game was made specifically for you.\t\t\t\t\t

Perfect RPG for a tired soul like mine.
Every little increment of progress was perfectly in my awareness, never got lost among my newly aquired information nor the fresh items, yet there were tons of them. Just wonderful.
