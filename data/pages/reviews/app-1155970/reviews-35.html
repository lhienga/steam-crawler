\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAny game that does a series of vignettes at the end describing how my choices affected the narrative is going to be good for me, especially if it is not nonsense (i.e. a direct results of my choices), and *especially* if many of those choices had to be earned. I didn't do everything that I wanted to in my playthrough (that's another positive from a compelling choices perspective), but I felt very satisfied with how the game supported the type of roadwarden that I wanted to be, including feeling that the narrative supported even unorthodox and/or seemingly inconsistent/contradictory choices.

The writing is pretty damn good. Not approaching say, Planescape Torment/Disco Elysium levels, but well above the high fantasy schlock I was expecting. This is a low story told in a high fantasy world. Sometimes hits the same tone as Citizen Sleeper and definitely has that subtle edge of being at the base of Maslow's hierarchy, scraping by for food and shelter.

The gameplay is all text and there's a lot of writing and there is a decent amount of roleplay type text selection (at least, I'm fairly certain it is), but it's compelling. The graphics are barely discernable pixel art (better than I could do, but frustrating early in the game when I was hungry for any scraps of understanding of what the world was like), but in combination with the excellent acoustic guitar soundtrack, it creates an incredibly evocative sense of place.

It is also great and letting you solve things for yourself. You will occasionally have to type in answers to NPC questions (a stranger might ask "What is my name?" and depending on your journey, you may already know it) or to ask for specifics (in response to "What do you want to know about the peninsula?" or in response to what part of the ruin you want to look at). It's almost always satisfying and rewarding and I don't usually find those mechanics compelling.

Overall, a quick (playthrough took about 8 hours), atmospheric, pretty well-written, and compelling game. Play it if you like good narratives, evocative soundtracks, the melancholy of living in an inhospitable frontier, and the thrill of exploration and investigation. Or if you really like warding roads.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOne of the best story driven games I have played in a while.

The story is not forced upon you, the player must venture forward and discover it for themselves.

The player is provided no map, locations are only revealed when you see them with your own eyes for the first time. Some locations and settlements can be missed entirely during a playthrough. Some places will be often referenced but shrouded in mystery. Until you see a settlement yourself sometimes you only have rumor and speculation.

The characters in this world are unique and have their own motivations or bitter memories. Information is not given freely as an entire group can live and die in isolation.


This is not a game that can be completed in a single playthrough.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm a frequent tabletop game player and enjoy singleplayer games. If not for both of those things, I'm not sure I would like Roadwarden nearly as much. It's very much what I've been looking for in a roleplaying game. Very old school in many respects, but with a simplified system for determining success in uncertain matters where you aren't looking at raw numbers so much as paying attention to how well-prepared you are for the current encounter you're in.

It's a game which rewards you for paying attention to past experiences and finding out as much as you can from other characters, because that directly translates into how well you're going to do in combat, how much money you can get from trading, and how little time it will take you to accomplish all the quests. I wasn't overstressed by the 40 day time limit and was able to play a little loose with it, but it does feel good when you can plan out routes which let you hit multiple stops and make you feel appropriately foolish for going an inefficient route. And to be perfectly clear on this point: the game really is good about getting you invested in the people and places and the care you pay to learning about them. The way you interact with them could earn you a trading partner or get you killed, which is exactly what I want out of an RPG. I didn't play WoW for the first time until about a month ago and found it to be really unsatisfying because it's extremely linear and gives you money and experience constantly for doing nothing. By comparison, every coin you get in Roadwarden makes you feel proud for earning it and improving other people's lives or ashamed for taking a quick route to wealth which directly impacts the lives and well-being of the other characters.

This is a game with serious choice. You aren't defaulted to a hero. From the very start you can choose between being in it for money, a faction outside the area, a faction within the area, wholly professional and impartial in attitude, or doing it for the sake of everyone involved. Because you can play it the way you want, playing the good route actually makes you feel good. This is vastly preferable for an rpg not only because it supports many playstyles, but also because not making good the default lets the players who do want to be good get that feeling that their decisions and sacrifices actually made a difference. It's not nearly as satisfying if everything you do will have an inevitable outcome. That alone makes this game leaps and bounds ahead of ME3.

I could talk for hours about the in-depth lore of the game world or the simple streamlined character creation process which is subtly woven into the introduction, but I'll spare the details. Simply take it from this review that Roadwarden is peak fantasy roleplaying unlike any other. If you are into or can get past the nature of the game being text-based with simple visuals, you'll find it to be fun, difficult, and bursting with replay value. I've only just played through it one time in a single 13ish hour sitting and I don't doubt I'll be replaying this one a dozen more times.

10/10\t\t\t\t\t
