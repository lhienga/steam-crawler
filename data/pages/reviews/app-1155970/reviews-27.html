\r\n\t\t\t\t\tUnbelievably good.

Go in blind, but if you want some specific guidance on what this game is about, I guess I have a review.

Roadwarden is the closest thing I can think of to original Fallout, done as a text adventure, and you're a postman is sword and sorcery Florida.

You're in charge of scouting a remote region, making contact with local settlements, and hopefully making everyone receptive to trade convoys from your masters, the merchants in the south. The scope of the game never really shifts beyond this, but in the minutia the game gets... complicated.

Roadwarden is a game that can force you into serious existentialist/transcendentalist contemplation over a single paragraph about a squirrel. Every character is fleshed out, complex, and you only see glimpses of their true self. Everyone's driven by their own motivations. Everyone's trying to survive. And some manage to be kind while others are purely mercenary.

This is a game that gives you a *lot* of freedom in how you approach each situation, and it's a game that *does not* judge you for your choices. Some crpgs have sort of a canon code of ethics that always gets you rewarded. Roadwarden isn't like that.

I don't want to drift into spoiler territory, but there were a couple times when the game felt arbitrary, and they were both random combats in an optional zone. I got oneshot from full health/armor/food by choosing to block low instead of blocking high against a wolf, and the same thing when I tried to back away from a large cat. These were noteworthy because they felt like the sort of goofy roulette logic I'd normally expect from this type of game, and nowhere else in Roadwarden is like that.

Again, play this one blind. Don't look at a walkthrough unless you're genuinely stuck.

This game is going to linger with me for years. It's well worth your time.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI personally find the game's writing overly wordy. It takes 3 paragraphs to say something that could be done with just 2 sentences. Also, there is no system for keep track of key words or information - for example, a 10 page conversation (not exaggerating) teaches you where different locales are, what threats are where, and what may be worth investigating. But it's SO much information. It would be nice to have 'auto-notes.'

I realize the 'old school way' was to take your own notes. But old school video games also rarely had 10 pages of monologue.

So I would have appreciated "auto-notes either a cardinal direction system (e.g., "South - ____ town, zombies and werewolves rumored present, also last known location of Mr. _____ ) or an town/village/area based system (e.g., "Peaceful River" - runs North/South and empties into ____ , the largest city ____ is on it . Rumors say ___ and ___ can be seen at night).

Note: these examples are entirely made up, but I wanted to give a specific example of what would help make the game more engaging and fun for me. Yes, there's a glossary, but it doesn't really capture what you learn in conversation.\t\t\t\t\t
