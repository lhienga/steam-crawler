\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game has amazing writing but falls flat when it comes to freedom to play the game how you want to play it; It's a fairly linear map progression and the order in which you encounter People/Beasts seems to be predetermined ruining the sense of an open world. In my almost 2 hours playing I created 3 characters both getting them to the 2nd crossroads hoping that there was some sort of randomness or sense of variety in the game, alas it was the same 3 runs with the very similar 3 dialogs with everyone you encounter and finding the same items in the same places. It seems the only "random" aspect of the game are the small moments where there are dice throws to determine outcome but even those are preset to be in certain areas and at certain times.

I really came into this game wanting to love it and I did enjoy the writing but if you're looking for an open ended adventure with a sense of replay-ability and open-endedness this isn't the game for you. I'll be waiting for Adventure mode in Dwarf Fortress to come out and have a truly open world adventure, until then I'm getting a refund as I don't find this game's game play loop to be what I was hoping it would be as it presented to be something close to D&D but it's too linear for that.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo good. It started off a little slow, but once I had met a couple more people and began unraveling the complex connections between settlements, I couldn't put it down. This is the closest I've seen a computer game come to genuine roleplaying, in the sense of deciding how you want to act out your role, that is, what kind of person you want your character to be -- and letting the game's world react to that much more richly than just watching the standard RPG stats go up. (Kentucky Route Zero comes close, but that story is very tightly scripted [which works well for its purpose], while Roadwarden is open-ended. Here I never felt forced to select an option that I really didn't want my character to say or do.)

Your character is clearly no hero, but just possibly might do some small amount of good and find some community with the folks here -- or might do their job well and get out of this backwater -- or whatever you choose to focus on. Some of the choices you make are "just for flavor" in the sense that they don't unlock quests or money or whatever, but let you express your character's personality (much like KRZ)... and even then, some of them get called back later in the game at unexpected evocative moments. The result is a gripping atmosphere and story, supported by excellent writing (and music and pixel art!).

There's also a late-game quest (optional like the others) that really feels like a culmination of what your character has learned so far. Did you assemble a crew that can work well together? Have you paid attention to rumors which give you hints on how to approach this quest's obstacles? Recall the dangerous encounters with deadly animals you (barely) survived before -- have you learned from past mistakes & how will you approach them this time? Or... do you decide this quest just isn't that important to you, and skip it? It's such great game design, & once again feels like roleplaying a character with genuine personality and history.

Plus it's not just you alone in the wilderness. It felt like everyone, even the most minor NPCs, has rich backstories that would come up in conversation with others. Everyone had been hurt by others, and many have reacted poorly and caused their own major problems; but as you talk further it becomes clear they're all trying the best they can to right the wrongs as they see them, and to make their corner of the world safer in the only ways they know how. A couple of the plot revelations felt like gut punches.

My first (& only so far) playthrough took 12-13 hours. I accomplished much of what I set out to do in that time -- but still had several major plot points unresolved (or outright failed) and many minor tasks unresolved. This doesn't feel frustrating, but rather a meaningful consequence of choices I made: there clearly wasn't enough time to do everything and so I prioritized! There's enough things I didn't finish that I'm eager to return to this world for another playthrough, trying different strategies and roleplaying my character differently. The endings I've seen (just 2 so far, thanks to reloading an end-of-game save and making one decision differently) were bittersweet: some of the short-term goals I reached led to devastating long-term consequences; some of the people my character cared deeply about achieved success and others were harmed. Yet it was a fulfilling journey.\t\t\t\t\t
