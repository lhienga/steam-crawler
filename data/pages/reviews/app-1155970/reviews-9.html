From the number of players, I'd call this a bit of an overlooked gem, and it's well worth the price of entry.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tText-based games live or die on the quality of thier writing, and Roadwarden is just... so full of life. The worldbuilding is delightful and strange. Characters are interesting and varied. Choices have consequences, both in gameplay and narrative that affect your game long after quests are completed. There is so much reactivity to your choices, your relationships, your chosen backstory and class, and your inevitable failures that the outcomes you end up with feel cathartic. Actions have consequences. This story gets dark, characters betray and manipulate and struggle to get by, and the roads are never forgiving of mistakes. The growing pressure of the shortening days forcing the player to cut corners, take risks, and make the most of their time in a world that punishes you for every mistake is a mechanic that makes every action matter. This world is harsh--but what good you choose to do isn't insignificant, and even your failures feel fleshed out and earned.

Nobody has what it takes to be Roadwarden.

Doesn't mean you shouldn't try.

SPOILERS BELOW

The last few days of this game were perfect: uncovering the fate of Steep House was one of the most gratifying moments I've had in an RPG, slowly piecing together a decade-old tragedy that shaped the peninsula and continues to haunt its people. Being dragged out of bed and beaten in Howler's Dell after betraying Thais was shocking, and losing Howler's as a safe haven so late into the game almost butchered my run. And then, finally, on my 39th day, assembling a crew to find Asterion... only to learn that the last thing he ever did was break his own legs with a rock so his shell wouldn't wander. Oof. Ough.

Wright, I love this game.\t\t\t\t\t
