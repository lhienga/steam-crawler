\r\n\t\t\t\t\t Roadwarden is one of the most delightful world's I have ever had the esteemed pleasure of playing. It deserves a thousand compliments for its story, mysteries, adventure, world-building, intrigue, characters, and so much more. Playing as the titular role of Roadwarden, you are to explore the Northern Peninsula with the intentions of establishing trade relations with your home city of Hovlavan. You have 40 days to accomplish all you can and return home.
A piece of the game I cannot get enough of is the fact your backstory grows as you play it! Everything about your character and your home town of Hovlavan is designed by you as you play the game, with NPCs asking you occasional questions of what it's like in the cities. This small detail is one of my favorite parts of the game because as you yourself learn more about this world and its system, you make a true and deep connection to your character.
There are many missions to accomplish, and the game makes completing those missions so rewarding. Every quest is its own saga-length adventure, I can hardly recount all that I've accomplished in my 40 days. After this review I am certainly going to play the game again; while I know some of the world's secrets now, there are many quests I never finished in time. And I am eager to see a whole new side of the game by playing as a different class. Perhaps I will be a Mage this time around.

I have never before written as long of a review, because I have never before had so much to say about a title. TLDR, It is simply one of the most intriguing and rewarding worlds I have ever experienced. And I'm so glad that there is enough mystery to play through it again.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRoadwarden is essentially a text based rpg where you role play a roadwarden who naturally travels the roads of the land delivering messages, fighting mosnters, doing odd jobs, etc. This game has no real time combat, only dice rolls. I bring that up because for many people that may be a deal breaker. However, if you are ok with a completely text based game this is an amazing experience. Perhaps what I enjoy the most about roadwarden is that the world doesn't spell everything out for you. You're forced to pay attention to the information you gather from npc's or the world in order to understand what is going on and make your own inferences whether they be wrong or right. For the right person this results in an extremely immersive experience. Throughout the game you will travel the roads completing quests for the various villages and villagers which in turn increases their trust in you as their roadwarden. Once you get in good with a village/villager they may be willing to share more information about themselves or the world around you, not to mention they may toss a few draon bones your way. (money) Which you can use to upgrade your gear or maybe buy a room at a fancy inn for the night. basically what we have here is an old school rpg done right that has a very interesting world with many things to do and places to see. Its a very fun time and i have no complaints.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe kind of game that makes a perfectionist a bit nervous. Limited resources, limited time, and risky combat make it important for you to explore widely and make resource-management plans early on. Setbacks will happen. Each settlement has its own items, inn costs, quests and story events, so get used to plotting out where to spend the day and where to sleep. Everything takes time, and you never feel like you have enough. A word of advice: spend money on equipment and/or repairs that will save you time in the long run (money you save and health you save is time you save).

Your rough goal is to find a way to time-efficiently make money, rest in safety and explore at a steady pace without letting your health and gear deteriorate to the point that you lose days recovering. You have to balance the risks of new exploration with the need to be healthy enough to continue to earn and perform other tasks effectively. An established and successful Roadwarden has answers for many situations; he controls risks by using his time wisely, and coming prepared. This includes the economic side: there are opportunities for bartering. Plan your next exploration or quest and figure out how to increase your chances of coming through smoothly.

There is an excellent Archive function that stores everything you know about people, places, and enemies, and you will need that knowledge to make sound decisions in combat and other situations. You tend to be rewarded for being perceptive and having a good memory, which makes it worthwhile to stay engaged with the text. There are also lots of chances to roleplay and express your character's thoughts, moods and feelings, which is pleasant in itself, but also can affect relationships and dialog outcomes.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCharming, immersive, not too easy, a few plot-holes/inconsistencies/one underwhelming mystery but not enough to ruin the experience. A decently large patch would make this game great, as it stands it's already good. I just finished it and it's having the same effect as finishing a good book.
On normal difficulty it took me exactly the number of days I was given to finish all my quests playing blind, and I had to neglect some places/characters. Though to be fair I immediately got tricked into losing myself in the most dangerous area of the map at the start. I'd say you're good for 2 or 3 playthroughs in order to see everything/get the endings you want/get every achievement.\t\t\t\t\t
