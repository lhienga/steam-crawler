\r\n\t\t\t\t\tReally a brilliant game with a semi-branching narrative that lets you make not world-changing choices, but some rather mundane ones- choices that made you feel like you were really a warden making choices rather than driving forward a plot.

The author had a really brilliant interview where he talked about how awkward it was in some games where you'd walk up to a character and one of the first dialogue options was "So, tell me about yourself!" which would then prompt 3-4 paragraphs of background story, presented as a monologue. It was really impressive how all the dialogue seemed to flow naturally, even though it was mostly guarded and wary.

A criticism I have was the "text entry" locations- while I understand this is reminiscent of old-fashioned text games, I also thought it was funny there was another interview that said something like "text entry grants the players more freedom than choosing from a list", and I'm thinking -Ok you're still choosing from a list, except that all options except those on the list get ah 'ehm... I don't know that place stranger' or 'you don't find it' answer.

I really appreciated the main focus of the game being that you were helping merchants from Hovlaven, as though you had sort of a quiet goal that these peasants were unaware of. Thais one of the mayors had an interesting story about how hamlets who gain from trade from merchants would eventually become dependent on the merchants that showed she really understood the power dynamics of trade.

One of my favorite interactions was getting my "choice of rewards" in town, where in a fishing village the mayor there told me I could pick between 5 repairs of my armor, a free room to sleep in, a discount on a boat, or several other benefits.

My favorite dialogue string was where I was being pestered by some idiot child about what my job was, which I patiently had to explain. The idea of someone who patrols the roads and keeps an eye on things, clears obstacles, negotiates trade deals, and secures shelters, let me feel like I was working a "job" that there was no real corollary for in another game: part trade representative, part policeman, part messenger but in all aspects having no salary- yet also no real authority besides that granted by some far-off city.

Hope this game paves the way for more in this genre, as those "a choice of" books don't seem appealing to me.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn Roadwarden, you play a traveler hired by a big city to wander the "wild" peninsula outside of the cities, where beasts roam free and small settlements are few. As you wander these roads, you can choose what do do, make a fortune, help out villages in need, help form connections in the peninsula with the city. Just keep in mind, not everyone feels the same about Roadwardens...

Roadwarden features:
-Quests, lots of them
-Loads of endings which change based on every little thing you do
-Different religions to pick from, or none at all
-Zombies
-Lots and lots of text
-Ethical and moral dilemmas a plenty
-3 classes to pick from

Pros:
-Top notch worldbuilding, making you really care about every character, settlement, and story you come across
-An amazing soundtrack that makes you feel fully immersed in travelling the roads
-A pretty carved out economy and money system
-Easy to pick up, no quick time events at all and infinite saves in case you want to undo a choice.
-You can learn about the beasts of the game and fight them more effectively with the knowledge you have

Cons:
-The game is essentially a text adventure with pictures, so if you don't like reading a LOT then this might not be for you
-Pretty basic class system with few mechanics
-Somewhat unengaging combat, though not frequent\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI found the Roadwarden experience to be totally unexpected.

I heard about it from some online review recommending Indie games, otherwise this would have escaped the net entirely.

I don't usually like text-based games (albeit with some cool pixel art tossed in) or visual novels, but I found this one to be extraordinarily good, with some awe-inspiring writing, which, in addition to the atmospheric music and stylized pixel art, gives it a really cool feel.

It also gave me a strong feeling of nostalgia, with its weird mishmash reminiscent of late 80's and early 90's culture in PC games (only deeper), post-apocalyptic films and choose-your-own-adventure novels in look and feel (think of a mix between Angband, Quest for Glory, Oregon Trail, Mad Max Road Warrior and the Lone Wolf Series).

Roadwarden gives meaningful choices with consequences and characters with fascinating depth. It also never gives away too much, and deals with mystery and intrigue in a very effective way that keeps you wanting more. The writing is of a very high caliber, bite-sized, absorbing, thought inducing, but punchy.

Nothing is over-explained, you really have to delve into it, think carefully and follow small scraps of information which make it more engaging and the stakes so much higher.

I also really liked the way in which the game just seems to be so darn difficult at the beginning, and as you get to know it more it becomes way simpler, and in that way it feels like you sort of grow with your character.

I don't expect that this game will be everybody's cup of beans, and really wasn't expecting this game to "hit me (right) in the feels", but in me they found their exact target audience. I give it all the stars.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOkay so this game is super interesting for what it is. Ultimately, it's a text adventure. However, it's one with really well done pixel art, and a really bleak and well done atmosphere in my opinion.

Combat: Non-existing for the most part. You make educated choices based on your knowledge (Or lack thereof) of your foe. There's some RNG aspects there I think, but I am not 100% sure how much is RNG and how much is "you just picked wrong, idiot."

Story: In my opinion it's pretty incredible, it's a very down to earth game. You end up feeling like you really are interacting with people who have lived among dangerous monsters most of their lives. They are harsh, untrustworthy and skeptical, and it's one of those games where it felt like earning trust/reputation was an actual challenge, not something you can just pay your way through.

There is a LOT to this game, and it's a LOT of reading as is any text adventure can be. There's secrets to be found almost everywhere.

Overall though, the game is pretty damn hard. It takes a lot of planning, some math, and some logic to work your way through this dark fantasy world. It's grim, and it gives off those GOT vibes of feeling small in a much larger world often. It isn't the longest game in the world, I beat it at about 18 hours using the scholar character, but I dabbled with the warrior and mage, each class did feel balanced and able to excel in some social interactions due to their chosen skill set, and it made other conversations much more difficult.

Now one thing I *Really* like in a game is player agency. This game gives it to you in spades for the most part. I will be honest here and say I played the game on the longest time frame, and honestly I have no idea the amount of min-maxxing needed to complete the game on the shortest time setting. There is just SO much to find and do. You'd have to do some Stardew Valley level time management if you want to get far. You need to consider your provisions, your travel time, your rest, armor status, and more. All of these things factor into your choices in battles and in other scenarios that test your characters abilities both mentally and physically.

I am absolutely in love with the world that the Dev created here. I'm a huge fan of low/dark fantasy where magic is there, but it's not some over prominent overpowered ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥. It's grounded and it makes sense.

I played this game on steam deck. It wasn't the smoothest experience, but I was able to play the entire game on steam deck, and given what the Dev states in their updates, they've put work into it as well, so kudos to them there, I doubt they thought they'd need to make a controller config for a text adventure. Definitely extra points for being adaptable.

Overall, I can't recommend this game enough if you like to think and figure out actually hard puzzles, or you enjoy text adventures or just simply a fantasy addict. It's very unique and one of a kind in a lot of ways and honestly one of my GOTY's. The price is good if not generous as well, given the amount of work that had to have been put into this in terms of making everything this game throws at you work in such a cohesive way.

100% Recommend if you are into this game from the screenshots etc. Good on the Dev for being responsive and squashing bugs too.
