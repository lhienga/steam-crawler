\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is an extremely well-designed and deep game which draws you in with its story and characters. It can be overwhelming at times to remember all of the information and quests you've been given, but thankfully there's a useful journal which automatically stores most of this info, and can be accessed at any time.

At first, you start as a fledgling adventurer with nary a coin to your name. But you have many options to progress the story, either through helping the locals or carving your own path. You will gradually progress your skills (such as lying, tailoring, and fighting), and upgrade your gear over time. By the end of your 10-15 hour journey, you will likely be well-known throughout the peninsula, and ready to decide its ultimate fate.

I highly recommend Roadwarden if you want a highly engaging story in an immersive fantasy world.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAbsolutley fantastic! This game would just not leave my head even after playing it through twice already.
I love the writing style, beautifully brought to live with the simple graphics and sound design.
Every character you encounter has a believable motivation for their action. They have reasons to trust or distrust you and I found navigating the politics of this world incredibly satisfying. Many games promise that "your actions will have consequences" but I rarely found it as true as in Roadwarden, that will show you them all play out down the line.
I am amazed how well Roadwarden manages to offer this complexity without overwhelming the player or ending up holding my hand through all decision.

I can´t recommend this game highly enough!

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAAA games have their own problems these days, and I foolishly seek refuge in indie games...only to have discovered time and again they have just as many flaws - albeit different ones.

It really is an equal and opposite issues situation, although I do pay much less for indie games, so...

What I find is that indie games inject artificial difficulties...I guess to keep up with AAA games?
To show we're too cool, and don't make no easy games, man!?

I do not know.

I only know that like Darkest Dungeon, I was supremely frustrated with what would otherwise be a perfectly fine - if not a great - game, ruined into tediousness by some artificial standards.

In this dumb indie game's case it is:
Appearance


Yes, dear reader, if I do not wash or mend my clothes regularly, then people will not have anything to do with me, and I am in a soft fail loop not knowing how to fix it.
I was low on life and rest, and suddenly NO inns would rent a room to me.
AND it cost me a LOT OF RESOURCES to clean 1 or 2 points.

Up until this point - I was actually enjoying these games as they resembled the excellent Lone Wolf series of books from childhood.

Indie Game Developers:
THERE IS NO REASON FOR ARTIFICIAL, CRUDELY INJECTED DIFFICULTIES!
THIS DOES NOT MAKE YOUR GAME "COOL"!

A well-balanced game is good - not something like "appearance" as a stat which affects EVERYTHING!

Also, the main character stupidly gives away the REAL reason for his mission - essentially to pave the way for his merchant city to conquer the free-region.
So, secret, secret, cannot tell anyone, only hinted at even to the player THEN:
"Yes, my city sent me to provide information and contacts to directly control this area not claimed by other great powers."

I felt like I was watching The Phantom Menace with characters simply announcing to enemies, passerby and the audience key and secret motivations.

Ultimately, this game could have been great, but an amateurish obsession endemic to indie games set in and ruined it.

Not worth the time.\t\t\t\t\t
