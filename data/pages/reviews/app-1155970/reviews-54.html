\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR: Absurdly stellar game. Please partake immediately. I highly recommend this game for its polish, its fun factor, its story, its balance, and its atmosphere.

Roadwarden plays a lot like what you'd expect a classic "choose your own adventure" game might play like, but with some very modern appreciable qualities. There's mechanical crunch and resource balancing beyond just "picked the wrong option? you're DEAD, bucko." The balance for a player like me, who is not forward-thinking and is fairly impulsive and is rather meticulous in wanting to DO EVERYTHING and comb the land clean, is impeccable. I finished off my entire quest log down to the very last day of the game's normal-difficulty time budget. Resources aren't scarce, but they're not in abundance either, and grinding is (rather graciously) not an option.

Rudimentary planning of your day and routes are fun and necessary to maximize productivity and balance your resources. Even in make-or-break things like combat encounters, the game is dutifully forgiving and allows you to restart immediately from the encounter should you fail, and there are a plethora of options for varying situations. Throughout the game you can collect an abundance of adventurer's tools to make various encounters easier and less taxing on resources, and make you feel clever for planning ahead. Further, the game mixes things up a little in various places, where you don't just get a dialogue box of spoken lines or actions to click, and where you instead in fact need to apply critical thinking and enter in keywords to a "type in the action/question/name/location you wish to know more about" box.

The game as a whole rewards forward- and critical-thinking, is deliciously moody with its music and background noises, is very fun and solid in its writing, and really good at making you appreciate (or despise) the people in it. There's a plethora of fluffy actions and choices, and so much interwoven connectivity where completing an action or learning about a thing on one side of the map will offer up a question to ask elsewhere. There are even some simple conversations which allow you to shape the past of the world by recounting your part in it, which is phenomenal for world building.

Given a greater time budget, I would have definitely done some things differently and made different choices and preparations, but I still felt very satisfied with my conclusion and the story I wove with my actions. I had to leave some stones unturned for the sake of time, but even that felt good for the sake of having to make tough decisions.

For a while in the beginning of my run, I overextended myself very hard. I was coasting on nothing - little health, no money, no resources. I spent the majority of a single day from my delicate 40-day time budget working for a single coin, scarcely enough to fill half a belly, and not enough to even sleep a night on a bed where I was. I traveled with that coin to a distant inn, spent it there for some generous hot meals, and regained just enough strength to continue exploring until I tackled a giant spider and collected its web. The web would be very useful - I could use it at a tailor to upgrade my armor, OR sell it for a healthy chunk of coin to get my legs under me again. Tempted toward the adventurer's need and greed for the former, I instead chose the coin for the sake of practicality - and promptly spent the majority of it to get myself back on track, after which I finally hit a solid stride.

I cannot stress enough how good this game is and how worth my time it was.\t\t\t\t\t
