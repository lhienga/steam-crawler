\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo this game has decent writing, decent art, but I just can't recommend it.

It's similar to Citizen Sleeper, and I'd say overall a better game (better writing for sure). But I just can't recommend it because the balance is way too funky.

Rather than hard killing you if you goof up, it will put you into a annoying soft fail state, where you have no money, no vitality, and you can't really progress but you don't hard fail either. I don't think that's so fun. I'd much rather it just hard kill you and force you to reload.

And you're going to end up in that sorta state a lot, because the game is pretty punishing. Citizen Sleeper was snooze fest easy. Basically everyone loved you and there weren't real consequences in terms of having to weigh decisions. Roadwarden is the opposite where no good deed goes unpunished. Sometimes to pretty extreme extents.

Obviously there is foreshadowing, but you can still get really screwed sometimes without realizing it until quite a ways later.

Adventure games are only difficult to whatever extent you avoid spoilers. Something like Outer Wild's is pretty much perfect as it gives you a gameplay loop to learn more about the world without feeling like you're cheating. Roadwarden doesn't capture that magic, so you feel that to win you need to be unfairly spoilered.\t\t\t\t\t

Incredibly good game with one weakness: for all it's depth, once you know the story and learn all the surprises and twists, there's no way reclaim the level of immersion you have when you play Roadwarden for the first time.
Phenomenal storytelling, straightforward game mechanics, great music. The replayability factor is extremely low. I'm crushed with the disappointing ending of my first playthrough, and now I'll have to wait a good long while before I play it again. Please do yourself a favor and play on the lowest difficulty setting so that you aren't time-limited. I'm sure I would've enjoyed the experience more if I had been able to achieve more by the end.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWowee. A text-based adventure game won't be for everyone but this is surely among the very best. While it is text-based both the pixel landscapes and the music/sound effects are superb which goes a looong way to helping you get invested in the world.

But beyond that, of course, we have the writing. If you are doing a text-based game, you better have mega confidence in your ability to write. And boy does Moral Anxiety deliver. The game takes place in a relatively small world of the peninsula with a handful of settlements. And each settlement feels incredibly detailed as they may have different religions, different staple products that define them and of course multiple fully fleshed-out characters in each settlement. Just like a different vibe in each area ya know?

The game had me invested in its world really quickly. Desolate, beasts of all varieties abound and a lonesome roadwarden heart set on protecting these lands. Its so good. Just go play it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is an exceptionally well written game, with a lot of paths you can take, a variety of friends and enemies to make or take down, and the option to end the game at your discretion (or by running into the time limit on any difficulty other than easy). It feels like there's a lot of depth in this one, and it's only around 15-20 hours for a full blind playthrough, depending on how fast you can read. It may feel like a slog towards the beginning, when you're exploring the map, but the days do speed up on the traveling front, once you start to make the roads you're taking somewhat safer. It helps keep the focus tight on the quests (both mundane and very complex) as the game progresses. Do note that if reading isn't something you like doing in your video gaming, you're likely not going to get as much out of this one as I did, but I do hope you'll give it a whirl all the same.

Please do yourself a favor and go into playing this title as blindly as you can. I managed to avoid spoilery stuff, and made the story truly mine, including a lot of successes, a lot of helping people who needed it, and some not-insignificant failures. Some of the best parts came from remembering (or referring to the journal) to get information out of "fill in the blank" sections, where you have to guess the right actions at the right time, or ask about the right persons/places/things. Many of them felt straightforward, but the game is programmed well enough to expect and respond to a large number of inputs in these scenarios.

The Unnamed Peninsula is going to be shaped by your actions in both big and small ways - choose the path you feel is the right one for you, and you'll be rewarded with an outstanding experience (and maybe even a purse full of dragon bones).\t\t\t\t\t
