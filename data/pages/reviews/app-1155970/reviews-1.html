\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTruly a great text-based adventure game (though with enough of a pixeley art style and soundtrack to give the game some wonderful atmosphere). The forest peninsula you find yourself the warden of is dark and full of terrors. As one of the few people willing to venture beyond the walls of the region's small towns and hamlets, your every word and action carry significant weight for the denizens of the region. Assist the townsfolk by completing quests, follow their intrigues, uncover their secrets, but also earn their trust and reap the rewards.

Combat is both difficult and dangerous, but creatures can often be outmaneuvered or outwitted with the right knowledge and preparation. In addition, the ability to save multiple files and load them freely prevents a great deal of frustration. That said, given that most of the combat encounters are pre-scripted rather than random, by the time you have upgraded your equipment and found yourself a few epic artifacts, you will no longer have a real need for them. Still, it feels nice growing stronger in such a hostile wilderness.

The main story is engaging and compelling, and will likely take more than one run to fully experience. While the three different class types each offer unique gameplay, dialogue, and combat mechanics, there is not much more value in replaying the game once you have completed all of the major questlines. By the end of the game though, you can use the relationships you've developed to shape the future of the peninsula. Using your influence to open the region to the City's merchant guilds, looking after the various "best interests" of the townsfolk, or following your faith to cleanse your rivals and advance the power of certain groups over others. While the game leaves you wishing there was more, it is perhaps the mark of a good adventure that ends if only a little bit too soon. Quite possibly the best five bucks I've ever spent on a game, and if they ever release a DLC or sequel I would get it in a heartbeat.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRoadwarden is, without a doubt, the best game in years. I'm 37 years old and have been playing since the early 90s. Video games have always catered to the fantasies of youth, but lately, it seems like the concept of 'fantasy' is spiraling into a pit of conformity and banality. Today's youth only seem to dream what they've already been spoon-fed, which is becoming increasingly uninspiring.

Roadwarden offers a mature, profound, and captivating experience. Back in the day, book-based RPGs never reached this level of depth and polish. This game is truly a masterpiece, elevating and redefining its genre, which appears to be in a state of decline.

It's a solid 10/10 from me. The only downside? After a few playthroughs, I'll probably need years before I can fully enjoy this game again.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis was the first non-card game to really captivate me in quite some time. The worldbuilding was specific and cozy--aided by the soundtrack--, a fantasy land to which one can escape in which there are in-game stakes but without the stakes of your real-world toils, and with just enough personalization to immerse you in the interactive fiction.

After halfway or two-thirds through, however, the novelty wears off, and the game devolves into a recursive quest cycle of running around in circles on the one island you're allowed to explore, fleshing out characterizations and secrets of the map yet padded with filler. My eyes started glazing over at the increasing paragraphs, as I cared less about the specificity and worldbuilding now so much as I did the concerns I'd picked out from the story as I experienced it.

For those interested in plumbing all the depths of that story, choose the Story mode, as the Standard mode--to say nothing of the Expert mode--on which I played is too difficult for a first-time player to understand how to come to all the conclusions of the various story threads. (The opportunity costs seem somewhat artificial, as it becomes just a matter of having enough coins to pay for the quest checkmarks you need.) The many grim outcomes are thematically befitting a setting struggling with recovering from major bloodshed, and the optionality for avoiding combat with creatures that recontextualize humans further down the food chain is to be respected, but you then can't fault negative feelings in the player when their campaign to help people comes up short--especially in light of the trope-history of role-playing games.

And this is a by & large sex-less world, with admirable queer representation and some minor romances but too cozy for that fundamentally adult content--such that you wonder how anyone comes to reproduce in this realm.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you're even remotely considering buying this and wondering if its the game for you, it is. It's exactly what you think it is.
Buy it right now.

People have blown millions trying to make RPGs that are a tenth as engaging as this. The music, the systems, the world... it's all fantastic. The game is almost entirely text driven, but at the same time it's sparing and evokes a world rather than drowning the player in exposition. The world and the characters in it just come alive in ways that again, far more expensive games utterly fail to pull off.

I haven't been as interested in exploring since I played Subnautica, and I haven't been as intrigued by the mysteries in a game since I played Obra Dinn. This is that good.\t\t\t\t\t
