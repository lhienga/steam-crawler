\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game expertly cuts away a lot of the cruft of the RPG genre, while still keeping a lot of its mechanics. Instead of overly-complex or abstracted combat and traveling, the moment-to-moment play resembles more of a visual novel, with you keeping tabs on your reputation, managing your time, maintaining your needs, and thinking critically about the words you're reading. Every part of the game is delicately interwoven - the narrative, themes, UI, and mechanics - and it all comes together to create a melancholic whole, every piece reinforcing the desperation, not only to remain alive, but to remain whole, a struggle that every other character in the game also faces. The art is occasionally a bit crude, and I don't feel like it's particularly well-served by pixel art, but such choices are cost-effective, and the art is primarily used to accentuate the writing anyway. Also, as is often the case for games with this many words, there are several spelling and grammatical errors, but rarely did they take me out of the experience. The writing allows for a lot of player agency, and it's high-quality enough to make more reading feel like a reward as opposed to a slog. Overall, I'd say the structure of the game alone is enough to make it one of my favorites, fitting itself snugly between two good genres to make a great experience.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI have been missing a game that focused on a good story for the last little bit and boy, does Roadwarden deliver. This text and picture game does not need impressive graphics to get your attention. The story telling, and world building, and the music will take you there instantaneously.

There is so much you do not know about the world when you come in as the new Roadwarden. The game does a pretty good job at making survival hard, and throw mysteries on your way than I felt compelled to unravel. Forget battles, there are some, but even as a warrior, you will rely on your wits and wisdom.

I found managing your appearance and purse challenging at the beginning, as well as some times the few times where the game ask you to type in text to solve riddles, or search a place. But overall, I felt like I opened a good book I could never let go until I completed the story. I want to start again, and this time, I won't lie to NPCs, I promise.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really like this game - austere and brutal, thematic and flexible in its mechanics and story design. Highly recommended for those who like a deep RPG experience.

A few things for the Devs:
- it would be helpful if names, dialogue, objects, and key words were highlighted in the text like place names are. As a skimmer, I often found myself missing key information
- the lack of a hint system is quite punishing and took the steam out of the game for me. It doesn't need to be explicit. In an example, if I'm trying to get someone to trust me a line suggesting 'they might trust me if I offer them X' would be extremely helpful and make me feel I can make progress. If I'm to solve a puzzle I appreciate a clear view of the puzzle I need to solve. This systems presence could be an optional mode of the game for users less interested in a long term grind and more interested in exploring the wonderful story this game offers
- the user entry "what do you look for" sections would benefit from a great deal more guidance, as I found the lack of steering really difficult to overcome and I ended up ignoring those sections, probably to the detriment of story opportunities
- the lack of active quest logs with waypoints and instructions makes coming back to the game after a time away a real chore; reading back through all the logs is a big task

Thanks!

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRoadwarden is not a game that I expected to enjoy too much, as I hadn't played many games like it before. I only bought it because it was on sale and I was running out of other things to play, but man am I glad I did. Roadwarden has so many elements built into it that I didn't expect to find when first playing. I think this game is best to go into mostly blind, as much of its charm comes from learning and adapting to this world that feels familiar to our own, yet noticeably different and more dangerous. The people you meet and locations you travel to are all changed based on your choices, and I loved seeing my actions slowly shape and even tame all of the different places I once knew nothing about. This game constantly surprised me with just how in depth I could get with different locations and characters, and I truly think its worth a try especially if you haven't played anything like it before.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHonestly, i'm kind of shocked by the amount of positive reviews on this game. I had incredibly high hopes for this game after reading the reviews but it truly felt like a chore to play. I understand this is a "story driven novel game" but that doesn't really emphasize how much reading there is. There are very few choices for you to actually make. There is so. much. reading. And so little actual playing to do. If you're looking for a game with hours upon hours of reading and about 20-30 minutes of actual "decisions" to make, then this is the game for you. But if you're hoping for an RPG style game where you get to actually control a character and make choices that dictate the story line you're going to be extremely bored like I was.

I honestly cannot recommend this game. The endings are a massive let down and the game (unless played on casual mode) doesn't give you enough time to truly explore all of the options to get the most satisfying ending.\t\t\t\t\t
