\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI grew up reading a lot of CYOA books and playing fantasy tabletop RPGs, so I thought this would be excellent based on the reviews. But the writing is really bad. I got tired of every character quote being interrupted by a completely unnecessary description of a random thing the interlocutor was doing, like "looking away", "trying to clean an invisible stain", "passing the tip of their blade on the table" etc. I don't know where people learn that this kind of narrative with excessive description is immersive or good; it's just distracting and lame. I also caught a few typos in less than 2 hours and had to quit.
The setting also feels generic and bland.
Maybe there is a good game down the line, but I expect flawless writing on a text-based game, and this is not the case.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAn absolute gem of the game. Having finished my second play through (of at least 3 or 4 more to come) I can say that I have enjoyed the experience this game provides quite a significant amount more than most Triple-A titles released within the last 5 years minimum. (EXTREMELY light spoilers ahead for gameplay effects)

The experience this game provides puts you directly into the mind and soul of an adventurer who has the odds stacked against them, yet prevails. Travel the lands, encountering nightmarish scenarios that batter and bruise you, and send you limping back to the nearest tavern or town, aching for a brew and some place just to be able to lay your weary head for a night. Your encounters only provide you with experience and skills that you can use in your next encounter with all of the secret bonuses you get to rolls as you play the game that come and feel very natural for the kind of person your character is.

You also are able to gain tangible knowledge that not only affect secret chance-based rolls in the background, but open up entirely new scenarios between yourself and the creatures that prowl the mysterious Peninsula, as well as the people who inhabit it. Talk to the locals, figure out secrets and knowledge only certain people have, unravel mysteries and intrigue, slay/drive off the creatures who inhabit the land, or attempt to make your peace and subdue them through nonviolence; however you want your character to act you usually have that option.

Roadwarden is a game meant to be played on the normal difficulty, which is under a time limit that will take, on average, 5-10 hours to complete. There *IS* a story-mode where the time limit is a non-factor and encounters are easier, but that takes away from the experience that is Roadwarden. Some people find the time limit to be too stressful or a detriment to the experience, but the reality is that the world and area that Roadwarden is set in are exactly that: Almost insurmountably stressful and dangerous. However, as you travel and make decisions, your choices have a tangible impact on the world around you, significantly reducing that stress and danger through one method, or another. Travel times between well known places decrease as you solve problems plaguing the land and make allies, traveling during certain weather conditions either benefit or harm your chances at certain actions or traveling, but you can reduce the impact that random events have on the world through your own actions.

The game is not without flaw, of course, but they are so few and far between that they won't bother most people. To give an example, there are certain areas where you have to type out when looking for specific things or people, and searching for relevant information can be somewhat difficult or not entirely accurate, but if you spend the time to think critically, you should be able to figure it out. Another example being that solutions to a few certain problems are *quite* vague, but as long as you're open to exploring and leaving almost no stone unturned, you should figure them out eventually, and if need be, come back on your next play through with that knowledge already in hand and bust out these problems with little issue. These problems, as stated, don't come up often and don't detract from the overall excellent experience.

Overall, Roadwarden lends itself well to avid readers and explorers. You will stumble, you will fail, but you will learn to prosper. Roadwarden lends itself, as well, to something important for a game so short: Replayability. Your first play through, you will likely mess up. You may not even get what could be considered a "Good Ending." As you learn from your first play through, however, if you're anything like me, you will become determined to change the outcome. You will play through again, and possibly more than a few more times, using your previous knowledge on how to handle problems and interact with the people of the North to quicken your goals and unravel more and more of the mystery that surrounds the entire peninsula.

Verdict: 93/100\t\t\t\t\t
