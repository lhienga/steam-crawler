\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI have to give this a thumbs up because this game clearly shines, and it's obviously top-notch in its genre. Having said that, it's not exactly my cup of tea.

I read books. I read dense books, heady books, long books -- from Brandon Sanderson to Heidegger or Nietzsche. So this is not an illiterate smooth-brain talking.

Here's the thing: a picture is definitely worth a thousand words. In most rpg games, I can walk into an inn and assess the goings-on in about 30 seconds, give or take. In Roadwarden, it takes me about 10 minutes to get the same amount of information. Don't get me wrong; the atmosphere is amazing, the world's character is very pronounced and unique, and the game deserves all credit for that. It's just... not exactly what I come to a video game for. In a game, my mental expectation is to quickly analyze things through a combination of text, images, and numbers, leaning more on the visuals. What Roadwarden offers is something I get when I turn to books.

Otherwise, I know this is exactly what some people are looking for, and you should definitely try it out. Despite my short play time, the merits of Roadwarden are very much on the surface.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat an experience!
I think in a way I played this game at the perfect moment in my life. As I tried to become a better DM in our weekly tabletop RPG campaign I was thinking about fictional worlds, the structure of stories, and how characters interact with each other and the player a lot lately.

Then I played the fantastically written Roadwarden. A game that would best be described as a chimaera with the head of a visual novel, the body of an RPG, and the legs of a text adventure. In the beginning, the game lulled me with its beautifully atmospheric pixel art and its equally fitting melancholic music. But it wasn't long before I was fascinated by its world. A rough world, where survival isn't a given and death lurks around every corner. Where every little hamlet, every path through this land had to be painfully carved out of the wilderness and relentlessly protected against the wild beasts (one of which is humanity itself).

But the aspect that really made me fall in love with this game was how it handled the player's involvement in this world and the web of relationships the player has to navigate. I never felt railroaded, always in control. There was no clear direction to take, not even a clear goal. And so it felt more like a real world, like a real life I was living. I was trying to improve things, yes. But ultimately I was just a small cog in this machine that will keep on running long after I finished the game. Every decision I made was mine. Every path I took belonged to me. Every friendship I made along the way was personal. In the end, there isn't the best way to play this game. Not the right decision to make. Not the correct path to take. Every player's story will differ, no outcome will be the same. It was my story and my story alone. Never has a story in a game felt so personal to me.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI had no idea what to expect playing a game like this, but after putting more than 200 hours into Disco Elysium on the old Steam account I called mine before I decided to give it all up and become Golden Hat Mick, I was pretty confident Roadwarden would be a worthwhile journey in the early days of my new gaming identity. I was wrong — it was way better than merely worthwhile. It shattered those humble expectations, crushed them as if they were a fragile human shell in the hands of an enraged frightape.

After five playthroughs and just over 80 hours, I've managed to uncover a good chunk of what this game has to offer, and I can confidently state that it's a world more gamers need to experience. Whatever is lacking in the graphical element is easily replaced by competent sound design and sufficient geography artwork to stay connected to the environment. This is largely a text-based experience, but presentation quality is superb, and I don't mind reading, especially prose as polished as Roadwarden's.

Each of the major locations is unique, its characters memorable, and the impact you as the roadwarden can have on the final outcome is significant. You won't get far in your first investigation unless you're playing on the easiest setting, and if you're like me, you'll be eager to jump right back in and start anew regardless. Even now, after having finally finished a satisfying run that cleared my lengthy quest list, not everything went how I planned, and I forgot to tie up at least one loose end. And echoing thoughts of others, the ending narrative never feels quite as satisfying as it should.

That's a small matter, however. Credit to Moral Anxiety Studios on a fantastic adventure. I hope for further content down the road, if not in Roadwarden, then in other creations. I'm a full believer in the talents of this small-but-promising studio, and I'll be following their progress closely. As for me, I don't think I can stop my visual novel momentum at this point. I've already got my eye on several other titles like this one, and I'll be mixing plenty of them in along whatever winding path that gaming takes me.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA lush, tightly constructed visual novel in a gritty RPG setting. Highlights included the hard-hitting exploration in the first 2/3rds of the game, the superb writing, and some clever choices that led to surprisingly deep role play.

As an example of the role play, I somewhat randomly picked a religion from ~6 options at the beginning of the game. I prayed numerous times early on and then became frustrated with the religion's other practitioners and had my character abandon it. But then, near the end of the game, the knowledge of the tenets of the religion helped me experience one of the game's most powerful moments.

Without spoiling anything, I found the end of the game less strong than the beginning and middle. *Edit: The dev improved the end! Fab. Previous review resumes*: But overall, this is gem that carves out a new niche between text-based RPGs and visual novels and - if that sounds enticing - it is a must-play. That is, if you're willing to do some reading.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFantastic little gem that reminded me of choose your own adventure books of my childhood. Be aware, this is a game about reading, you're gonna get the most out of it if you're ready for a mid length novel. The beautiful pixel art scenes help to illustrate the environment you will be travelling through, but the action and exposition is all a theater of the mind kind of experience, if you are a tabletop game player or a CYOA nerd (me) this is an absolutely worthwhile experience. the worldbuilding and storytelling are deep and immersive even if they borrow from common fantasy beats, but hey, how can you not in the common day?

The choices you make shape the world and the conclusion of the story, and I can see that it is possible to have a large number of possible permutations based on the path you travel through the game. Solving the deeper mysteries of the peninsula can seem challenging but do have a significant effect on how much of the story is revealed to you, and whether you can be satisfied with the number of loose ends you left undone in the conclusion. Some elements felt like a throwback to old RPG's on the computer, without spoilers, in one section you will have no dialogue options to further the plot and instead have to manually type things like "bed" "trade" in order to find out more about the area. these old investigation elements allow the player to sleuth out extra information by testing the limits of the inputs, and also a couple easter eggs if they are clever, or immature enough ;)\t\t\t\t\t
