\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI finished my first play-through yesterday and Roadwarden is one of those games capable of burning your heart as you click through the epilogue and leave this fascinating world. I wish I had the time or better time management skills to allow myself to get into another run immediately.

Regardless of the text adventure RPG aspects, to me the first attention grabbers and the main culprits for this immersive experience were the visuals and the music. Not only the illustrations, but even the tiniest visual elements of the UI were in harmony with the soundtrack and aided the world-building.

The world, the characters and the story are classic RPG, content-wise nothing you've never read before, yet much more immersive and alive. Especially the relationship of the Roadwarden with the various characters and places will resemble the Witcher a lot. Honestly this felt even more cohesive and complete. On top of that, it's more grounded. You're neither a nobody, nor the center of the world.

Although towards the end I did find myself in the save-load-hell a lot, I believe that says more about my personal time pressure than about the game. I'd have to revisit the games a couple of times, but on my first impression I don't think you have too many misunderstanding choices moments that force you to play safe - yet they did exist. Another challenge for text adventures is to make sense regardless of the paths chosen. Sometimes it did feel like the narrative was written with other preferred choices in mind. What else I have to nitpick are some freezes in the journal section for a few seconds. But those are tiny little inconveniences in an otherwise breathtaking text-based RPG game,\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRoadwarden is an amazing game. It is the first text-based game that I've ever played and is one of my favorite games I've played so far. The world is extremely engaging and interesting and the music and pixel art are stunning too. I would absolutely recommend it to everyone, but it does have a few issues.

1. The lying mechanic is quite annoying. When you start a quest you have the option to for example say "I'll cover for you" or "(lie) I'll cover for you". This is quite flawed since you have to decide at the begging of a quest and you can't change your mind later. I pretty sure you can't fail to lie unless there's a dice symbol, so why not let the player decide if they want to lie or not when they'll be doing the task instead of doing so at the start?

2. Sometimes if you finish a quest by saying for example "I won't find them" and later on you find what you needed you can't continue the quest, since the dialogue option doesn't appear, which is especially annoying when you want to get all achievements. (slight spoiler) Since most quests are not time gated why not have the option to continue the quest, but for example get a smaller reward?

3. (MAJOR SPOILER) At first when you enter the north you're there to fulfill the merchant's guild quest, but as you go on (at least for me) you grow more and more attached to the isolated feel of the land, but in the ending you don't have an option to tell the guild that they shouldn't trade at all. And why ins't there an option to come back to Peninsula after talking to the guild? I know you have the option to stay at certain tribes, but wouldn't you be able to remain a roadwarden and just rest at different inns?

4. (SPOILER) I've encounter a few annoying bugs:

1.With high enough reputation you can give items to the mayor and shaman of The Tribe of the Green Mountain, but it bugged out for me and i couldn't give them the Asterion's cloak, so i couldn't get the reputation high enough to where they would allow me to go to High Island :c.

2. After i sacrificed enough things to get The Seed I couldn't cut off the roots, which again didn't allow me to get high enough rep in the green tribe. \t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI tend to be turned off by text based games. Make no mistake, I have always been an avid reader until recent years so it isn't hard for me. I just feel like a game would have to pull a lot more through just words alone. That being said, I found Roadwarden to be an exciting experience. Not only does it provide an intriguing story, it gives you free reign to discover the world you live in as you please. The roads are dangerous, though, and you will often find yourself save scumming after biting off more than you can chew. Yet the story and the thought of what I might discover brought me back every time. It has been forever since I literally did nothing but sit on a game all day long. Every time I felt I spent too long on the game I would take a 15 minute break and come right back. I even turned down multiplayer with the guys to play this game. The normal difficulty gives you 40 days to fulfill your main quest along with a personal goal and whatever side quests you choose to partake in. There are factions and characters that respond to every choice you make. You will never feel overpowered in this game as combat is such a minor part of all the things you can do here. Bargaining, exploration, fishing, foraging, meeting new people, diplomacy, and combat all come together however you feel like going about it. As there is a time limit you will be forced to organize tasks by importance and convenience. You will make things easier for yourself the more you do in your short time. I have had a single playthrough so far and I feel I will definitely return to try new paths and finish quests I had to leave behind for the sake of time limits. Overall great text based game, it is worth the dosh.\t\t\t\t\t
