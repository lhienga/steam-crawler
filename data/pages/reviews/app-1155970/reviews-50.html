\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is my kind of game. I love the Zork series. I love pixel art and CRT visuals. I love adventure games where my choices matter; and that's why I can't get behind this game. For everything is does right, such as developing an interesting world, setting tone and mood, and convincing you to invest in the lore and characters, it does everything else entirely wrong.

It tells you not to take unnecessary risks- that this world is cold- but then every bit of progress requires you doing something stupid. The game asks you a lot of questions, and even has 5 different response types when you meet people, but no matter what, you're going to end up with the same conclusion, with the same new mission or clue. It comes down to collecting "keys" from around the map, and making your way back around to put them in the locks. It's very basic gameplay, with little reward for completing any given mission.

It's a game that looks well-built from the beginning, even asking you (in a conversation that develops the story and world) about your character's previous profession (and talents) as well as religious beliefs (for which there's like six!). You make these decisions with utmost care and strategy, only for the game to ALWAYS have a solution for you if you aren't a "pagan doctor" or any other choice you could've made. Sure, it's cool when I get to heal someone with my specialties, however it undermines it when I can see an all-choices right below it simply saying "patch up their wounds," which would've been my only and go to option if I weren't. It's lame- and it gets tiring very quickly when the game DEMANDS you read all the text they're providing, when you know that not one line of it will be needed or useful later. I was told that one character didn't like an attitude, only to discover that it doesn't matter how you treat them, they'll always give you the same mission.

This flashy game is tonally and stylishly dynamic, but the bare-bone gameplay and "player choices" are abysmally dull. I didn't finish- I don't think I'll be back. If the devs are reading this, please know I really wanted to like this, and I look forward to you improving this formula. As you can see on your reviews, you seem to have a winner- so my word doesn't mean much- but if you're going to improve on this formula (or series), I would consider taking some notes from Disco Elysium and other RPGs about improving your player choices and how to better branch your narratives for improved engagement. Thank you to anyone reading this- I'm not looking to troll, but I really can't recommend this game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI have played a few "choose your own adventure" games in the past, notably "80 days" and the "Sorcercy!" series from Inkle, as well as a couple of longer, more game-like titles like Opus and Citizen Sleeper. Despite having a lower production value and being less ambitious than most of these, Roadwarden is probably the best in the genre.

The reason why I feel that way is simple: the story feels believable and challenging enough while minimizing repetition. You do need supplies to stay alive, but there's so many missions and different things to do that it never feels like a chore, just another adventure.

The premise is basic, you're a Roadwarden sent to explore a peninsula and report back to your city's guild. It's an alternate medieval world with its own customs, words and daily life. There's enough descriptions to make the world feel alive without burdening you with tons of pointless lore. The growth of your character is organic and you don't turn into a demigod even at the end of the game- people still don't trust you if you haven't helped them or proved yourself...just like in real life.
You can get by without glory, and on my first playthrough, without too much save scumming, I got a decent result but with many things left to explore.

The entire game is text-based, with decent looking monochrome pixel art to help you visualize things, although characters and enemies are never shown. Depending on how you like things, this could be a pro or a con since it lets you use your imagination more. It's easy to read and didn't tire my eyes at all even after many hours of reading. The music of course can't last you throughout the entire game without repeating, but it's very nice and atmospheric.

The most important thing is having a coherent story while letting your choices feel impactful and real- and this is where the game excels. Characters act in a believable way based on what you do, and even things like your cleanliness level affect how people see you and react to you.

Negatives? For what it is, this game doesn't have that many. First off, there's a few times where you're asked to type things in (like asking about certain characters) without prompts on what you could ask for. This is a little annoying because sometimes you could've missed a text snippet from a previous character and it's never mentioned again (even in the journal), so you need to memorize things or write them down somewhere.
Sometimes you can get lost in a sea of side quests, and the game map isn't great- it just shows you some basic waypoints and where your character is. There's no quest icons or anything like that, so you keep having to open your quest log to make sure you don't miss anything when you visit a city. All of these things were negative for me because of my terrible memory- however, players who don't like handholding could enjoy this level of challenge. I would like for these to be togglable options though.

Last but definitely not least- the writing. Is it good? Yes. There's LOTS of descriptions, to the point of reminding me of R. R. Martin's books. Food sounds delicious every time it's mentioned, and there's vidid detail added that helps flesh out the world and make it feel old, lived-in and complete. Flawed, wild, interesting and ripe for the taking. Characters come from all walks of life, and none of them will like you at first. Some of them never will, and that's okay, you make choices and have to live with the very real and impactful consequences.

All in all, a very strong title in this genre where it's difficult to stand out. I do very much hope to see more from this developer, and I'm glad to see that the game got some official recognition in the indie scene with some awards.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI know it looks humble and it really is, but it's also one of the most enjoyable narrative experiences I've ever encountered. Don't be fooled by the text-based gameplay. It's more than worth it. The characters truly feel real, the encounters are elegantly described and the value in replaying is really good. The story, however, is what takes the crown. Maybe I enjoy this game because I am a fan of the "theater of the mind". Maybe I enjoy it because it's just that good. Whatever the case, it is worth it, even if you're reluctant because of the need to read that much. And it saddens me that this gem of a game is so under the radar.\t\t\t\t\t
