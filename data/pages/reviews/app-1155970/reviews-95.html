\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBasically a young adult-level CYOA book with tons of illustration, dice rolls that don't get over-used, and sensibly succinct prose.

You are a roadwarden, a job that spends as much time in the saddle as it does in danger. Although keeping roads clear is part of your work description, you're really little more than a lawfully-sponsored adventurer: You're to explore the paths of a wild peninsula and build a rapport with its isolated villages to make them malleable to the suggestion of foreign commerce. You have 40 days to gather a report and make vital connections for the investments of the merchant guild that hired you. How you spend your time is your own discretion, but it should always be advancing your goals in some way. There's always things that need doing, and it's up to you to adequately prioritize them.

There are choices both banal and important, with equally polarized consequences. Items and equipment to collect, some of which offer utilities to make your travels less weary and others that have no readily apparent usage, and encounters to face with varying degrees of assurance. Do you use your meager savings to invest in a paper lantern for help with dark and dangerous places, or treat yourself to a teeth-cleaning kit that will help you make better impressions of trustworthiness on people? Do you sell an old bronze axe head you found for some quick and pivotal wealth, or keep it while looking for a handle so you can turn it into a new weapon? Engaged with a saurian beast, do you prefer to keep an eye on its tail in case it whips at you, or do you pay attention to its maw to be sure it won't bite you? Decisions aplenty across all kinds of interactions. Some correct, some incorrect, some pointless either way, and some based on chance.

Ultimately however, a playthrough won't be difficult. It's very easily save-scummed and meta-gamed, especially going through it subsequently. But if you go blindly with the flow, through good and bad, the narrative will immerse you in ways that haven't existed since the MUDs of yore. The characters you share drinks with, the questions you ask each other, and the struggles your journey drags you through will feel like more than just text on a screen.

It takes all the engrossment of a good book and applies it to an interactive medium. And for practically being the price of a decent paperback book? It's hard not to recommend.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is the first game I immediately bought after playing the demo; I'm not disappointed at all! This game is filled with lore and mystery, sprinkled in with side quests. The character's personalities are touching and impactful, even those I don't really like.
The option to choose what you say adds to the experience, and it actually affects the outcome of a situation– either right in that moment, or later down the line. And while the time limit (at least in easy game) is a challenge, it doesn't feel restricting or irritating.
I love the characters in this game, their dialogue, and histories with each other. It feels like a real world I've actually stepped into!
Initially, I thought a text-based game would upset the immersion, but it does the exact opposite. The combinations of art and descriptions really give me something to ground on, while allowing me to imagine the setting even further.

However, I do have a nit-pick with this game, as great as it is.

The journal. While it is such a helpful tool, and keeps track of characters, quests, dialogue, and lore– I would have loved either a section you can write on yourself, or have a more in-depth explanation of quests and events. There are plenty of little side quests, or extra info, that aren’t noted down in the journal, and the only solution is writing down on paper.

And I completely understand why this wasn’t added! There’s more than enough information already given in the beginning, with lore and enemies you’ll encounter in the game; and if everything was added into the journal, it would get full and overwhelming quickly. Not to mention the amount of space it would take up, along with the game itself. And to be frank, if the main issue I have with a game is having to write down extra info– while it may be a hindrance, it doesn't make or break the game (for me, at least).

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGreat RPG, great writing. First text based RPG ive played other than Six Ages and a bunch of degenerate adult RenPy games and I really liked it.

Not a lot of tedium for resource gathering or traveling. Most of my game I spent playing various characters and villages against each other. Your choices matter and I thought the worldbuilding was top notch. Lots of branching paths though halfway through my second playthrough im unsure about how really replayable this is.

Would still highly recommend.\t\t\t\t\t
