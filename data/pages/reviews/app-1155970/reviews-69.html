\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs far as text-based adventures go, this is one of the best I've seen yet. Very enjoyable gameplay, polished storytelling, and good RPG elements including dialog options and choices in several questlines that actually matter down the line.

When starting my first playthrough, I certainly had the feeling of drowning in tasks and sidequests (as is common in nearly all RPG games) which was intimidating with the 40-day timer at first, but as I steadily crossed things off my checklist I started to realize this was a very effective world-building instrument. There's a lot to do, because as you are told in the intro, your job is a demanding and often unforgiving one, and your predecessor has apparently abandoned his post which leaves his prior responsibilities to fall unto you, alongside the existing duties you already have.

As you gain the trust of different characters and communities, and in the process improve your own skills, it is truly satisfying to complete the plethora of duties dropped on your head, one by one. It really immerses you in the role of a roadwarden:
- spending most of your day on the road and taking refuge with an initially small circle of contacts who barely tolerate you,
- forming relationships with characters that eventually share stories and secrets they previously didn't trust you enough to reveal,
- receiving discounts and new amenities as rewards for your services,
- being entrusted with personal requests and private contracts by characters you've proven yourself to,
- and eventually having secrets and plots of your own to hide from others.
You really start to respect and enjoy your occupation, as difficult and dangerous as it is, and this is also reflected in how other characters commend and revere (or even condemn) your efforts too. The sense of accomplishment is notable and certainly satisfying. Props to the developer for capturing this feeling so well.

I was also surprised by the narrative depth of several questlines - the game throws you a few twists and curveballs, and characters treat you differently based on dialog choices, past interactions, and your reputation. I also applaud some welcome QoL features like increasing your skill at certain activities the more you do them, without an explicit ability point/skill tree system - very creative way of subtly adding gameplay depth without adding even more menu screens that could either overwhelm the player, or conversely disappoint them with the relatively small scope of abilities in the game.

The only complaint I have is that at times, I felt my job was a bit too easy (at least on normal difficulty). For example, the central path through the heart of the woods is not nearly as dangerous as all the other characters ominously make it out to be. I was playing a scholar on my first run and I didn't even have to use potions or heals, I was able to handle nearly all the threats with minimal consequence (with a few exceptions) by just using common sense and occasionally some details from my journal, and essentially sleep off all the damage I took. I understand that individual civilians are less prepared than a roadwarden for these threats, but I wasn't even a fighter or mage, I was literally a scholar with modest combat experience. I feel that even a dozen troops (which several settlements could have spared) should have been able to dispatch most threats I encountered. Hardly the "path of blood and death" that I was frequently reminded it would be. That was the only thing that felt slightly immersion-breaking to me, everything else was very reasonable and polished.

As a completionist and achievement hunter, the achievements were all very doable and 100% was reasonable in 2-3 playthroughs, though maybe you could manage it only 1.

Overall, very enjoyable with decent replay value. 8.5/10 \t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWow. What a treasure.

Roadwarden is a robust, text heavy adventure RPG unlike any I can recall having played. The closest recent experience I can compare it to would be Disco Elysium or Citizen Sleeper, though less like Disco primarily for the absence of an isometric UI, favoring instead a fairly simple renpy-esque design.

The premise is fairly simple, and I won't give away too much as the world is a huge part of the beauty of this game (and discovering / exploring / drinking in that world): you are a kind of mixture of an adventurer, guard and mercenary, the titular Roadwarden. You have been sent to an untamed land in the north to fulfil the duties of a Roadwarden, namely to guard the rural portions of the Empire of this world which are too vast to be secured through patrols of the army. This world contains an abundance of dangerous flora and fauna, and while many may have familiar names they are in almost all ways uniquely redesigned in this world.

You move through the world guided by simple static images accompanied by lovingly crafted robust writing that fills your mind with the details and images of this world. Immersive is the word that comes to mind most insistently for me when it comes to the flavor of this game, I truly felt like I was living in this world while I played this game. Choices range from complex to simple, mundane to fantastic, and from guaranteed to chancy (though your true odds are hidden except by discerning the context, randomness indicated only by the presence of an unclarified symbol of a six sided die).

Choices are a driving feature of this game. With the time allotted in a standard session (though you can turn that time limit off) I don't think it's possible to do everything in one play through. That's even more the case because some choices are exclusive: there are a variety of decisions you make about your character at the start, for instance, that heavily influence the nature of your playthrough. Further, almost anytime you meet someone new you can decide the verbal and non-verbal 'tone' of your interaction from five choices, in theory altering the initial character of your relationship with nearly anyone. While many of the 'quests' might be repeated between playthroughs, many have a variety of possible outcomes. If you love to feel like your choices matter, I feel you ought to be very satisfied with this game.

I just finished my first playthrough and it was delightful. I really can't give more glowing praise to it, with only a couple of asterisks:

First, it is text-heavy. Big time. If you aren't a reader, or if the quality of a game's writing isn't enough to make you enjoy a text-heavy experience, you may sadly need to take a pass on this one.

Secondly, and this might be totally me specific but...the name of the studio, Moral Anxiety studios, couldn't be more accurate. Sometimes I don't have the mental fortitude to boot the game up because the immersion is so intense and the burden of so many of the choices so visceral that I just don't have the heart for it. Its a hard world in so many ways, and with things as they are in our own real world, heavy games are not always what I want to boot up. That said, the game is underscored heavily by a funny kind of optimism. The characters feel very believable. There are only a few (honestly, maybe just one) characters I might accuse of being evil; generally, they float around neutral in a way that feels very believable and authentic. Most people, in my experience, aren't good or evil in clear terms - they are just who they are, good and bad. Do with all that what you will.

Long review summed: 9.5 / 10; a treasure for the right player who can look past a writing heavy UI.\t\t\t\t\t
