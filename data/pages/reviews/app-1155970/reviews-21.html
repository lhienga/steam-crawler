\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game has been an absolutely fantastic surprise for me. I thought it looked like a cute little story-based game that I could play on my Steam Deck, in-between matches in other games, etc., but it turned out to be so much more.

The storytelling, atmosphere, world-building, and just overall feel of this game will completely suck you in. I got so hooked I "finished" it straight through, putting in something like 20 hours in 2 days - even playing remotely during downtime at work. Not completely satisfied with my ending and decisions, I immediately jump back in and played through it again. I've now completed it 3 times, and I think I'm finally ready to put it down for awhile: though I'll probably feel the itch to play it some more soon.

Overall the game is just really fun. You definitely get an immense sense that your decisions are important, not just to you as the player, but to the entire world that you are playing in. It feels like a much better (no shade to them, I love them) version of the Telltale games. The simplistic graphics really fit the world and game style perfectly, providing just enough to get a sense of things while allowing the player to fill in the world with their imagination. Like a book, but with just enough assistance to make sure you understand the descriptions (especially with directions) well.

I'm in love with the universe/world of Roadwarden, and can't wait for the next thing from this developer. I'm even considering setting my next DND campaign here. It would be an excellent backdrop for one.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRoadwarden takes a fairly interesting premise and implements a mechanically competent game about it, but is fatally dragged down by wordlbuilding that's in practice fundamentally uninteresting and moderately unpleasant to inhabit.

On a technical level, the game is impressive. If it weren't for the familiar save menu I would have no idea this was derived from a standard RPG engine, and its writing and art are really consistent with their tone and aesthetic. There were only a couple spots where the mechanics got in the way of the game, and I particularly praise Roadwarden for only having a couple examples of the classic RPG problem of selecting a reasonable prompt resulting in an insane course of action - no, when I said to make some space I did not mean you should blindly step back toward the growling you heard behind you.

The only consistently bad mechanic were the bits where it asks you to manually query a text box. These were always a bit frustrating, frequently missed obvious potential inputs, and in a couple places were maddeningly obstructive. For some reason the big spider in front of the bandit camp has one of these, and it wouldn't accept any of the reasonable courses of action I tried - fight, look closer, go around, distract, nothing. No idea what the issue with that one is.

Roadwarden also has a setting with a fairly interesting base premise. Medieval humanity, but the wilds are harsh and aggressive, full of beasts. If any settlement cuts too far into the woods, a tide of monsters will be roused to anger, and everyone gets their torso atomized by unicorns. Neat idea!

The problem is in the execution of this - the world turns out to be basically the same as reality, except there's some magic around and everyone's kind of an [meaniepants]. On the worldbuilding side of things, it's just weird that there's no response to this new aspect. The cities have some form of walls around them, but nobody is trying to develop solutions or defenses to the monsters. There are no specialists who train to kill them, no movement promising to eradicate and tame the woods for good, nobody theorizing a way to mitigate this issue, no real exploration of the smattering of different opinions people have about humanity's relationship to the wilds.

The more fatal problem is the second half - everyone in the game is kind of a shallow jerk. I made it through a ten-hour playthrough without finding a single character I was invested in. Nobody with big dreams, only a couple people that were nice to talk to. People charge a fortune for everything - the important secrets or services seriously require the majority of the cash in the game, you can probably buy like 1.5 of them, maybe two if you buy nothing else.

Beyond selling, nobody's willing to tell you anything? People are so tight-lipped and standoffish it genuinely stretched my suspension of disbelief. Seriously? There's not a single person on this entire peninsula that will tell me what happened to that town, except for the one innkeeper that wants to charge the equivalent of two months of room and board for the info? Nobody mentioned the tribe that lives off to the east and keeps to themselves when I was asking about the major settlements of the area? It feels less like a realistic part of the setting and more like the developer reaching out from behind the scenes to gag the setting because it would be too easy otherwise.

I ran out of time after uncovering what seems to be the main mysteries of the game, and just went home because there wasn't really a financial path forward and I didn't care about these people. Got a postgame report that the merchants guild decided not to expand north, every faction either died under a horde of undead or barely held on, and I felt nothing.

I went to start another run, to see if I could figure out the grand mysteries of my predecessor on the secret island or awakening the spirit of the forest to cure a plague or figuring out what the deal with those bandits was or learning what happened to that town... but while I was selecting the difficulty for it I just stopped and took stock.

Why bother? I don't actually care about any of it, really. I'm not invested, in any of these people or places or groups. Hell I'm not even invested in Leto's wellbeing, the game really feels like it should be a roguelike but the one time I died (killed from full health and armor in one hit without any real recourse, thanks for that by the way) the game just lets you restart that one encounter and continue on your way. The game claims to be about razor's-edge survival in the savage wilderness but the moment actual stakes rear their head it automatically savescums for you. The only real failure state is the time limit - the game would actually be much more engaging if it was as lethal as it claimed to be and reset the run when you died, letting you get a little better each time as you gradually uncover the mysteries of the peninsula and work toward a successful run.

There's a critical lack of dynamism in the world here. The only group that wants to change things are the merchants that sent you north to scout, and you only talk to them, briefly, once. No people or factions with great dreams or aspirations, nobody that even wants to get along. I mean, get this - for most details about the civilization you come from to the South, y'know the only source of change in this setting, when you're asked about them you have a full range of options to essentially make up that bit of the setting on the spot. When the most important aspect of your setting is so nothing that its details and relationships to other elements can accommodate a full range of different and even contradictory options with no impact on the broader setting, that's... I don't even know what that is. Not a sign of interesting worldbuilding, that's for sure.

Why would I want to keep playing a game that's just like real life but worse? Humans aren't like this in real life, the universal and inexplicably [meaniepantsness] is a bigger obstacle than the monsters, and there's no aspect of the game that makes me curious or interested. For a fantasy game, I can't think of a more fundamental condemnation.\t\t\t\t\t
