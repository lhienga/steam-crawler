\r\n\t\t\t\t\t"We have nae to give and do ne wish to trade with outsiders."

Roadwarden presents itself rather straightforwardly. You are a roadwarden, a person hired to manage roads in an isolated peninsula, a stranger to the people who inhabit it and as such, are very often rebuked by the townspeople when you barge into their homes and begin asking questions. You will not have the trust of people you speak to initially and it takes time, work and much conversation for people to open up to you. The game also lets you starkly know that you will return back to the city of Hovlavan within 40 days and use your time carefully. However, there is a great deal to explore, many past events and relationships to discover and the fate of the peninsula to decide.

The game is engaging and doesn't hold your hand, forcing you to choose what you wish to accomplish within 40 days. It constantly remind you that you are only an outsider and you won't have your way in a grand fashion as you have with many other RPG games, and are just the messenger before the flood gates open. There are a few complaints I have; Old Pagos and the white marshes really felt underwhelming in terms of the interactions you can have and the number of villagers present to interact with. There are some other choices which could have been fleshed out far more, namely magic feeling very underwhelming and reliant on others, something that could have been explored was the mage directly curing the people of Old Pagos at some self sacrifice (More cost requirement or a permanent debuff), scholars not being able to gather more information from surroundings and of course the lack of content with the green mountain tribe post Asterion discovery . However, overall, this game is a gem and I recommend it for people who enjoy text based adventures relying on imagination. I do hope for a DLC to expand on the peninsula or even a second game where the player can play a more centred role and interact far more deeply with characters.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPoignant exploration of medieval fantasy through the lense of nature. A perilous world is presented to the player, nonetheless capable of being understood, and if not tamed, at least respected.

The text goes to great lengths to contextualize the locations, and most text is descriptive of locations or situations. The colorful characters are not far behind, with each having deep connections to their surroundings. While the actions of some of these could at first present as actively nefarious, they each have an internal logic which, if prodded carefully, unfolds to the player. It is this sense of deep humanity that I most like about the game, where religion, custom and location deeply inform the way of being of the characters.

I wanted to also point out that, while always on the background, this game doesn't participate in the usual sexism that medieval fantasy often borrows from our world, and you will easily find that the most interesting characters will be female.

I don't want to talk much about the lategame, but this also contains a masterclass on how to do a bossfight in a game all centered around exploration and understanding your environment.

So yeah, play it if you enjoy walls of text, RPGs and imaginative, sometimes gritty, medieval fantasy!\t\t\t\t\t
