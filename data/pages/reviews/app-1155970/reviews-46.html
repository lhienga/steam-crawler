It cares about the details; how you set up camp that night, how you clean off after your fight in the mud, and where you tie up your horse. It takes place in a fantasy world that feels grounded and well researched, with little details like describing the break medieval Europeans would take in the middle of their sleep, or how dangerous soft surfaces are for horses to walk over. The rhythm of taking care of your resources and getting home before dark is really fun if you like travel management. The characters and quests are compelling, the art and music is gorgeous and evocative, and it feels like playing an incredibly grounded game of solo D&D. Highly highly recommend if you like rpgs.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI finally decided to write a review for the game after full achievement completion and three thorough runs of the game. In general, Roadwarden is a great piece of visual novel style RPG game. While the story is very well written, a tremendous amount of freedom is given during the exploration. The stylish writing along with cleverly designed visuals and musics adds up to this masterpiece as a result.

The major pros that makes the game stand out is the writing. The overall atmosphere created by the story makes you forget about time. The plot twists and the depth of the story makes the journey quite dramatic. During my first and even second playthrough, I kept trying to piece every evidence I know about each characters together to figure out some truth. The fact that not every potential goal of the game is reviewed at the beginning makes the experience even more exciting. Most of the characters are very well developed which could greatly affect your decision with them depending on your own personality. When playing without any spoilers, I assure you it would be a well-deserved mysterious journey for you.

There are many creative design in this game. One of which I appreciate the most is text-entry options. In this game there are many choices involving a custom input, which significantly broadens your pool of choices. Although such design sometimes leave you clueless if you missed some of those dialogues, it was quite novel and I appreciate the creativeness behind it. The RNG system is also well designed to a point that re-roll was not necessary at all. If you wish to have a undisturbed role-playing experience, this game is perfect for you.

The amount of content in this game is quite impressive, as there are secrets that my first two completed runs did not uncover. As I start to hunt for different achievements, I found most of them natural to be an achievement, as they involve major decisions throughout the game. Spoilers are not necessarily needed for any secret in the game to be unlocked. In fact, uncovering those mysteries can be a great joy playing this game.

There are also criticism I have about the game. For example, despite having an extremely high degree of freedom, there are moments where I felt "wait... that's all I can do?" during my playthrough. There are certain characters that I wish my character can have a deeper relationship with, and I believe I'm not the only one thinking that way. Some of the important hinty dialogues are not recorded after you trigger them, so if you eventually accumulated a long archive, they will be lost. (I have a specific moment where I could not remind myself about a keyword while knowing I have heard someone mentioned it to me) And lastly, many of the decisions are quite forgiving, I was expecting a much darker journey when I first started mine. Nevertheless, I personally don't think these drawbacks hindered the game to be a good one.

Overall, I would recommend this game to anyone who likes story-rich RPG. It did really well in terms of its story telling, character development and roleplay experience. The visuals and musics only enhanced the already good experience playing the game. The length of the game is worth the penny, you are paying for much more than a well written novel.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRandom recommendation - Do you think there's something more "Witcher" than the Witcher? After getting totally absorbed for a bit in the world of Roadwarden I can safely say: Yes.
Roadwarden is a funky mix of half book, half game. Most of the game is in the text and getting used to reading instead of whacking a monster with a sword and imagining stuff instead of getting AAA graphics rending a murderous unicorn is a paradigm shift. But if you are patient and "Just go with it" you'll find a world that's like tolkien, but grounded and very focused if you've eaten recently, bathed and if you've kept your armor intact.

Here's some fun stuff I've done:
- Established pagan authority over the industrial town. Nature rulz.
- Negotiated with necromancers to effectively "quit that ♥♥♥♥" - they're actually pretty progressive once you get over the introductions.
- Duelled a giant duck with teeth: I freaking love just getting the bare boned explanation of what a monster looks like and then "Oh its a horse sized duck with teeth and carnivorous." There's also a unicorn, but unlike a kids tv show, these ones are extremely territorial and really want to murder you.
- Got mauled half to death by hostile apes, hard lesson to not have quarrels for my crossbow...
- Cleaned up after the previous roadwarden who, SCREWED THE POOCH ON THIS ONE JESUS HOW ARE YOU THIS TRASH AT KEEPING THE ROADS SAFE ASTERION YOU HAD ONE JOB
- Got through everything quite ok despite my first roadwarden being illiterate.
- Convinced a bandit camp to stop by saying "I'm telling on you and mom if you dont stop". Then I got mugged and left naked in the heart of the forest.
- I really invested a lot in personal hygiene.
- Got friendly with the droods (druids) and rode my horse through some goblins. Eat boot knave!
Soundtrack is hauntingly melancholic and the world is just well written and I can't remember last time I got absorbed in a fantasy world that has left me empty as I got done playing it. Probably after "Planescape Torment"or "Neverwinter Nights Hordes of the underdark. "

All made by one person, well done Moral Anxiety studio, you're one of the real ones. https://twitter.com/MoralAnxiety\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRoadwarden is one of those games which I like to call "games I like to read". Do you like reading?
Specifically, do you like reading prose that is very detailed and well-researched? Prose that, unlike most visual novels, isn't just composed of dialogues and monologues, but provides many interesting worldbuilding details through means of a narrator? Do you want it to immerse you in a quite down to earth, believable fantasy world that's a mix of historical facts and fiction? Or rather a small excerpt of it - a peninsula in Dragonwoods, some of it rustic, populated by common folk rather than kings and nobles, most of it an untamed wilderness. It is a harsh place, yet strangely tranquil, its mood rather sombre but not cynical, its characters human, flawed, yet many of them decent people just trying to carve their path through life to the best of their abilities. It is rarely clear which of those paths is the right one.
That's what you'll find in Roadwarden.
Dragonwoods is a solace if you find the incessantly bombastic narratives of 'a Powerful Honorable Badass Chosen One single-handedly saving the (hapless) world from Ultimate Destruction at the hands of Absolute Evil' exhausting. If you want to focus on smaller, more grounded and intimate slice-of-life stories for a change. To play a tiny part in a much larger world. Where you take pleasure in small everyday things, like a meal shared, a friendship forged, work completed.

But perhaps what you'd like even more is venturing into the unknown, the joy of discovery, observing as your map gradually fills up with places that become more and more familiar? Perhaps you dream of taming the wilderness, getting rid of beasts (violently - or through trickery), clearing up the paths, filling it up with traps to catch fish to sell or eat? Perhaps you like helping people while earning some coin in the process - to purchase better equipment or for your friend back home? Perhaps you want to see people get to trust you more and more? Maybe what you want to do is carve out your own little place in this new, hostile world?
That's a crucial element of the Roadwarden experience as well.

But how does it play, you ask?
Well, do you like computer role-playing games? Do you cherish the freedom of doing things in whichever order you like, an order that subtly influences how the interconnected events play out? And if you ever played old-school isometric cRPGs like Fallout - perhaps you fondly remember the way they ended, how the game summarised the future of the places and lives you encountered on your adventure, the future strongly influenced by your actions during the playthrough? But at the same time... perhaps you are getting tired of complicated combat systems constantly interrupting the flow of the story and, worse, creating a ludonarrative dissonance where your noble sympathetic hero is at the same time a bloodthirsty, and somehow invincible, mass murderer with a body count in the thousands? Perhaps you are annoyed by time wasted on repetitive animations, most notably of walking, and wish more and more for the immediacy of "I click it and it happens"? And all the grind, oh, the grind...
And maybe you find some solace in the visual novel genre for your story-centric needs but are starting to find their experience a bit too passive? Maybe you wish they had more interactivity, after all? Perhaps you asked yourself: what if they experimented with so-called *RPG systems* a bit? Like simple but impactful inventory and resource management? And dice rolls maybe? We all hate RNJesus, yet we miss Him so when He's gone...
Roadwarden is an answer to all those wishes. Even more than that, it innovates.
Speaking of resource management, for example, are you a bit bored of the concept of "health + stamina + mana"? Have you wondered if the game could keep track of something else? Maybe... how clean you are? Or the quality of the clothes you're wearing?
Yes, Roadwarden does make you challenge the stereotype of a "dirty smelly dumb peasant".
Also, are you unafraid of the fact that you can't do everything and instead cherish that your choices make for a unique playthrough that will be markedly different from your next one? More specifically, does the notion of a progression of in-game time* fill you with dread, or do you rather see it as a world-building tool, as an incentive to strategise and carefully plan your in-game days and, again, something that will make one playthrough different from another?
Indeed, Roadwarden is maybe the first time I felt that day-night cycle is not just a gimmick. A roadwarden's life is bound by the rhythm of seasons just like anyone's, peasant's or king's. By the time you finish the game you'll be older - your character too. The weather will be colder, days shorter. You will have to say your goodbyes.
Lastly - we all know the good old trick of "choose one option our of four", but are you open to less conventional means of interaction - such as puzzling out what word to type? Yes, having to come up with a word in your head, not be given one by the game. I found it quite refreshing, myself. Though it might be an issue if you're not confident about your English vocabulary. But don't worry, it's not too hard, the game's quite reasonable in its demands and always offers other means of progress. It only comes up in a few places.

Finally, the presentation. Does the idea of sepia-toned pixel art that depicts only places but leaves people to your imagination appeal to you? Do you find that when the image is revealed gradually with each step of exploration it stimulates your explorer's curiosity? Would a combination of melancholic guitar music and the sound effects of nature be exactly what you need to immerse yourself in that world?
It is all "yes" for me, and the OST should be a fine addition to my bandcamp collection. For autumn walks. After some time passes - I have the tunes drilled into my head by now.

Have all these questions helped you decide if Roadwarden could be for you a bit? Myself, I will forever remember the Dragonwoods' peninsula as a place I've visited, got to know intimately, make my home, for a little while. Where I've befriended, or antagonised, various people, like Iason, Foggy, Eudocia, Dalit or Thyrsus. As much as it mostly happened in my imagination, there remains a reality to that. And that's the thing I say about all the best stories.

*Although there is a time limit on normal difficulty and up, there's also a non-limited difficulty option too, but to me the time mechanic was an essential component of the game's unique character. Without it, it wouldn't have been the same.\t\t\t\t\t

If you like: interwoven narrative, text-based gameplay, or indy developers, then you'll enjoy this one.\t\t\t\t\t
