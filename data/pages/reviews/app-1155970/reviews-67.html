\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo I finished my first playthrough after 25 hours. Here are some assorted thoughts:

- Roadwarden is very strongly themed. Fans of The Witcher books will notice how well it encapsulates their core themes — arguably better than the Witcher games themselves. The lone wanderer whose aim is ostensibly humble monster-slaying, who somehow always gets dragged into intrigue way above his pay grade? A morally gray world, where being 'right' is impossible? The humans often being worse than the monsters? Come on.

- Difficulty in this game is very cleverly implemented, and adds to the general atmosphere. You want to help people, but you're constantly dirty and bloodied, so villagers avoid talking to you. You want that cool crossbow, but you're perennially broke, spending your last few coins on renting a room at the inn so you can have a good night's rest. It's very immersive, and really makes you feel like the titular lone ranger.

- Immersion, and a sense of connection to your character, is what Roadwarden does best. My warden was a disillusioned atheist city dweller who just wanted to help everyone, but more often than not his lofty promises fell short; desperate times sometimes forced him to sell the precious parcels people entrusted him with.

- I'm very torn on what difficulty to recommend playing this on. Standard, with its 40-day limit, does add a sense of urgency to the proceedings; but it ultimately comes down to whether you find being unable to investigate several major plot threads by the end of your playthrough immersion-building or frustrating.

- Roadwarden really proves that giving the player a sense of 'place' is divorced from graphical fidelity. With nothing but words and static pixel art, it builds a world that's so vivid that I still remember where every single location is weeks after finishing the game. You'll grow so familiar with the circuitous roads of the North — circumambulating the peninsula dozens of times to deliver this or that — that the image of the map will be burned into your brain.

- Depth oozes out of every system in Roadwarden. Money, for example: every vendor is interested in different things, and offers different prices for commodities. Learning this is tied to your understanding of the world itself. As you learn of how a particular land-locked village is boycotted by the rest of the peninsula, you'll figure out that metal is highly valued among its traders. A coastal village won't be interested in fish, and an inn occupied by hunters won't care for your monster trophies.

- The thing I love the most about Roadwarden is how information is power. It's hard to explain this without mild spoilers, but it's genuinely delightful just how the game is designed to reward curiosity in this way. Ask people about everything and everywhere, it'll help you!

Roadwarden is one of the best games I've ever read, and one of the best fantasy novels I've ever played. I bought it twice to support the dev, and I continue to recommend it to everyone I know. Get this game already! <3
