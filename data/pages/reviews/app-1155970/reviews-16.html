\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's a decent game. I respect what it's doing but it didn't grab me, so sadly that's a thumbs down from me. You may like it, try the demo. I think it showcases the game well.

It's a Choose Your Own Adventure game in kind of an open world. Gameplay mechanics are basic, you juggle resources like health, nutrition, armor weariness, etc. Resource management is a background for exploration, some light puzzle solving, and interactions with people and the environment. There's roleplaying, you can define your character in a variety of ways, but no stat crunching, no XP gains, and no skillpoint allocation. The game is mostly text and has a beautiful soundtrack, there are some reductive graphics but they mostly function as maps. In a few hours of play I didn't see any graphical depictions of characters, and in general, the game is designed with the text in mind, images are there mostly for convenience.

It may look like I focus on details and that's cause really there isn't anything else about the game, it's much more about reading descriptions than about making meaningful decisions. You can easily starve and die, but it's not because you mismanaged gameplay systems but rather followed the wrong path. The closest comparison I can think of is a game called Age of Decadence, but that game was less consistent in the design in writing and tried to have too much stuff in it with none of it working, while Roadwarden doesn't bother with vestigial RPG mechanics or allowing you to click on the map to move your character. It focuses on the story and the story didn't grab me as much. It's fine, I can name very few games that you should play for the sake of story, I don't care for story and writing in most of the games I play, but here there isn't anything except for the story and writing, just a tiny bit of immersion from the soundtrack which is good but not good enough to keep me playing.

It's a kind of high fantasy setting that might as well have been a historical adventure if the author hadn't wisely decided they wouldn't spend years on research to make it authentic. It is down to earth, so to say, like Gothic or Witcher worlds, as in people are portrayed in a realist style, with very mundane interests and goals. The societies of the game are small and simple, there's no talk about nations or kings or guilds, and the hero travels over mostly wild lands with a few villages. It's decently written and I can't say it's bad, it's just that kind of story bores me personally. If you like the sound of it then I won't spoil anything, just go try it, it's a decent game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTLDR: Decent gameplay, excellent story, excellent artwork, excellent music
Rating: 8.5/10

When the store page described Roadwarden as a “text-based” RPG with elements from visual novels, it sure wasn’t kidding. Pages on pages of dialogue and descriptions paint a deep and richly detailed game world, one where fantasy and politics clash on the far flung frontiers of a storied civilization. Both artwork and music are used to set the atmosphere to great effect, the latter especially so when ratcheting up the tension during tense scenes that otherwise would fall flat in its purely text form. Still, Roadwarden’s visual novel-esque gameplay brings with it its own flaws that, unfortunately, do no favors to the path the game sets ahead of the player.

I’ll admit that my general dislike of visual novels does color my view regarding Roadwarden’s text-based design. Frankly, this gameplay is well suited for the sort of diplomacy and political maneuvering present throughout parts of the game. Had this RPG been some fully 3D affair or a more traditional pixel RPG, I’d imagine scenes like Thais pulling you aside for gossip on White Marshes and you confronting Thais or Orientius would be less impactful with the two character models or sprites dumbly staring at each other. Politicking is hardly an action-packed affair (until someone flips a table and starts brawling), so I can appreciate how Roadwarden’s text-based gameplay works hand-in-hand with the story. Combat, however, is a different story and one that really didn’t capture me during the times the player character needed to fight. The ease of save scumming really doesn’t do any favors in both combat and diplomacy either. Unfortunately, all of these problems are inherently present in text-based gameplay and I’m at a loss as to how Roadwarden could have resolved them without sacrificing the atmosphere and story it crafts.

Regardless, if the idea of reading a novel’s worth of dialogue and descriptions does not put you off immediately, Roadwarden is a wonderful game with an enchanting story and lore. From the varied factions vying over the nameless peninsula all the way down to the little quirks in linguistics, this game is definitely something worth immersing yourself in.\t\t\t\t\t
