\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe experience has been rather mixed.
Sherlock Holmes The Awakened from 2007 was the first Sherlock Holmes game I got to play.
Accordingly, I was excited about the remake.
However, the game couldn't quite live up to the anticipation, which of course can always be difficult.

Graphically, everything is top notch, and the game mostly sticks to the original storyline. Somehow, however, the atmosphere was not quite coherent, or rather present. Especially this game should be particularly eerie. It didn't manage that. Also, the landscape was too darkly lit in parts. Without a proper map with movement markers, it was sometimes difficult to find your way around.

Even though this part was supposed to have more of a creepy touch, the original game still had some humor in the right places. That is missing here, overall the game takes itself too seriously.

The dynamic between Sherlock and Watson also develops rather slowly and also takes a somewhat unexpected turn towards the end. But that was an interesting twist compared to the rest.

They took the game mechanics from the previous game and then simplified them again after all. As a result, there was just something missing from the gameplay.

I've played pretty much every Sherlock Holmes game from Frogwares so far.
This one was not their best. Still, it was solid and surely there will be more games to come in the next years.
Things will certainly pick up again.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHighly recommend if you liked the original!!

I love the Sherlock Holmes games. They basically got me through Lockdown, and of the original games, The Awakened was by far my favourite. Finding out that Frogware was doing a remake of the game was like Christmas for me, and I bought it as soon as funds would allow,. But only after I finally got around to playing Chapter One (Side note, LOVED THAT GAME).

This game is an interesting blend of new and old game play styles. I missed some of the new mechanics introduced in CO (disguises, much more humour, open world, mostly Jon's presence and notes tbh). None of these would make sense in the game's structure (or tone. I miss the silliness of CO) which is understandable, as these parameters were laid out long, long ago in the original game and the story is told very straight.

As a remake, it was incredible. It was fantastic getting to venture back see familiar faces and walk remembered territories with current generation graphics. Edelweiss in particular felt so very familiar and it was eerie walking its halls again. I may be a bit miffed that they cut out Baby Poirot's cameo in Switzerland, but I can live with that. The characters were much more fleshed out, and I felt I had a much better grip on the story this second time around. And that final confrontation hit a lot harder!!

I am loving this new take on Holmes and Watson, even if I do miss the dynamic they had in the last game. I'm looking forward to any future instalments in this new timeline, though I do hope they tend more towards CO's open world exploration rather than TA's linear world bubbles.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is strange, because I enjoyed my time with it; however it wasn't what I wanted out of a Sherlock Holmes game.

First of all if you are looking for a game that is like Chapter One, this isn't it. The main reason this isn't the case is because unlike Chapter One, this game revolves around a singular case. In Chapter One you would figure out unique mysteries whose outcomes allowed you to get more details about your past.

This game does not follow that formula. Instead of solving other cases to get you closer to your objective, you are travelling around the world looking for clues that tie into this one mystery. The game play becomes very repetitive and tiresome quite quickly, since there's no satisfying conclusion to when you leave the area which just makes a majority of the game feel like filler.

You don't work with the police, you don't help a victims family get closure, you don't get a bad person locked up. Instead all you do is chase this big boogeyman who turns out to be just some insane dude, WHO DOESN'T EVEN GET PUNISHED FOR HIS CRIMES. The whole way this game plays out, makes me feel like this was supposed to be DLC for Chapter One, but some executive forced them to drag it out for 10 hours and sell it for 40

Moving on from the story, this game just outright removes some really cool features compared to Chapter One, one of these being disguises. You can't disguise yourself as another person, even though there are times in this game where you LITERALLY DO THAT. Instead of finding the target and recreating the perfect disguise to pretend to be them, you just grab their hat and say "Hi, I'm this person"; yet they still have the option in the game to change how Sherlock Holmes looks.

Now, I understand that it's a miracle this game is even here. Since, from what I know, a majority of the team was affected by the war in Ukraine. I am sorry that the team has to deal with that, and honestly it's amazing how dedicated the team was to getting this completed. However I just think this game was doomed from the start, it actively goes against everything that people loved the first game for and because of that; this game feels hollow.

I know this team can create a brilliant Sherlock Holmes game, they've done it MULTIPLE TIMES already. They are hands down one of the reasons I love the character, I adored Sherlock Holmes crimes and punishment, as well as Chapter One; I guess that's why I'm just really passionately disappointed by what this turned out to be.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a remake of the original, and so a lot of what this game does is takes the original formula and combines it with new mechanics from more recent Frogwares games. So I found the addition of the mind palace and investigation scenes quite fun, and found the story to remain compelling and fairly true to the original game. Though I was sorely sorrowed they did not bring over the chemistry puzzles from Chapter One that I found immensely enjoyable despite them being very fitting in multiple places for this title.

The updated UI and mechanics make this game a breeze. Almost too easy, actually. There really isn't any challenge or danger here, you can fail as many times as you want, and you're not likely to get stuck on anything for very long.

My cons for the game are the lack of puzzles and any form of difficulty or challenge. If a puzzle based mystery game that requires some effort is what you'd like you'll prefer the original version over this one. Despite it's dated appearance and the difficulties which arise from the earlier games of the genre as a whole; the original still offers a unique experience that may be preferential to you over the updated one with the same story.

All and all, I would recommend this game on a sale. The mix of the old style of the game without any of the challenge or puzzles, and the new style of Frogwares games without any of the extra footwork and details really leaves the game being an experience that is compact and simple, making it one of Frogwares less idealistic titles. Due to the stress the studio had to undergo during the duration of development on this title I am taking the game at face value and would still recommend it, though I am disappointed to not see the same Frogwares fervor I am used to from this release.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t100%. SPOILER FREE Sherlock Holmes: The Awakened

OVERALL RATING: 8.6/10

LENGTH: 6-10 hours

100% ACHIEVEMENT EASE: 1/10

WORTH THE PRICE?: I would pay full price for this game again.

OVERALL: Sherlock Holmes the Awakened is a Lovecraftian detective game based around Sherlock Holmes and Dr. Watson. This is a fantastic game to get your feet wet in the Lovecraft genre, as it is not completely overkill, but there are major portions that are based around Lovecraft lore. This game differs from chapter one as it mainly follows one major case and does not have bunches of side quests. Also, this game is NOT an open world experience like Chapter One. The game is set after Chapter One, with several references to that throughout the game. There were also several references to Frogwares other Lovecraftian Detective game The Sinking City. (Which if youve played that and enjoyed it, you will enjoy this. This is a shorter game, but a positive for the achievement hunters is that you can get them all in one playthrough. There are also only a few achievements that are even missable, as most come with progression through the case and chapters.

PROS: Mind Palace, and the Archives have been greatly improved in this game. I was one who enjoyed going to the archives as I thought it felt more authentic, but we can all agree that got old too. The archives are available in the menu. Mind palace has some major changes, which in my opinion make it more logical to follow, and more than just a click until things match type of mechanic. The Lovecraftian lore is deep in the game, but the actual scenes are few and far between (maybe once a chapter), so it stays fairly authentic for a newer player to the genre. I had no bugs, glitches, or crashes throughout my playthrough, as well as no control complaints on M+K. I also want to add on and reiterate that it is a good intro to the lovecraft genre, as it isn't super creepy at all, and its not overkill with the lovecraft.

CONS: Truly very few cons come to mind playing this game. I was a bit upset at how short the game is, as I had some struggles with a few cases and it still only took me 8.5 hours for 100% completion. Sometimes conversation with Watson is pretty awkward, and he is just kind of in the way. Also, by the end of the game I was able to predict when certain events were going to happen just based on how he was acting. Some of the cutscenes were kindof cheesy, but that was very few and far between.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is a treat for Holmes and Lovecraft fans alike. There are lots of subtle nods to lore from both universes. I can't speak for their other Holmes games, but the Sherlock in this Frogwares product is the closest to the original Holmes that I've encountered in any other media. Logical and abrasive, but still retaining a sense of humor and genuinely cares for others, especially Watson.

The graphics are beautiful. Every new locale is completely different, no reusing textures or maps. Highly impressive, given the small studio. NPCs travel around the area and are distinct and interesting.

Sound and music are likewise excellent. The muggy ambiance of the swamp, the disturbing noises in the asylum, and the sinister music are all very atmospheric.

Gameplay is straightforward, presenting a challenge at times, but overall very enjoyable. You do need to use your brain while sorting out puzzles, which is nice.

My only complaints are the length of the game (I finished it in about 10 hours, and I wish there was more), and one very particular scene at the end. You have to press keys very rapidly in a very precise manner or it's game over. On the controller I suspect it would be easier, but on the PC it was a bit of a nightmare.

Bravo, Frogwares. A wonderful game.\t\t\t\t\t

Elementary, my dear Watson except this time its not.

Sherlock Holmes The Awakened is a remake of an older game going by the same title made by the same studio, Frogwares. At first, my decision to purchase this game at full price was made more out of willingness to support the Ukrainian developers, but after playing through this wonderfully dreadful journey, I think that it was money well spent. I havent played detective games before, but this one caught my eye since it featured a dark Lovecraftian mystery, creepy worlds and the famous crime-solving duo. If you love Sherlock Holmes and horror, this delicious mix is something you cant miss!

Plot
I suppose Holmes and Watson are in no need of an introduction the genius detective in the first flush of his career and his faithful sidekick, an ex-war doctor, residing in the 221B Baker Street and untangling webs of mysteries together. One case, starting as a random kidnapping, soon evolves into something larger, like one quivering string of a criminal network run by religious fanatics, involving mental asylums, cursed rituals, ancient gods and bloody sacrifices as well as flashes of the ominous Lovecraftian world. For the consistency of the plot, it is recommended that you check out the previous game (Sherlock Holmes Chapter One) where our young detectives past is unveiled, so youll make the most of your time with this adventure and grasp the references embedded in it.

The blooming start of Watson and Holmes relationship can be seen in how they interact with one another. Those fleeting moments of togetherness build the foundation of their future and are the utmost delight to watch, especially when Watson sheds light on his time in Afghanistan or consoles Holmes during his episodes of madness. With some silly humor thrown in for good measure, this game isnt really as desperately dark as it seems at a glance, and its amazing that way. With that being said, there are still disturbingly terrifying elements incorporated into the lighter side of the narrative, but it all blends together perfectly, and the ending is satisfying, though a little hasty as everyone keeps saying. But I wont lie to you; the cut scenes along with the transitions are sometimes so poorly executed that they're bordering on cringey

Gameplay
In spite of there being quite a few mechanics to learn at the beginning, once you get a hang of them, the game will start flowing smoothly. Sherlocks intellectual prowess is conveyed through his capability of accessing his mind palace to piece together clues, making close observations of the environment (shown as highlighted objects) and also pulling people apart with glances by choosing the correct character portrait. Sherlock can also ask for help, engage in a few quick time events and access the archives to aid his research, so you have some variety there. There is some trial and error game play, but nothing too frustrating its mostly about presenting the right piece of evidence to a certain person or constructing a logical chain of thought. The good thing for those who enjoy a more relaxing experience is that you cant actually fail or make any big mistakes (and if you do, you get to try again like nothing happened). The Awakened truly holds a gamers hand more in that regard, therefore there are little to no consequences in any of the actions you take.

There are improvements made from the first game, such as the lack of clunky combat or eavesdropping. My only serious complaint about the game is that, despite the beautifully crafted world reflecting the gloomy late 1800s, it lacks content. I dont mean linearity or small maps, for it feels easier if youre just navigating from one level to another instead of a confusing open world like in the first one, but rather the soullessness of the locations and the sheer amount of non-interactive objects. What might make the world of dead NPCs feel more alive are the side quests which I highly recommend you to buy as a DLC, otherwise youll just walk around pretty yet empty levels in search of that one clue needed to move the main story forward.

Verdict
Although a bit overpriced, you can decide if you want to support the studio behind the game or simply buy it on sale. Considering the circumstances under which the development took place, I say the end product is quite decent, an improvement from the first episode.
