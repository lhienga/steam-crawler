\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNormal Game Stuff

The environments are really nice, lighting and graphical fidelity overall is awesome with Unreal Engine 5 goodness, and the sounds are really good. You can feel the London gloom and the Louisiana heat. Stuff like footsteps on various types of terrain and the sounds of opening and closing doors are some little details that really increase the realism and immersiveness. High level of polish on everything.

Game wise, initially my main point of comparison was Call of Cthulhu - I now feel that’s a bit unfair at least from a gameplay perspective. CoC is an RPG. The Awakened isn’t. It’s a detective game, so pretty much all the gameplay is puzzle solving, mostly piecing together clues to figure out what happened at a scene. There’s never any time pressure apart from a few isolated events.

The game is also completely linear. There are dialogue choices you can make, but they don’t affect anything in more than a cursory way. There’s only one ending.

Horror

I consider myself a coward when it comes to horror, and this game is fine. There are a few semi-jumpscares (and they’re delightful), but not many, and most of the “horror” is the analytical discovery of what happened at a crime scene. I actually wish the game had more horror content to make the threat feel more palpable.

Mythos Treatment

This is the stuff that really matters to me. CoC had jank, but I liked how it treated the Mythos, so that’s the bar to beat.

To me, there’s 2 crucial elements to this kind of Lovecraftian tale: the slow buildup of dread truth, and the protagonist’s inevitable descent into madness (i.e. their reactions to the truth being revealed).

The Awakened unfortunately falls over a bit here. Where CoC got freaky not too far in and then kept ramping up, the Awakened layers on the Mythos to hook you in at the start, pulls back through the middle, then ramps it up again for the end. I was particularly disappointed at the middle section, where human horror is the focus. I don’t want the Witcher - humans are not the real monsters. If you’re telling a Lovecraftian tale, sure, humans are monsters, but the real monsters are the monsters. This is the Awakened’s biggest weakness - we never directly, incontrovertibly see a Mythos creature. We don’t see a Deep One, and certainly not Cthulhu. We never get confirmation that the Mythos is real. There’s definitely brain-bendy stuff going on that speaks to Sherlock’s mental deterioration (in particular there’s 2 actual scares in the first half that have stuck with me), but there’s always plausible deniability.

Sherlock’s reactions to stuff are mostly good - like I said, the voice actor for Sherlock does a really good job of someone supremely confident in their mental faculties struggling when those faculties suggest things that don’t make any sense. This is particularly true in the final confrontation with the big bad. I love the climaxes when there’s the dialogue between the insane, zealous antagonist and the protagonist who desperately wants to deny the truth but can’t. The moment when it all becomes too much, and the protagonist’s hold on traditional reality breaks and they finally give in to the awful truth of the universe - those are the moments that I love the most about these tales, and despite the lacklustre buildup, the Awakened sticks the landing really well.

Conclusion

I would wait until this game is on sale to play it. It’s too expensive unless you’re craving this specific thing. It’s consistently middling with a very good climax.

If you’re craving a Mythos hit, play Call of Cthulhu - it’ll be way cheaper for one thing. It has lower lows (older game jank, and prolonged tedious stealth sequences) but also higher highs (getting violently dismembered by a Dimensional Shambler, actually summoning Cthulhu), and you’ll get to see way more actual Mythos stuff and feel more scared of it. It has skill points and skill checks. It has multiple endings. It has a R’lyehian language. It has a sanity meter. God I love the sanity meter in that game. I know it’s a reductive and inaccurate depiction of mental health but I don’t care, it’s amazing. It starts full and never ever increases, it only ever goes down. Some dialogue options are locked if your sanity is too high. The descriptions of your character’s slowly deteriorating mental state are freaking great. If you want this, play CoC.

Anyway, that’s the end of my review. Hope that was helpful.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI feel awful leaving a mostly negative review because I love this series and I know that the devs had a difficult time trying to get this game out while their country was being invaded but I don’t think it’s as good as it could be.

For starters, IMO the best two games in this series are Crimes and Punishments and The Testament. Both get at the heart of Sherlock Holmes mysteries and really demonstrate the game play one would expect. The Devils Daughter was so bad I almost didn’t play Chapter One. And while I thought Chapter One was a little sad, I enjoyed it so I was excited to see what they would do with The Awakened remaster.

Now this remaster looks great and I like that it references Chapter One but that’s about it. It’s too short (which I get because of the devs having to deal with real life issues) and linear which can make the side cases added to the main story difficult to complete because you can’t go backward. The mini map is difficult to use because there’s no pointer to show where you are on the map. And I know the devs like horror games, but I think this leaned to heavy into that direction while forgetting how to tell a true Sherlock Holmes story.

And the thing I really didn’t appreciate the most was the ending. Sherlock is quirky but he’s not insane. I think the devs are leaning too hard into exploring what makes Sherlock tick but have gone off the deep end. I really hope that if they continue this incarnation of Sherlock, they pull back a bit on that. Or I’m really hoping they go back to what they did for C&P and the Testament.

I think next time I’ll wait to preorder the game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSpoiler warning

This game is about half as long as Chapter One was, even if you do every optional case. That by itself is disappointing, but the story is also really lackluster.

A Lovecraftian story needs to have unexplainable cosmic horror. A Sherlock Holmes story, meanwhile, need to have every mystery explained in impressive detail. So a Lovecraft story where Holmes is the protagonist can't have both; it has to pick one. Either Cthulhu is real, or Sherlock figures out that it was just a squid-shaped hot air balloon that was part of some plan to steal the crown jewels and murder the butler. Frogwares, bizarrely, chose the option of "neither". Nothing truly unexplainable happens, and Sherlock never wraps up the mystery.

Three times in the game Sherlock has hallucinations of some alien landscape where he has to solve some (trivially easy) puzzle to get out. Despite there being multiple obvious explanations for the visions, namely that he was drugged and envisioned landscapes that were implanted into him subliminally by all the bizarre paintings hung around the bad guys' lairs. Sherlock suggests that the visions are caused by drugs a few times, but inexplicably dismisses the idea as if it weren't less ridiculous than many of his other deductions in the games (and books). But for some reason, this causes Holmes' sanity to completely unravel. It's like Frogwares wanted to go full-Lovecraft with the story, but didn't have the guts to pull the trigger and have an actual eldritch abomination show up. I know they were somewhat tied to the plot of the original game, but why stick with the original plot when they rebuilt everything else?

Thankfully, there's multiple murder mysteries you get to solve along the way. Unfortunately, they don't get the attention they deserve. The worst example is in the insane asylum when you find the director dead after discovering all the horrible things she was doing to inmates. You examine the body, and then without even determining the killer he's like "we got what we need, let's get out of here". It's implied that it was Gerda who killed the director, but since when has a Sherlock Holmes murder been so straightforward? It was such a wasted opportunity for a murder case in an insane asylum. And the game's short length could have used the help.

And on top of all that, the mystery-solving segments are all pretty easy. The most challenging part of them is figuring out what you can interact with (I played on the hard setting where they can't be highlighted). Everything basically falls into place after you find everything to interact with, and the mind palace portions can be easily cheesed by just spamming all your evidence into the equation until something sticks. It's a LOT less challenging than Chapter One was, because there's no either/or conclusions that require you to actually think about all the evidence you gathered. The game just gives you the answer every time.

In short, if you liked Chapter One, you might like this, but will feel disappointed. If you didn't like Chapter One, you won't like this at all. I'm more disappointed than anything.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI’ve patiently awaited this game, and it hasn’t disappointed.

The play style is the same as Chapter 1 (C1), despite that, I repeatedly made the mistake of not pinning evidence before using sight. I did the same thing in C1, so this is very much a “me” problem and not an issue with the game!

There is a singular overarching main case - the story is relatively linear, with side quests throughout. If you have a side quest in the chapter, complete it there and then – once you leave an area you can’t go back later to finish off. I’ll be going back and replaying the first chapter for that reason – it’s not like C1 where you have free reign to go back on yourself.

There was only one thing I found annoying at times – Watson! He kept following me and then trapping me in a room and taking ages to step out the way. I did have a glitch in the game where Watson was managing to row a boat while hovering in the air alongside it…silly Watson!

The voice acting is on point and the music and produced very atmospheric scenes. The game is graphically stunning and leans heavily into Lovecraft, which separates it from C1 entirely. In terms of game play time, it’s a touch shorter than I would have liked, much shorter than C1. I’ve seen some completing it in approx. 6 hours, but for me, I’ve finished on 17 hours – I like to spend time exploring and collecting all there is to collect, racing through isn’t enjoyable for me.

I cannot leave this review without mentioning the beautifully interwoven patriotic shades of yellow and blue. Such delicately placed nods to solidarity brought a smile to my face, and the team are to be applauded for their resilience in the face of adversity – slava Ukraini.

Overall rating 8/10\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI am a massive fan of Sherlock Holmes, within the universe from Frogwares, and in the original works and adaptions of the character.

It goes without saying that Frogwares developed an incredible game during a very difficult time, and they continue providing such a compelling and analytical portrayal of the character, through his mind and his emotions, it's a beautiful new perspective, and an amazing follow-up from Chapter One.

Although it is evident that this game is shorter than CO, it provides the same joy and excitement, and the discussions about Sherlock's past was a wonderful touch. There are reduced puzzles, and more hand-holding in regards to deductions and observing, but the narrative makes up for it entirely, and I am slowly playing through it as I want to experience everything this game has to offer.

Thank you Frogwares, for giving us such a beautiful remake!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRemake > Original

Yep, you read that right. The remake of the classic SH game The Awakened is fantastic and the expanded storyline alongside the the side cases (which are quite a lot compared to what I expected) make it so much better than the original. I am a proud backer of the kickstarter campaign for this project. I have no regrets. It was worth it.

The story is almost the same, some minor changes here and there. It was rewritten to fit in the new timeline. There are some references to Chapter One but if you're a new player, don't worry. You can play it as a standalone game. You are in for a real treat !

The characters, what can I say ? The chemistry between the new friends-to-be Holmes and Watson is on point. This journey tests their relationship to the maximum.

The music is splendid. It makes the experience even more immersive. The dark atmoshpere of the game is *chef's kiss*.

The gameplay mechanics are the same as Chapter One's with some little differences. There is the mind palace (which is different from the previous games), deductions, pin an evidence to search for more clues, interact with people, you can change Holmes and Watson's clother.

The graphics are great, the game ran very smoothly. The team did a great job in optimizing it.

Frogwares deserves all the praise and our respect for what they've done. Delivering a game in such tough times and in so little time...it's incredible. The game was announced in July 2022 and now in April 2023 we are playing it. Frogwares, I take my hat off before you ! Well done !\t\t\t\t\t
