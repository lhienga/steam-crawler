\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOverall a good game, though I'd recommend waiting for a sale as I did.
For starters: it is short. I also expected more difficult puzzles and not a lot of walking around, simply collecting clues.

To the good parts:
- It runs perfectly smooth on my pc, no bugs encountered at all
- the story is well written and engaging. Sherlock and Watson are both written phenomenally and I'd like to see more of them
Why did I not buy the DLCs to get more?
Because i'd have to replay a significant part of the game in order to unlock them.
- Soundtrack and sound design in general is great
- so are the strange levels in the 'abyss' I'll just call it.
- stays very true to the cthulhu mythos overall

Some bad parts:
- I do not know if it is the case, but it feels like the choices you make do not matter. You can not go wrong, which disappoints me greatly
- the puzzles are boring and repetitive. None of them were challenging and if I did not immediately solve something it was because I'd missed a clue somewhere not because it was difficult
- historical accuracy in terms of speech could be greatly improved. They are talking more 'old fashioned' but I expected more

So, do I recommend it? Ehhhhhh...to anyone who, like me, is a die-hard cthulhu and Sherlock Holmes fan? Yes. To anyone who just wants a nice puzzle game? No, you're definitely better off with other games.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI found the game to be passable, though not without its flaws. The hallucination/magical levels felt boring and out of place, and I would have preferred if they had been removed. After the disappointing Sherlock Holmes Chapter One, I had low expectations for this game, so I am not disappointed too much. I somehow enjoyed big part of the game.

If this were not a Sherlock Holmes game, I might have enjoyed it more, as I wouldn't expect interesting cases to solve. However, here is only one case in the Lovecraftian world, and it is completely linear, with no choices or suspects. There is no way to fail, and no need to think. It's a waliking simulator and not a detective game!

I will admit that the atmosphere is dense and good overall, and I had planned to give the game a positive review, despite not feeling any Sherlock Holmes vibes. However, the ending was so disappointing on a gameplay, story, and narrative level that I cannot recommend it.

I guess, I am not a fan of magic in the Sherlock Holmes world. And the end has a big problem with tempo and logic. The death during the final conversation, if we question the existence of other worlds, is stupid. Why Watson falls? "WHY?" in general. It also destroys the peace of the ending, during the conversation with the bad guy, Watson walking from stone to stone is tedious and destroys peace. The final cutscene was also a letdown. I didn't enjoy the story, only atmosphere.

If you do not care about Sherlock Holmes, but like Cthulhu myths, you may actually enjoy this game. It is short, but doesn't feel like that. My problem is that I do not feel like I solved a single case.

Sadly, Testament of Sherlock Holmes and Sherlock Holmes Crimes and Punishments are still the best Sherlock Holmes games, and much better than anything new. This is especially disappointing after Sherlock Holmes Chapter One, which was a game with great detective mechanics that was ruined by a few terrible decisions (fighting, open world) and very poor writing of cases.

The linear approach to this game was a good decision, but [again] it does not feel like a Sherlock Holmes game, and we do not solve cases! There is only one case, and it is solved in a strange and unsatisfying way. We got a walking simulator instead of a detective game!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Great Detective meets the Great Old Ones...

If you've ever played one of Frogwares' Sherlock Holmes games (or The Sinking City, which also draws on the Cthulhu Mythos) before, you'll be instantly at home here. You'll be spending most of your time talking to people and examining crime scenes in order to gather evidence and make deductions. This mostly works well, although I personally found the implementation of the "Mind Palace" rather clumsy this time: you can only progress once you've found the exact correct answer. No deal-breaker, but I think earlier instalments (especially Crimes and Punishments) did it much more elegantly.

Interestingly, even though this is a remake of a 2007 game, Sherlock Holmes: The Awakened (2023) is also a sequel to Frogwares' previous game, Sherlock Holmes: Chapter One. It uses a lot of the same mechanics from Chapter One, as well as the same likeness and voice actor for Holmes himself. Knowledge of the previous game is not necessary in order to enjoy The Awakened. Just know that whenever there are references to Cordona and Jon, they're referencing Chapter One.

So, this is a distinctly spooky take on Sherlock Holmes games, with a thoroughly oppressive and unnerving atmosphere. The crimes you investigate are gruesome, often involving ritualistic murder and mutilation. I don't know if I'd go so far as to call The Awakened a full-blown horror game, but it is certainly horror-adjacent if nothing else. The story is well-written and engaging, the dynamic between Holmes and Watson is exactly what you'd want it to be, and the production values are incredibly impressive considering the circumstances under which the Ukrainian studio Frogwares made this game.

It's also rather short, especially if you don't have the DLC that adds side quests (which I don't think is a particularly cool business decision, but I'm willing to cut this particular studio some slack just this once, because... you know. The circumstances. Just please don't do it again). As long as you don't mind that the game can be completed anywhere between six and ten hours, it's a very good and spooksome time. The Awakened is not the strongest in this Sherlock Holmes series, but certainly not the weakest either.\t\t\t\t\t
