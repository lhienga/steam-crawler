\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTl;dr - it was GOOD, very good and I will play again and totally recommend it to others. A few nit picking things, but all together it was a great game!!

This is the third Sherlock I have played, and I did enjoy it. I find some areas a little frustrating, but it was a great game. Graphics, story and game play were all top notch (I do wish some of the key bindings wouldn't change in the later games in the series). You get used to opening mind palace one way, switch to this game and a whole other key is now used. A little confusing, but a minor issue.

I also wish, and someone is going to hate me for this, that we can turn Watson off sometimes. Ugh, he gets in the way so often. He does play a more integral part than I remember happening in Crimes & Punishment, and I enjoyed that and gained a LOT more respect for his character as a result. He just physically gets in the way and it is maddening. Maybe adjust the AI a little so he doesn't breathe down Sherlock's neck when he is reconstructing a scene, examining something or stand in the way when going through a door.

I have to play through again, I didn't unlock everything and missed some achievements and never saw the "God" - I feel a little cheated. So another run through hopefully will fill in some of those gaps for me.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI cannot genuinely recommend this game. I found it to be an improvement on Chapter One, but still underwhelming at best and frustrating at its worst. I enjoyed it a bit more when I started approaching it as a walking simulator with some light puzzle elements rather than a detective game. The horror elements are generic and never present a real threat to the player. Sparse gameplay elements put the writing into focus in Awakened, and unfortunately, the writing largely sucks.

Some upsides to this game: it seems the devs did listen to complaints about Chapter One and tried to improve on them. Common complaints like the combat system, eavesdropping minigame, and unnecessarily large open-world maps were all removed. (Unfortunately, without these mechanics or new ones to replace them, the gameplay itself feels pretty barebones.) A sonar system was added which helps a lot with finding items in the environment.

Downsides: The Lovecraftian horror elements are pretty generic and not engaging due to the way they're presented. The few possible deaths in the game are all pretty mundane (e.g. getting shoved off a tall building) and the spooky stuff rarely poses a threat. Most of the "Lovecraft" stuff happens within contained zones in the game, clearly marked as hallucinations, and doesn't mix with the real world. The worldbuilding is pretty sparse and focused only on information that is relevant to cases.

The detective work itself is not challenging and is heavily railroaded. I was disappointed by the lack of agency the player has in piecing together clues. You are given a very specific track to follow and are not allowed to deviate from it. The game does not allow you to make mistakes and is quick to inform you when you've drawn the wrong conclusion about something. You are required to go through a clunky pin system and piece together notes in order to get the game's permission to do what you've already figured out you need to do. I regularly found myself brute forcing the mind map, not because I didn't understand the solutions, but because I didn't understand the semantic differences between some of the clues or why certain connections were required when others weren't. (Not only that, but the game doesn't tell you when you have all the clues you need.) It feels less like you are an independent detective making connections while solving a case and more like you are being quizzed by an overly pedantic teacher.

I can see why many people enjoyed this game since it combines two popular franchises, but I find it disappointing that we are no longer getting mentally engaging Sherlock Holmes games that allow the player to do the detective work without being guided every step of the way. I wish this game gave us the freedom to make mistakes. Crimes and Punishments filled a niche in that regard and allowed players to feel like a detective and make their own decisions. Sadly, it seems that era has ended.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI played SHtA in 2008 and it impressed me favorably. Then I played it again in 2015 but this time it disappointed my expectations based on my memory. Now I played the remake and I can say that it’s the best version for sure. It’s not a masterpiece but it’s still an enjoyable game if you like detective and mystery tales and if you are fan of Lovecraft novels. If you like games as Call of Cthulhu, Sinking City, Conarium etc, and obviously the SH games by Frogwares, you will enjoy the remake of ShtA. The trend of developing remakes is not so bad when good old stories are brought to the attention of new generations by implementing more modern gameplay. Especially when developers are in the middle of a stupid war: it’s way more easy to develop a remake while hoping in better times.

ShtA is meant to be almost as a new and different game. Developers have eliminated the most abstruse puzzles, rejuvenated Sherlock, introduced third person view, added more chapters, new sequences, more cutscenes, expanded the story, etc. Obviously graphics, animations and sound are so far better, however don’t expect for next gen quality; it looks like good old gen. In the original version there were very few lovecraftian suggestions. Now lovecraftian visions of alien worlds have the lion part, so much that Sherlock's rationality will be put to hard test. Some ingenuity remained (i.e. see the ring stolen by the raccoon) but in general the story gained more depth and consistency. Narrative joints are not well linked, the whole affair is not totally clear; don’t worry and let you lull by the overall atmosphere of mystery and supernatural horror. This is a feature of many Lovecraft’s novels, there is not so much to understand when you tell a story of chaos and irrationality! Best choice for a game developed during a stupid irrational war! You are tempted to see the fall of Sherlock into the abyss of irrationality as a metaphor for the irrationality of war! I can't separate the ending with the situation that the developers are experiencing in Ukraine. Yeah, the chaos is real and it’s a heavy burden to bear.

At the beginning SHtA looks not so easy, a problem of many old games. This is because developers have not inserted a sort of chapter zero for tutorial, as many today games use to do; they inserted a sort of written manual to learn to play. Not a good choice! So you have to learn to play while following the story and conducting main investigations; that’s a bit frustrating especially for new gen gamers.
However it’s not hard at all if you know how to play. Everytime you find a clue, you have to pin it on the screen, so that you can ask people and receive new clues. The pinned clues also give you the opportunity to use Sherlock’s special sight. You have to browse and read your inventory very often, the game gives you a lot of hints. In the Mental Palace you have to associate clues based on colors; try different combinations, it’s easy. If sometimes you have no clues, look for traces in your surroundings through special sight.

You need around 10-12 hours to complete the game without neglecting or forgetting anything. Enjoy the ride into chaos and irrationality!\t\t\t\t\t
