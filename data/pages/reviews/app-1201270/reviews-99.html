\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR: Gorgeous narrative-driven game with great story and a strong message. Can be a bit repetitive but the game's beautiful pixel art and its simple and linear gameplay override that.

As an Indonesian myself, I am generally skeptical about Indonesian-made video games which depict the country. But ASFTU, despite the mouthful title, is in my opinion objectively a fantastic game. It does not "force" its Indonesian-ness to the player; rather, the game uses it simply as a backdrop to a much stronger narrative. The game is not about Indonesia, it is simply set in Indonesia (huge difference!). I really respect the devs' decision to do it this way, as it makes the game relatable to worldwide audience instead of only catering to over zealous Indonesians (#adaindonesiacoy anyone?).

The main theme of the game carries a strong message about mental health and it was delivered in a very beautiful manner. The allegorical representation of mental states in the form of fantastical elements in the game is spot on. It may have several plot holes (I won't state them to spare you the spoilers. ASFTU is best played cold, neither knowing nor expecting anything from it), but who cares? The overarching message is much more important than the petty details.

Gameplay is very simple. While you can control the main character using multiple buttons, most of the interactions will remind players of old school point-and-click adventures, akin to the Monkey Island series. It does get a bit more variation as the game goes, with the Ace Attorney-style court gameplay and the obviously Street Fighter II inspired battle sessions. They remain simple, but they get the game running smoothly enough. Some mechanics like the stealth scenes can be a bit repetitive and even annoying, but never to the point of you yelling at your monitor.

Graphics can only be described by one word: gorgeous. Makoto Shinkai and Ghibli-level gorgeous. Sure, not everyone may like the old school pixel art style, but I guess it was a creative choice which, for me personally who grew up with pixellated PS1 RPGs, paid off immensely. And I suppose ASFTU's intended market is exactly my profile: millenials growing up during the 90s. I always consider that Ghibli's strength is in its attention to detail, and ASFTU definitely shines on this one. Cigarette ads, postal boxes, Javanese blangkon, the details in the game is fascinating.

Music is an achievement of its own. I do not think it is the best video game music I have had, but it's still a strong and memorable soundtrack. More than anything, it fits the scenes and themes of the game nicely. Slipping some traditional instrument and musical styles to some of its music is well appreciated. The theme songs also resonate to me as strongly "Indonesian indie music", with all the best meanings that come with the term.

Overall, if you like gorgeous pixel art and narrative-driven games with a great and tear-jerking story, ASFTU is definitely for you. It just so happens that the story happens in Indonesia during the 90s; for some people that is reason enough to grab this game.
