\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR Scarlet Nexus is an action game that is, admittedly, pretty mediocre all around, but by no means a bad game. I think a key factor that is vital to enjoying it is just embracing it for what it is, which is basically a playable anime. For the story, expect one that gets progressively more ridiculous as the game goes on and do not take it seriously whatsoever, or you'll regret it. Aside from a couple of obnoxious enemies/bosses and a bad lock on system, it's a decently fun time, and if nothing else, this game's environments are really bright, vibrant, and just beautiful and all have a fitting soundtrack that builds a palpable sense of atmosphere. I'd say it's worth a shot on sale for sure as long as you know roughly what you're in for, and you just want a game to vibe and have fun with.

I purchased this game almost exactly one year ago. I won't overshare, but at the time, I was going through what I consider a hellish and extra stressful point in my life. Being completely out of â™¥â™¥â™¥â™¥â™¥ to give and a sucker for all things anime, I saw this game on sale and bought it (along with Code Vein - did I mention I'm a sucker for anime?) despite the kinda mixed reviews. I'm glad I did too, because this goofy, mid-at-best game kept me sane throughout that time, and because of that, I'll openly admit I have a heavy positive bias for this game. I see and understand its many flaws, but they don't really bother me or impact my enjoyment of the game; I platinumed it.

Scarlet Nexus is an action game. You play as one of two characters who, while having the same power of telekinesis, have different fighting styles - Yuito has a sword, fights up close and personal, and is less dependent on powers while Kasane is more dependent on powers and tends to stay back a little. The difference here is important, because the more you use powers, the more your psi gauge decreases, and the primary way of restoring it is with weapon attacks, and Yuito is a lot better in that department. Alternatively, you can execute special finishers on enemies (which sadly all have the same animation that you'll get tired of seeing real quick) to restore the gauge once you stagger them. To stagger an enemy, you just keep attacking them until their crush gauge is empty, and thatâ€™s when you can land a finisher that will instantly kill a normal enemy, or in the case of a boss fight, will deal heavy damage.

I'd say the gameplay is kind of what you'd expect from a game like this and you'll pick up the "mechanics" (or lack thereof) pretty quick - it isn't too deep of a game and you mostly just hit the RT button a lot while occasionally hitting X or Y. What you might not expect, as many others have already pointed out, is that the lock-on in this game is *bad*. If you play the game, you'll know what I mean, but to say the least, it is inconsistent. Additionally, this game does have a couple of enemies (particularly a couple of bosses) that are absurdly tanky and can take everything you throw at them like they're nothing and being difficult to stagger and execute finishers (your primary way of dealing damage) on. However, there is one thing that partially counteracts this, which is this game's use of elemental status effects. Some enemies are weak to powers that your party members have (and that you can use through a mechanic called SAS), and by exploiting those weaknesses, fights become more tolerable. Even if they aren't weak to anything in particular, you can still, say, throw an oil barrel at an enemy with your telekinesis, then use SAS to borrow your party member's pyrokinesis, giving your weapon attacks fire damage, which will then set the enemy on fire, damage them, and quickly decrease their stagger gauge.

Now something that I personally find most fun about this game: The SAS mechanic gets really broken towards the end of the game. Eventually, you'll be able to borrow 4 powers at once, which is really satisfying and will make you feel like a god as you not only throw things at enemies, but also have your 2 clones doing the same, while the things they throw are on fire and deal more damage, while also having movement so greatly increased that time is slowed down for your enemies. SAS is broken, and it became really apparent to me when I defeated the hardest ("secret") boss in the game with an example similar to the one I just listed. I was underleveled and he didn't stand a chance, even if it took a while for him to go down.

Gameplay summed up: You and your party members have psychic powers, you use them to destroy enemies.

Speaking of party members, both Yuito and Kasane get their own "squad" of 4 other members (at first). They aren't exactly complex characters, and if you've seen a single anime, you'll be able to identify the tropes pretty quick (the main characters themselves are no exception), so there's not really a whole lot worth saying here. That said, I still liked them and found myself attached to them. And in anime game fashion, you'll be able to buy them gifts and stuff too while you're all hanging out at your hideout (Note: I really wish the hideout had more utility - it's kinda just a hub you hang out at between chapters but there's nothing to really do there).

Now the story. Oh boy. Without spoiling it, I canâ€™t really get into specifics, but the bottom line is: Donâ€™t ask questions. See, this game actually does start out interestingly enough and in an interesting setting. After a certain point, I realized I was actually, truly invested in what was going on and what would happen next. But eventually, around probably the middle of the game, this story just decides to abandon its ruse of seriousness and say to the player "You're in too deep now, so you have to see this through no matter what happens." Under any other circumstances, I may have been annoyed by this, but I wasn't because...It's anime. And that kind of thing is pretty common. Ever seen an anime where season 1 was really interesting and season 2 was just a combination of bad, weird, or unfitting? Scarlet Nexus is kind of like that. And it doesnâ€™t back down for even a moment - it knows you bought the game and it knows youâ€™re going to play to the end no matter what, no matter how absurd the story gets (provided youâ€™re having fun).

I went in expecting a playable anime, and I got one. Long before the point I mentioned earlier happened, I was definitely vibing with this game, and I just didn't care because I was still having fun. And I think that's what separates the people who like the game for what it is and what pros it does have, and those who think it's the dumbest â™¥â™¥â™¥â™¥ ever and a total waste because of its cons: Expectations, and how familiar one is with the ridiculousness of anime. Don't take the story seriously - if you're here, you're here for the action and the vibes.

Now what makes up this game's "vibes" exactly? When I first saw Suoh (the very first environment you start in), I was wowed - this place is beautiful. And pretty much all of this game's environments are too. The graphics and art style of this game are just so charming to me and they're backed up by a fitting (and underrated) soundtrack. And those vibes were exactly what I needed to keep my sanity during the aforementioned hard point in my life. Seeing Suoh, hearing its soundtrack, and just running around it felt healing to me in a way I didnâ€™t expect.

In conclusion, Scarlet Nexus is basically comfort food, but a video game. You won't be getting a deep story, but you will get a game that you can kick back and relax in and just enjoy the atmosphere, while throwing randomly spawning objects at enemies. This game is a passably fun action game and playable anime that is unapologetic about what it is and how ridiculous its story gets. And that's why I like it.

Thanks Scarlet Nexus. You kept me sane through an insanity inducing point in life, even if youâ€™re a little insane yourself. Negative times negative equals positive I guess.\t\t\t\t\t

I came in with ZERO expectations as to it's quality, art design, story telling etc. And quite frankly I was left with a bitter taste in my mouth after beating the game as Kasane. I will probably not do a playthough as Yuito as I really didn't enjoy the game.
Credit where credit's due; SN is a beautiful game with amazing art and environment design. A dedicated and high quality english VA cast which I greatly enjoyed. Finally it's one of those games where the difficulty falls off a cliff and your appreciation soars above when you fully understand the mechanics. (Kinda like Neir, and Souls games but not really.)
However that's where the praises end. The aforementioned points the game has can't push the game past a solid 3/10. I am not going to be kind to the game from this point onward and I suggest you play the demo if you are interested in the game, as it's not good.
I take issue with the simple, repetitive combat; painfully annoying characters; atrocious plot-holes; and most importantly the user experience (Camera control ruins a lot of the scenes too.). Full disclosure I played start to finish on Steam Deck.
UX: SN's UX can be described as lazy and sloppy. Menus are slow, loading (ssd) is slow, camera is restrictive, character tutorials repeat over and over on certain maps, frame rate can affect the gamespeed. All of these issues are annoying at best and detrimental at worst. Then there's how SN handles cutscenes and conversations.
The devs (Who are Tales Series Vets) decided to go with this ugly and jarring voice manga panel style. The team has very talented animators, who make amazing animated cutscenes. However every tense moment is always undercut by this slow impact-less manga style bogging down the pace of each plot point. A plot that isn't very good at that. Then there's the 20 min length of many interactions. They were so long my Deck would almost go to sleep as I watched them. Also there's NO pause option only a skip, and often they take away the ability to skip line by line instead skipping the entire scene.
Characters: I hate Kasane. I hate Naomi. Why? Kasane makes literally every situation worse actively, to the extent where I wanted the game to end with her committing -spoiler-Suicide-spoiler-. The game wears all it's twists on it's sleeves, and as a rule Kasane always has to choose the worst option for the sake of drama. Now Naomi on the other hand was a character that screams -spoiler-"I'm going to die right now"-spoiler- and she was unrealistically annoyingly nice and well liked by everyone. Thankfully she got Fridged so hard that I cheered because it meant maybe the story could begin to move. (it didn't)
Shiden the RivalTM ended up being my favorite character because you could actually tell he changed on the inside. And actively pushed to be someone who wouldn't hold down the crew. All in all his character was written as the most human of all of Kasane platoon, and left me feeling touched on a personal level. However it doesn't offset the 25 hrs I spent to get there. (Steam didn't track playtime correctly I have 35 hrs on my save file.)
Plotholes: Don't write time travel if you don't understand what a grandfather paradox is. Heck, don't write time travel if you don't have a concrete version you want to use. Don't make time travel the main plot point for the main villain's goal if the MC's only exist because the main villain using time travel. Oh this is just the beginning of the issues with the plot, characters just do whatever they want with no concequences. The story made no sense, and really pushed no moral premise. It attempted to ask deep questions, but gave shallow answers. This game is one of those with a nobody's the bad-guy endings. Honestly it feels like you control the accidental bad guys who genuinely screw over everyone else, and do a poor job of cleaning up their mess at everyone's expense.
Combat: Combat is inbetween Nier & and a Tales game. However it doesn't do a good job at copying either even if what came out is consistent. Combos are simple since you only have 5 attacks in total when you begin the game and about double the attack limit by the end. Another issue I encountered is that the combat can cause frame dips which change the game speed for several seconds, affecting the timing of dodging and even shutting down telekinetic throws which are one of your most powerful attacks. Furthermore basic functions for a Neir style game are missing like a double jump, no sprint, alternative combos from swapping from heavy to light attacks. Some of these are features you can unlock later like the double jump. But the game still feels restrictive due to the exclusions that would make movement more fluid and the clunky camera and lock on. Then you get your Tales style hard limits on combos, weapons you can use, party size, item capacity, etc, which don't exactly elevate the gameplay. Though thanks to its roots as a Tales game there are some neat link effects (Powers/SAS) you can use, but nothing like Arts or Combo Arts from the Tales games. These choices all add up as you play the game and result in unexpected jank.
Powers: However I'm going to move on to the powers. You have a cool suite of powers in Kasane squadron; Telekinesis, Duplication, Electrokinesis, Superspeed, etc. However they are typically under utilized by both the writers and game-play designers. This is a problem because these powers don't have described limits, so it leads to me asking why humans are even under threat to these Others in the first place. Especially with Karen being as OP as he is. Telekinesis for example is super OP, you can manipulate ANY physical object with your mind. If you've seen Mob Psycho 100 or any ESP. focused anime you know how broken it can easily be.
In Kasane's case you see she can easily manipulate several tens or hundreds of tons and I'd guess she can use it with the dexterity of a human hand (the knives are my reasoning). She can do all of this at the beginning of her story, as a super elite who's been trained since she's 8. Kasane quickly learns she's not OP and still has a ton of room to grow. So why doesn't she just learn to lop these things into peices like Lucy from Elfen-lied? The game really loves to make a point that she's growing stronger and more experienced but she doesn't have any notable changes to her moveset from the start. Why can she fly in one level but not the rest? Why can she manipulate the monsters directly in brain crush but not before hand? Why can she do that in a cutscene but not in game, and vice versa.
Again the powers are under utilized, where Shiden is the only character who makes sense to have trouble, and is arguably the most useful SAS link. He has potential but he's weak and has to continueously train to not hold back the rest of the group. Furthermore he is scared of his powers and won't use them to their fullest extent. In game play his attacks don't always work to stun enemies nor does he have the capacity to instantly kill anything, unlike Seto can in the story (who he admires). The other characters don't have an excuse. Either in the story or in the application of their powers they feel underwhelming or plain stupid.
In conclusion: This game really sucks, like royally bad. I can stomach the combat and I enjoyed the artstyle (Even if it's style over substance). I just think there are better games out there. Like Code Vein the game I installed after I beat this game is way-way better than SN for a lot of reasons. Big reason being, in CV You as a player have the agency to decide how the story ends even if it's only superficial. Your crew doesn't only make bad decisions for drama. SN is like some ugly love child of two better franchises by comparison.
TLDR: IDK how TF SN nearly got a GOTY, its bad.\t\t\t\t\t
