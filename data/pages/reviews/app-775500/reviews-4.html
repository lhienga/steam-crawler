\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI am getting close to the end of this game, but I don't know if I will even finish it. It is a flawed game, but no flaw is more glaring than it being interminably BORING. It just DRAGS at every opportunity, despite not really having that much to say.

Non-combat systems in the game are as simplistic as you can get; equipment, leveling, side-quests, all of them are so basic and flat that you can tell they were tacked-on because they had a checklist of "things to include." Only a few pieces of equipment and a minority of unlockables from leveling really feel like they add or change things enough to warrant existing, and even then, I can't help but feel like their lack in the early game just feels more like an arbitrary handicap. Dungeons and enemy variety are also very meh.

Combat itself is fine. For a good chunk of the game, it feels overly limited, but it does get better as it opens up. However, even then, combat is SO SLOW, and dungeons get increasingly large, turning it into a grind. You don't really have to resource manage, so the only goal of fights is generally to survive and, if possible, get more loot drops by doing certain things. This would be easy but most enemies just have too much health, which doesn't make it difficult, just repetitive. A few don't have too much health but can only be taken down in very specific ways, which can be a problem when the game throws several of them at you at once (you basically have to run around waiting for a bar to refill to kill the next one). You get alot of fun tools to play with and I enjoy fighting to a point, but I get bored at chipping away at enemies and constantly spamming special abilities. And there's no ranking system like DMC, if you're the type to enjoy mastering a system and getting recognized for it. Also, admittedly, some aspects of combat are still rough; you too often get hit when you shouldn't, too often hit the wrong opponent, sometimes inputs just don't register, etc.

As for writing elements, well, the best are character interactions. However, most of these are entirely optional side-content, most similar to S-Links from Persona. You buy gifts for people and eventually you have a "bonding scene" where you discuss them and their hangups and they grow as a person. It's not bad, but most of it has nothing to do with the plot of the game, it's just there as filler. And you have to do these in chunks, at the end of each chapter in what they call "standby". Meaning you may spend an hour or more making gifts, giving them (which is slow), then going through these dialogues. Don't skip them though; building bonds improves your combat ability immensely. Oh, and perhaps it's worth noting that the cast is bloated; you can tell they NEEDED 10 characters because of the way the combat system and 2-protagonist setup works, so many of them feel like window-dressing--even I could easily rewrite this story without half of them in the course of a weekend.

World-building is absolutely atrocious. It's hardly done. Phrases and names are bandied about with no grounding, there's almost no sense of place or time or distance, our special abilities are simply not explained, etc. Some of this may be cleared up in the final chapter but even if so, that's entirely out of order--you establish the world FIRST. We get a cool aesthetic style, but no real sense of the reality of this world--if anything, the style just makes it seem fake. Oh, and cities and NPCs are, naturally, very shallow and basic and add nothing. Heck, the fact that most cutscenes are static images with audio overlaid also makes it feel like a stiff, even dead world.

And for the plot...well, it's not the worst. However, it falls into the trap alot of stories do when there's alot of intrigue and secret truths--the story just becomes hopping from reveal to reveal, drawing the curtain back ever further, without really saying anything. "This is a friend...wait, no, he's a villain...wait, actually, he was a friend the whole time...wait, he's neither, oh dear!" It often lacks connective tissue, leaving events to feel like, well, just a series of events. You do later learn how it all fits, but it makes most of the story feel like filler. And as for those "secret truths," that doesn't work well as a writing tool if you forget to give us "fake truths" in the first place. You can't tell me absolutely nothing about the history of the world except a single vague fact from ancient times, then expect me to be blown away when you tell me the truth--it was all a mystery to me anyway, so the truth isn't surprising me. Likewise, you can't tell me nothing about a person, and then expect me to care when you finally do tell me about them. The actual plot, beyond all of that stuff, is fairly basic; yes, it has the over-the-top anime flair, of course, and I am fine with that, but it doesn't feel all that special--in fact, it feels like a rejected story treatment for a Tales game was just repurposed. Having 2 heroes was also a waste; there's not enough difference between their stories to really matter, you'll only miss out on a small amount of unnecessary details. Which just sort of feels cheap; a few emotional beats are robbed from you for being with the wrong protagonist, but not enough to justify an entire second playthrough. This was a silly decision that ultimately required alot of fluff to make work.

Overall, I can't really recommend this game to almost anyone. If you LOVE anime/JRPGs, but also action games to an extent, and also character-focused dialogue ala Persona, and don't mind a game that feels very incomplete for how lackluster so much of it is, while also being full of time-wasting fluff and long grinds for minimal rewards, maybe you'll like it. For most people though, it just doesn't do enough of anything well enough to be worth the time--the RPG elements are shallow, the story is basic, the characters are mostly tacked-on, the combat system is fairly simple and a huge timesink. the world is flat and lifeless, the presentation feels a little cheap, and on and on. It really does feel in many aspects like a rejected Tales story and combat system, and the team was given a limited budget to produce "something" with it to make a buck, and not really anyone's passion project or something Bandai Namco really cared about.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tScarlet Nexus gives a bad first impression. Your move-set is very limited in the first few hours of the game. However, if you stick with the game, it will open up and get much better the farther in you get.

The first thing I would want new players to know is that the dodge sucks and you should not go into this game expecting the dodge to work like most other action games. Scarlet Nexus doesn't have a standard "block" button, so you might assume you are intended to primarily dodge enemies attacks. There is even a "perfect dodge" mechanic (similar to other action games like Bayonetta's "witch time" mechanic) that will entice you, but you will quickly find that the dodge just isn't reliable. Whether the dodge being unreliable was intended by the developers or not, Scarlet Nexus is actually better that the dodge is not reliable, because dodging would be overpowered if it was reliable as perfect dodging provides a ton of invincibility frames. Instead of perfect dodges, you need to focus on understanding enemy attacks and positioning yourself outside of hit-boxes. You will also unlock an ability that grants temporary invincibility on a cool-down and that should be your go-to method of defense if you cannot avoid a hit-box. TLDR: don't rely on the dodge; Scarlet Nexus isn't that kind of game.

The second thing I would want new players to know is that Scarlet Nexus has numerous settings that I would recommend changing such as 1 setting the camera distance to "far", 2 setting SAS activation animation to "off", 3 setting camera motion blur to "off", 4 setting brain crush animation to "short", and 5 setting difficulty to "Very Hard" (more on difficulty in next paragraph). So don't hesitate to check the settings menu; there are even more settings I change from the default, but those are the most important in my opinion.

There are multiple difficulty options and you can change the difficulty setting at any time from the settings menu. I have been playing on "Very Hard", which is the highest difficulty currently available for me to select, and I have enjoyed playing the game at this difficulty. I occasionally die to mob fights, and I tend to die several times to each boss fight. Although I would recommend playing on "Very Hard" if you are experience at playing other action games, there are some enemies that are absolute bullet sponges with WAY TO MUCH HEALTH! Specifically the large "others" boss fights are the main bullet sponge offenders, and their fights are just painful for how long they can last, so I wouldn't knock anyone for turning down the difficulty for the specific boss fights I have in mind. Usually Action JRPGs are way too easy, but Scarlet Nexus is no push over and will give you a decent challenge which I really appreciate.

Luckily all cut-scenes and dialogue scenes are skip-able. I found myself skipping multiple of the dialogue sections in the early sections of the game, but as the game has progressed I stopped skipping the cut-scenes because I am actually enjoying the story. I think the story is quite good, and the voice acting is top notch. Everything you would want to be voice acted is voice acted. Also the cut-scenes are not pre-rendered, so your skins will show in the cut-scenes.

I really like all the characters in Scarlet Nexus. It is so common for animes and JRPGs to have annoying, whiny characters, but Scarlet Nexus doesn't fall prey to that trope. Most of the characters in Scarlet Nexus exude confidence and it is so nice to see it.

The game has an auto-save feature that saves very frequently, so you should never lose any considerable progress. If you die you are given the option to retry the current fight. You can also manually save at the frequent save spots. There are multiple save slots per game.

One of my major complaints is the controls for items. You cycle through items with the left/right buttons of the directional pad and press down on the direction pad to use an item. These controls are just really clunky for how fast paced the game is, and it is really hard to distinguish one item from another. For example there are about a dozen different healing items: light jelly, medium jelly, all light jelly, all medium jelly, etc. and all these healing items are the same green color with nearly identical icons. Expect to use the wrong item on accident a lot.

Although this is marketed-as/considered an "RPG" the progression systems are pretty straight forward. Your character levels up automatically as you gain experience. As you level up you will gain points you can spend in the skill tree (which the game calls the "brain map"). You can occasionally talk to your NPC allies to increase your "bond level" which will upgrade your "SAS" abilities. You can change your equipment, but equipment very simple with just a weapon and a handful of passive boosters. Overall, the progression systems are very simple and quick, which is good; the game will keep your focus on what is important, playing the game, not being stuck in menus.

The game has a very good file size. Despite the developer requirements listing a necessary 50GB of space, the game is currently using <20GB for me and I have created multiple saves. Most modern games are making no effort to reduce file sized, so I very much appreciate Scarlet Nexus for doing so.

Overall, very happy with this game. :)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDespite many hours spents, despite getting every achievements, despite getting max bond level with every party member and after having experienced pretty much all of the content outside of the ranked missions, I still can't recommand this game.

Now don't get me wrong, this is one of my favourite games out there, I love it, but not because of its gameplay.

Comparing this game to something as old as Kingdom Hearts 1, released in 2002, Scarlet Nexus is an embarrassement. It is the bare minimum that an action RPG can provide. The hits with normal attacks feel weak, there is no magic but instead you have Psychokinesis, which is just Telekinesis. You can throw stuff on your enemies but it isn't satisfying, nothing is satisfying in this game. The enemy design is wacky, and most enemies have attacks that are hard to telegraph.

The gameplay is helped by powers of your party members, but they aren't interesting, they momentarily buff your character, but it doesn't help the sheer dissapointement and lack of feedback of your attacks. The game only become reasonably okay when you unlock the combo visions, which deal good bursts of damage, but they come too late, at that point, I wouldn't be surprised that you already refunded the game.

All of these problems are slightly mitigated by the "skill tree", which is just slight stat increases and things that you should of had from the beginning. There also are items that you can equip that gives you upgrades to some mecanics that should have been permanent. The game is baffling in its design, it's barely fun at the end game, you're only saved in the end by being overlevelled compared to everything, which, by the way, it takes 20 hours to go from around level 60(that you naturally reach at the end of the game first playthrought) to level 99. It is a grind that isn't fun, with barely no way to speed it up, especially considering the 20 hours were WITH whatever EXP boosts i could find.

Watching a friend play FF7 remake and explain to me the mecanics of that game made me realise how bare bones Scarlet Nexus is, it has no strategizing, you do the same things again and again. There is no build you can make, you can only give stat boosts to your characters, this is a nothing game. I have never felt such resentement towards a game, because it promises so much, delivers so little and just fails at being an interesting game with depth and variety. There is nothing special to do, all you can do is grind, complete arbitrary requests and try to S rank every mission with their arbitrary requierments. The gameplay is shallow with artificial replay value. It is a frustrating mess.

So why do I love the damn game so much ?

Well, the story stuck a cord with me. It talks about many things : humanity, humility, pride, worth, responsibility, purpose, life, death and mourning. There are so many themes, and all of that just make the characters feel human, even despite the very "anime writing". The characters breath life like no other game I've played yet.
I legitimitly wanted to hang out with the cast, even those who seem annoying at first glance. Getting to learn everything about those characters drove me to keep playing the game. The standby phases where the game's focus was on character developement were the best parts of the game, and they could last as long if not longer than the story phases.

The story is not good writing, I won't even go to that direction, but the characters feel human, they all have flaws, emotions and ambitions, you get to know, understand and support what drives them to do what they do. The characters are the reason you keep playing the game, because they and the world of Scarlet Nexus are written extremely well.

There is so much content in this game, a lot of it is gameplay padding, but the sheer amount of dialogue, character interactions definitly go a long way to make this game one of my favourite games of all time.

I honestly have no idea how good the anime is, it might miss out on the party member bond chapters, and if it does, it will be hard for me to recommend any of the two. Pick your poison, I guess.

I don't know how to end this review, I usually rate games on a scale of "it's ok / not ok", but I'll sit out this one. This game is just too special for me to rate it.
If you get into the stories of games you play, go for it, but if you never pay attention to stories of game you play and don't care for anything any game will show you, this game will leave an extremely sour taste in your mouth and you will most likely take a long time to recover.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe short of it is that after 100% and 45 hours, this is a mediocre game. There are multiple small problems that add up over time into one big problem. At best this is a 6/10 for people who are new to action combat + anime tropes and at worst is around a 4/10 for people that can sit through it all.

If you are set on playing this then do not listen to the people who say to choose the Male MC's route first. No matter what, you are experiencing 80% of the story through both routes with small differences between the two in the overall plot. Choosing the Female MC has more growth and personality than the Male MC and sets up the latter half of the plot with more enjoyable party members at the cost of her having a weaker combat flow. If you are also going to only play one route only, then I recommend having a friend play through the other route and sharing information between the two since the characters refuse to do it and watching a 10 hour video only to learn a few details isnt worth it.

- The story is scatterbrained and moves from one idea to the next in record time
- The characters believe in the holy word of anime tropes: "Miscommunication" for more than half the runtime
- If the main characters are doing something that would not seem right given the situation it is usually because they already came to a conclusion based on information only the other side would know and they themselves wouldn't or good old Miscommunication
- Combat can be both stiff and rigid with the lack of movement options where every attack has an animation lock combined with the absence of defense other than a dodge
- Characters are simple with a few exceptions, and you only get 80% of their "depth" per route
- Most cutscenes are told through still frames with a box for the character talking. Even for some action scenes the cutscene combat is told frame by frame
- Sidequests are almost always combat related but require very specific attacks on certain enemies to be completed. This usually leads to searching for the enemy, travelling to the location, lowering its health to just barely dead and repeating a move
- There are hundreds of resources from enemy drops so to upgrade gear you either buy it at a story point or farm enemies to craft them which themselves are barely needed for how little stat gain you get from weapons/plug ins
- Since you have no options to stop damage other than dodging or use a certain power, fighting comes down to using powers to get into close range until it runs out, running around and attacking slowly at far range or getting stunlocked
- Characters speak in questions all the time and its never natural. "This reminds me of X" "What is X?" "X is what we do when Y" "Did you say Y?" "Yeah I love Y, especially in Z" "Could Z mean..." "Z is where blah blah blah"
- The ending will likely seem far removed from the rest of the story that you just played. Good thing the devs hid an extra hour of cutscenes behind DLC to give more background on the Who, Why and How. Thanks Bandai Namco.
- I do like the English voice actors, they sold their characters to a great degree. Especially Shiden. That's a good character.
- Surprisingly there are a lot of world building details that are hidden behind the loading screen tips, but there is no way to search for them in a menu.
- Different combos and moves such as Brain Fields are at their best only when you have specific skills invested into them like an extra combo hit, but since you will never naturally get all skills without a lot of grinding you could miss out on some that make combat feel much better/easier through playing the story

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOne of the things that a lot of people give reasons of recommending Scarlet Nexus is because of its great fluid combat. You start of with just mashing attack over and over to at some point strategies just click in your head. Of course you should use invisibility to go behind enemies, of course you should use fire after splashing oil on your enemies. This strategic dance comes to me with hours of playing and testing but once it clicks and a strategy comes it feels really great.... or you can just mash the buttons until something dies.

But I want to give reasons why the story/writing might be Scarlet Nexus's strongest tool and is the hook for me to play and reach 100% achievement. The story and the gameplay are intrinsically intertwined, there's a reason why you have the powers you have the way it is and the fact that Bandai Namco are able to weave it beautifully and make me invested in the story was something I felt was worthy of applaud. My power level was increasing on par with my investment in the characters story.

The game is literally the power of friendship - the game. Even the final villain's objective is to push their own version of the power of friendship or what they are willing to fight for their friends... and the kicker is that the antagonist actually won, and the execution was great. I had to give props to the writers for being able to pull that final part off. Yeah there are a lot of cringe moments. the female mc is always brooding and sometimes you wish you can shout at her to just grow up, and the male mc is a goody two shoes, but as the story goes their characters become more mature and develop more. Its a good simple story in my opinion.

The writers knew the themes they wanted to push, they write and weave it in the best way they can with a great gameplay and the ending was great if there is some foreshadowing for some unknown sequel and they respected your intellect and didn't pull some unknown crazy stunt. Worldbuilding was spot on and felt lived in, everything that existed had a purpose and didn't just felt like it was there just to look cool, it all served a purpose. One gripe however was the Kodama mystery which I thought was a miss opportunity.

The last part I wanna give props is to the way they do new game plus. There are two separate characters line but they both eventually converge in the end. I thought this was brilliant. By the time I've finished playing the male mcs story, I felt the story was already good and finish. Any other player that was strict on time I believe can finish there and get their money's worth imo. Doing the second mcs story is just to tie loose ends and to get more game from the combat. I think they executed this really well. One of the benefits I thought that having two mcs with two branching story paths with the finale coming together was that I notice the mc didn't fall into the calamity of the mc syndrome where it felt like the world revolved around them. In essence whilst playing one of the mcs there is always that idea in the back of my mind that other events are happening, this made the world felt more lived in as well as allowing for different perspective of the mc when playing each of the story. I personally wish Bandai Namco can try out other games with this style of new game plus.

In conclusion, Scarlet Nexus is an underrated game and you should really give it a try, at least for the great gameplay.\t\t\t\t\t
