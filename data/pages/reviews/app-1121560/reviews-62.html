\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWith so many games in the Atelier series it can be daunting to try and jump into these games, Atelier Ryza seems to be designed with new players in mind with a much more casual pace having the time limits from older Atelier games removed, something that was an immediate turn off for many players.

But beyond being an easy entry point for the wider series what else does the game have going for it? Well the battle system is one of the best designed turn based systems I've seen even if certain other parts of the game fail to make it shine. You engage in combat by attacking monsters on the overworld, you'll them begin a fairly standard active time battle but each normal attack you launch will build up AP, a resource you use to execute your characters magic and special attacks, on top of this you can also spend AP to advance your tactics level once it reaches a certain threshold, upping this increases the number of hits basic attacks make, increases damage and can add special effects and debuffs to magic attacks at certain levels but at the cost of saving up AP to raise these levels. But thats not all, in order to tempt you into spending those points instead, your companions will call out for you to set them up for a special attack, if you meet the conditions (using a item, a magic attack or debuffing an enemy etc.) they will instantly launch a special attack for free even if its not their turn, you can even use AP to skip straight to your turn letting you maybe get in a finishing attack before the enemy turn. Basically the combat system is all about building and managing resources and if done well you can juggle your opponents and prevent them from ever attacking. This management of combat resources extends to items as well, as your items have a shared pool of charges which only replenish when you rest. This prevents you from just spamming healing as you only have a limited pool for a dungeon and that pool is shared with attack and buff items which also act as triggers for companions super attacks. this means you'll often have to choose between saving for healing over a powerful attack now. You can of course restore charges by sacrificing an item, stopping you from using that item again until you rest. The combat system is just beautifully designed and it would great if other devs took note.

However I did say that other elements prevent the combat from shining, one is the just the crafting system, its a core aspect of any atelier game but its easy to craft an overpowered item even without really trying, by rights you should be limited by your ability to gather resources and there before mentioned charge system, but the fast travel system in the game allows you to instantly skip from anywhere back home to instantly recharge charges and drop off resources and full heal before heading right back, you don't even need to start at the beginning of a dungeon, you jump straight to start of each sub area (which are pretty small) this allows you to easily abuse items which can often 1 shot normal enemies, without the trade off of not having any charges left for higher power monsters; Add on top of this that monsters clearly appear on the overworld you can always avoid strong monsters if you have no charges and you'll almost never be caught flatfooted.

The crafting system itself is enjoyable as it is a core part of the Atelier experience, you'll often be running back and forth collecting items to craft with and make better version of existing items which is great, however the results from crafting can easily outpace the games difficulty because its way to easy, I played at first on the highest difficulty available to me and cruised through the game without trying, there were higher difficulty setting that unlocked but by that time I had come to grips with the crafting and was able to abuse the game even worse, there are few monsters able to challenge you, not to mention monsters that have enough HP to get a chance of using all the features of the battle system. Even the endgame elemental bosses are no threat as you craft up enough resistance gear to render them ineffective and thats if you don't use a setup that stops them from attacking before boosting your stats high enough to one shot them. I beat the endgame super boss and only later learned more about the crafting system that would of let me push my gear and items even further but there was nothing to test it against.

So is the game worth your time? yeah it is, the battle system is great and worth taking note of even if the difficulty prevents you seeing it at its best and the crafting is fun and can be pushed to make some OP stuff even if you never need it to beat the games content. But most of all Ryza provides a nice entry point to the series that isn't overwhelming and gives you an idea of what an Atelier game is like and wether its a series you might be interested in.\t\t\t\t\t

My journey takes me almost a hundred hours.
First of all, the character design is eye catching. But that isn't all this game offers.
You should spend more time thinking about the synthesis and combat.
Not just thighs.

Character development is significant.
Keep reading, no spoilers.
Everyone has struggles and at some point they overcame hurdles and showcased how great they could be.
They aren't all that special, comparing to us. We aren't perfect. We failed, we gave up... but most importantly, we could learn and grow.
Especially Tao, I didn't like him at first, a boy who think very negatively, worrying about everything.
But he is the most developed character. I'm convinced.
I can say in the Finale, his role is as significant as Ryza.
Lent, a brave and reliable partner.
Come on! Learn to like these guys, not just anime tiddies.

Story is okay. More casual than the typical JRPG cliches.
But don't expect a climax or plot twist. Enemies and villains are forgettable.

The synthesis system is more understandable then previous titles
so I believe Ryza is the right title to enter Atelier series.
It's rewarding to make your own ultimate secret weapon and see how powerful it is.

The biggest problem is the game development is rush.
As the directer and the illustrator said that
they are under pressure to the deadline.
You can see reskined enemies everywhere at a point you even try to avoid all battles.
you know, KOEI always forces devs to launch half baked products so
accountants can play around the numbers in financial reports to please investors.
but the game ended up quite unpolished.
And the DLCs won't help because you only get more colour variants of the same enemy.

So I only recommend this game when it's on sale.
Among DLCs, only weapons skins and costumes are worth your money.


Part 2:

Mechanism wise, the Synthesis system is definitely the core of this game,
as you collect resources which are raw materials,
then you combine them to make various items.
And you keep developing new recipes by combining more different items.
You could rebuild those items as you add on more material to further strengthen the items.

Basically, you need to consider:
(1) quality
(2) elements
(3) genres
(4) characteristics (passive skills)

Quality is self explanatory, items with higher quality do more damage, heal more efficiently and so on.
Some quests requires you to make specific items with a certain level of quality.
But you can't archive the highest quality until the last 2 chapters.
The system is deep so you need to work bit by bit, understanding how it works, do some calculations.
Keep repeating the processes. And finally you could master it.
It feels really rewarding to make the best gears and items,
overwhelming anyone who comes into your way. That's exactly the core value of Atelier.

You start the Synthesis at a cell, and it branches into more cells,
you can only progress when the requirement of elements is satisfied.
Like a skill tree. The interface is more understandable then ever before.
So it isn't hard to pick up.

It doesn't mean you could put everything with one element into a cell,
because every cell requires different types of items.
For example, fabrics/ metals/ liquids.
Therefore, make sure you collect various resources.

And passive skills are powerful but a bit complicated.
It's because not all passives could be enchanted on an item.
Those passives are specific to : Weapons, armors, accessories, combat items and medicines.
Stacking up the same passive to level it up.
There are limited slots so you need to be careful about
what passives you wanna bring forward to "work in progress"items.

The key is: always think one step further. Period.

The best part is there isn't time constraints, so you can mess around,exploring, taking pictures,
or just sit in front of the pot combining stuffs all day long.

What's the point to spend hours to create an overpower item??
The combat is exactly the stage to showcase the result of your hard work on synthesis.
Although this game is tagged as turn-based, is more like the "Active Time Battle" (ATB) system you see in Final Fantasy.
You are actually commanding in real time.
Speed (SPD) decides how quickly your turn will come.

Let me explain the key numbers one by one.
(1) Action point (AP)
(2) Core Charge (CC)
(3) Tactics Level

You form a team of 3, you control the main character and AI handles the rest.
In negative mode, NPC members only do normal attack. Otherwise, they spam skills.
Since some characters are more speedy so you might be able to manually control all 3 characters
as you switch between them while the others are waiting for the next move.

Active skills require AP. You need to gather AP by normal attack or using items.
You can instantly take turn by consuming 10 AP. It could be helpful when your enemy is stunned or his guard is broken,
that allows you to do burst damage and repeating until you clear the AP gauge.

Using items cost you CC but not AP.
CC is a separated resource so you can't gather CC by what you do to get AP.
You need to recover CC by sacrificing another item. CC would be fully charged again.
The sacrificed items are disabled until you back to hideout.
So you need to think about when to use and when to give up your items.
Also to make best use of every point point of CC,
your action should be calculated so there isn't residuals of CC before you charge.

Last but not least, Tactics level gives bonuses to active skills, stats,
and extra hits on normal attack. it means you can gather AP much faster. The AP gauge also increases.
But leveling up Tactics clears you max AP. Timing is really important or you might ruin the whole battle.
Moreover, only At Tactics level 5, you can use powerful ults.
But the ults also bring you back to level 1.
So make sure the ult is your last shot.

Action Orders requires player to act in concert with teammates.
If you do what they prefer, powerful moves will be triggered.
It's meant to show teamwork but some of their requests aren't possible.
For example, a character might not be able to do specific elemental damage.\t\t\t\t\t
