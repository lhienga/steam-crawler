\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI feel like I owe the game a huge apology for waiting a month after finally beating it to make this review.

Atelier Ryza is one of those JRPGs with what initially feels like a cutesy and kid-aimed aesthetic. This along with the characters being younger teens and the subtitles probably being written more "childish" than the actual spoken vocabulary can put people off if they haven't played an Atelier game yet.

Putting this game down over that would be a huge mistake. I'm sure it's been said better in other reviews, but this and the sequel are the only games I've cried after finishing and immediately started a new file on. It's true that if you play on Normal and pay any attention at all to how Alchemy works you can walk all over the enemies in terms of difficulty, but to me this is a game about the journey.
The separate pieces may all be a bit tough to accept if you're like me and initially reject "simple" things, but they all mesh together to create a game that stands out to me as an experience.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI can't recommend this to English speakers who prefer a faithful translation over localization. Be wary of the Americanization of the dialogue. None of the character's speak the way they do in the original Japanese script. One example I will bring up is the expression used by Lent when he speaks to Claudia's father, "Ojamashimasu" - which I will point out is a polite phrase used when you're a guest in/entering someone's home. The English version translates this as "Hey, what's up?".

This type of butchering is completely unacceptable, and I will go far as to say is character assassination. If you have a good enough understanding of the Japanese language to pick apart where the localization team have failed, you'll probably have a much more enjoyable(or frustrating) time with this game. That is, unless if you're an American/Californian, then you'd probably prefer it this way.

Edit: Earlier today while playing the game, I have noticed an obvious mistranslation where Ryza says "is she okay?" as opposed to "will she be okay?" or "I wonder if she'll be alright?" in Jonna's quest completion dialogue. Later on, "Ointment" is translated as "Pain killers". Some materials are even named incorrectly. A piece of log is called a "tree"(Fragrant Honey Tree - as opposed to Fragrant Honey Log). \r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is the first game I've played in the Atelier series, and it ended up being a lot of fun! It felt a lot like a 3DS game due to the graphics/art style and RPG mechanics while having modern quality-of-life features - the fast travel system in particular is great, you can pretty much travel anywhere from anywhere, making exploration easy but still fun and rewarding. The alchemy in the game - basically a complex crafting system - is very enjoyable to mess with. Unlocking new recipes by modifying preexisting ones, combining items with different qualities, traits, elements, and skills to create the perfect items for whatever you need, and just the fact that crafting is something fun and engaging, rather than a chore. It also makes exploration that much more interesting, since literally anything you find can be used in your alchemy to create new combinations.

The story starts out really slow but picks up after a while, which is ok because the story in this game is very calm, relaxed, and character-driven (and the characters are a lot of fun). Don't expect a super action-packed game, even though there is a lot of combat, this is generally not reflected in the story, other than a couple of parts in the middle, and the ending segment. Not a phenomenal story by any means, but it fits perfectly with the atmosphere of the rest of the game, which is relaxing, cute, and low-stakes, but still interesting.

Combat in the game is fine, interesting enough to hold my attention and make encounters consistently not boring, but not interesting enough that I really wanted to engage with it. Most of the game is very easy, with the exception of a few late-game bosses, which are very difficult. For these bosses, I ended up lowering the difficulty to easy to beat them, since this late in the game, the difficulty was not enticing to me as the whole rest of the game had set itself up as a calm adventure. It is very nice that the game doesn't punish you for lowering the difficulty, so don't be put off by the endgame bosses like I almost did. The game does, however, reward you for raising the difficulty with better item drops and more experience points from combat, so if difficulty is something you're looking for, there are a lot of higher difficulty options and challenging bosses here that really let you cater the game's challenge to whatever you're looking for, which are pretty ideal difficulty options for a game like this.

Overall, Atelier Ryza 1 was a pleasant surprise, much more fun than I thought it would be (and in different ways). It was easygoing, while never getting boring, with lots of stuff to do: places to explore, items to create, characters to optimize, and quests to complete. I went from tentatively interested in the Atelier series to very interested, and I'll definitely be playing more games in the series, starting with Ryza 2 and 3.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAtelier Ryza tells the tale of Ryza Stout, a young woman craving adventure to escape her mundane life on a secluded island. Joined by friends Lent and Tao, the trio embarks on a thrilling quest, epitomizing the longing for excitement that many can relate to.

The combat system in the game is distinct, utilizing a timeline-based approach where characters and monsters have cooldown periods. This dynamic combat often resembles auto-battler gameplay, sometimes leaning more towards automation than strategy. The limitations imposed on Action Points (AP) and Items can make the combat momentum feel sluggish. The delay in reaching higher AP levels can make strategic choices less appealing compared to saving resources for more impactful moves.

The crafting and alchemy mechanics are intricate and engaging, emphasizing preparation and gear enhancement. Crafting nodes tied to elemental requirements and traits create a resource-driven skill tree. However, material gathering can become repetitive, leading to grinding for past items rather than exploration. The loop of material gathering and crafting adds depth to the preparation phase, making it a significant aspect of the game.

The storytelling is commendable, with well-developed characters and a gradual progression from simple adventure to world-saving heroics. The characters' inquisitiveness and well-explained plot points enhance the immersive experience. However, the pacing occasionally slows with forced waiting periods between quests.

The game's soundtrack contributes to engagement, offering a variety of tracks that match the diverse environments. Despite some shortcomings in combat and alchemy mechanics, the game is enjoyable overall, with potential for improvement.

In the grand tapestry of this experience, Atelier Ryza stands as an unpolished gem, gleaming with potential yet occasionally marred by rough edges. The beauty of its characters, the innovative combat system, and the alchemical journey entice like treasures waiting to be unearthed. With the sequel on the horizon, one can't help but wonder if it will shine even brighter than the sun's reflection on the open sea...
