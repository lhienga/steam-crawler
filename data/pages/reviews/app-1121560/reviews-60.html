\r\n\t\t\t\t\tive been playing for 3 hours and im still not out of the tutorial. it might be a good game, but their are just sooo many small things that add up. Like how you get this massive pop up that lasts forever that fills 20% of your screen everytime you get out of battle letting you know you are 1/10 to completing said challenge... 2/10, 3/10,4/10... then 1/10 for this other challenge. and it does it for like 7 or 8 challenges at a time after EVERY single battle. I did multiple fights and was without 20% of my screen for a solid 30 minutes. (depending on how many you make even the slightest progress on... ANNOYING. just let me know when its COMPLETED.
-------------------------------------------------------------------------
Another, small/big but annoying thing. While in full screen mode this stupid game takes priority over all your other functions. Example, it wont let you use your fricken windows button. and i cant do windows + D to go to desktop. This is infuriating cause even though their tutorial is several hours long, they still do a horrible job describing everything and the built in help guide is trash. So i was hoping to do a quick google search... only thing was i couldnt remember how to spell this convoluted name and i couldnt go to my desktop to look at it, let alone pull up the browser. So i have to abandon what im currently doing, run all the way back to the ONE save point in the ENTIRE game so far, save, completely close the game, search what i want, then reopen the game, and run all the way back to where i was to pick up what i was doing. All to have one little question answered. Retarded... and thats just been the first 3 hours.
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
now 10 hours in - giving the game a fair chance. The issue with the windows button doesnt go away, ive found the only way to work around it is to put the game in windowed mode and get it as close to full screen as possible. But it still kinda kills the "being drawn in aspect". Looking at the achievements... an early story based acheivements (Building a hideout) only 40% of people have been able to complete. As this is not an optional acheivement, this tells me that 60% of the people that purchase this game also can barely stand the 3 hour long tutorial and that fact that theres more reading than there is gameplay. I spent $60 on a poorly written book and not a game it seems like.
----------------------------------------------------------------------

After finally getting past the small stuff, looooong tutorials, books worth of reading, bugs, annoyances and am now to where i can finally have some freedom to start crafting some gear and do some tougher fights. The game has become far more enjoyable. It's no longer a chore to play this game. However, based on how poorly the developers did with introducing the game... i would still not recommend this even if the end game content is the 8th wonder of the world. They really need to shorten the tutorials and fix the bugs in this game. Then at least half the people that purchase it might give it a genuine chance. Another great game ruined by shotty design concept/ development.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI don't normally write reviews but this game seriously needs more attention...
If like me you tried to get into the series in the past but couldn't... GIVE THIS ONE A CHANCE!

I saw some gameplay from this on YouTube on an unrelated commentary video and was so interested, I did some research and discovered it was Ryza. I was surprised when I saw it was an Atelier game and put it in my wishlist but had my reservations due to past experiences with the series. I always found it too cute, too girly and too confusing in previous games on PS Now. After reading reviews and seeing more gameplay, I decided to wait for a sale to try it out, it sounded like if any Atelier game was going to appeal to me it would be this one.

Two days ago it went on sale so I bought it and have played 12 hours so far. This is THE BEST JRPG I've played since Persona 5. The writing is fantastic and has kept every minute very interesting, entertaining and had me laughing out loud to the excellent dialogue. The characters are brilliant and fit perfectly with the game's theme and story. The mechanics are tight, intuitive and fun with plenty of scope to experiment and lots of variety to keep things interesting. The soundtrack is great and voice acting is enjoyable too (although there is no English dub so you will be reading subs).

Some complaints I've read are from long-time fans of the series who don't like the hand-holding and the overly long tutorial but being new to the series I actually appreciated the slow learning curve and was never bored, overwhelmed or stuck reading pages of information. The tutorial is done well (although I'll admit it does restrict you in a very linear fashion so on a 2nd playthrough I could see this being frustrating). However, Persona does the same thing and won't let you do ANYTHING for a much longer amount of time, at least with this game you are free to explore and gather very early on and will even get some side quests too whilst it is still guiding through the first steps main story arc.

The game looks gorgeous and runs smooth as butter. The controls feel great (I only wish there was a way to change the gamepad button for sprinting). I was very happy once I turned off auto camera rotate, adjusted sensitivity to max and turned on minimap rotation. I would highly recommend this game to any JRPG fan who appreciates good story, great mechanics and fun characters. I honestly think they completely NAILED it with this game and I'm looking forward to playing everything it has to offer!\t\t\t\t\t

A quirky mix of systems to create a somewhat homely JRPG, Atelier Ryza is a unique experience, to say the least.

Atelier Ryza is basically what happens when you take a slice-of-life anime's summer vacation arc and pretty much make a JRPG out of it. As one would expect out of my description -- This game isn't exactly focused on a macro-level plot save the world plot, but instead opts to keep everything fairly close to home as it primarily focuses on the sleepy island village that our titular protagonists inhabit, as well as the surrounding landscapes that make up this distinct area of Atelier Ryza's world.

We have the titular tomboyish protagonist Reisalin Stout (Ryza), along with friends Lent and Tao who are basically roped into her schemes to escape her daily life filled with boring repetition, that's overall structured in what I -think- is a coming-of-age story about these individuals, along with some other people they meet along the way.

The game itself basically has a core loop that some will find enjoyable, while others might find it tedious.

Essentially you have three phases that make up the Atelier Ryza Experience:

1. The Gathering Phase, in which you roam around fields either collecting items directly from gathering points or monsters if you're farming monster drops. What's unique is that the same gathering point can give you different items depending on which gathering tool you choose to harvest it with. So theoretically, you could end up gathering in an area early on, get some mats -- unlock another tool, and come back and get different mats later. It keeps area's relevant, and lets the player revisit area's for a practical purpose.

2. The Alchemy Phase, in which you take the aforementioned gathered materials and convert them into synthesized goods that you can use for a variety of reasons, including quests, combat, and other miscellaneous objectives.

3. The Combat/Questing Phase in which you use the synthesized materials from before for a practical application, whether it be fulfilling quest objectives that required you to synthesize an item, or using the newfound equipment that you made to become stronger in combat.

Progression is interesting in Atelier Ryza because unlike most other JRPG's, Atelier Ryza's "item progression" is mostly only limited by areas, versus levels. So every time you unlock a new area to explore, the possibility of being able to harvest materials that you could use to strengthen your party is a great incentive to continue wanting to press forward in the game. It makes for an insanely interesting approach to the typical: "Get better items at set intervals in the story" approach that most JRPG's elect to do an instead opts to allow the player to directly control the pace of improvement based entirely on how much effort they're willing to put in in order to maximize their gear.

"Sounds neat, but how's the combat?"

Well..... I gotta be frank, it can be hit or miss.

You're not going to get something revolutionary here, or even something that's particularly visually stunning with Atelier Ryza. Combat is fairly straightforward, and while it may initially sound overwhelming or confusing, it's actually plagued by simplicity to such a degree that I find myself struggling to really get a firm challenge from the game yet.

This game incorporates both real time and turn-based combat by using a "turn order" system that sort of reminds me of Grandia. Based on a players speed, their piece will move down the board quicker to the action slot, which when reached will allow that person to take an action. Players then use AP to use skills, or can instead burn 10 AP to immediately cause your character to act, which can be immediately followed up by spending even more AP to activate a skill, or using an item from their character's alchemy core which consumes Core Charges.

What makes this game's combat system unique is the addition of the Core Charge system, as well as the Tactics Gauge system.

The Core Charge system essentially allows you to use an item that you've synthesized infinitely both offensive and defensive healing items, provided you can handle the Core Charge cost associated with the item. This sort of takes the place of a magic-system proper in this game, and adds a bit of a resource-management factor to combat, as Core Charge's are finite, and can only be replenished by converting an item to Core Charges in the middle of combat, which renders you unable to use that item for the rest of the encounter, and won't become usable again until you rest in bed.

The Tactics Gauge can be seen as sort of a gauge that enables character to make additional basic attacks, which in turn means that you'll be restoring more AP per turn, but in addition to this it also alters some skill effects as the tactics level goes up further. The catch is that in order to advance another level of the tactics gauge, you have to sacrifice progressively increasing levels of AP in order to rank up.

So while rank 1 -> 2 only requires a 10 AP sacrifice, rank 2 -> 3 will require 20, so on, so forth. It creates an interesting dynamic where a player has to evaluate whether it's more important to end the encounter quickly by just burning AP on powerful skills, or if the long-term benefit of increasing the tactics gauge will pan out to have more positive results.

Overall, it comes together to form a passable battle system, but it's nothing to write home about. It becomes painfully obvious that a lot of balance wasn't considered when it comes to usable attack items, as they're able to do massive sums of damage to even boss enemies, and wind up trivializing most normal encounters with ease.

So basically, the game isn't very challenging and if you're sticking around hoping to get an elaborate battle system, i'd say look elsewhere. If you are however, interested in the story, and exploring a unique game play approach that will make you question conventional JRPG logic, i'd say give it a shot.

Would wait for a sale though, and i'd make damn certain to make sure you want the Digital Deluxe edition before buying, as if you buy the regular version you cannot just upgrade to the DD edition, you'd have to literally remove this game from your library, and repurchase the DD version.

Yeah, Koei can suck on a fairly fat ♥♥♥♥♥ for that decision, but at least the game is okay.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInteresting how the Atelier game to be most criticized for being over-sexualized is actually the one with the least sexual content... Other than a few odd camera angles and a single character with some very interesting assets, i can't for life remember any sexual innuendo/jokes within Atelier Ryza unless it was completely taken out of context. No girls grabbing each other's breasts, excess of lesbian undertones, and it even managed to skip the obligatory bikini/onsen event in the base game.

I for one am glad this came out to be a heartwarming story of friendship and kids growing up to see their small world in a different, more positive, light. Sure, the story can be a bit (subjectively) weaker than other ateliers, but it's slightly less silly and at least Ryza doesn't end up building devices for traveling through time/space/dimensions, nor synthesizing humans within just a few weeks of her first alchemy lesson.

Side-quests were also pretty decent for an Atelier game. They finally got rid of that quest board which always had the same 3 quests, and gave a handful of NPCs their own story quests, which can range from serious to extremely silly ones. And I first hated the idea of only having 6 playable characters, but after playing i realized where they were going with this: trying not to leave any char as dead-weights like in every other in the series (although poor Klaudia is sorta one). Almost every scene helped develop them slightly, instead of going nowhere with pie jokes or whatever. Heck, even the males ended up being better than average.

Gameplay-wise, the engaging alchemy system helped me not to sleep while navigating menus 90% of the game, but the recipe list could have used more work... It lacks proper sorting and searching features. But some of its mechanics feel like they weren't planned initially, sorta like when an mmorpg updates with new features but they don't really fit in and then you end up with lots of currencies for lots of different things to do that barely do anything.

Combat is alright, I'm not a fan of monotonous turn-based combat so i'm glad they also got rid of it, but it's a bit of a mess since the time still moves on while you navigate through the menu to pick items/skills, or even while holding the button to convert items which take a long while; and changing the character you control in battle would reset the action orders (support attacks from party members).

I've been playing Ateliers since Rorona, and Ryza is now one of my favorite entries. It has managed to bring new standards to the series, and hopefully KT/Gust will use all the thicc money she gave them to improve it even further.

Oh, and i didn't have any issue with the port as well !\t\t\t\t\t

Thicc Thighs Saved The Franchise

Disclaimer: Before I go into this review, I'd like to point out: I am not a longtime fan of the series. Like many, people saw the game pop up in an ad and saw the thighs. To me, I saw it as a pre-order ad and liked the artstyle. I looked up a gameplay video on it and then knew what others were talking about lol, but the combat looked really neat so I gave the series a follow. Then I saw the alchemy gameplay and got REALLY confused what was going on. Seeing it was $60, I was skeptical. So I did some research on a good place to start. Then I picked up Sophie on PS4 and enjoyed it enough to try it out on here.

So what the heck is Atelier?

From the experience of what I played and researched, the Atelier series is an RPG series with an alchemy focus developed by Gust and published by Koei Tecmo (You probably know KT from games like Dynasty Warriors, Ninja Gaiden, Dead or Alive, etc.).

Rather than games starting off with conflict so intense, Atelier is more focused on a "slice-of-life" RPG, where the main protagonist(s) of each game starts off their adventure and learns alchemy. Instead of just mindlessly grinding to kill a boss at the end of the dungeon to progress the story with side quests along the way, the Atelier series has a flow of three categories: Exploration, combat, and synthesis. I'll explain my opinion on that later.

Atelier Ryza's Review

Pros

+Great character development
+The world you explore looks small but feels big and makes it fun to explore and gather materials without fast travelling constantly.
+Unique twist on turn-based combat in that it's real-time as well. Seamlessly switch party members for efficiency.
+Alchemy. The core mechanic of the series. Synthesis items to use in combat or gather in areas. No two items will be the same.*

(Example: I want to make a bomb, but I have made one that just deals more damage than usual. I want to make a new one, but instead of just high damage, I'll keep it about average and maybe add burn DoT. My last bomb has properties that made the bomb stronger, but this one could also drop an enemy's defense.)

+Great soundtrack**

Cons

-Not the greatest story. Very "slice-of-life", has a hard focus on what it should be about sometimes.
-Stiff animations during cutscenes. Not a big developer, so not a hard loss. I see those expectations from big companies.
-Japanese dub only, but only con if player prefers English only.
-Bad optimization. Launcher with limited options. Also at launch, lacked kb+m but now patched.
-Needs more boss fights.
-Option to switch party members in battle. Just nitpicky for that one.

*I kinda prefer the Mysterious' "Tetris-style" synthesis system than the skill-tree system in Ryza, but still fun to use.

**I say it's good, but mostly for the series in general, which you can add previous OSTs. Ryza's battle BGM is repetitive and generic, but the rest is nice. You can listen to it on Spotify.

Overall, it's a good game for a niche series. I'd definitely recommend it on a sale or an earlier entry if you're skeptical though. Just to be safe.

9/10\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo listen,

I'm a huge-huge fan of the Atelier series, but this game was a total let down for one main issue that was an instant game-drop for me. It's the lack of voice-overs for almost ALL the side dialogue in the game. For the most part only the main storyline is voiced, and even then some of it is not. Most of the previous games were fully voiced except for random npc/townie dialogue. This game is a step down.

The graphics are far better, and the game is much more free-roam which is a thumbs up. Despite these huge improvements, the lack of voice acting in large swaths of the game breaks the immersion for me almost instantly. I can't recommend it as a result.

I'm sure a lot of people will disagree, but this comes down to personal preference. If you want to go back to the "lets read a book" style of gameplay where you're practically reading a whole frickin light-novel, this is the game for you. My priority has always been on story, characters and voice overs. In my opinion this game is weak on all three.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tProbably my favourite Atelier game from those I played (but I played just a Mysterious trilogy... so I can compare only to those.)

It is a great coming of age story, where all characters are pursuing their personal goals, fighting their own insecurities and overcoming them. I liked that they scaled down fan service moments and some character tropes, so the cast actually comes out as a more realistic and natural one.

Story is quite dense and there was lot more of attention to its pacing than in Mysterious trilogy. There is a less chances to get completely sidetracked. Almost all Alchemy here is a part of a main story or side stories, with zero randomly generated tasks as in previous games. So if you actually want a game where you are an Alchemist with a lot of work and where you want to feel like a profitable business woman, this is not an entry in the franchise to chose from.

I would definitely recommend this entry even if you never played any Atelier game. Its completely standalone and great.\t\t\t\t\t
