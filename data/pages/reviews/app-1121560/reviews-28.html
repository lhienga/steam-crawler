\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is an Atelier game. It's not a very good Atelier game. If you've never played an Atelier game, you should probably start with Sophie. This review is for people who have played Atelier games and are wondering where this one ranks.

There are four aspects to Atelier games: exploration, synthesis, combat, an story.

Exploration: basically unchanged. No timers thankfully, but still long animations and lots of running around grinding items. Still can't jump over knee high walls, still initiate combat by walking things with a stick (though now there are multiple sticks!) etc.

Synthesis: a massive step back. Sophie and L&S both set very high standards, and this game ditches all of that in favor of "put the square peg in the square hole." It might feel deep compared to series newcomers, but this is an Atelier game: I expect to spend more than an hour carefully optimizing single recipes, which is totally missing here.

Combat: the new "real time" battle system is problematic, not because it's harder, but because without a menuing system player choice is very limited. That means you only control one character who uses one of there skills or one of three items each round. Ironically, the combat ends up feeling slow, because the real time system means you can't just input all your commands instantly in common cases and must wait for the battle to progress. It's not completely terrible, but it is the worst in the series going all the way back to Iris.

Story: a couple of characters have compelling storylines, but most are completely one-dimensional. The story is also drip-fed between slow text scrolling and walking around to talk to people. This has always been more or less of a problem for Atelier games, but the ratio of interesting characters to throw-aways is on the lower end here.

UI: the game is adequately pretty, with acceptable though unimpressive music. The controls are ok, though scrolling through text conversations rapidly feels laggy. Animations aren't terribly long, but you'll get bored of them quickly anyway. The UI gets in the way by slowing the game down, which is not something Atelier Ryza can afford.

Overall, this game is more enjoyable than the terrible time-limited entries which culminated in the disaster that was Firis, but is worse than all the rest. Much of it feels phoned-in, with core systems moving in the wrong direction. It's not *bad*, it's just not what it could have been.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo I'm going to try to not mention thighs too much (though the thighs are noice).

For anyone trying to get into the series, this is my first game as well and it's a pretty nice first-experience to the series.

This game was a pretty fun time! It's a simple coming of age story for Ryza and her friends. A point that I read online that kinda fits this game (and possibly the series, but this is the first one I played) is "You know when you're playing an RPG and you make it to the final boss room. You look over and see a merchant, just chilling there. You think "How did they get here? The area is incredibly dangerous. Isn't my party supposed to be the strongest people alive right now?" This story is about the merchant, how they got strong and how they attained the items they sell".

Going into mechanics: From the quote above, I wanna confirm that you aren't merchanting or anything like that. Some systems aren't really explained that well or could use some more in-depth explaining. I found myself looking up how the role system works past the second tier, specifics about alchemy, and other stuff like that. Most can probably be found in the manual, but it's easier to look up.

The game is honestly really easy, and that's not bad, it's honestly more there to tell the story than to be extremely difficult. I played on Hard mode (around 1.5x enemy stats) and only had trouble with 1-2 harder enemies/bosses. If you use the "Item rebuild" system that is unlocked somewhat early in the game to your advantage, you won't have any trouble at all. For anyone that does want a more difficult experience, there's still Very Hard (around 3x), Charismatic (around 6x), and Legend (around 10x) difficulty.

The main downsides to this game are how little the basket are (even with the free knapsack from DLC and stuff, but you can upgrade it later), how slow Ryza moves before speed upgrades (couldn't imagine playing without the free windshoes from that same DLC), and the cap on how many gathered items you can have at your base. 3000 item slots sounds like a lot at first, but late game you're going to be cleaning out the inventory so so much.

Some tips to help with the game (for anyone that wants to go into the game bind on this stuff, just jump ahead): Try to get Gathering L effect on any gathering item. Traits don't really matter with gathering equipment. When you get the ability in the early game to combine the scythe and axe for better gathering slots, use that to attack enemies as it will put on an attack and defense debuff on whatever was attacked. When you get Klaudia, replace Tao as Lent is a good tank and Klaudia is pretty much the only character with healing skills. You can get gems for item rebuilding quickly when you unlock the northernmost autumn forest and using a scythe/axe gathering item with gathering L on it on the trees, within the first area there are 4 pretty easy to hit trees that grant a bunch of really decent stuff to convert.


Overall, it was a pretty positive experience. I tried my best to hit every nook and cranny. I unfortunately didn't have the time to do all the side quests as I had college exams to worry about. You'll get about 20-30ish hours if you just go through the story, and about 50+ hours if you try your hardest to be a completionist. I'll be playing through Sophie next, which I'm honestly really excited about as I've heard pretty high praise from that one!\t\t\t\t\t
