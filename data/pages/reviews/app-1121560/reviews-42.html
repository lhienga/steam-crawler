\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs a newcomer to the Atelier series and someone who's always been curious about these games, I found Atelier Ryza easy to get into and very satisfying to play. I've been looking for a "comfy game" with no pressure, and this is definitely it. The music is gorgeous and the scenery is very nice, so gathering is really relaxing. I was surprised by how intricate the crafting system is and quickly found myself planning out how to stack buffs from intermediate materials to make really powerful weapons and armour in order to prepare for the first major boss. The combat system takes a bit of getting used to; I do wish that I could control all of my party members in a more traditional turn-based flow, but having other party members act independently does make combat faster-paced, which isn't a bad thing. Overall, my experience with this game has been a very positive and cozy one, and I'd definitely recommend it to anyone who's new to the series and interested in what Atelier games are all about.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI almost didn't play this game because I thought it was just a thirst trap, but as I was looking for good JRPGs to get into to try out the genre, this kept popping up. I took a look into the reviews and found a lot of people with very good and insightful reviews that weren't just "Thyza" etc that showed a genuine appreciation for the game and the franchise. This is my first Atelier game and has honestly become my favorite JRPG and I am excited to try the older games. I'll talk a bit about the overall feel of the game, the alchemy system, combat system, and list a few negatives at the end.

I've always loved Slice of life style dialogue, and the way the main cast interact with each other as well as those in town are very well done and are a joy to watch. You see each character grow as individuals through mundane, every day tasks through the side quests which makes them more relatable. Though the main story of course has a crisis going on, what I appreciate is that it didn't escalate to a "kill an all powerful being" like so many JRPGs end up being (Though the final boss's looks did give off the vibe). The story is confined to a small, under developed town right off the coast of the continent and stays that way.

The alchemy system is addictive. It makes you want to go out and gather more things, so that you can discover the next recipe or create an item with game break traits and effects, though personally I didn't understand the alchemy system fully until the end of the game where you had to start thinking about what you craft (more in detail in the "negative" section later) Most of the time, collecting materials doesn't feel like a grind and making a high quality item feels accomplishing.

The combat system is probably sealed the deal for me to make this my favorite JRPG. I don't know what it is, but most JRPGs are either turn based, or some weird combat system and most of the time turns out really boring really quickly. This game uses a real time battle system that is like a turn base game. There's a timeline on the screen for when players and enemies will make a move, and the game doesn't stop for you to make a decision. if you take too long to decide to cast an item or attack, an opponent could have attacked 2 times already. This creates the tension that turn base combat always needed, and is absolutely perfect in my opinion.

With all that being said, here are some critiques about the game:
The base game is super easy, which isn't necessarily a bad thing. but if you're looking for a challenge this isn't it. You don't really have to think about your traits or effect until you get to the post game where there is a huge escalation in difficulty. Once you learn how to apply traits and effects though, items become OP it's kinda hilarious.

Another thing is the open world aspect is like a game from 2000s. If there is a hill, and a very slight overhanging cliff that drops 1 foot, there is an invisible wall there. This is fixed in Atelier Ryza 2, but this game did come out in 2019 so I don't see a reason for these invisible walls to exist.

You pretty much rely on online forums for finding ingredients since the in game guide just gives you a location but not what gives you it

The overall story is nothing exceptional (most JRPGs honestly). It's more of a slice of life, kids finding their purpose kinda story (which is a positive trait for me, it doesn't blow things out of proportions)

Anyway, music and graphics are amazing, I would definitely recommend this as a starting point into the series over Ryza 2 since the mechanical and quality of life issues get fixed in Ryza 2 and it may be frusterating to go from 2 to 1 because of it. Of course Ryza 1 also offers context for how the characters interact with each other.\t\t\t\t\t
