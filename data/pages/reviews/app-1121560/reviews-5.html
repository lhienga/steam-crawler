\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRyza is part crafting game focused on finding new materials and learning new recipes with a pretty addicting crafting system where ingredients are added to nodes on a graph, and depending on the ingredient choice, new nodes or new properties (or even new recipes) are unlocked.

Ryza is also part hybrid action/turn-based RPG, where each character acts according to their position on a timeline, and the timeline does not pause for you to input actions. It took awhile to click for me, but it's pretty fun in it's own way.

These two parts are tied together by a coming of age, slice of life story, and a number of really engaging check-list gameplay mechanics. NPC quests, character quests (complete X actions to unlock Y skill, that sort of thing), and recipe books all give you a clear set of goals to work towards in between story beats. It winds up being the sort of game where you can get focused on doing a run for ingredients to make an item to complete a quest and before you realize it you've been playing for a couple of hours.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's a somewhat amateurish and low-budget game. The graphics and character designs are good. But the game itself is missing a lot of polish. There are reused NPC models visible side-by-side. You cannot interact with most of the world's objects. You cannot talk to most NPCs, they either go transparent or they can't be clicked on. And several NPCs stand there frozen in a pose like statues. The interface is clunky like an older 3D console game. You have to click to walk between areas, you can't simply walk between maps. For the most part the story and objectives are very linear, which makes it pretty easy. The real-time battle system is fairly fun, but with everything else that is wrong with this game it makes it difficult to recommend.

If you're looking for a game with hours of exposition and cute anime girls. Then this could possibly fit the bill for you. If you were hoping to a role playing or adventure game where you experience an fantasy world, then keep looking this ain't it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's solid.

It's not gonna blow you away, but it's just nice and chill. You spend a lot of the game running around doing chores and collecting things, talking to villagers, some of which whom are kinda offbeat and funny.

The battle system is decent, it's interesting creating new bombs and gadgets and then testing them out on the next monster you see. I never felt like I had to grind levels.

Some of the alchemy and menu systems were a little confusing, but I got the hang of it in the end. I've heard the sequels have more quality of life changes.

Characters are all likeable in their own way, though not super interesting.

Overall, decent little JRPG, even if it's a bit generic at times. I'll probably check out the sequels.\t\t\t\t\t
