Is Atelier Ryza Worth Buying?

Pros:

Great weapon and item crafting. Lots of character customization.

Fun battle system with great mechanics. The combat feels very different from other JRPG's which is good.

Battles are fast in the game.

Enjoyable cast of main characters.

Soundtrack has great battle and boss themes.

The way the story ties the game world and main characters all together into it's plot is done well.

Exploring areas is a lot of fun and it is cool as you get new upgrades you can go back and explore different parts of prior areas leading to rare items etc.

Cons:

Battles try to be like FF with the ATB system where you gotta put commands in as fast as possible. Problem is, you gotta swap characters each time to input their command. This game's combat doesn't do it for you. So all at the same time while deciding what command to give you must manually swap back and forth as each character's turn is coming up. If you don't then the A.I. automatically does something with the character. I don't like A.I. controlled characters in turn based JRPG's. The combat is too chaotic. They need to stick with pure turn based like all the other Atelier games. Either that or like Final Fantasy does with the ATB system put in a wait option so the battle system pauses when a character gets their turn.

Cast of characters while quite good don't seem to have the level of depth or development other Atelier characters have. This could be due to the removal of character storylines where characters had their own branching story paths that gave you a ending for that character at the end.

Music in the area themes is hit and miss. Some good themes in some areas, but some of the area themes are very bland forgettable.

Battles feel too lop sided. Either you got the right setup and you destroy the enemies/boss or you get stomped. It rarely feels there's a in between.

Overall, very good game I suggest Atelier Ryza. I had a great time playing through the game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAtelier Ryza is a very wholesome JRPG, the kind that will cure your depression with its positivity and optimism.

This is my first Atelier game and I'd say it's pretty similar to the Tales series (Berseria et al), but the stakes are much lower, you're not saving the world, you're just exploring a fantasy land with a cute alchemist girl and her friends, helping the villagers, experimenting with alchemy and finding new recipes etc. The writing is surprisingly fun, there are no edgy or grandiose moments, it's just daily life and light-hearted adventures. Even the village bullies are just misunderstood and will eventually turn to your side.

Despite the thicc design of the main character, Ryza, there's actually no smut in the game. The most you will get are some yuri gags about holding hands while blushing and the like. It's pretty similar to Code Vein in this regard, which also featured very sexy character designs but no sex jokes at all. The bikini DLC is extremely revealing though.

Almost everything is voiced aside from minor sidequests, but there's no English dub which might be a minus for some, however as a true otaku I'm somewhat fluent in Japanese and can get most of what is being said without having to glance at the subtitles so it's not that big of a deal for me.

Alchemy:
Now, this is the main draw of this game and the Atelier series on the whole. It's very sophisticated. Essentially, there are numerous gathering spots all over the game's world, and you get a number of tools to harvest materials from them, e.g. a scythe to cut shrubs, an axe to chop wood and rocks, a fishing rod to catch sealife. And then some gathering spots don't require any tools or can just be whacked with your weapon. You put all these materials in your 'basket' and head to your homebase where you interact with your cauldron and initiate alchemy.

Each alchemy recipe is basically a flowchart, e.g. (wood) + (medicine) + (something) etc, sprawling like a spider's web. And then each item has different potential depending on its quality and elements. Almost everything you gather in the wild has randomized stats to an extent, so the result is likely to be different each time. You unlock new recipes by either finding them somewhere or experimenting with your existing ones. Your alchemy level is separate from your character level and unlocks more potent combinations as well as increases the quality of your creations.

Still, there's no need to be intimidated, as outside of unlocking new recipes the whole process can be completely automated, you can just choose to use either lowest or highest quality components and the game will craft the item for you in seconds.

Combat:
The combat is a variation of ATB (Active Time Battle), you take turns but in real-time, there's no way to pause the game when selecting your attacks. Only 3 party members can fight at the same time and you switch between them or just play as Ryza all the time. You can cast spells and use alchemical items (e.g. bombs, healing items etc), and there's also an interesting system where you chain your attacks with other party members. E.g. a party member tells you to attack with ice damage, you do, they follow up with their own attack (for free), and then you can follow up again with your own spell/item, and so on. This allows you to deal a lot of damage to a single target. There's also party formation and attacker/defender/support etc roles offering some more tactical options. Overall, the combat system is a bit unconventional and might take some time to get used to, but on the upside it's very fast so you never waste a lot of time, even on trash mobs.

Misc:
- You can only save at your homebase, and there's no auto-save of any kind. But on the flip side you can't die, if the party gets wiped you'll just respawn at the homebase with some of the loot lost, which is no big deal as all the gathering spots and monsters will all respawn on the next visit.
- There are few travelling options in the first few hours, but after the prologue the game will give you a world map with instant fast travel anywhere, similar to the The Elder Scrolls games.
- The soundtrack is very good, and the game also gives you a free 3GB DLC which includes music from all the other Atelier games and you can set these tracks to play at certain places (like your homebase), either always or randomly. You'll literally never get tired of the music in this game.

PC Port:
Supposedly there were some technical issues at launch (and which is why I decided to wait for a sale), especially with performance and physics tied to framerate, but they seem to be all fixed now. I have a 1080p 75Hz display and a GTX 1070 Ti, and the game runs fullspeed for me at max settings without putting any strain on the GPU (very low temps, no fan noise). The physics seem to be working correctly at 75 fps, Ryza's jacket is flying up high when she's sprinting, just as it's supposed to. It's a pretty decent port, and my only nitpick would be the lack of a more advanced antialiasing option, the game only offers FXAA and there's still plenty of jaggies and shimmering. Sure, you could downsample from 4K, but 4K here seems to be very demanding and not an option on the GPU's of 1070 Ti's caliber. Hopefully they'll add TAA or similar later on.

The game supports all kinds of modern gamepads, as long as they're XInput compatible and/or supported by Steam. Personally, I use a DualShock 4. Just turn on Playstation controller support in Steam's controller settings and it will work here and in all the other games without any extra hassle.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThings that I like:

- Charming world, pretty places, calming atmosphere.
- Characters are a mixed bag, I think some are interesting while many appear to be too one-dimensional (hoping for some character development for them later on).
- The alchemy system is really fun and it's easy to spend a lot of time playing around with it.
- The combat system is interesting, but personally I'd prefer a "wait mode" for this ATB system, allowing the player to calmly select actions for all three characters. But it's still fun the way it is, just different from what my personal preference tend to be.
- I'm having no issues with the port, though the Keyboard&Mouse support could probably be a bit better (there's full keybinding and minor mouse support, but full mouse support would probably help a lot). I ended up playing with a controller instead.

Things that I dislike:

- Time management is nonexistent. What's the point of having stuff like seeds and such if you can just wait it out with no penalties?
- Game is a bit too easy playing on "Hard", and the harder difficulty is locked behind beating the whole game once.\t\t\t\t\t
