\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm an older RPG player; I played my first computer RPG around 1986. I've played tens if not over a hundred RPGs since then and I'm very happy to be playing Atelier Ryza. This past year has been good for me as far as gaming goes - Disco Elysium, for example, was an especially important game for me.

As someone with a relatively full schedule with work and such, it's rare that I take 17 hours to watch or play any media at all, but I'm already at 17 hours for A.R. and I'm looking forward to playing however many more hours there are of it. This is my first Atelier game. I've avoided them in the past because I heard that there was a time limit to them. This is probably my loss because this particular Atelier game is engaging and adorable.

As you can see, the graphics are top-notch. The music isn't bad but certainly isn't on the level of something like Child of Light or the more recent Nier: Automata. The crafting system is wonderfully intricate - I'm still not sure I have it 100% figured out but I recently got to being able to rebuild things and I've spent quite a while just synthesizing things. My alchemy level is significantly higher than my battle level. :) The story and dialog are fun and internally consistent; it doesn't challenge the reader-hearer at all but it's pleasant and doesn't detract from the experience. I like the fact that you're not a troubled hero saving the world from a planet-destroying monster. Something that would make the "talking" bits better, in my opinion, would be to add a dash of Persona's social systems to the mix.

The battle system is very nice, with a fair number of options. It's an ATB sort of system and combines the usual elements, but it's especially cool to see things that you've worked to build in action. If you're losing your battles, it might not be because you're underleveled, but rather because you haven't spent enough time synthesizing or rebuilding your gear.

I've had no technical issues whatsoever. It runs fast and without fail on my machine, a graphics workstation built late last year. I'm using an Xbox controller and I'm glad that I am.

I'm not sure of what else to write. Atelier Ryza is a bit of fresh air for me and, even if I don't check out older games in the long-running series due their quest time limits, I'll definitely pick up any subsequent games in this particular arc. If anyone has any suggestions for Atelier games or for games in general, I'd be happy to hear from you in comments.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs always, I wish Steam allowed more nuance than a simple Yes or No. I've now played 6 Atelier games. Out of those, Ryza is not the worst, but it's definitely not the best either.

If you enjoyed most other Atelier games, I'm sure you'll enjoy this, but as an entry point into the series? Eeeehhhhhh, I have so many issues with this game, all the way down to her thighs. Yeah, her thighs are great or whatever, but if that's such a huge asset, even in jest, why aren't there more outfits or art? Not even a frickin color palette change? Literally, there's one DLC outfit for Ryza on Steam, and that one doesn't even show off her thighs. Do the developers not understand how to make money? We joke about fan service, but that is clearly a selling point for these games.

And while I'm on the subject of fan service, why does the character art still suck so much? This is a 2019 game, and Gust is still struggling to make their character art as appealing as their early Arland and Dusk titles. Of course, it's not as bad as Atelier Firis, but I'll still take a 2D exasperated Rorona-chan over any of the girls in this game. If they can't figure out how to make their CGI models more expressive, then this is never gonna be ideal. *sigh* Next topic.

The combat gets bland after a bit. For a couple minutes, I was excited that they went for real time battles instead of turn-based, but honestly.......it's not much different. Everyone is still stationary. Turns still occur based on speed. And it's another entry where you can only have 3 characters in your party at a time. *sigh* The only difference is the player can only control one character at a time and other characters and enemies just do an action whenever their turns come up. In the end, it feels even more brain dead than many other entries. I do like the tactics level mechanic in this one, but the order system is pretty derpy. The ability to command your teammates also feels limited. Like, how do I not have an option to tell Klaudia to heal without controlling her? This is basic stuff.

The synthesis system is pretty good as usual. The branching system is a bit different than most of the other ones I've played, but not bad.

The quest system though? Lazy trash. This quest system is what happens when developers have to do some additional world building, but don't really care or had zero time to implement anything. Jesus christ. If you're not gonna put in effort, just give me another Tess or Esty to host the quest system in one location instead of making me teleport around town. I'll take one decently developed character over 20 cardboard cutouts please. This town doesn't feel alive at all.

Finally, the story is kinda crap too. Well, it started out alright when it was slice-of-life stuff. Some of the subplots are passable, but the main conflict becomes so ridiculous that I don't even want to read the text anymore. I mean, okay, we're not gonna expect JRPGs to be hyper realistic, but......god, this is so asinine, it makes the writing in Lydie and Suelle read like Hugo award winning science fiction. The reocurring melodrama is also a chore. Ryza is more aggressive, confident, and outgoing when compared to a lot of other Atelier protagonists, and I definitely enjoy her the most when she's being Captain Ryza. Like, "We're going an adventure, and nothing is going to stop me. Wahaha." But later on the game, it feels like she's just brooding constantly. I think it would've been amazing if the main plot was them sailing a ship around and fighting pirates or something. But nope, instead it's giant albino cockroaches that can't swim (Why not make some water bombs, genius alchemists?) and they make everyone go 'angst angst angst angst angst'. *flips table*

So yeah. I'm gonna click that 'Yes' button, but I can't say I'm entirely happy about it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAllow me to begin this review with a caveat - while I have had some exposure to the Atelier series over the years, I wouldn't have considered myself a fan before playing this game. I understand that Ryza is divisive among long-time fans of the series, and after reading opinions of the same, I believe I can understand why they feel that Ryza has gone in the wrong direction.

I do consider myself a core JRPG fan, and in 2020, JRPG's seem to be split into two salients: niche titles, and AAA games that, for better or worse, are divorced from the genre's roots. From that perspective, Ryza is a compelling JRPG of depth, accessibility, and heart that manages to implement traditional JRPG design philosophies in a manner that feels modern, fresh, and exciting. It is also a case-in-point of how games with modest budgets can shine, given the quality of design and attention to detail on display here.

If you're considering buying this game, and consider yourself a fan of the genre, you should just take the plunge and enjoy yourself. However, there are some things you may want to know beforehand.

The main gameplay cycle involves exploring, gathering materials, and synthesizing items and equipment. 80-90% of your time will be spent doing this. The battle system is an ATB implementation, with a neat synergy mechanic wherein performing certain actions with the controlled character activates unique abilities in other party members. There's also a Bravely Default-like mechanic where you can bank action points and burn them for instant actions/interrupts. It's engaging, and requires some level of tactical thinking, but make no mistake -- the most direct predictor of your performance in battle is the quality of your gear, and the combat challenges are really tests of your crafting skill.

The script is more akin to a slice-of-life anime than anything else. It's heartfelt, and told in such a way that it encourages you to take the game at your own pace. This, combined with a generally comfy vibe, makes Atelier Ryza a great game for relaxing and winding down. It's a soothing, satisfying experience.

I realize also that the character designs have been the subject of controversy. I care little for such things, but make of them what you will. The sexual/fanservice content of the game itself is nil.

If you don't like JRPG's, I don't think this is your gateway drug. But if you are a fan of the genre, it's comfort food.\t\t\t\t\t
