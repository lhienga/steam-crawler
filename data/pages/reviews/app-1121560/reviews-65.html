\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs someone who got into the franchise with the Dusk trilogy, I feel that the recent Atelier games have been on a slow but steady decline. Sure, the production values have, in some regards, been improving but the ultimate appeal of the games have not. Atelier Ryza is exceptionally hand-holding and linear compared to the earlier entries in the franchise and I think that it's the primary reason why I find myself disengaged from the game.

In my opinion, the Atelier games are at their best when you are let loose to explore and play the alchemist at your own pace. Synthesising your way to success can be incredibly engrossing but only when you have the freedom to act as you please. In Atelier Ryza, you do what the games tells you to do. This is its downfall.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOne of the most solid JRPG's I've played this decade, with a very interesting gearing system that requires the player to essentially design and craft almost every piece of gear and usable item for every character in their party, except for a very limited amount of items you can get from treasure chests or merchants.

The story and characters are very dull and anime cliches, but at least they are wholesome. The combat system and alchemy system are more than enough to keep me interested and there are actually many side encounters which can be quite the challenge if you don't spoil yourself by reading guides about how to exploit the crafting system to make an overpowered party.

This is a really good game for a blind-playthrough experience. It is accessible enough for your to become powerful by experimenting and crafting, but not so obscure that you can hit a roadblock you cannot overcome, or by messing things up. A must play for fans of jrpgs.

The combat does require something like reflexes through, since you have to manage your party in real time. If you dont like 'action' jrpgs this may not be for you.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tStory: I kinda fell off the Atelier series when it moved away from your classic adventure narratives and into a more pure slice-of-life style, this feels like a healthy mix between the two that will be enjoyable to fans of both the old and new style.

Gameplay: Gameplay is also improved over previous installments, from a bog-standard turn-based RPG (which while I enjoy, is kinda very middle of the road) to a more active battle system that requires you to pay attention to triggers from your party members and to keep an eye on your health and AP to keep the flow of battle in your favor.

Of course, you also get the standard alchemy/crafting gameplay with a bit of extra visual flair to it.

Overall, the new systems took me a bit to fully utilize, but I started having more fun when I did, both on the alchemy side and the battle side.

Characters: As I've only played this for 7 hours (practically nothing in JRPG standards), I can't talk much about the characters, though the initial cast seems fine, I like your main trio enough to not be bothered by them.

Nothing groundbreaking here, if you've played any of the previous Atelier games, you'll know what to expect.

Music and Sound: Sound design is solid, nothing out of the ordinary so I guess the team did their job well enough, good work.

Music is great too, some of the tunes are pretty catchy and shine through when the occasion arises, but be prepared to hear the town theme a LOT.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI gave this game a negative review before because of the new battle system. Now I've completed the game and decided to give it a positive review instead.

- In the end I still can't stand this not-really-turn-based system, so I changed the difficulty to Easy. After that life became much better. I can't really make use of my items (because of the core charge thing) or have a item-focused match like I usually have before, but battle a big boss with a lot of HP feels better now since I can just watch the auto play.

- The story is not as fluffy as usual, but it has a certain depth that I like it. I don't have to grind as much as I usually do in the previous Atelier games (maybe thanks to the 100000 gem pack came with the Digital Deluxe version).

- I don't like the "bill board teleport" at first, but after a certain event the game give out a better teleport, and also a puni and a farm (they are not really useful, but feels having a lot of stuffs).

- The synthesis system is really fun fo play, how to morph an item to get new recipe is really nice too. Once again, save us a lot of time on grinding (in the mysterious series open new recipes sometime require grinding for ideal pts or synsthesis a bunch of the same items).

So with Easy mode, this game is really fun and chill. I still don't understand everything that happens in a battle, but managed to clear it, so it's fine.\t\t\t\t\t

With all the talk of 'thicc' and Ryza's tight shorts and all the massive thighs in this game I was worried about Gust turning Atelier into a game that's only about fan service and diminishing the quality of their games. I like cute anime girls as much as you all do, but I do also require good gameplay and a somewhat interesting story.

Ryza has streamlined some things such as making the alchemy easier to understand while still maintaining enough complexity that it's enjoyable.

Ryza herself is actually really wholesome, adorable and an overall great girl.

The combat took a bit to get used to since it was so different from the Arland games and Lulua which are my favorite games in the series. But I actually like it a lot now.

I saw some complaints about how tedious the open world aspect is, but once you unlock the map, Ryza becomes like any other Atelier game where you just fast travel everywhere.

Overall, while different in many ways, Ryza feels like an Atelier game to me. Albeit different, but still Atelier. The art is honestly pretty different from the games I've played in the franchise and that took the most getting used to. But Arland + Lulua will always have the best art in my opinion and, while Ryza is great, Totori is still best girl.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you are fan of turn based combat and dislike real time action - stay away from this one. Even as a fan of atelier series (and I've played every game since Atelier Iris) I'm not sure i will finish this one.

On the other hand the new alchemy system looks interesting.

Few points after finishing the game:
* Story is really good for atelier series.
* Miniquests are nicely integrated and feels like a part of the story, instead of the usual - kill this, get that.
* Alchemy system is fun with easy enough item duping.
* Combat system - didn't like it at the start, don't like after finishing. In hard battles it's too fast, removing tactical element of the game. In easy battles it's too slow - with turn based combat such battles are quickly resolved by smashing one button. Here you have to wait for character turn to come. Especially annoying when you try to gather something and some low level mob sneaks up. Starting combat without initial 10ap feels reaaaaaly slow.

Overall with the change to alchemy and combat I have feeling they tried to reach wider audiences rather than just giving atelier fans another game.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR: Great game, expanding upon its gameplay, and overall a game you'll enjoy.

Atelier Ryza gives you a good amount of play time for the price of the game. And I do heartily recommend it, especially if you're new to the Atelier games, because this is my first Atelier game, too. I've noticed no connection to the previous games (possibly missed?) and there's a good bunch of content and expansion to its gameplays (normal exploration, alchemy, and combat).

Everything is great at such a price (except the season pass for some reason?), and Atelier Ryza is (as far as I've played through) bug free, with just some minor glitches (some shadows will have some diagonal stripes, but isn't that big of a deal) and a overseen name (Pamila's name is supposed to be Pamela?).

To end it with, I'll say that Atelier Ryza is a good example of what games of today should do more of, giving more for what you pay, without being obnoxious in terms of time requirement, not doing the dreaded "FOMO" strategy, and expanding further of gameplay rather than just sticking to the basics.

And I do not want to use numeric ratings and just say: Great game for its price, and introduction for potentiel for new players to explore the rest of the franchise.\t\t\t\t\t
