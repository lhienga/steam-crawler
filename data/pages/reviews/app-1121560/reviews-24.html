\r\n\t\t\t\t\t6/10 Mild Recommendation.
Note: This is my first time with Atelier franchise. I'm not into crafting games normally. Wanted to try a new type of JRPG that was well received and this seemed unique from others I've played.

I took a chance on this game to see if the franchise is for me. My conclusion is that its not. This game was good enough for me to play it through but I'm not in love with the game play loop. I would probably say this is my favorite crafting game of all time because its the one I've spent the most time with.

Ryza definitely has its charm. Characters are likeable and the story is very simple for a long time before things really start to happen. The game play largely works fine especially for the first half of the game. There's some fun here for sure but to me the mechanics and crafting hunt got out of hand by the end.

Ultimately the story and writing were not engaging enough to recommend this game for its story or characters. It's decent...but nothing special. Character development and growth does occur but its very basic.

I recommend the game because I see the appeal even if it didn't land with me. Almost nothing about this game was bad. Though I did find myself resorting to google and youtube for component locations far more than I would have liked. I see the appeal of this franchise even if I don't see myself engaging with it again.\t\t\t\t\t

Being an alchemist, you gotta keep the cauldron bubbling…

The game start with the story of Reisalin Stout, a young girl living on the quiet island of Kurken who desperately dreams of escaping on far-flung adventures alongside her childhood friends, Tao and Lent. Ryza lives on a farm with her parents who try at every opportunity to stymie her dreams, preferring that she stay at home and live a quiet, simple and safe life.

The other members of the group have similarly troubled existences, bookworm Tao suffers constantly at the hands of relentless local bullies while Lent has all manner of problems dealing with his abusive alcoholic father. All three wish desperately to leave their worries behind and make something more of themselves and when they happen upon Empel and Lila, a pair of mysterious alchemists investigating some local ruins, they get the opportunity they’ve been waiting for.

In terms of the story, it consists of excellent narrative that is well paced and packs with emotional punch. The plot is thoughtfully paced and heartfelt especially in term of characters development as they did a fantastic job on detailing Ryza’s journey from a complete beginner to master alchemist. This very aspect made Ryza, Tao and Lent are extremely likable and strong central characters.

The alchemy system is also brilliantly deep and user friendly. I also enjoy the reinvigorated combat that lifts the entire gaming experience up to new heights. Not to mention, the graphics engine is impressive as they look gloriously beautiful. It was a very eye-opening sight.

Overall, I genuinely find the game pretty worthwhile as my first Atelier’s gaming experience. I have never touched any of the Atelier series before, so I am literally going into Atelier Ryza: Ever Darkness and the Secret Hideout vacantly. But now, I am even more excited to play its second season, Atelier Ryza 2: Lost Legends & the Secret Fairy while waiting for its third season.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThere are so many quality of life issues and nonsense developer design choices in this game that I imagine the only way this passed playtesting is that all the testers were heavily distracted, as I was, by the thighs on screen instead of reporting gameplay issues to the higher ups.

Overall game is good. Beautiful art and environments. Pleasant music. Combat is smooth. Crafting in unique and has some depth. The biggest grind in this jrpg is gathering materials for synthesis. The gameplay loop is as follows: watch cutscenes, explore new zone, gather everything in sight, go back and synthesis for days. Repeat.

Some of the more egregious QoL/design issues that I gotta get off my chest:
- Farming and customising your hideout is useless. (since you can teleport directly into your house, you don't even need to go outside lol)
- Horrible UI clutter and menu designs (synthesis/item filtering consists of mainly scrolling through a long lists)
- Bosses have party wipe mechanics that require breaking their stance, but the conditions to execute combo moves that do so are random.
- New cutscenes auto-play when you enter a zone. This means when you teleport somewhere there is a chance you are forced to watch consecutive cutscenes.
- Too many invisible walls, can't jump over small ledges/rocks.
- Item uses in combat are too limited use. Time also does not pause when you need to look through your items to find the right one.
- Treasure maps require you to go to 5 different locations, but each time you must return to your hideout to view where the next one is. (so you spend 5-10 minutes just teleporting back and forth)

Also this game may give you motion sickness if you are playing below 100fps, as the camera focus is centered on Ryza's ass without giving you the option to tilt it upwards.

But anyway the thighs tho.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really wanted to like this game. I've being eyeing the franchise for a long time, but was always put off by the shallow discounts on any of first games in the various sub-series.

Ryza is just very clunky. It feels like a game that was designed to be developed early in the PS2 era and then was forgotten about for over a decade. The environments are full of nonsensical invisible walls, there's a long animation for collecting anything, the opening hours of the game are glacial, it takes too many button clicks to do anything, there's tons of mechanics all with the depth of a single drop of water, all the interactable NPC's are completely static, etc. A personal pet peeve is that the game CONSTANTLY moves the player character in cut scenes to some other location.

The battle system is likewise pretty mediocre. The mechanics with the active time bar are neat, but the hopeless party AI absolutely obliterates any enjoyment of them. If you let your party members off the leash their AI is too dense to actually manage and prioritise their skills, but if you don't let the AI at least try to be useful you'll often find yourself swapping to a character to respond to something only to find them smacking the enemy with a wet noodle resulting in a wait you probably can't afford.

The characters visual design is a high point, but unfortunately their writing is very thin, across the board. The overall presentation is passable at best - music is middling, the sound design borders on non-existent much of the time, the cinematography would feel very at home on the aforementioned PS2, etc.

I'm really disappointed.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is the first of another trilogy in the long running Atelier crafting RPG series. This one did a lot of changes to the formula and mechanics, some of which worked and some fell flat. I'm a bit mixed on this one, but would still recommend it if only because it leads into the wonderful Ryza 2.

Graphically it's a big improvement over previous titles. The terrain is far more detailed, and the use of lighting is extremely well done. I run on Linux and it works almost flawlessly. The only issue I have is more with running with 75 FPS monitors, I get graphical stutters every 2-3 seconds. By limiting the FPS to 60 (using the command parameters: DXVK_FRAME_RATE=60 %command%) I got perfect performance; the same can likely be done with Windows.

The art style is OK, with a lot more fanservice compared to some previous trilogies, like Ryza's and especially Lila's design. That said, there are no creepy hot springs/bathing scenes like in Ayesha and Shallie. The rest of the character designs and monster art are great. The bosses look wonderful and feel good to fight.

Sound and music are also well done as expected from an Atelier game. There are no longer any dubbed voices, everything is in Japanese. It's mostly good, though some idle dialogue (like during crafting or gem reduction) is vastly overused and can get frustrating.

Mechanically I'm a bit more mixed. A big part of this game was changing the formula and experimenting, and a lot of aspects just fell flat and didn't work out well.

Combat is a ATB style system, where combat is done in real time and your actions come up on a bar on the left side of the screen. When your icon reaches the bottom your turn is up, and you can attack, use an item, or use a skill. Your two active allies will just use normal attacks to build up AP unless you switch to aggressive mode, where they will use their skills. As you do normal attacks (which are quite weak), you build up AP, which can be spent on skills. You can also spend an entire full stock of built up AP to upgrade your tactics level, which boosts the power of some skills, makes it easier to gain AP, and allows for better triggered abilities. You can also spend 10 AP (by default, some characters can reduce this cost) to trigger a quick action when it's not your turn and use a skill or item. You can also switch between your 3 active party members. Your allies will list special actions that you can do to trigger a support skill, these can be things like using magical damage, using an item, or using a fire skill. Trying to maximize the effect of these is quite important.

Another big part of combat is that enemies have a "stun" bar, and stunning an enemy will cancel their next turn. If you manage to stun an enemy while it's already stunned, they'll be pushed way back on the bar and you can lock them down for a while. This makes the Craft and Lightning Bell items extremely powerful, as they do the most stun damage. Some skills also have high stun power. Locking down foes is pretty much essential for late game bosses.

Items are a source of contention. Ryza abandons the classic two crafted item styles (consumable items, then items with charge counts refreshed when you return home), and instead uses a global set of charges that apply to the entire party. All your characters can use items now, not just alchemists, however there are limitations (the dexterity stat) that determines how powerful of items each character can use. The global set of charges starts at 10, and can be upgraded a bit with treasure hunts and sidequests. To restore used charges, you will need to disable the use of an item for the remainder of your trip out of Ryza's room or the titular Secret Hideout. This can be done from the item menu, by slowly holding a button while the combat happens around you; or during a quick action (which costs no ATB time). It's good to have your allies item slots full so that you can "sacrifice" items to refill your charges during tough or long fights.

Crafting is quite different from previous games, in this one there's an interface, sort of like a molecule diagram, where you have nodes you can put items into to unlock effects and unlock the connections to the nearby nodes. You then navigate the nodes until you have an item to your preference or you run into the limit of crafting items able to be put in. This number increases as you raise your crafting level, so raising the crafting level is quite important to making useful items. There are also special nodes that change the recipe into something else, which gives you even more "slots" to drop crafting items into. A large complex craft can involve dozens of items and multiple recipe morphs.

Another important aspect of crafting is that you can destroy crafting items to make gems, these gems are used as your currency for duplicating items, for generating miniature gathering areas (not the best system but a good idea), and for item rebuilds. Item rebuilds takes an item you crafted (not bought from a store) and lets you add more crafting items into it to raise its effects and power. It's a VERY powerful system that can make some ludicrously powerful battle items and gear. It's possible for top end gear or battle items to be built out of 50-100 crafting items.

Finally the story, which is not the best. The worldbuilding is decent, but the other aspects aren't that great. A big issue of Ryza is the pacing. Way way too often you will need to spend an in-game day (there is no time limit like in older Atelier games, but there's still an in-game calendar and day/night system) in order for the next event to happen. You're expected to do a little gathering, combat, or synthesis to pass the time but it really breaks up the flow of the story. I was frustrated more than anything else, especially with the sheer amount of scenes that were necessary to progress through the final quarter of the game, each one requiring a day's wait. In addition, character scenes that were a mainstay of previous Atelier games are no longer present, which hurts the characterization and "cozy" feeling of spending time with your companions.

Overall, it's a good game but not a great game. It may not be the best game for longer time Atelier fans, since so much of the previous norms of the series are changed. That said, it's still worth playing as Atelier Ryza 2 is a great game in the series and Ryza 2 would be a terrible entry point (Ryza 2 expects you to know a lot of details from Ryza 1). Story pacing is terrible. Combat feels a bit clunky, but has a lot of potential that was improved upon in Ryza 2. Crafting is both complex and simple if you're used to previous Atelier games. You can make ludicrously powerful gear way easier in Ryza, but basic alchemy takes a lot longer to do and can sometimes feel overwhelming with the sheer number of crafting items needed. A lot of the side aspects just didn't work out. Pick it up on sale, get through the main and most of the side content, then enjoy Ryza 2.\t\t\t\t\t
