Story

R&C: Rift Apart opts for a general audiences narrative, which unfortunately strips the experience of soul. Make no mistake, meeting NPCs is still fun. The weapon vendor Mrs. Zurkon is a highlight because of Zurkon Jr's hilarious previews (I wish him the best in his Existential Economics course) and Pierre's one-sided love/hate relationship with Rivet was a delight. However, character flaws are so minor they (a) barely make a ripple on your moral compass, and (b) they're completely exorcised by game's end after a heavily rushed 'redemption arc' if you could call it that, which makes everything feel extremely bland. Kit's struggle to overcome her dark past (as giga warbot) could have been more impactful if she was still loyal to Emperor Nefarious and Rivet slowly convinced her that true friendship was more valuable than blind subservience to a megalomaniac overlord. Dr. Nefarious had the chance to outdo himself by temporarily siding with R&C to take down mutual enemy Emperor Nefarious, and then quickly revert to his typical dastardly self. Instead, disappointingly, Insomniac turns to surface level character growth that quickly dissipates once the action wraps up. If you thought the 2016 reboot was toned down, then Rift Apart has practically been chiselled into emotional oblivion. Creatively, it's always harder to follow up on a bold, original idea with something equally impressive, so it makes a great deal of sense that devs would take the road most travelled: keep it lighthearted, snip anything potentially offensive, make the final product palatable to all. But is the trade-off worth it in the end?

Graphics

A visually outstanding product with clean, shiny character and environmental design that's more life-like than ever before. I took the raytracing for granted in a lot of rooms/didn't always notice it in outdoor areas, but it makes a huge difference anywhere that includes windows. Ratchet and Rivet's fur textures look so soft! The ear physics are also highly realistic; ears bob up and down and flap as the character model walks/runs which isn't entirely 'new' compared to the 2016 reboot (or the 2002 game) but it's a definite upgrade. My PC just meets the recommended settings for Rift Apart (NVIDIA GeForce RTX 2070 Super, AMD Ryzen 7 3700X, 16GB DDR4) so perhaps it's to be expected that frame drops would occur with alarming regularity. They were particularly noticeable during swinging/grind rail sequences, but the worst drops (25-35fps) struck during busy animated fights towards the end of the game (inducing dizziness) and dimension hopping (literal freezes even with the latest graphics driver). I've read mixed performance reviews from people with better PC specs so ymmv, but Rift Apart would probably run better on its native PlayStation 5. I also had a weird visual glitch where Zurkon Jr previews were temporarily blank/unplayable, and a single crash (when I tried messing around with Photo mode), so hopefully Insomniac/Nixxes continue optimising the game in the coming months.

Gameplay

If you liked the gunplay over platforming ratio that the 2016 reboot delivered, you'll love this. Rift Apart's 'hurr durr big gun go boom' approach to combat is immensely satisfying and the range of weapons is good, too. My go-to was the Drillhound because blowing up robots and thugs with a giant, barking explosive with good AoE is pure awesomeness. The Headhunter (unlocked toward the end) was also cool because of the sniper/headshot ability. General enemy diversity is fine, but the main bosses weren't as fun as R&C 2016 because they lack unique attacks and aren't really memorable. The penultimate planet Viceron contained back-to-back mob fights which quickly grew so monotonous that when I realised Insomniac had packed in even more unnecessary battles before the finale with the two Nefariouses, I sadly just wanted the game to end. I enjoyed the Glitch minigames but Ratchet's puzzles were pretty lacklustre. Nothing really stood out within levels, either. I still remember the epic train section in the 2016 game, and those punishing but rewarding hoverboard races where mastering the navigation was a triumph in itself. None of that spark carries over to Rift Apart; you can use hoverboots to zip across terrain at light speed, which is fun, but their implementation in challenges (eg saving monks on Savali) feels so tame and safe in comparison to the skill required in older games. I wanna feel the rush damnit! Speaking of questionable decisions, there's a certain trophy (Shifty Character) which requires you to hit every single blizon crystal on both Blizar and Cordelion. Now I know this probably only matters if you're a completionist/trophy hunter, and most players would naturally hit every crystal as they're not too difficult to find. But! Should you happen to miss any whilst grinding on the rail, you cannot return to that area later as Insomniac weirdly decided to lock the elevator that would otherwise grant access. Some legend discovered a workaround for this, to help players from doing another playthrough from scratch, but it was inexplicably patched on PlayStation 5 and the PC port of course released much later and incorporates the patch. This is a very frustrating oversight (similar to the problem with exploring sections of Dathomir in Jedi Fallen Order if you want to get 100% collectibles AFTER the main story has progressed) and Insomniac could easily make the elevator permanently accessible in future patches. If, like me, you happened to miss one blizon on the rail, you can still get back up the elevator using the method listed below. Otherwise, I strongly recommend using a guide to collect everything correctly on your first playthrough.

Audio

Rift Apart's OST is a little bit on the generic side for epic orchestral music. Tastes vary significantly so I know these melodies will really gel with some people, but none truly interrupted my stream of consciousness in the way games like Hogwarts Legacy did (fairest comparison I could think of). The one exception was the Nefarious credits song. Absolute genius and very catchy, playful, circus-like tune that captures Insomniac's sense of humour oh so well. Rift Apart also excels in weapon sound effects, which are one of the many reasons combat feels so good, and the voice-acting is solid across the board. Special praise for the portrayals of Zurkon Jr and Dr. Nefarious which are absolutely flawless and steal the scene every damn time. Bravo.

Final verdict

6.5/10. Short and fun, but lacks the inventiveness and pure adrenaline of older titles. Insomniac needed to take more risks. Buy on sale only (preferably 50% off) not at full price.\t\t\t\t\t
