\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI want to preface my recommendation with this: the game has some tremendous flaws that make it a down-grade from the PS2 era games. It isn't a bad game, but it is more style than substance. I guess I should be thankful that it doesn't feel like wasting your time with a giant open-world and collectibles like most games want to do.

Lots of people seem to either hate or love Rivet and Kit. Kit's the standout character of the game for me, mostly because of her personality. I deeply appreciate more grounded characters with less "zany" attributes in video games, and having one that's realistically awkward, contemplative, considerate, and hesitant is a nice break from the norm. I don't have much to say about Rivet: she's written as the generic hothead shonen protagonist. Some of her dialogue and reactions tread dangerously close to "well that just happened" levels of ineptitude, but that line is never crossed. I actually wouldn't hate to see more games with them included, but I don't know how well that would work, or how it'd be received by the playerbase.

I don't have much of an opinion about Ratchet and Clank's depictions in this game, since I fell off the series when it stopped being released on PS2. The last game I played chronologically was Deadlocked. With the reboot that happened some years ago (and its reception from fans), I don't know what their characterizations are supposed to be.

The game's level design is, in a word, cinematic. In older games, each level was a unique shooting gallery, and the fun was figuring out how to best get through it. Or, when you were sufficiently stacked, to just level the shooting gallery with superior firepower. Levels in this game (with the exception of Sargasso) really aren't designed like this: you go to a planet, play through it to experience what happens, then you leave. There isn't much reason to go back to places you've already visited, because you can only watch the big set-piece once per playthrough. That, and the game completely gutted the idea of gadgets.

In previous games, certain areas of certain planets were blocked off until you possessed the necessary item. It could be a digital lock-pick for doors, a way to traverse a large gap, something to make a door or break the floor: something that made you need to come back later when you have the right tool. This worked with the level design of the older games, especially since you could visit planets out-of-order. Rift Apart's level design only includes one instance where a new gadget is used on an older planet, and it's at Zurkie's, so it doesn't even count.

Speaking of, the arena's offerings are laughably poor. There's one arena with a grand total of fifteen matches, and you can't grind them all out because of the story. Going Commando had two arenas, and a plethora of arena challenges, which you could grind out in one sitting if you so chose. The arena concept may have hit its peak with Deadlocked, where the whole game was an arena challenge, but I wish Zurkie's had more to offer than cutscenes and 15 measly bouts.

I don't have much to say about the weapons: they're the standard fare for an R&C game. I wish there were a disability option to tie half-trigger pulls to the left-trigger instead: half-pulls don't work well with my XBOX controller, and it triggers my carpel tunnel when I do try to use it too much.

The rift puzzles and Glitch segments were actually really fun, and I kinda wish the game had more of them. This ties into the point about gadgets I made earlier: some of the lockpick style gadgets in older games actually had really fun minigames tied to them. Maybe there's extra ones in this game's Insomniac Museum, if they still include it, but I never found it. For those who might not already know, the Insomniac Museum is a recurring feature in the older R&C games that was basically a development museum for the game. It was all developer commentary, and sometimes even included a playable beta for some cut content, like road racing in Up Your Arsenal.

All said, I think the game is charming for what it is, but I don't think it's a good R&C game. It's got lots of production value, so it's pretty to look at, and the characters/story are just "fine". The gameplay is lacking in almost every regard compared to the PS2-era games, which is disappointing. I wish some of the development time had gone into making better levels, rather than lengthy cinematic shots of levels being destroyed or of the player falling through rifts. Oh, and the collectibles are a joke: they're not well hidden nor hard to get to. I accidentally picked up most of them on my first playthrough. Bring back the nanotech boost hidden in the glider section from Going Commando: make me get mad and look up a guide for collecting your "hidden" collectibles.\t\t\t\t\t
