\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's an interesting final fantasy. I'm working my way through chapter 10 now and the story is enjoyable. However, I cannot recommend the game and don't believe anyone should take the time to play through it for the following reasons:

1. No volume control. There is no way adjust any audio settings in game. The game is loud as hell. I've got my computer volume on 4/100 and it's still exceptionally loud. It's frustrating when I have to go into discord to turn the boys up to 200 and my computer down to 4 to get any sort of balance.

2. The AI is one of the worst I've seen. As a mechanic to the game there are preemptive strikes. You get the jump on enemies and this starts them 0.1% off of being staggered (where they take huge damage). I'm sitting there just hoping my allies use an AoE attack to stagger them all at once to maximize our damage out the gate. However, not only do the allies only use single target spells with random AoE spells tossed in (you never know when it's going to happen or why the AI decides to use it when they do), your character's recommended attacks are also always single target. This is just a weird decision when building the AI that was never fixed or adjusted. It's super frustrating and makes the preemptive strikes very difficult to take advantage of because you have to manually set things yourself if you want to accomplish any early AoE. In a combat system that has allies and enemies randomly walking around the battle map during combat, this overwhelmingly ends in failure.

3. Camera controls. Quite possibly the worst camera controls in a game that I have played to date. The camera is sluggish and fights back any time you move. With tight corners in many maps, it makes getting a good look at enemy pathing incredibly difficult. There is also no camera sensitivity controls which means there's really nothing you can do about.


So, overall the story is pretty good and the combat system is a neat take on what could have been just another turn-based JRPG. It had many cool ideas that were not well executed. Save yourself the time and money and just watch a let's play.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt took owning three different versions of this game since 2009 for me to actually finish it, and I'm glad I did, even if it took nearly XIII years for me to do so.

Final Fantasy XIII is often met with lukewarm praise by even the most ardent defenders of the Final Fantasy franchise, but I really quite like it. Not every character in every game needs to be as profoundly loved as FFVII's Cloud, and this was the point in the series where the key mechanics were due for a change. Some of these worked, some of those didn't. The protagonist here, Lightning, is perfectly fine as a conduit to journey with throughout this world and despite being the game's alleged protagonist, this does end up being a linear ensemble story with specific party members excluded or forced upon you at sett intervals.

Everything about this game is a little more linear than the FF games that have come before, and does seem to present as a very "on-rails" experience. Even the character progression and weapon enhancement never ask the player to make any meaningful choices. You just acquire and dump points. Character builds and job diversity are lacking compared to many of the games before, and this contributes to a feeling of "restrictivness" for franchise veterans, while is likely to help new players greatly.

The game exists as a linear experience for roughly 30 hours before dumping you into a much more open area with options, side missions, chocobos and a choice of party members you never had until that point. This jarring switch up causes much of the game's pacing problems and is the exact mirror of Final Fantasy XV (which spent the first 30 hours selling you the open world only to stuff you into a linear final section).

Lightning's adventure isn't likely to capture your heart if you're relying on that alone, but solid mechanics and a handful of likeable characters do enough to make this a competent, if not spectacular game in the series.

The graphics hold up quite well, but there are significant issues with the launcher and running this on some versions of Windows causes problems that require you to make Registry Edits. There are also very few quality of life adjustments made for a PC or contemporary audience - you're just getting the PS3/Xbox 360 experience you would have got in 2009 on your computer.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm a sucker for sci-fantasy, and this fits the bill. I really like the combat system -- it's a lot different than the FFs 1-9 that I'm familiar with, and switching paradigms to get the abilities you need is pretty fun. I like the gist of the story and the world we're in thus far too.

At the time of release, people complained a lot about this game being on rails. Rails is kinda what I want right now, and it's working for me. Extremely beautiful game, considering the age.

I had to install a very popular patch to get over janky framerate issues, but now it plays like a dream. Very happy I got this for < 20 bucks, it's well worth it.

Edit: ~60 hours later, and ... wow. There were so many things I loved about this game, but the main two were:

- The cast: I've heard that the characters in this game were polarizing, but this was the first FF cast where I really felt like I loved all of them.
- The battle system: I was learning new stuff about the battle system right up until the final boss. There's a lot you can do with team makeup, accessories, weapons, and paradigms to drastically alter the course of battles, and this remained super engaging all the way to the end.

Incredible game. I really did not expect this to be so enjoyable. It gets in its own way a lot, but what's there is quite incredible.\t\t\t\t\t
