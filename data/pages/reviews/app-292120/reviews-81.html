Blinded by light from Dissidia Duodecim brought me here and I’m glad it did.

Final Fantasy XIII…..Finally, my 2nd FF series that I actually finish. Back when the game first came out, it was a port disaster and it still is, with the random FPS drop here and there. But I decided to press on and kept on playing and during my playthrough, I stumble upon a fan patch that fixed the performance issue. That changes the experience of my playthrough and made the game a lot more enjoyable.

The Pros
  • Top notch cutscene animation as expected of SE
  • Story is a bit complicated but will make a lot more sense as you progress
  • Very active combat mechanics
  • Game is on the tough side, going to need trial and error
  • Sazh and his antics
  • Blinded by light
What got me hooked to this game is obviously the soundtrack since that’s what brought me here in the first place. But if had to take that out of the equation then It’ll be combat mechanics it’s a bit different from the usual turn-based combat that FF series used to offer (I’ve only played I, IV, and Crisis Core). The game forces you to be a lot more active and switch between stances in order to survive the fight, brute-forcing your way through and going all out from the get-go won’t get you far in the game cause some bosses have a ridiculous amount of HP.

During most of your playthrough, the game would feel like a hallway until you reach Gran pulse on ch.11, after that you can say goodbye to those very linear maps and hello huge open-world map. Pro-tip, try clearing Cieth stone 14 before anything else, would make traveling a whole easier and make small Gil on the side at least while you’re still on Gran pulse. The thing that I like the most about this map is the changing weather effect and for some mobs instead of staying put, they roam around the map making the environment feel more alive.

The last few things that charmed me were the character development and story. Those two are well executed and delivered in a way it kept me hooked for hours during each session. The story can be a bit confusing at first since you’re suddenly thrown in a huge skirmish with little to no info on what’s going on but, as I’ve said before, as you kept playing everything will make a lot more sense.

The Cons
Aside from the lousy port which you can fix with the patch, I didn’t experience any game-breaking bugs or random crashes during my playthrough. But the one thing that I didn’t enjoy as much was doing the side quests….the game really loves making you go from one end of the map to the other….I mean the Cieth Stone locations are spread out everywhere and I mean everywhere….I wouldn’t be able to finish (or at least would take a lot more time to clear) those if I didn’t unlock Chocobo first…god bless Chocobo.

Verdict
So, did I enjoy the game? most definitely! Big kudos to the fan patch for fixing the port and made my playthrough a lot more enjoyable. After playing through the game, I can understand why it’s either a hit or miss for some people but do give it a try, since it might grow on you. This is one of those games that starts a bit slow and gets better later on. All in all, if you enjoy JRPG’s I can confidently say this would make a fine addition to your library. I highly recommend it

The story is fun, even though it's confusing. Some mechanics are interesting like leveling up through the crystarium and changing paradigms during battle.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFinal Fantasy XIII isn't as bad as some might make it to be but it does have some flaws and to be honest it doesn't feel like a Final Fantasy game which isn't inherently bad but it might throw some people off especially given the weak beginning of the game. By far the biggest flaw is how confusing the first 3-4 chapters of the game can be and this is because they just dump a lot of information that isn't explained at all and throw you into the action immediately. The beginning isn't difficult mechanically since the combat is very simple at first maybe even too simple to hook players but it gets better as the game progress. There are 13 chapters just to demonstrate a bit how long it can last but thankfully after the terms are clearly defined and explained everything feels more coherent even thought it's still not the best story a Final Fantasy game has to offer for sure it is still interesting enough at least to me to keep me going. It is very linear, like run down this narrow corridor, fight some monsters (some might even literally block the path) and watch a cutscene and this is a thing for the entire game aside from chapter 11 which offer some side content in a slightly more open area everything else is rather restrictive with no NPCs to talk to, no towns, no exploration or side content to offer. It is not to say it's not good, the pre-rendered cutscenes are beautiful, the music is good and the voice acting (at least in japanese which is what I selected) is pristine and still to this day great graphics.

The gameplay is also rather interesting, it retains the ATB gauge but time doesn't ever stop however there are 2 options for selecting actions auto or manual and each action have various amount of the ATB gauge necessary to be used. Sadly it is only possible to control 1 character in this game but there are paradigm shifts which essentially are formations where you can select which role each character may fulfill and it is essential to shift multiple times in battle at least for the more difficult ones. It is essential because there is a mechanic to stagger enemies and consequently do more damage when they enter that state meaning that there is a role designed for it like there is one to deal more damage, tank, debuff, buff and heal. Paradigm shifting is the most important part of battle and while the character direct management isn't as involved it makes you feel like some sort of commander directing troops in combat which is kind of fun actually. There is one problem with paradigm shifting it's that every time they do a small animation and enemies do not stop moving or attacking while this is happening and it can lead to free hits for enemies if you're not careful which can be a bit difficult at times. Another aspect I don't personally favour is the star rating after each fight dictating the rewards, it isn't difficult to get 5 stars but I still find the system superfluous. Last complaint would be how useless eidolons feel in this game, they are cool looking and all but they feel a bit weak and just there because it's a Final Fantasy game and it's a staple. To conclude gameplay the levelling system is simple, each character have their role they are a bit better at and each fight grant some CP which is the unit used to unlock nodes which can be abilities or passives differing from one role to another and some character unlock abilities earlier than others so it gives them something to be different from one another.

Story as mentioned already is good but it does a poor job at telling its story and the beginning is a big information dump that almost led me to stop playing early but after giving it time and making sure I understood what I heard by reading some datalogs everything was fine and I enjoyed the plot much more. Some characters are rather hit or miss, Hope made me want scream "Say it already!" for a good period and after he thankfully matured quite fast and was totally fine. Lightning and Hope development was probably the best part but I also appreciate Sazh and Vanille a fair bit. The ones with the least development are Snow and Fang I'd say which both are kind of there but don't get too much attention especially Fang but they have some moments to them. It's not the best cast of characters but they're doing fine and I didn't dislike any of them particularly. It's very sci-fi compared to any other Final Fantasy out there I'd say and being not my favourite setting it might have influenced my opinion of the story so take it with a grain of salt.

Conclusion being it's a good game that feels quite different from past Final Fantasy games I've played (3, 7, 8, 9, 10, 10-2, 12) but it's an interesting experience for someone that do not mind the linearity and can get past the confusing beginning. The port didn't give me any problem with crashes but I had some lag in 1-2 areas otherwise it was a smooth ride and I didn't use mods or tweaks. It's not what I would recommend as an entry point but it's a decent game to play if you're just looking at playing another Final Fantasy. 7/10\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFinal Fantasy XIII is good, actually.

It's even a great game. It's a different game than anything that's come before it in the series and was released in a really poor time: In 2009 JRPGs were seen in the west as a "dying genre", open world games were the current hotness, and the game was marketed pretty heavily here as an action game when it's still, through and through, a JRPG.

The game isn't perfect, by any means, but a lot of its flaws were magnified by the above context and given it a stigma as the Final Fantasy series black sheep that's stuck with it.

Which left me pleasantly surprised when I finally sat down to play it (after steam brought it down to like $7 so I could at least say "At least I didn't waste much money on it") and by the time the credits rolled, it was a strong contender for my favorite Final Fantasy in the mainline series.

Let's get two things out of the way, two of the very common cricitisms: the game IS very linear early on and the game does take a really long time (even by JRPG standards) to "fully come online" and be full gameplay. Both are perfectly valid criticisms, but I think both have tended to get overblown in the game's legacy. Final Fantasy has always been a linear series - this one just hews more linear than most.

The time it takes the gameplay to get to full speed is long and a harder criticism to disagree with, but the time to get to that point isn't entirely without its entertainment. The game follows a structure where it focuses on different combinations of characters in different places, experiencing the same time frame and the focus switches between them. This allows for some incredible character interactions, where each ends up feeling fully fleshed out and with their own arc in relation to the others rather than in previous instances where you'd find many of the secondary characters shallower compared to the main focus character and certain others (See: Squall and Rinoa vs Irvine and Selphie, for example, where the former are quite fleshed out and the latter are forgotten about until the plot needs them to exist). I think that, while this act of separating characters and focusing on their small interactions does limit gameplay options during these periods which many Final Fantasy fans may not be used to, it has the even more important benefit of making the moment of when the characters come together, functioning as a team, feel all the more triumphant because every character feels fleshed out and real. They've earned the right to be called "a party" - they've shaken off their initial impressions and become well-rounded characters.

This aspect of character development taking center stage over plot focus for the first half of the game is a new attempt for Final Fantasy (except for, maybe, VI). It's not the only thing that contributes to making this one feel way more different than anything previous. Typical Final Fantasy story elements and even monsters are twisted and reworked and reflected in a new light. Characters are written in ways to defy the usual tropes of Final Fantasy protagonists. Combat becomes an absolute joy once you reach the point of the game where it's fully open to you and never feels like a grind.

The game is great, but the port is so-so. There are some issues with the quality of FMVs, framerate can bounce around a bit, and there's some crashes. For that, I guess, you'll have to blame Square's PC Port budget of twelve dollars, like usual. Fan made tools are plentiful to get the game running up to a caliber expected of a modern PC game, and apparently the Windows Store version is better?

Either way, if you're a fan of Final Fantasy, if you like heartfelt character-driven stories, and you like to see what happens when the devs of a long running series upend some series traditions, don't let this one pass you by. I promise it will work its magic on you, if you let it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is difficult for me to 100% recommend, but I want to start by saying it's far, far better than the years of negative reception that has plagued the game since its release ever suggested. I avoided it since launch because I always had heard it was just an irredeemably bad game. A few things I want to highlight that I often heard as horrible, game destroying issues that actually aren't that bad:

- The game is a hallway simulator. Yes, the game has a very linear approach to progression, though the later game isn't as strictly the case. That said, it's never a complaint I see for the beloved FFX, a game with the same fundamental linear progression and limited open areas. If linearity isn't your bag, I get it, but as someone that has played many JRPGs from the most traditional to most modern, I didn't find this to degrade the experience, especially with beautiful environments making up the hallways you travel.

- The lore is supposedly all locked in the menu option referred to as the Data Log. This is not entirely true. A huge chunk of the Data Log is just rehashing cut scenes, story progression, and character details the game organically provides in the storytelling, as well as the bestiary. The one thing that is hard locked in the Data Log is location lore, but that's more a consequence of the game design that doesn't allow you to organically learn world information like you often do in other JRPGs. I promise the Data Log isn't hours of required reading though, and by the last few chapters, I was largely ignoring it.

- The characters are supposedly love them or hate them, no in between. This is of course by far going to be the most subjective positive or negative, but I personally took no great issue with any given character and actually enjoyed the character arcs.

- The story is supposedly too complicated, but calling back once again to the beloved FFX, I found nothing difficult to understand about the story in this game except some of the naming that the game will make sure you know quite well early on anyways.

It was refreshing to find that all these supposed horrible, game destroying flaws were nothing but subjective opinions. That isn't to say the game is free of things to criticize, and so I'll note my personal grievances below:

- This game went through development hell and multiple directors, and you can tell as you play through the game. Without getting too specific, I will say Chapter 11 to the end almost feels like a completely different game, and that isn't necessarily a good thing even if introduced elements are otherwise a step up. Lack of consistency in game design is the biggest issue this game suffers.

- The battle system is actually pretty solid, but it takes several chapters before you start really seeing the depth, while also seeing the flaws especially of the auto battle system and not being able to directly control more than your leader character and the class composition of your team during battle. You also don't get to control your party composition freely until the end of Chapter 9.

- The enemies are a mixed bag, and its clear not everyone working on this game entirely understood what they were doing with enemy design in relation to it. Especially the late game enemies will require very specific class compositions to stand a chance, and even then, there can be a horrible amount of luck involved.

- Boss battles are by and large great, but there's one specific type of more puzzle type battle that is just not good. There's only a handful of them in the game, but they don't feel fun in the slightest. If you play the game, you'll know exactly what I'm talking about by the end of Chapter 2 (though that one isn't as bad as the rest).

Keeping those negatives in mind, I will say I adored the game through Chapter 9, thought Chapter 10 was decent, nearly quit the game regularly from Chapter 11 to the end. The one thing this game really has going for it was that I found the story compelling, and I've heard the direct sequel really helps to clean up a lot of the flaws in this title, some things for better, some maybe for a bit worse depending on what you want. You have some minor nitpicks like the battle camera sometimes being a chore, the first paradigm shift in any battle being a recipe for disaster if mistimed, but nothing as major the main criticisms I outlined above.

If this game was still $60, I wouldn't recommend it. At $16, you don't have much to lose, and you get a game that holds up very well graphically with one hell of a good soundtrack. If you go in knowing the true negatives to be wary of, you likely won't end up as down as I did when the game transitions into different director visions, and it sounds like the trilogy gets things together going forward, so I'd say give it a go and maybe you'll find a new favorite FF game.\t\t\t\t\t
