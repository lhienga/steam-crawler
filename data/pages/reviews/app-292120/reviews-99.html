\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFeeling nostalgic for a simpler time in life? Need something to add some spice to your preexisting existential dread? Do you like cutscenes that are so disruptive that you can’t manage to play a game for more than about two minutes before another burns through ten minutes of your time? If you answered “Yes” to any of those questions, try Final Fantasy XIII!

Final Fantasy XIII (FFXIII from hereon out) is one of a slew of games under the moniker “Final Fantasy” that is neither final nor actually a sequel to any of the previous games of the same name. Basically what this means is that FFXIII is a video game that could have been named practically anything else had Square Enix decided not to sprinkle a tidy handful of references to earlier titles in the series. That is to say that FFXIII is exactly that. The narrative is simple enough that you could easily swap out commonly used terms in the game with just about whatever you want, and the narrative remains mostly coherent. Don’t believe me? Swap out “Pulse l’cie” for Jedi and “Fal’cie” for Sith and the whole game takes a giant sigh of relief that it doesn’t need to hide any pretense that it’s probably exactly that. In fact, that is what I’ll be calling that for the synapsis for the game.

You follow an ensemble cast of characters consisting of Cloud Strife with tits and pink hair, a generic blond protagonist with a penchant for punching things, a whiny teenage boy with daddy issues, two confused Australians, and a diversity hire with a hygienically questionable bird living in his afro as they band together while on the run from the Empire led by Sith-lord Palpatine 2.0 in hopes of saving their dying world from total destruction at the hands of the Dark Side. Do they triumph over the Sith using fantastic Jedi magic powers and guns? Yes, yes they do. Well, the blond guy just punches magic but you get the idea. It’s not exactly a fantastic story, but it’s satisfying in the same way Chinese take-out is.

Honestly, FFXIII is a bit of a weird game altogether. Aside from some common naming conventions endemic to the Final Fantasy series and an incredibly tiny handful of iconic creatures isolated to one area, the rest of the game is drastically something else to the point where you’re given tutorial instructions nearly to the end of the game. The majority of the gameplay is strictly linear outside a large expanse area that you don’t even get to see until you’re 3/4 of the way through the game. Honestly, that would have been fine had they just stuck to the linear structure entirely, as the open world portion of the game is clunky and wildly inefficient. During the open world portion you gain access to exactly 64 missions that aren’t entirely optional, all of which force you to bound back and forth from different parts without any rhyme or reason. This wouldn’t be so bad had it not been for the fact that it forces you to retread the same areas repeatedly with little reward to actually motivate you to do it.

Character progression is also handled very strangely in this game as well. Instead of traditional levels, you have access to something called the “Crystarium” which essentially acts as a series of overly convoluted talent trees that become progressively more chaotic as the story progresses. To accompany this, your weapons and accessories have a level structure to them that requires you to burn through item drops to increase their power. Between the two systems there’s enough to keep you grinding for hours at a time just to build an efficient set of characters.

Combat is mostly automated, requiring little actual input to actually complete encounters outside of pressing a single button to attack or pressing a different button to alter your fighting tactics. It’s a lot more convoluted than it sounds, but that’s realistically the simplest terms for the mess that you’re handed.

One place that the game really shines is the art style. Apart from a handful of bizarre monster designs, the game is stunning to look at, whether you’re stuck in cutscene-hell or just taking in the scenery as you bound from one linear path to another. Seriously, some of the vantage points are jaw-droppingly gorgeous. It doesn’t make for a fantastic game overall, but it does ease some of the teeth-grinding tedium that the game has in spades. All in all, I’d say this is a decent choice as a bargain purchase on sale, should you feel a desire to play what amounts to a poorly structured interactive movie.

The roles available are: Commando (physical damage), ravager(magical damage), sentinel (protects you party and takes less damage from attacks), synergists (casts buffs to allies), saboteur (casts debuffs to enemies) and medic (healer). So, to manage what role each character is going to carry out, you have the paradigm system, which lets you swap roles to a selected list that you can configure from the menu. This lets you adapt to the diferent stages and situations you can have in combat. So overall, even with the auto-attack feature, you still have to actively react to the situation.
- End game: So the game is pretty much linear, with lots of enemies that are almost imposible to go around, but at this stage it becomes much more open, lots of side quests start popping, and when you finish one another one becomes available.
- Archievement completing: To be honest, it was a pain to farm some of them, the worst one is having each ande very weapon and accesories. You will find yourself killing 1529835298357283 long gui for platinum ingots, so you can upgrade everything, its very grindy. The rest of them are quite enjoyable.

Quick sidenote: Square Enix game ports are usually bad, they have some performace issues, patches for this exist on the internet, made by the comunity.

To summarize, the story couldve been better, the combat is very entertaining and that one archievement was a pain to farm.\t\t\t\t\t
