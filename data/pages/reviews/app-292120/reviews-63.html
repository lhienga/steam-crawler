\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFFXIII has a reputation for being the worst Final Fantasy game, and I remember intensely disliking it on release 12 years ago. Sometimes a bit of distance can give you new perspective on media – maybe it was a matter of unrealistic hype, perhaps it went in an unusual direction or maybe you just weren’t in the mood for it. For what it’s worth, FFXIII is a little better than I remember, but it’s still not good. It’s not the worst Final Fantasy game (It’s not even the worst Final Fantasy XIII game), but that’s only because it’s not an ancient NES title. It’s definitely slap fighting it out at the bottom with FFVIII.

A quick note on the PC port – it’s garbage. This is pretty much standard practice for Square, and by their standards it is at least fixable. While you’re at it I’d recommend trying some of the excellent mods to give it an extra coat of gloss. And really, a couple of model swaps along with higher res and framerates are all it takes as even now FFXIII looks absolutely stunning. While we can rag on the game for it’s many faults, presentation is not one of them. The art team was firing on all cylinders, and it’s clear that a staggering amount of money went into making this one of the best looking games on the 7th gen consoles. Supposedly there’s enough cut from FFXIII to create a whole extra game, and I suspect that’s at least partly why we got two sequels to such a divisive game.

And now it’s time to lay the boot in. It’s honestly kind of shocking that FFXIII was released in the state that it was, and even more shocking that contemporary reviews were so kind to it. Let’s start with the easiest criticism – the story and characters. Outside of Sazh, who seems to have got lost on his way to a different, much better game, no-one acts like a human being and every piece of character drama is due to easily resolved misunderstandings or just acting like a complete idiot. Lightning is a moody ♥♥♥♥♥. Hope is Shinji without the empathy. Snow is actually, legitimately mentally handicapped. Vanille is a manic pixie dream girl from space. Fang actually isn’t too bad, but there’s not much to her. I will say that the PC port at least comes with option for Japanese, which is definitely a big improvement as the English dub does the dismal sub-anime characters no favours.

The actual plot is a muddled, uninteresting affair though I am surprised that people found it hard to follow. FFXIII has a reputation for hiding all of it’s worldbuilding in the in game encyclopaedia, but honestly I found the story easy enough to follow without reading any of it. The main issue is one of texture and context. We know basically nothing about Cocoon or its inhabitants, so we don’t feel any connection to it in the same way as we did with Spira or Gaia. This is because our party is on the run throughout the entire game, so you never actually visit any of the living, breathing parts of the world.

This brings us onto the next big criticism, which is linearity, though I’d argue the main issue is one of pacing. The defenders of this game will point to FFX - that game was in many ways a corridor full of monsters, and everyone loved that one! The big difference is that game knew when to slow down, and it was full of pace breakers like towns and temples between the combat to give the world and characters some texture. FFXIII legitimately has nothing but combat and cutscenes, and it is exhausting to play for any length of time. Some of those combats can drag on for minutes at a time, with certain creatures being absolutely massive time sinks. Some of the bosses can take upwards of 20 minutes.

FFXIII does sort of open up after about 25 hours on rails, which is where it got the reputation that it gets good halfway through. It doesn’t. The fundamental structure of the game doesn’t change, the only difference is now you’re running down non-linear tubes doing optional sidequests. Sidequests that are given to you MMO style from talking rocks, and are literally all just monster hunts. This area is essentially just a mandatory grinding area that you’ll be stuck in for about 10-20 hours getting strong enough to do the final chapters of the game and it’s selection of tedious timesink bosses.

Thankfully the combat system to it’s credit is rather good, and considering that’s basically all you do in this game outside of watching melodramatic cutscenes it’s good they at least got this part right even if they clearly didn’t trust their players to do the same. Rather than controlling your party members directly you act essentially as party manager, quickly changing “paradigms” (essentially a set of job classes) to control the ebb and flow of combat. Once it fully opens itself to you it can be a lot of fun in short bursts, and I’d say it’s one of the strongest battle systems in the whole series. The one big fault is the boneheaded decision to have an instant game over if your lead party member dies – even if there is a medic who could revive them, or a stock of phoenix downs. This is particularly infuriating later on when certain enemies can cast instant death on you.

The problem is that the game just doesn’t trust players to know what they’re doing, and the training wheels last almost half the game. During those chapters you can’t change characters, and you’re often stuck with only two party members which really does not show the battle system at it’s best. You don’t get access to the (terribly explained) weapon upgrade system for about 10 hours. Even more bafflingly the game doesn’t even give you access to classes for the first 2-3 hours, meaning you are legitimately just mashing attack while running down a tube and using the occasional potion. Those opening hours give an absolutely awful first impression, and I wouldn’t blame anyone for quitting there and then.

So FFXIII is one of the few pieces of media that I have returned to years later with an open mind, and found my opinion basically unchanged. Everything I hated back then has not improved with age, and while it isn’t a complete catastrophe, it does get dangerously close.

Supposedly there’s enough cut from FFXIII to create a whole extra game, and I suspect that’s at least partly why we got two sequels to such a divisive game.

And now it’s time to lay the boot in. It’s honestly kind of shocking that FFXIII was released in the state that it was, and even more shocking that contemporary reviews were so kind to it. Let’s start with the easiest criticism – the story and characters. Outside of Sazh, who seems to have got lost on his way to a different, much better game, no-one acts like a human being and every piece of character drama is due to easily resolved misunderstandings or just acting like a complete idiot. Lightning is an awful ♥♥♥♥♥. Hope is Shinji without the empathy. Snow is actually, legitimately mentally handicapped. Vanille is a manic pixie dream girl from space. Fang actually isn’t too bad, but there’s not much to her. I will say that the PC port at least comes with option for Japanese, which is definitely a big improvement as the English dub does the dismal sub-anime characters no favours.

The actual plot is a muddled, uninteresting affair though I am surprised that people found it hard to follow. FFXIII has a reputation for hiding all of it’s worldbuilding in the in game encyclopaedia, but honestly I found the story easy enough to follow without reading any of it. The main issue is one of texture and context. We know basically nothing about Cocoon or its inhabitants, so we don’t feel any connection to it in the same way as we did with Spira or Gaia. This is because our party is on the run throughout the entire game, so you never actually visit any of the living, breathing parts of the world.

This brings us onto the next big criticism, which is linearity, though I’d argue the main issue is one of pacing. The defenders of this game will point to FFX - that game was in many ways a corridor full of monsters, and everyone loved that one! The big difference is that game knew when to slow down, and it was full of pace breakers like towns and temples between the combat to give the world and characters some texture. FFXIII legitimately has nothing but combat and cutscenes, and it is exhausting to play for any length of time. Some of those combats can drag on for minutes at a time, with certain creatures being absolutely massive time sinks. Some of the bosses can take upwards of 20 minutes.

FFXIII does sort of open up after about 25 hours on rails, which is where it got the reputation that it gets good halfway through. It doesn’t. The fundamental structure of the game doesn’t change, the only difference is now you’re running down non-linear tubes doing optional sidequests. Sidequests that are given to you MMO style from talking rocks, and are literally all just monster hunts. This area is essentially just a mandatory grinding area that you’ll be stuck in for about 10-20 hours getting strong enough to do the final chapters of the game and it’s selection of horrible timesink bosses.

Thankfully the combat system to it’s credit is rather good, and considering that’s basically all you do in this game outside of watching melodramatic cutscenes it’s good they at least got this part right even if they clearly didn’t trust their players to do the same. Rather than controlling your party members directly you act essentially as party manager, quickly changing “paradigms” (essentially a set of job classes) to control the ebb and flow of combat. Once it fully opens itself to you it can be a lot of fun in short bursts, and I’d say it’s one of the strongest battle systems in the whole series. The one big fault is the boneheaded decision to have an instant game over if your lead party member dies – even if there is a medic who could revive them, or a stock of phoenix downs. This is particularly infuriating later on when certain enemies can cast instant death on you.

The problem is that the game just doesn’t trust players to know what they’re doing, and the training wheels last almost half the game. During those chapters you can’t change characters, and you’re often stuck with only two party members which really does not show the battle system at it’s best. You don’t get access to the (terribly explained) weapon upgrade system for about 10 hours. Even more bafflingly the game doesn’t even give you access to classes for the first 2-3 hours, meaning you are legitimately just mashing attack while running down a tube and using the occasional potion. Those opening hours give an absolutely awful first impression, and I wouldn’t blame anyone for quitting there and then.

So FFXIII is one of the few pieces of media that I have returned to years later with an open mind, and found my opinion basically unchanged. Everything I hated back then has not improved with age, and while it isn’t a complete catastrophe, it does get dangerously close.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFinal Fantasy XIII linearity has aged better today although early stages are still too restrictive and hand holding in gameplay and level progression.

The story and characters in FFXIII are not that remarkable or interesting. While cut-scenes look great, the story is confusing at first but becomes more understandable though is quite weak. The story can be understood further and much better by delving into the datalog journal which contains a ton of information that fills out a lot of character backstory, terminology and missing parts.

It's gameplay battle system is where the game shines and is its strongest point. Creating and strategize different paradigms of party and character roles has a deep level. Attack types, roles, buffs, de-buffs. FFXIII also introduced a stagger mechanic where chaining attacks to stagger an opponent and maintaining a stagger is crucial to winning and dealing massive damage. Auto-battle unit commands most of time carries out intended move so choosing and timing paradigms becomes most important in battle.

Presentation, environment design, combat effects are very nice, graphics, cut-scenes and soundtrack still standout and are excellent and even better modded.\t\t\t\t\t
