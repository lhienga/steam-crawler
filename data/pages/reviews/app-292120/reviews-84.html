What more do I need to say

In all seriousness though I think it's a great game. There are glaring issues obviously and pretty questionable game design choices but other than that everyone can pick this up and enjoy it. If you do decide to buy, make sure to check out a Community Guide about improving the game's performance because Square Enix can't be bothered to optimize the game themselves. The guide is complete from improving performance (without the fix the game will always run at 30FPS no matter what your rig is, even if the Store Page says that they increased the fps cap to 60. It's basically due to a limitation of the executable that only allows the game to use 2GB of RAM. A patch is available that makes it use 4GB and significantly boost your fps to 60fps, especially during fights), all the way to optional enhancements that will make the game look more modern instead of a poor port of a PS3 game. Do note, however, that some of these graphical enhancements take a lot of disk space (especially the improved FMVs and audio bitrate) but if you have the space and the patience to download it from NexusMods (or if you're a premium user of the website that allows unlimited download speed), I'd say it's pretty worth to get them.

The game itself is pretty straightforward. Like, there's a reason it's tagged as "Linear" - because it is. Each character has their own "roles" but at the end of the game all of them will have access to any roles, defeating the purpose of using different characters for different roles (though, of course, some characters still excel in specific roles). Story is pretty much what you would expect from a Final Fantasy game and a JRPG - which is to say, kill god of course.

The game will also throw you into an open-world-esque area that, at first, will make you think that it's not linear, but trust me: it definitely still is. This open world area offers side quests for you to take but they mostly unlock the next side quest to take, making it quite hard to miss one unless you deliberately ignore them.

The game's graphics is pretty good for its age, although as I've mentioned earlier there are now mods that will further enhance it to make it look more modernized.

For those looking for a reference on how long this game is, the playtime mentioned in this review (50 hours as of time of writing) includes grinding for materials and "experience" (which is not neccessary to do, it's just my prerogative in RPGs) and progress up to the 2nd to the last chapter of the game. Without the grinding, you could probably shave 4 to 6 hours off of it. It would still take maybe 5 hours or so to completely finish the game, and another 5 hours or so to do all the post-game content. Unfortunately, there are no DLCs available for the game that may make post-game much more worthwhile but I'm pretty sure there should have been one since most post-game content are all about making your characters even stronger. But eh, it is what it is.

My brutally honest review of Final Fantasy XIII

Back in April 2012 I tried the game for my first time on my PS3, and was blown away by the graphics and gameplay back then. But now that I've finally finished the game in 2021 my entire view on the game has changed. How people perceive games is obviously subjective but I'll try and give you my reason why this game hasn't been the best experience.

(In my review I wont discuss the graphics since it can be tweaked if you desire it. I also wont discuss the fact that the game will "stutter" most of the time because it's searching for a controller and it makes it unplayable, unless you find a fix for it. There's is a fix out there, no worries .)

The Narrative:
The game starts out lovely in medias res and gives us a nice introduction to the cast and the world around them. That's fine. You get some backstory as well here and there, cool cool. But there's a couple of things that needs to be addressed:
The game lacks spice and the characters feels almost soulless. It's like this game, when you compare it to the other games, is focused on the battle aspect and forgets the narrative and exploration. You get the exploration part thrown in the head in chapter 11 and that's it.
The game requires you to grind some and it can take it's toll on people that just want the the little narrative the game has to offer.
People who play this game the first time will have a tough time to figure out what a Fal'Cie actually is. It would have been much easier if Square Enix used terms that weren't so close to one another, like "Devils" or "Angels" to describe them.

All the characters are bland and you feel almost no connection to them except for very few instances and then that flame is gone. The villains are flat characters and the game gives you no reason to feel with them or even remember them. One of the villains gets TWO cutscenes and then they are written off. In short: there's a huge lack of interesting characters.

Every single ending to a dialog can be summed up to: "Let's do our best and believe in ourselves" and thus leaves no room for empathy towards the characters because they aren't evolving or expanding themselves as characters.

This is basically how almost all of them are: holding information that would have been REALLY important for the rest of the cast to know ASAP and they are constantly frustrated because of their situation.
I don't know what's so special about the place we have to save because the game didn't allow space for that feeling due to lacking storytelling. A lot of the things you, as a player, really could need to know is all the small story bits written in plain text in your Datalog, but it's not like the game encourages you to read them, it will just give you a Datalog "update" from time to time.

If you want the best narrative experience I would recommend you play the game on "Easy" and

Exploration:
The game in contrast to the other games are very linear and doesn't make room for any further exploring. You can't interact with any NPC's in the open world to chat or hear about something in depth which can make the environment feel hollow.
The game is made so that it wouldn't make sense to backtrack. When you've completed chapter 1 up to 10 it's done, and you can't revisit that area to get things you might have missed. In this aspect the game provides almost nothing compared to the other games where there's tons of room for exploration.


The Battlesystem:
Square Enix should get some positive credit for making the combat system unique and that they took a different approach. Each battle in the game requires a certain amount of strategy to get through because every enemy is different and can't always be rushed through, except when you've farmed a ton.

For every bossfight there's almost always a recipe you have to follow in order to win there isn't much room for fun and improvisation.

Compared to FF12 or FF15 where you get to chose one of your other characters when your controlled character dies you get slapped straight into the "Game Over" realm and has to try again.

When you upgrade your character it's done with the illusion of choice just like in FF10 but the predetermined path for your character is the most efficient one in terms of where to use your CP.

The Soundtrack:
It's clear when you play the game that it tries to be different where it can and when it came to soundtracks I must say it has taken the series in a new direction. In the previous games the soundtrack tended to be more orchestral whereas Final Fantasy 13 gives it a modern twist with its inclusion of modern instruments. Even though I missed the Anthem used in the other Final Fantasy games, I must say that I instead was blessed with gems like "Sazh's Theme", "Daddy's Got the Blues" or "Can't Catch a Break".


Short summary of my experience:
I forced myself through this one and I feel incredibly sad that I at one point tried to justify to others why the game was good without having fully completed it. When the game finally was done I wasn't left thinking: "man this was a nice experience" no, it was: "I can't get 60 of the 64 hours back I've used on the game." 2/10.\t\t\t\t\t
