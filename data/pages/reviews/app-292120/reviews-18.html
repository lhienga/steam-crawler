\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFinal Fantasy 13 - The game where edgy emo kids catch their inner Transformers in order to stop the Decepticons from reviving Megatron.

I can see what they were trying to do with Final Fantasy 13. The characters are likeable enough, but the game struggles to get the player to care much about their stories and what comes next. The story is very hit-or-miss, missing the mark more often than not with me personally. The combat system is very streamlined, yet boring and repetitive, even for a Final Fantasy game. I've had to take several breaks from this game to prevent myself from getting bored, as the battles are much, much longer than they should be in some cases. As much as I want to like this game, I struggle to maintain a desire to play. If you love Final Fantasy 12, you'll probably like this game. If you dislike 12, you'll probably dislike 13 as well.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPLAYED ON STEAM DECK

FF13 is a weird game. It's uneven, slow, and narratively bare. But it's also visually astounding, fun, and has great music. Sounds like another Final Fantasy doesn't it.

In all seriousness, my biggest takeaway from the game was its presentation. This ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ game came out in 2009, and I would argue that aspects of the visuals could compete with games like Redfall and even FF15. "But Oggdo, those games aren't even known for having great graphics," yeah well they came out much much later. When thinking of visuals, I like to look at the Mortal Kombat game. MKX and MK11 came out at the start and end of the PS4/XBONE lifecycles respectively. But those games look leagues apart. FF13 looks miles ahead of whatever games were coming out in 2009. I know because I played those games in 2009. The FMVS are immaculate, and are (in my opinion) still the best in the franchise. The music is a departure from the traditionally orchestrated fanfare of the older, perhaps more medieval, games. But the music puts on a suit appropriate to the world of FF13. It's stylish, sleek, jazzy, futuristic, but it doesn't forget that it's a Final Fantasy score.

Yet, the game is far from being great. For starters, its too slow. It takes the game about 3/5ths of the playthrough before the combat opens up. And when it does, its great. Its the most engaging interpretation of rock paper scissors with an rpg twist that anyone could come up with. But, just like the gameplay, the narrative drags for a good portion of the game. The 6 party members spend a majority of the game apart, and it doesn't really help the player fall in love with them. Time is wasted on trying to give everyone equal screentime. in the end, it becomes clear that some characters deserved much more time than others. Personally, though some may disagree, it was abundantly clear that 3 of the main cast were infinitely more interesting than the other 3. Still, the story powers on giving everyone an abundance of nothing to work with. FF13 is pretty, but its not full.
Yes, while there are problems with the story it isn't horrible. It's a hard game to recommend, because I know I liked it. But I also remember points in the game that were tedious and so so boring. I recall my enjoyment (which really soared) to be mainly compressed in the final part of the game.

I'd recommend this game to Final Fantasy fans, or people who want to try out a game that is obviously very ambitious. It's an interesting game, and it's definitely worth playing. To newcomers? No. You're better off starting from 10, 12, 7r, 16, but not 15. I have a review on 15. I hate that game.

6.9/10, it gets a passing grade with more for the visuals and music. Don't play this game in English.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat makes it unique to the series?
It’s the worst one. It was ill-conceived at every step of the way. I would not play this unless you’re looking to complete the series, there’s very little to recommend here.

Positives
- I liked the main song the first couple hundred times I heard it. Unfortunately, this only accounted for like the first 2 hours of the game. By hour 20, you will mute your TV and listen to anything else.

- Once it opens up and you get what they’re going for, the combat isn’t that bad. It’s like a semi-automatic system where you’re giving people general orders about what to do rather than dictating every step in the process. There’s two problems with that though. First, it takes about 20 hours before you’re really about to change much of anything, with different parts of combat slowly unlocking along the way. Second, sometimes you really want that discrete control so an attack does wipe out your main character and immediately end the game. I’ve never actually finished this game (watched the ending on YouTube) because the final boss has an instant death attack and even when following a walkthrough I couldn’t consistently avoid it. Absolutely maddening.

Negatives
- Some of the dumbest, most insufferable characters in all of gaming. Here are some lines you’re going to hear constantly throughout:
“I can’t forgive him, he killed my mom.”
“We have to complete our focus.”
“Blah blah I’m a hero blah blah.”

These people go on and on repeating the same crap for basically the entire game. They make decisions that are so blindingly ignorant of what’s in front of their eyes that it’s more believable that they all got massive brain injuries in the opening train crash. I’m sure some of this is intentional, but it’s like the writer is 13 and just learned what character development is. Nobody would have liked Jaime Lannister if he just spent 5 seasons going “But they took muh hand!” – characters can talk about different things and still show growth.

- You’ve probably heard that this game is very linear and from the defenders that it “get better later.” Let me tell you what that last part means. Around the last 20% of the game, you can run around a big open field with nothing to do but fight big monsters and take boring side quests from glowing orbs. No big cities to explore, no side quests with memorable characters and fun moments, no secrets waiting to be uncovered in the environment. Assassin’s Creed has more interesting quests and environments and it routinely gets raked over the coals for them. There is nothing worth experiencing at the end of this game.

- Some very confusing storytelling. A lot of the lore here is hidden away in the in-game encyclopedia and I wouldn’t mind this if it wasn’t critical to understanding some moments in the game. Hell, I read it all and I still didn’t understand the ending. It seems to blatantly go against everything the game tells you possible. Final Fantasy stories were often ridiculous (this is a series where amnesia is as predictable as chocobos) but they usually made sense and stuck to their own logic. Most modern entries don’t care, though. They just go with whatever feels like the most emotional outcome and justify it later. I just don’t get why they think this is effective storytelling.
