\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFINAL FANTASY XIII is one of the games people either love or hate, without a middle ground, mostly because of some of the design decisions introduced in this title. Personally, this game got me hooked from the very beginning. The story might be confusing at the start, but if you are catching up with datalog, it’s actually very interesting. Characters are great as well - every single playable character is different, and in the second part of the game, you can shape your party in any way you want to, with the team members you prefer.

Gameplay-wise, the majority of FF XIII is extremely linear, and this is the biggest problem for most of the players. It might sound strange, but I kinda enjoyed it. We’re living in an age with a huge amount of “open worlds”, or games that are giving us free hands, and it was actually something different, refreshing. I could fully focus on the story, characters, and the world of FF XIII, which is beautiful, because even today, after so many years, this game looks great. It’s also worth mentioning that the second part of the game, after reaching Gran Pulse, is more open. You can freely explore some areas, do side missions, or even ride chocobos!

The second piece related to the gameplay, which is dividing the community, is Crystarium - progress system introduced in FF XIII, which is somehow based on what was proposed in FF 10. You are developing your characters first in three, and later on, in six different roles, expanding their Crystarium by unlocking new skills, and improving parameters like strength or HP. If you are interested in only beating the game, you won’t need to grind CP - you can naturally progress with the story, and if you are not spending points in a weird way, you’ll be good. Other than that, I can say that the only downside of Crystarium is the fact that you’ll spend a few hours there. Upgrading all characters is time-consuming, and you are forced to do this because the maximum amount of stored CP is limited.

Switching to cool things, the battle system introduced in FF XIII is one of the best in the franchise. Encounters are very dynamic - in most of the fights, you’ll be forced to switch paradigms in order to change skills used by all party members. It’s also very satisfying, especially during boss fights, when you need to be really good in adjusting paradigms, using skill, etc. in order to beat the most difficult opponents.

As in almost every entry of the franchise, if you’re interested in having 100% in FF XIII, you need to be mentally ready for the grind. You’ll grind plenty of items that are sold for Gil (it’s not given after encounters), catalysts, etc. This part of the game will take you around 30h after beating the story. Personally, it took me 94h to fully complete this game.

Important note: Square Enix is known for its bad PC ports. Before playing the game, it’s strongly advised to read some guides on how to improve the performance and stability of the game. It worked like a charm for me - FF XIII was running absolutely great, and I had no crashes.

To summarize this review, in my humble opinion this is one of the best Final Fantasy entries in the franchise. If you can accept their design decisions about linearity and the infamous Crystarium, you’ll enjoy this game a lot. It has a great story, amazing characters, a captivating battle system, and visually it’s simply stunning, even today.

9/10\t\t\t\t\t

-La curva de dificultad es buena pero quizás se demora un poco en despegar, durante el principio el juego es bastante fácil pero cuando ya tienes más opciones de combinaciones de equipo y desbloqueas más habilidades se vuelve bastante más entretenido ya que si algo falla tienes otras opciones para probar.
-El Soundtrack es espectacular, si hay algo con lo que voy a recordar los momentos que pasé jugando este juego es por la música, sé que después de muchos años vuelva a escuchar la música de este juego voy a sentir esa nostalgia que no muchos juegos consiguen.

Contras:
-El diseño de niveles es sumamente simple, quizás no se pensó mucho en este aspecto del juego pero es increíblemente lineal en ese aspecto.
-Un buen villano para este tipo de historia habría quedado genial pero realmente no sentí que los antagonistas de este juego tuvieran mucha relevancia ni mucho carisma, de hecho se ven poco durante el gameplay.

En general un muy buen videojuego con pequeños tropiezos pero si puedes ignorarlos es muy disfrutable y para mi fue una buena introducción a la saga Final Fantasy.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGod have mercy on my soul

This is without a doubt the most difficult review I've ever written, but this game is so fascinating from multiple perspectives; I've challenged myself to throw my hat into the ring and explain why I resonate with it despite the overt shortcomings.

It's my Focus, really.

FFXIII is a game that was fraught with a lot opposing views amongst Square Enix's devs on how to create a "perfect hybrid" between inputting command-based strategies for party members whilst being simultaneously cinematic akin to the battles found in FFVII: Advent Children, an animated film adaption of the highly adulated FFVII.

The major flaw with that is... there's no real way for the human mind to adapt to such a philosophy of game design, and you'd have to make a lot of sacrifices that distort that vision or automate party members to create such a feat, which isn't exactly an enrapturing way to design a game.

I say this not to bore you with history but because it birthed the--arguably--most controversial thing in the game: Auto Battle
From a glance, it seems like the game "plays itself" because of this, right?

Weeeeeell, no.



There IS a reason why people feel it's on auto-pilot, and I wouldn't dismiss that as a fallacy, but let me indulge you as to why that's not the case and why people still felt that way regardless.

In this game, you select between different Paradigms that are filled with ATB (Active Time Battle). Each Paradigm can allocate one of six different roles to each party member that the player has to manage and switch to on a constant basis to develop their own unique strategy to battles. Each Paradigm has a unique name that distinguishes itself from another Paradigm with a different set of roles for the player to select them.

For example, "Salvation" is a Paradigm that consists of MED/MED/MED (A role with three members being medics).
The six different roles you can choose are the following:

COM (Commando): It's focused on close-range combat for dealing high damage
RAV (Ravager): It's magic-based, casting a wide array of spells to stagger enemies
SEN (Sentinel): A defensive mode, reducing what would be massive damage on other roles
SAB (Saboteur): A role for debuffing enemies for quicker staggers and inflicting status ailments that weaken them
SYN (Synergist): A role for buffing party members with stronger resistance to status ailments, temporarily increase strength and magic, etc.
MED (Medic): Pretty self-explanatory.

You can assign one of these roles for each active party member, and you can have a total of six "Paradigm Shifts" you can switch to at any time. There's a lot of depth to it, and the game encourages you to experiment between them to defeat enemies as effectively as possible to survive harsh encounters.

Now as I said it before, I mentioned the Auto Battle--the thing I believe to be the most controversial part of the game. Why is it that some people felt the combat was automated when in reality, it's not?

In the beginning, you're heavily restricted with the party members and roles you can select. The game only allows you to pick three roles between two members that switch at different chapters beyond the prologue. For instance, in Chapter 1, you play as Lightning and Sazh, and Lightning's current, possible role is only COM, so the only thing you can do is attack and heal with potions if necessary. This limits what the player is able to do, and since the game opens up the roles and characters you can choose slowly for the player to be acclimated to the new gameplay style, it creates the impression that selecting auto-battle is the only way to engage with the game. The game feels like a long tutorial in the beginning with no diversity, and it's no surprise people did not like that at all. While the combat is indeed weak at the beginning, after the band of fugitives are branded as Pulse l'cie by the Pulse Fal'cie, the game starts to remove the training wheels and invite the player to experiment, which becomes very enjoyable.

However, your willingness to tolerate that is highly dependent on you and will affect your enjoyment of the game, but this is why people, while untrue, do vilify this game for being "automated." It's fair to say that long tutorials can hurt someone's enjoyment, but my point is more to debunk this notion the game is automated. It should also be noted that you can toggle the selection to manual first.

With that said, what is the purpose of Auto Battle, then?

It's to aid that "perfect hybrid" between strategic command-based inputs and cinematic battles.

It's intended to chain attacks seamlessly while you plan to select other inputs to stagger foes, but the problem is that they're merely recommendations, and you will find yourself not playing efficiently by relying on it for quicker selections of barely helpful commands. It functions like a low skill floor, and the high skill ceiling is the manual selections. You will have a much easier time if you select the abilities you need at the moment manually and chain them with auto battle selections once you're confident with the strategy you're opting for.

For example, say you earn a preemptive strike to stagger a group of enemies after the next hit, if you pick auto battle, you will lose the opportunity to stagger them all at once because it will only choose a standard ability. Whereas if you chose an ability that dealt damage in a wide radius manually, you'd deal a much higher damage output and defeat enemies quickly, earning a 5 star rating. A vast majority of battles are not feasible or even ideal with auto-battle, nor allow the AI party members to fight optimally without a libra, which can only be selected manually.


Speaking of characters, we have a story that's driven by them rather than the world-building of Cocoon and Pulse. It's an interesting way to tell a story, but the problem is, if you don't like the characters, you're not going to enjoy the story at all. The world building is actually amazing, but you need to read the countless datalogs to scrutinize it. If you're someone who craves for interesting world building, this approach to it will not be appealing at all, but I'm more character-driven, so I found datalogs to be a serviceable bestiary to engross myself more into the world.

For me, I enjoy most of the characters. I see them as flawed individuals who are woebegone to the cruel, unjust fate befallen upon them by the Fal'cie who has predetermined that for them. Should they fail their Focus described in a vague vision, they turn into monstrous cie'th. They become more and more crestfallen by the fact they're fugitives on the run with no hope to save their home, nor remove the curse of a l'cie. But through their will to protect what they cherish most, they believe in creating their own fate, defying literal gods--to make the impossible possible. They feel like believable characters who overcome their inner demons and develop throughout the game. I wouldn't say the execution is outstanding, but I did like the main characters.

The villains are very weak, though. There's a certain character who's so great; they were killed by the main villain at the time their moment to shine arose. Even the main antagonist is nothing too interesting, but the way they attained their goal is what I found to be intriguing.

What I DO find amazing about the story is how the developers unintentionally created such an artistic interpretation of the game's linearity.

You're moving through hallways with very few branching paths, and somehow this creates a very claustrophobic discomfort that made the game a lot more immersive for me. You're constantly being hunted, and the pathways are littered with enemies, making it difficult to navigate through. The only form of respite are the open areas like Grand Pulse that felt like a breather.

But yeah, that's FFXIII, and why it's a game "I love but everyone hates"

And you know what? I'm fine with that.\t\t\t\t\t
