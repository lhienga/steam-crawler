\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThere is so much to say about Sea of Thieves. My original review was 4,744 characters too long, so I have had to condense it somewhat:

I try to only review a game after I have spent a decent amount of time with it. In this case, 1000 hours seems like enough time for me to give an honest and warranted take on Sea of Thieves.

The short answer: I think Sea of Thieves is, in concept at least, is a masterpiece. A sandbox pirate game with hours and hours of content, that is prevented from getting repetitive by player interactions. So, let's talk a little about what kept me coming back for over 1000 hours, and why I think this game deserves a spot in YOUR games library.

First and foremost, the world of Sea of Thieves is immaculate and mesmerizing. I am not necessarily talking about graphics, though I think they've held up extremely well even for a game that came out 6 years ago. No, down to the very design of the game, there are so many things to do, and so many things to discover. Every island has little nooks and crannies to explore, and little features that make them unique, which encourages you to explore the world, be it through tall tales, world events, commendation hunting, or just to gather resources. Thanks to recent updates causing servers to constantly exchange players on dive missions, and paired with the upcoming PS5 release, this immaculately crafted world feels more alive than ever. This is undeniably where the game shines the most, because you can craft the most gorgeous game world of all time, and fill it with plenty of things to discover and do, but a limited map will inevitably get boring down the line and cause players to leave the game once "completed."

The player interactions are what make this game special. Sea of Thieves does not give any player an advantage after they have racked up a lot of hours. There's no progression system, the tools you are given at the start of the game are the tools you will have 1000 hours later. The gold you earn is spent almost entirely on cosmetics, and to a lesser extent resources, though those resources are cheap enough that after a minimal amount of effort, you will make back your money after buying them. So what is the incentive to do anything in this game you might ask? Well, aside from looking dapper, its player interactions! The fact that after you complete a voyage, defeat a boss, or raid a fortress you are forced to sail to an outpost (or reapers if you are so inclined) to sell said loot means you are never guaranteed to be rewarded for the time you've invested. At the start of the game, this can be somewhat intimidating. New players are often put off by being sunk or having their loot stolen by better players, and while many experienced players try to go easier on newbies, a chest of fortune is still a chest of fortune. Thankfully, this issue is somwhat addressed by the new "Safer Seas" mode. Just be aware that Safer Seas is designed as an extended tutorial of sorts, so you will need to enter High Seas mode eventually if you want to continue progressing in the game, and that is where that PVP aspect of the PVPVE world comes into play

But we need to talk a little about that label. After the shutdown of the Arena mode, a lot of players have been rather upset with this game. Now, I dabbled in arena in my 1000 hours. The issue with arena was that it removed players from the Sandbox, which is fundamentally where this game shines. That is why so few players played it. The new hourglass mode is an attempt to keep PVP within the sandbox. If you are inexperienced, you can still win an hourglass fight, because perhaps you have better food, or curse balls, or perhaps another ship showed up mid fight. Some players see this as a negative. Whilst I agree that it goes against the spirit of "competition" it does align with the games guiding principle of "freedom." There are no "rules" to PVP, pirate code not withstanding. The freedom of choice is what makes you feel like a true pirate. Arena removed that freedom in an attempt to level the playing field. This was fantastic for people who wanted to be "the best" at PVP, as for them the lack of throwables, chainshots, food variety, PVE, and inability to run away from fights meant that the best player would always triumph. In the sandbox, the variability means that while it pays to be good at PVP, that alone does not guarantee victory. While it is often frustrating when an outmatched crew is able to run away and sell their loot, if they weren't able to, those players would have no incentive to play.

Keeping in mind that Sea of Thieves was never built to host tournaments or be an Esport, there are still some options for people seeking that kind of experience with the game, though for the time being these events are organised through third parties.

A few negatives however. The aforementioned hit registration issues are a problem. They have gotten better, but they are still present, and that really is a shame. The issue of cheaters, though somewhat addressed by the implementation of Easy Anticheat is still present. There's also a few unpatched exploits floating about, meaning some commendations have become a lot less difficult to obtain than others, somewhat tainting their value. In all three of these cases, it is important to remember that Rare is aware of the issues, and the game developers are trying to fix them. Yes, its been 6 years, but the game has changed A LOT in those 6 years. When it first came out, many players became bored as there was very little to do, so that became the main focus. Now that that issue seems to be addressed, and we are now moving on to bigger and better things. From what I have heard of upcoming updates the Sandbox is getting a few welcome reworks and additions. If these rumors are true, the game has a bright future ahead of it, provided of course the aforementioned issues are not forgotten (though I really don't think they will be, especially given how often the more vocal members of the community like to remind Rare of their existance).

One last thing about Easy Anticheat (EAC for short). Rare, I get that you needed to address the cheater issue, but requiring us to install KERNAL LEVEL ANTICHEAT to play a sandbox game seems a little over the top. I get that cheating is a big issue, and I am thankful we have an anticheat at all given how long it took to come out but I will never really feel comfortable knowing there is a program on my computer that I do not own that potentially has access to my computers hardware and software at a level even I don't fully understand. While I welcome anticheat, I am somewhat cautious of the power it has, and I would really have preferred a non-kernal level solution.

In summary. Sea of Thieves is in my opinion, one of the greatest games of all time. It offers an experience unlike any other. It is not a flawless masterpiece. It stumbled out of the gate, like so many great games before it, and while it is absolutely important we hold companies responsible for flawed releases, especially nowadays, Sea of Thieves is one of those "Rare" examples where even after a somewhat shaky start, and a somewhat rough road, the game right now is truly doing better than ever. I am posting this review right before the March 20th 2024 Preview event, where Rare has promised sneak peeks at the next 3 seasons of the games future. It will be fascinating to see where the game goes, and I am sure I will come back to this review at some point to see how it has held up. But right now, I feel fairly confident in saying: there is something to love about this game for almost anyone, and with its wide availability, I cannot recommend it highly enough.

In any case, I hope you have found this review somewhat helpful, and I hope to see you on the seas!\t\t\t\t\t
