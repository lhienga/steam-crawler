Symphony of War is a strategy game that shares much of its DNA with the Fire Emblem series but fails to meaningfully implement most of its mechanics, leading to an ultimately shallow gameplay experience that is further plagued by incomprehensible balancing and bad writing.

\t
🟩 Pros
\t
🟥 Cons
\t
✔️ An expansive upgrade tree that invites interesting squad combinations and experimentation. Coupled with bonus challenges on certain maps, this allows for decent replayability.
\t
❌ Nonexistent balancing; there is a vast chasm between both story characters that are laughably broken and those barely above a regular unit, with the same issue extending to entire class archetypes. The regular game is absolutely trivial on all but the highest difficulty.
\t
✔️ Solid pace; maps feel in general a bit shorter than in comparable games, and the inclusion of optional Gaiden chapters helps both struggling players catch up in lategame and also tweak how they want to experience the rest of the game.
\t
❌ The U.I. is somewhat finicky. For example, scrolling over the map with your mouse seems overly sensitive, and there is a distinct lack of (QoL) options, such as the ability to speed up the turns of enemies and allies, leading to certain maps having minute-long waits at a time.

\t
🟨 Miscellaneous Thoughts
\t
The recent update made significant improvements to the portrait art, but many still lack expressiveness to the point where I wish they were just completely static. Late game characters still lack a presence in the game entirely.
\t
The marketplace, being your primary source for traits, units, and artifacts but being randomized after every map, introduces a layer of RNG to the game that some players may dislike.



Technical Issues and Performance
Overall, I have no major complaints about SoW’s performance. The game crashed once, but I did not otherwise encounter any game-breaking bugs. That said, the U.I. can be a bit finicky, and very late into the game, I encountered microstutters when initiating combat.

Story and Setting
The overall quality of the writing is far and beyond the worst part of SoW. The best way that I can come up with is that it reads juvenile, and I am not describing it like this to be mean, but rather that this is the genuine impression I’ve had. This comes partially from the presentation, featuring Fire Emblem-esque map-crawls between chapters, but also from the caviats of the worldbuilding, such as featuring support conversations between playable characters.

The basic idea, however, is that you are playing Lindly, canonically the female avatar and name, though both can be changed upon starting the game, and the name whenever you’re in the menu between missions. She is being enveloped in what seems to be a political conflict that, however, soon expands into the usual high fantasy, prophetic war.

The basic story isn’t bad in itself, but suffers a lot from being overly wordy, throwing tons of regions, names, and nobles at you right from the start that make it incredibly obnoxious trying to keep a firm grip on the overarching story, but more so the intense tonal whiplash that some scenes evoke. It tries to be incredibly gritty with genuinely not terrible twists, but then tosses cartoonishly evil ‘villains’ like the Black Knight at you, committing war crimes while brandishing their evil, glowing sword "Balmung hungers..". It’s atrocious, and where it's not, it’s boring.

Supports are barely worth mentioning as each character has merely a handful of scenes, all of which suffer from the same issues as the above but with the added weight of pacing. Characters that barely know each other will start romances; hell, characters for whom it makes no sense at all even within their own dialogue will do it, and it feels like it’s all just there to be formulaic.

Beyond this, I’ve spotted numerous other quirks with the dialogue, like grammar errors, inconsistent capitalization, and naturally, the cardinal sin of obnoxiously spelling rhoticity like Rrrrrrrraskuja.

Graphics and Sound
Graphically, SoW is okay. The sprites and combat animations are fantastic, and so are the once-atrocious-looking portraits after the recent graphical update. That said, the maps and in-game cutscenes still have a distinct RPGMaker vibe to them that is just a bit too low-quality to not stand out in comparison. There is also no option to tweak the graphics or the resolution, for that matter.

Sound mixing is a bigger issue; while the music and general sound are perfectly fine, certain elements are off, such as the voices of specific characters being vastly quieter than the others and two late-game units having no voice acting at all.

Gameplay and Difficulty
The main gameplay is split into two major parts: unit management and playing the actual maps. For the former, unit management happens in between chapters wherein your army is split into squads of 3x3 units; those same units are tiered and upgrade through reaching experience in their current class while secondarily leveling up like in any RPG and gaining stats according to their nature and archetype. The upgrade tree is expansive and, at first glance, the game's strongest point, although the game can be obtuse about what the point of all the different classes is. Instead, unit descriptions are buried within a submenu, while during upgrading you merely get a throwaway sentence of the broad idea of what the class is supposed to excel at. Furthermore, during my Captain playthrough, some classes severely underperformed in comparison to others, such as the entirety of the Archer archetype; the overall difficulty seems to be designed with Warlord and Permadeath in mind, although the latter comes with its own issues. The answer for most any map was to just vomit more units into any of my squads and merely play around the movement type.

Now, having put around seven hours into Warlord, it is a more engaging experience, but I can foresee the same issues arising there as well, namely, that stat bonuses from items are simply too explosive, to the point that any game would have trouble providing a consistent experience throughout different difficulty modes. Permadeath provides some much-needed pressure, but that pressure ends up being more obnoxious than a real influence on player strategy as you can’t control your squad's attacks and defense past their baseline formation, making character death largely a part of natural RNG.

It's too many turning gears that all end up shallow. Too many unit types that aren't balanced and different enough to feel worthwhile; too many traits that feel ultimately superficial. A nature/element system that's just a headache to fiddle with. I'm sure this sort of management is the reason why a lot of people enjoyed the game, but I don't feel it has enough depth to warrant engaging with it.

Final Thoughts
Symphony of War isn’t terrible. Despite all my criticisms, it does its best to emulate and build upon a genre that the developer is very clearly passionate about. Even now, I’m still tempted to go back to my Warlord save and just give it one more map to experiment just a little more, but at the end of the day, I just don’t think that this charm and passion are enough to recommend the game on. Ultimately, for me, the game suffers a death from a thousand paper cuts, starting with inconsistent writing, even if it might have some genuinely interesting ideas here and there, a difficulty curve that is utterly incomprehensible due to a myriad of design decisions, and just not being a very tactical experience. But by Donar, do I want Diana to pick me up by my legs and tear me in half like she’d a sheaf of papers.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI mean. Fire Emblem with a good amount of refreshing new tactics? I like it so far.

I'm currently at Chapter 11 and, honestly, the story is pretty alright B U T the mechanics and customization possibilities are what really make it for me.

Story itself is solid; it stands on its own two feet and borrows some familiar ideas from other similar places, but is very easily unique due to the perspective we view the whole story from. I don't want to speak of it too much, but while the writing could absolutely get an upgrade in some areas, the ideas are well conveyed and very nice to follow.

Mechanically its been fun to mess around with different unit combos by utilizing the squad-based unit formations! I have a few that are "y'know, JUST HORSES" or "y'know, JUST HORSEBOWS", but my MC has two mages supported by a light though armored backline and a strong defensive frontline. Classing up and down with characters is fun even if it feels a lil grindy- but that's just FE stuff for yah.

All in all, I like it. Its been fun to sit down and play with friends naming my characters, and us all making fun of the dumb idiots trying to run an army to the highest peaks when they barely know how to run their own lives.

Failed Pickup Artist part 1 is great btw.\t\t\t\t\t
