\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBeautifully done all around. This will be a pretty general review but I'll cover the big points. The story is well written, thoughtful, and sprinkled with healthy amounts of intrigue and twists. Certain elements were predictable, but much more importantly they were still satisfying to see play out despite having already clocked the narrative direction. While the cast of face characters isn't as deep as your average FE game, I appreciate the amount of effort put into character writing and the presence of the fan favourite support relationship system. Speaking to the game's relation as a spiritual successor (emphasis on the success part imo) to the GBA Era Fire Emblem titles: I was nearly giddy to see a team used high quality sprites and 16/32 bit maps in a 2022 title. There's something satisfying and concise about the amount of detail portrayed, and the color work is always fantastic. Personal aesthetic opinions aside, the game expounds upon the simple-not-so-simple chess like game of shuffling units around what equates to a giant board presented by their predecessors in a number of interesting ways. Single units are replaced by squads, lead by either face characters or particularly able NPC units, and a number of the over-arcing systems such as the weapon/magic triangles and individual armaments are replaced by easy to grasp traits associated with unit types and squad formation. This system broadens and deepens as the game progresses, but I never felt overwhelmed by squad management even into the late game where you have to *option* to field 20 full squads. Some things are notably missing or in some ways lesser, such as a less than thrilling class progression for some units and those oh so satisfying critical animations, but these are easily forgotten and overshadowed by the unique elements the game does bring to the table, no spoilers though.Overall I'm struggling not to slap a 10/10 on this beauty, but I feel that would be mired in personal bias so I'll say this:If you love GBA era Fire Emblem, 16/32 sprite work, fantasy medieval strategy games, any/all of that? 10/10If you're using a hyper critical eye and breaking down all the games systems and balancing? Still a solid 8/10If you're a weak man who's made uncomfortable by big muscly women? 0/10 Get out of here, you don't belong here. All hail War Goddess Diana and Warrior Queen Narima <3\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLeaving a positive review because I want to support the developers and I think it's worth getting on sale. Symphony of War is one of those games where the pieces are all there, but the execution is...lacking. The formula is great and relatively unique, being a blend of fire emblem/advanced wars/ogre where your units aren't individual people but rather squads of units with their own stats. But there's aspects of the game that suck so hard it drags everything else down. It's especially evident comparing the early and late games where the cracks really start to show.
Pros:
  • The autocombat system is satisfying. It's very "juicy" and fun to watch even 30 hours in as of this review.
  • Customizing your units is really fun. It's one of those games where you can pick some completely generic unit and get attached to them like in an xcom or wesnoth campaign. I didn't once care about any of the story units, but there were plenty of randos who I put a lot of resources into that if they died meant an automatic restart. If this ever gets a sequel this should be up front and center the thing to focus on expanding.
  • It's got a lot of nice QoL features and good stuff from other games in the genre. Restarting a failed mission is painless. There's an arena to grind XP that's smartly limited. Permadeath can be toggled on and off at will and losing story characters in combat(except your lord) doesn't matter. There's a bonus objective system that while it could use some work does encourage you to play better as opposed to turtling. Also if a certain number of turns pass you autowin the level, which is both a penalty and a blessing depending on what you're trying to do.
  • The game gives you a lot of reasons to spend your money and the economy is well thought out. Tier 1 common units are dirt cheap and reliable, while special units and items can be very expensive but feel good when you get them. I never felt like I had too much or too little money which is something to applaud.
Cons:
  • The writing is atrocious. All of the characters are mary sues or what I can only surmise as the main writer's amateur DnD OCs or fetishes(there are A LOT of giant muscular women in this game for some mysterious reason...). I think the plot somehow managed to hit almost every single SRPG plot trope you could possibly cram into a game. At one point a textbook deus ex machina happens as the main protagonist descends from the heavens and saves everyone, which is pretty much when I stopped giving any of my already limited ♥♥♥♥♥. The one saving grace of the story is the devs have blessed us with a skip cutscene option. I would be leaving a very negative review otherwise.
  • The UI is bad bad bad. It's super hard to manage your squads once you hit late-early game and it doesn't get easier. You're going to be spending a lot of time editing your units and squads and this is a massive time sink. Iterating through all of your units(of which you can hit 100+ easily) is pure pain. Also have fun deploying your squads at the start of battle since there's nothing to distinguish them from each other and there's no easy way to swap them around like in say fire emblem's deployment screen.
  • There's just not many classes and I was super disappointed at how little options there are. I'm willing to give that a pass because it's an indie dev, but seriously there needs to be like twice as many classes. It only gets worse as the game progresses and tier 1 and 2 units become rarer.
  • Healing is very awkward and it kind of kills the pacing and squad diversity. You can either put healers in every single squad or you can have outside of combat heal squads. The problem is in-combat healing is incredibly powerful while out of combat heals are punishingly limited and inefficient. This naturally trends the game towards self sufficient squads that can heal themselves and fight, whereas other games would force you to use your healers smartly to run around the battlefield without getting attacked. When every squad is running 1-2 healers by necessity, it doesn't leave a lot of room for other unit types.
  • At the midgame you get some ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ magic powers that let you teleport units, make them semi invulnerable, and get free moves. It kind of breaks the game design but there's a sort of implication that if you want to play efficiently and keep your dudes alive you'll be forced to use them at times. Didn't care for it.
