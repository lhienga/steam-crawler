\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI was a little turned off by the low quality appearance of the maps, but there is actually better detail in game that most pictures or snapshots give credit. I was worried the squad based system would be tedious but with each unique captain and getting to focus on the ones you want for your desired strategy, it was actually very enjoyable and rewarding (for the compositions that worked).
Honestly when you finish a campaign and you really wish there was more post game content, that's a good sign.
One gripe I have is that it seems most final tier classes (dragon riders as an example) basically become available for the last 1 or two story missions. If the game had a post-game dungeon expansion, or a bunch of optional quests before the end game boss it would feel a lot better toward the end.
I'd also like to see some form of carry over for 2nd play through, like choosing 3 artifacts to keep or something like that.
All in all it was a great game and very enjoyable (moderately challenging) play.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGood fire emblem-esque game. It was a nice surprise seeing inspiration taken from multiple classics such as breath of fire II, ogre battle: march of the black queen, shining force and more.

The positives are:
GAMEPLAY: The tried and true fire emblem tactics formula, with a couple of twists here and there. This is not its best incarnation, one could even say that overall it's only decent, but it works well enough to satiate the hunger for tactics fans.
ART: Very unique and distinct, it grew on my as I played and I ended up really loving it. Most characters look sharp and the girls are beautiful. The pixel art for the combat and maps is also really well made and nostalgic.
SOUND/MUSIC: Decent tunes all around, and in particular I was pleasantly surprised by the voices for the main characters, however little there was. Also the sound effects for combats and such are serviceable as well.

The average are:
STORY/WRITTING: The story is your run-of-the-mill "le big evil, word ending dragon" which we saw 1000 times on fire emblem and other games, so nothing special there. The author tried to add a few small twists here and there when it comes to the politics of the empires and realms, but they ended up being quite basic. The main issue is the writting; it is not exactly bad, but it feel really "dry" overall. Another issue is actually a lack of more flavorful dialogue to build some of the main cast, and one does feel like we didn't get a lot of necessary interactions. One example: A brother and a sister get reunited at the early stages of the story. In any normal game, you'd naturally expect a scene with conversation as these siblings reunite, but nope, nothing happens. Another case: the 3 main cast, including two presumed dead, return from the heavens as ultmost holy and powerful beings. You'd naturally expect some interesting interactions and conversations between these leaders and their army of followers being in awe or shocked with the sudden transformation, but nope, no such dialogue happens. As you can see, it is quite confusing why the author omited so many possible interactions, that comparing to fire emblem, are used to build up the characters.
MAPS: Mechanically speaking, maps are not the best in this game. They aren't bad, just uninteresting. They mostly boil down to one or two chockepoints and that's it, not much interesting mechanics or design here unfortunatelly, so not many advanced tactical decisions are necessary.

The bad are:
MICROMANAGEMENT: The game creator was inspired by ogre battle: march of the black queen when it comes to your playable squads in the maps. So instead of your unit being just a single character, that charactter is the leader of the squad and with him there can be a soldier, mage, thief or healer, etc. While at first glance this was very appealing in terms of customization and such, honestly by the end I was over it because it leads to way too much micromanagement, when you have literally a 100 characters to work with and deciding their class, element, squad limit, positioning, artifacts, etc. Now having finished the game with 48h, I think a good 25% of my time (if not more) was spent on the army management screen. I think for a future game I'd prefer a return to 1 unit means 1 character format.
ENDING: This is tied with the story being cookie-cutter and unoriginal, and compounded with the dry writting. The resolution of the game's misteries and plot points is pretty underwhelming. Even the character epilogues, a staple of fire emblem games, feel like they could have used a little more flair.

Conclusion: All in all I'm happy with my experience with the game, even tough as far as price goes I feel it is a bit high, for what ultimatelly is just a neat but flawed fire emblem clone. Waiting for a sale is recommended, and then so is the game. And I wish it would have been shorter on time (like I mentioned, in great part due to the micro management of the army, so cutting down on artifacts and too many soldiers) and having improved writting and characterization, as well as more interesting maps.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI just don't get the appeal of this game. Symphony of War looks and feels like an old Gameboy reboot but I don't say that lovingly. The look and feel may trigger a little nostalgia but SoW's gameplay is dreadfully simplistic and the storyline and dialogue are sins against God and nature. I abandoned the game somewhere in the middle of the story and still felt like I stuck with the game far longer than I should have.

Pros:
+ There are a lot of components here that could have made for a great game. If you can stomach the story and don't mind overly simplistic tactics/strategy you might get some enjoyment out of this.
+ Art style might appeal to old retro-game fans

Cons
- Storyline and dialogue is atrocious...I mean, really bad... Kill it with fire bad. You'll need to *really* love the gameplay to subject yourself to this torture.
- Battles are 100% simulated... there is no tactical decision making outside of movement, when to attack and when to apply heal. There are a couple of "special" abilities that can be researched but the game is overly easy even without using them. This makes the battle part of the game (which is 80% of the gameplay) a grind and the only true strategy decisions come in-between missions when you form and upgrade your armies.
- The army upgrade screens are just horrible and violate a number of UIX best practices... such as requiring way too many button clicks to accomplish certain tasks, and storing similar functionality on different and far away screens. Do you want to buy something for an army, hire a new mercenary, and apply those changes to an existing squad? Well, I hope you have some patience because you'll need to click through some 7 different screens to accomplish that... ugh.
- The soldier upgrade progressions missed the mark by randomly (?) pre-selecting which attributes increased from battle experience (I'm sure there was some pattern to it but I didn't really care enough to research it). The game was begging for an experience points bucket so you could better customize how your soldiers progressed. I didn't appreciate at first how simple and annoying this upgrade process was until my units started hitting more advanced tiers and then suddenly didn't have the necessary attributes to upgrade how I wanted.
- Enemy AI was just pitiful and relied completely on brute force tactics and overwhelming numbers. Out-thinking and whittling down larger enemy armies was far too easy. To be fair I only played on normal difficulty. Mid way through the game I toyed with the idea of restarting at a higher difficulty but I couldn't stomach the thought of replaying that god-awful storyline.
- Voice-acting was just horrendous and made me wish that there was permadeath for army units... I'd have ran a few of my characters straight into the gates of hell just to avoid hearing them speak if I could have.\t\t\t\t\t

8. Some sense of good and bad in my units

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTactics games have, and always will be, one of my absolute favorite genres. From the Ogre Battle series to Shining Force to Final Fantasy tactics, the classics just had this perfect mix of content: iconic music, stylistic art styles, strong stories and characters, and a powerful game play loop that makes you feel progress as your army grows in tactical prowess and strength. While many modern games pay homage to the old titles, few seem to just fundamentally get how to make a game which pushes all the buttons these old titles did. Fell Seal is, perhaps, the greatest example of a modern game which just nailed the gameplay loop of the Final Fantasy Tactics branch of the genre, but flopped about with mediocre characters, a godawful story that took away any sort of grand feeling to the game. Those of us old farts who still remember playing games like Final Fantasy Tactics or Ogre Battle 64 remember them fondly not only because the gameplay was good (even if a bit dated in modern times) -- but because they were deep and rich experiences where your humble band evolves into an army within the confines of a story that makes sense.

Above any other tactics game I've played, even those with solid gameplay, Symphony of War is absolutely a game out of that old mold. The game is a love story to the old titles, with an art style very reminiscent of the two aforementioned titles and a musical score with both original tracks and ones which you go "Oh yeah, FFT in the house here" or "Totally Ogre Battle." The pixel art is hands down some of the best and most detailed I've seen in a very long time, with incredibly rich tilesets, combat animations, and spritework. While, naturally, there are a lot of assets that get reused or refurbished, the amount of custom spritework that goes into making the cutscenes solid is pretty incredible. Combat is gorgeous and fun to watch, from the snapping crossbow shots of a ranger to the blistering arrow volleys of an archer squad. The interface and UI in the squad organization between deployments is fantastic, and you really get a strong feeling of your army growing as you move from map to map.

What really stands out, however, is just how damn good the gameplay is. I played on Warlord from the start, and the first half of the game, especially, had a lot of challenges and needed a steady chunk of restarts to get all the mission objectives completed. There's a lavish array of tactical breadth to be had in squad composition and mission execution, and squads behave very similar to how individual units would in a conventional single-unit tactics game like FFT. Combat, however, is very similar to Ogre Battle...if all the flaws from that series were removed and polished to a great level of detail. For example, let's say you want to make a mage cadre that can roast an enemy infantry unit in one turn flat. In Ogre Battle, you'd plop your tank in the front, your casters in the back, and feel largely invulnerable. Not so here -- casters may have incredible damage, but archer squads or assassin infantry units can butcher your back line as if it were your front. The answer is to make squads with heavy units in the front and the back, right? Absolutely...except you also have to deal with firearms squads that will decimate a squad of all heavy infantry without some way of their own to get at the back.

The end result is an absolutely great army building feeling where you need to use all the tools in the toolkit to put together a good army. You need units capable of taking out assassins. You need units with mobility to take objectives and to wipe out enemy mages. You need pikemen lines to bust cavalry charges, and that invicible super-unit you're used to having 1v30 doesn't last long unless you have some way of taking out archer units sniping its healers while you're bogged down in combat and morale starts falling. There's no "perfect" unit that does it all, and many mission objectives require you to move quickly and decisively instead of just letting the AI flounder into you haplessly.

I could go on, but you get the picture. While the story isn't anything particularly exceptional, and dialogue in particular is a bit mediocre, the themes and concepts are refreshing and appreciated in a gaming world dominated by woke BS. There are some particular themes thrown in, such as cultural degradation and banking exploitation, that are right on the money with real world topics and yet don't break the immersion of the game itself. Most importantly, the game has a sense of scale as you progress that just feels right. As your army grows in power and you're waging actual wars, the battlefields feel the part. The full-scale war missions are saturated in units and feel overwhelming, with tactical groups of say 4-6 cavalry which will charge your back lines or strings of archer squads spraying rains of arrows down from fortifications onto your squads below. Not only this, enemy squads are not designed like garbage -- I frequently ran into squads which were basically the same as ones I was using as the player. It's challenging, it's often unfair, but it's absolutely fun and rewarding to able to use tactics to outmaneuver human-like compositions.

All-in-all, Symphony of War is a title that sits within the top echelon of games in the tactics genre. While I want to fault the writing a bit for needing some improvement, the fact that this quality of a title is put out by two guys doing all the programming, design, and music with the help of only three core artists is incredible. Not everyone needs to be an affluent writer, and the game is written well enough. Anybody who likes tactics games should grab this in a heartbeat. Kudos to the developers -- this game is truly a work of art I'll definitely return to again.\t\t\t\t\t
