\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's like Ogre Battle borrowed the best of Fire Emblem and ran with it in the world of Triangle Strategy. Between the satisfying & diverse battles, and the deep character customization, I had a great time. The story is serviceable, and with the hand-drawn portraits even the visuals are a net positive. Really, my only knock on the game is the absolutely insane menu designs, which make the significant amount of army tweaking way more time-consuming and painful than it needs to be.

Protip to the developer: when your army can include up to 200 soldiers, every item/trait/artefact decision starts with the item in question, not with the soldier. The question is never "which of these 100 artefacts should I put on Bob?", it's "who should have this new sword I found?".

That aside, it's a fantastic game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe star of this game is the gameplay. The rest is rather mediocre but since it's a cheap game as well, I consider it a worthwhile purchase.

Graphics, sound design, controls, etc. are all kind of average. Good in some parts, not so good in other parts. Nothing stands out incredibly well or incredibly bad.

Story and character writing are by far the worst aspect of this game. To be honest, you probably improve your game experience by skipping every single cutscene.

Some of the worst parts are one of your characters turning into a female giant straight out of some vore Hentai, a furry assassin character joining your party and becoming an "alpha male", and one character talking about how he never used to find women when he was nice but now that he is a bad guy every girl is into him. Very neckbeard-y and cringeworthy in general.

The best part is the gameplay. The battles are fun and challenging but not too unfair. Building your army is lots of fun and feels very rewarding. Adding new units to your squads, leveling those units up and so on is very fun. The game also doesn't overstay it's welcome, in that it ends before becoming repetitive. The soon-to-come DLC should also introduce NG+ and more missions which is a great thing for this game.

A Glorious, Albeit Superficial and at Times Impenetrable Saga

It's like Game of Thrones if all the budget went into the action scenes, resulting in the writing and presentation suffering over time. Or to put it another way, it's like Game of Thrones.

Rated from someone who has never Fired an Emblem or Battled an Ogre (at least not Tactically).

Raise the Standards!
- Forming battle units is an engrossing puzzle thanks to formations, class interactions, capacity and more.
- Good mix of classes with specialized roles, which enhance the battle unit puzzle and tactical space.
- Encounters are quick and occasionally have a shake-up to keep you engaged.
- Lovely pixel art and animations in combat.

Lower your standards
- Obscure mechanics. Traits and tactical interactions are all buried in guides that are difficult to navigate.
- Default difficulty is pretty easy (so far)
- Permadeath option more tedious than challenging.
- Character writing inconsistent to the point where it gets distracting.
- Multiple contrasting art styles also distracting.

Recommend To:
- Fans of "dudes on a map" board games.
- Fans of tactical games who skip cut scenes on first playthroughs.
- Pixel art fans with selective memory.
- Aspiring cosmetic artists - I haven't yet found the tutorial outlining the beauty routine these war torn veterans must practice, but it's gotta be a doozy.

Alternatively Suggest:
Songs of Conquest: - More a Heros of Might & Magic kinda game, but it provides a lot of tactical depth while addressing many of the issues I have with Symphony. Better teaching tools integreted within its campaigns, sharper dialogue and a steady difficulty curve (mostly). It gets bonus points too for doing more with the music motif in the title via its bard songs.

I'm still enjoying my time with Symphony of War. While the elements surrounding the gameplay need help, the gameplay is substantive enough to keep me coming back. If you're a 'games are to be played, not read/watched' type who can spice up your own experience with self imposed challenges, I think you'll get a lot out of this title, but if you need some presentation to get your feet marching, consider my alternative first.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA fun mash up of Fire Emblem and Tactics Ogre.

Lots of customization in how you handle your army, can go all in on dedicated units of heavy infantry or light cavalry, or you can make an army that's made up entirely of mixed unit type squads. The use of a leadership stat to limit how many soldiers can fit in a squad works pretty will, it keeps you from steam rolling the enemy by putting everything good you got into one full size squad and shows how you're progressing over the course of the game, getting to fit another guy into a squad makes a big difference. Theres traits you can slap on individual soldiers and items that you can attach to squads that really lets you get into the fine detail of how you want a squad to work. The battles themselves are pretty great to watch, the art, sound, and animations make it punchy, unless an attack is deflected you can hear the impact. You can see how the game handles different unit types and their speeds in combat by when they attack, which helps you learn how it all works.

Story is mostly what you'd expect for something like this, with a couple of interesting twists, but beyond that nothing that blew my socks off. There's also a relationship system where characters can have conversations between missions and you can learn a little bit about them and the world that they live in.

The game is pretty good, there's only a few things that hold it back. Some of the unit traits you can give dudes seem pretty pointless, like "No movement or combat penalties in desert/snow" sounds pretty good until you realize that only covers like three or four missions and you'd be better served with something else. The whole relationship thing can get that same problem that Fire Emblem has, where two nerds hang out like twice and go "Hey, I love you now", or how one voiced character is barely fleshed out and only has one conversation with another, more developed character. Generally that part of it feels a little half baked, it might be fine if you're not looking for more interesting interpersonal relationships in the plot. Also parts of the game are very "HELLO, THIS WAS MADE IN RPG MAKER". Nothing that keeps the game from not being fun, just stuff to be aware of.

Overall, I'd give this 8 squads surrendering in terror because their morale is at "Shattered" out of 10, if you like strategy and tactical stuff maybe check it out.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTLDR: it's like Fire Emblem but more fun IMO.

This game is a wet dream. Idk what the hell happens in the story because I skipped all the cutscenes (thank you, by the way for adding that into your game. You didn't have to do that, but you did), but the gameplay is S tier for a indie strategy RPG and if you like Fire Emblem, this game will probably be right up your alley. If you hate strategy RPGs, IDK if this game will change your mind, but it's a good time if you're remotely at all a Fire Emblem/Strategy RPG fan. Recommended with flying colors.

Oh also, I recommend playing with Permadeath turned on. The game's clearly balanced around it, and they don't make it too hard unless you're on max difficulty (which is still way easier than some other SRPGs)\t\t\t\t\t

This review contains spoilers.



This game was exceptional in many ways, and it is extremely rare to see a game which has its characters and its world be unafraid to incorporate elements of philosophy and theology into its universe's religion, let alone one that isn't afraid to make a God figure that is actually benevolent.

The gameplay is great, but I'm not a usual TRPG player. The extent of TRPG games I've played is Troubleshooter: Abandoned Children (which I think classifies as a TRPG?), Fire Emblem: Awakening, and Fire Emblem: Three Houses. I enjoyed all of them, so maybe I'm just easy to please; truth is, I value a story and world building much more than I value gameplay itself. That said, I had fun as I played this game, and I enjoy a game which gives you versatility in team composition, which this game allows for.

I have a lot to say on the topic of philosophy and theology in the in-game world, Tahnra. I seriously respect and am impressed by the level in which the developers went to flesh out the structure of the world in their games. From what I understand, this game is a loose sequel to the Echoes of Aether game; perhaps my opinions would change (better or worse, I can't say) if I played that game, but judging from this game alone, the lore, the religion, the philosophy, and the characters in this game are very well designed.

The relationship options are extremely cringe, and I don't use that word liberally. I played as the male MC, and a majority of the relationship dialogue was like sandpaper to my brain. Stefan x Diana, Narima x Jules, and Lysander x Beatrix were probably the only ones I could tolerate, but with like the sole exception of Stefan and Diana, there were still unbearable elements in each of them. Lysander and Beatrix can also be excluded, mostly on the basis that it was pretty damn funny. Narima and Jules, despite reasonably being the most logical pairing for the two, felt incredibly rushed; like Jules only found her interesting because "hot war goddess awooga", while Narima was there having some massive inner conflict about her womanhood. Very weird. The others were somehow even more jarring than that; Sybil and Barnabas, coming in that late with what could only be described as the most whimsical and childish pairing yet, and then there's Zelos and Abigayle...

In general, the between-character dialogue was very mediocre. The sole exceptions were Lysander's. Lysander's dialogue practically made this game's story as good as it was. His whole character, in fact; and I'm not even referring to his whole plot story, but his character itself. He was a doubting Thomas who had a strong personal conviction, was philosophically astute, and yet the writer very impressively managed to avoid making him seem like some kind of "Writer's OC" with extraneous information to the game's world at his disposal. Overall, a very well-written character, who unfortunately managed to raise the bar for character dialogue in the game, only for it not to be matched by the other characters. Also, the lack of character dialogue is pretty sad (though maybe it was for the best, seeing how little of it was actually tolerable...)

Still, the strongest thing this game has going for it, personally, is its worldbuilding. I'll eventually write a larger review on that topic, because it's integral to why the universe presented in this game is so impressively constructed, and why it felt so satisfying to play, in my opinion.

Overall, this game is a strong 9/10 in my eyes, only losing that 1 point because of its embarrassing dialogue and lack of more, and better, dialogue. There's more to it than just that, though, but as mentioned earlier, that's to be explained later. I'll link to the review when I make it, if I ever get around to it, which will revolve around the topic of theology in this game's universe (Donar, Zanatus, the Avatars/Nephilim, etc.) If it's not clear already, I highly recommend this game for anyone who enjoys any of the aspects I've mentioned this game excels at.\t\t\t\t\t
