Seems to be a love letter to Fire Emblem 4 & 5 with some Ogre Battle mixed in. Never thought I'd see the two series combined like that at all, let along successfully.

There were a lot of concepts and world building that just never really got fleshed out. I'm hoping that there will be a sequel one day that develops the world a bit more and focuses less on the hero and his little band.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI thoroughly enjoyed playing this game, which combines elements from Fire Emblem and Suikoden in a tactical role-playing game (T-RPG) format. The gameplay involves starting with two small squads, each led by a storyline character. These characters are confined to specific class types, like archer or infantry, which can be upgraded or side-graded within their respective roles. Squad leaders can be both storyline and non-story characters.

Squads are composed mainly of non-storyline characters, with a maximum of 9 units based on the squad leader's leadership stat. Units are organized in a 3x3 formation, with front, mid, and back rows customizable to your liking. Initially, there are two male classes (archer in light armor or fighter in heavy armor) and two female classes (medic with no armor or militia in light armor). After mastering the tier 1 class, units can upgrade to tier 2, unlocking a variety of class options like sword and shield, cavalry knight, or archer on horseback.

The tier 3 classes further expand possibilities, offering upgraded versions or hybrids of tier 2 classes. The game encourages experimentation, allowing you to allocate upgrade materials without penalty, facilitating exploration of different builds. One of the game's strengths lies in the flexibility to try different classes without consuming upgrade materials. Allocating and reclaiming resources upon dismissing or demoting a unit encourages players to explore diverse builds.

While the lack of a color palette customization feature for units is a missed opportunity, the game compensates with a 10/10 rating for the sheer amount of content. I spent over 100 hours exploring the game, and there's still more to discover.

While the gameplay, menu interface, and tactical role-play elements receive positive scores, the story and character development fall short. The narrative progresses too quickly, leaving gaps and plot holes. Character depth is lacking, with some individuals having minimal impact despite regular appearances in most cutscenes. A dedicated story writer could significantly enhance this aspect.

In summary:

Story: 6/10
Gameplay: 8/10
Game Menu: 8/10 (Considering it's a T-RPG)
Story Characters: 3/10 (Lack of development in most characters)
Tactical Roleplaynessly : 7/10
Maps: 5/10 (Most are similar, missed opportunities for variety)
Amount of Stuff to Do: 10/10 (Over 100 hours of gameplay with more to explore)\t\t\t\t\t

Where Symphony of War may slightly lack in story, it more than makes up for itself in game-play. Unlike its' predecessors, SoW has a leader which is part of a Squad. Squads may have up to 9 characters in them depending on the Leadership level of the leader. This gives you endless customization options, for fun and for beating up baddies. Also, the way of going about upgrading units is unique in that they have a handful of options to change into. For example, Medics can become a Sorceress, Templar, or Valkyrie.

My only qualms with SoW is in the story and characters themselves. I find Lindly/Devoa (The main character) to be just OK. She isn't full of personality. I also noticed as the story progressed that Lindly's dialogue style changed from formal to very casual-she would randomly use slang for words she may not have in dialogue prior.

While some of the support characters were super fun and full of personality (Beatrix, Sybil, Stefan, and Diana to name a few) others fell flat or just weren't really part of the main story (Abigayle and Raskuja among others). They would pop in and out randomly during dialogue or not at all.

Game aside, I also purchased the guide and found it super helpful since some things are not entirely explained in the game. BUT I think it could use some improvements such as:
-Add a comprehensive guide for the main characters that shows their base stats and possible growth rates, biographies, and a portrait. I found myself going back to the "relationships" tab for reference in the menus but it's a bit confusing.
-Mini guide for what units are weak/strong against. For example, Magic users are strong against heavy armor units but average against other magic users.
-A Unit Tier/Upgrade guide WITH visuals. It would be really nice to have a comprehensive visual guide to units and their possible classes. I ended up printing a fan made one to reference.

All in all, SoW is worthwhile if you love tactical strategy RPGs! You'll find yourself losing an hour to building up each squad and trying new artifacts to equipped. Battles themselves are fullfilling. And though the story may be subpar at times, it's still a good one! But don't take just my word for it, the reviews speak for themselves.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you're a fan of classic Fire Emblem games, this is a very nostalgic homage. It takes a lot of classic Fire Emblem ideas and mechanics, and iterates on them in very creative ways. The main difference is that each unit is a squad instead of a single hero, so you can customize the composition of your army in a lot of new and interesting ways. Each unit type has different rules for how they interact with squads (ie attacking frontline, backline, randomly etc.) so how you setup each units squad plays heavily into what sort of engagements they will be strong at (for example a squad with a lot of assassins will be strong at taking out healers/mages in backlines, but will struggle if that backline is covered by a few armored units instead).

The story is fairly straightforward, it has a few decent twists but for the most part is pretty much what you would expect from a game as inspired by old Fire Emblem as it is. Overall, had a blast playing it on steam deck, was very nostalgic.\t\t\t\t\t
