\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSymphony of War is a delightful take on the traditional Fire Emblem style turn based combat (with optional permadeath options.) At its core, it's an army building tactical turn based RPG with some light story elements. Where it differs in the traditional Fire Emblem style is that rather than individual units to command, you have "Armies" that are comprised of up to 9 units, of varying different classes and traits. The "main" unit of an army is its leader, which is often a story character but can be any unit, with their leadership stat being the primary means of determining how strong their army can be.

I love the concept behind this. It allows you to really strategize, or even go for thematic groups based on the personality/class of the leader of an army. It has a tremendous amount of depth in what you can take into a mission, which I found very fun and engaging. I will note that the higher difficulties will have varying degrees of return on this, as is often the case. When the numbers are just stacking against you, the min/maxing strategies tend to reduce your options, which isn't a bad thing, it's just something to note if you're planning on playing harder than the normal difficulty.

The army building is absolutely the highlight of the game, and if you like these kind of games there is definitely something for you. With that being said, there are a few points of criticism I would offer:

1- There is an element of micromanagement that does get to be a bit tedious. While it's great the game has so much depth with equipment and traits and such, it does tend to bog down the game quite a bit, and it can be kind of a nightmare to manage it all. I don't know how you go about it without sacrificing some of that depth. Perhaps rather than traits for each individual unit, you could have the traits apply specifically to all units in the army, or just have the leader use traits. You may need to expand the number of traits available per army to compensate the scaling down of depth, but I do feel there is a balance somewhere that can be found.

2- The story is kind of a low point, honestly a really low point. The characters were rather stale, and you really don't get to spend a lot of time with them. The relationship building is also pretty barebones. I found it odd that you don't really have an influence over when they happen, especially because if you don't do them the round they appear, you're locked out of it forever. This is odd because you don't have a way, short of external guides, of seeing who can pair with who.

Outside of characters, the story is a bit nonsense. There is futuristic tech in places for....reasons? That's never really explored. A character has the ability to see into the future....until he just doesn't anymore. There's several times of "yay we're winning we're winning we're winning, oh we lost everything," as well, which I found rather basic. There's obviously a lot of thought into this particular world, we just really don't see any of it, to the point I wonder if there is external media that was published explaining some of these things. Mind, Fire Emblem games are not the epitome of fantastic story telling, so I doubt this point will be of particular significance to others, but at least for me it was worth noting.

3- While the army building element is a highlight, it feels a bit underutilized when it comes down to the late game. In particular, your store is the primary means of getting resources and traits, but it is also randomized. While you do have the ability to buy arena tokens to keep earning more rewards, you will eventually reach a point where the arena rewards don't give enough to buy more arena tokens, meaning you have to stop building your army eventually. I think the game would benefit greatly from some kind of endless or roguelite mode. Really playing into that element of building up your perfect army.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tStory driven, tactical turn-based RPG squad battler. I best way I can describe the gameplay is if you took combat elements from The Shining Force series and the Advanced Wars games, implemented minor elements from the FF-Tactics game and clones (without the 2 1/2D combat map), blended in the out-of-combat customization and management of squads, abilities, level-ups, equipment and abilities of a Disgaea game. It's very likely I've spent more time reworking my squads than actual combat. I personally enjoy that, but it you don't there's auto-assign shortcuts to optimize.

Easy game to learn and the curve will challenge you once you're (over)confident. I had no problem with the flow of the game and the addition of mechanics, nor did it come off over tutorialized or hand-holdy. But ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥, seemingly out of nowhere, the game will slap you in the balls if you've been slacking off. Not in an unfair or cheesy way, it ramps up to where your previously forgiven mistakes, mine being a lack of planning or poor squad optimization, are going to hurt a lot more.

It's a fun play-through. I've seen some people have their complaints about the game, but even with the parts that I didn't care for, you can tell that the decisions weren't made because it was "cheaper" or "easier", that the developers made what they wanted and did the best that was feasible.\t\t\t\t\t
