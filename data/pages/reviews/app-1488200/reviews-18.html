You can organize and rearrange these groups, similar to how you can customize a JRPG party of characters. Part of the fun is piecing together a bunch of different classes of characters together for an optimized or balanced party (ie: excels at tanking damage, or excels at defeating cavalry units). The story is pretty good as well, about what you'd expect from a typical Fire Emblem game, but with less anime tropes. The biggest draw-back is the art & visuals, but it is certainly serviceable.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really enjoyed this game! It's a very solid tactical RPG; nothing revolutionary or mind-blowing, but still really enjoyable. If you are familiar with this style of game, I recommend going in blind and playing on the "Warlord" difficulty. I was still figuring out strategies and nuances up until the last level, which helped keep things fresh. Further, some levels were difficult, but at no point was any level *too* difficult.

My only gripe is about the story which, while not bad, was ultimately pretty shallow and nothing more than just a vehicle to progress the gameplay. This is especially true in the game's middle (about 1/4 to 3/4 of the way through), where you'll beat a level and get a message to the effect of "The army now turns north, where its next challenge is waiting..." basically just a single droplet of plot.

If you like tactical RPGs and don't mind a skim story, this is definitely one to pick up. :)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIts always disheartening reading other reviews and seeing how inaccurate they are.

While this game is legendary for good reason, and deserves most of the hype and praise, it seems most people are flies, in that they have ♥♥♥♥ taste and would be just as happy to eat ♥♥♥♥ (disney wokewars fanfiction for starwars as an example), as they would be eating an actual delicacy, like this game.

Most people seem to compare this to Tactics Ogre, and FiWeR eMbWuM.
Besides alot of simping for a blonde skank, Symphony Of War doesn't have that much in common with Tactics Ogre Beyond Turn based strategy.
The same goes for FiWeR eMbWuM, adding only the similarity that there are 'relationships'. The difference being that in FiWeR eMbWuM, the relationships are alot less cringy, and actually contribute to gameplay. They are a main focus of the series.
In stark contrast, the so called 'relationships' in Symphony Of War, are...insignificant. Beyond some boring reading, they contribute nothing except a little flare to the overall experience.

What Symphony Of War DOES do to stand out, is give control of squad composition. Every Squad has a leader and can have different types of soldiers; essentially, any combination of rock, paper, scissors. As you can imagine, this places a high value on strategy, as opposed to the other two mentioned titles, where you can get along just fine power grinding a hero and steamrolling everything.

The soundtract is excellent. Reason enough to buy the game honesty. Whoever put that together is a genius. Give it a listen on the tube or something.

The manner in which the over arching plots are conveyed, genius. Classic, subtle, and well written.
Don't mistake that for the overall story, which is actually quite bland, to the point I find myself constantly skipping. Its the story telling over the world map, and expertly dont cutscenes that deserve the praise.


While the gameplay is quite refreshing in that it offers a tactical challenge and the freedom to address it as you see fit, It doesn't quite hit all the high notes as it were.

First and most irritating, the constant, obsessive, and ceaseless crapping out of the name DONAR.
Just to give you a warm up so you'll know what you're in for...
Well praise DONAR! its DONAR day! DONAR bless us all for saving DONAR temple on this most auspicious DONAR day! DONAR DONAR DONAR! Why if it DONAR wern't for DONAR all the DONAR dirty cultists DONAR trying to DONAR destroy the DONAR world, DOOOOOOONNNNAAAAAARRRRRRR.

Its enough to make you think that the cultists are actually the good guys.

While there is a surprisingly diverse set of weapons for a fantasy game, it still falls very short with the ever cringy "Magical sword that is the ultimate weapon of da wholest world" ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥. \r\n\t\t\t\t\tI haven't played a lot of strategy games like this outside of Fire Emblem Awakening and Fate, and advanced wars. This game definitely feels inspired by the Fire Emblem games.

What makes it unique and fun for me is the squad composition system. Each squad basically has 9 tiles, in a 3x3 layout. Most units take 1 tile (with exception of certain large units), which means you can have a squad with a max of 9 units. Each squad has a captain, who's leadership skills will decide how many units can be placed. This means at the start you can have a max of maybe 4~5 characters in a squad, but eventually you can have a super powerful 9 unit squad. There's a lot of fun and experimentation available here, as you need to think about squad composition and positioning. For example you can put healers in the middle column or back column to protect them with front line units, but when you get attacked (mind this is on opponent's turn, not when you attack and then they counterattack) by a rouge or assassin unit they can just immediately go for the back line. So then you might put a soldier in the back and have 2 healers in the mid column. Having more horse units also turns the squad type into cavalry, so you go more spaces per turn. There are multiple ways to compose a squad.

There's definitely some interesting depths to the game system, despite being not hard to learn. You have affinity for a unit which can be changed, which changes how much points go into stats. Lightening affinity builds towards higher skill, so you can class up a unit into something that requires skill.A unit can also class down (to a lower-tiered unit), so you don't have to worry about a unit being stuck as a certain class. This allows for experimentation and is also forgiving.

This game has a relationship system with conversation not unlike some of the Fire Emblem games, but this part of the game is less developed IMO. The writing is still cute and fun at parts, but there is way less a focus as well as quantity, so you can't expect a lot of conversations. Characters who are a couple (bonded) or friends will have some kind of buff when they are adjacent, but there won't be any assistance in the form of blocking attacks.

Overall I enjoyed the game a lot, and for fans of the Fire Emblem games I would recommend giving the game a look. Just don't expect to be the match maker of multiple couples like in Fire Emblem Awakening (not that you can't do it, just that there is way less couples than Awakening, and each character are limited to only a few potential partners), which might be a good or bad thing depending on what kind of player you are. To be honest, I'm happy about this, as it was really tough for me to pair a bunch of characters together deep into Awakening (hell now I gotta pair the kids together too?). Just too much hassle.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you like Fire Emblem then you will like this game. For those that have played FE before, I'll list the few differences below.

For one thing, instead of individual units you have squads. Your squads are limited by the cap of the leader, but max would be 9 units I believe (I have 7 most right now, not sure I'll ever get to 9, though I'm only at Ch 11). You can have whoever you want in the squad, and you can mix and match to your preference. This also means that if you play classic, you can lose all units minus one in a squad and still live (keep the squad).

Other than that, support is not based on sitting next to each other on tiles, it's story based, so as you progress more supports will open.

There aren't any items either like healing pouches, etc. You can heal others if you have a healer in your party, and that's about it. There are artifacts, affinity change items, increase stat items, and traits as well, usable in the camp screen (between missions).

The basic gameplay is very similar to FE; you position your troops, then you take a turn and then the enemy moves. Nothing really wild and crazy as far as traditional FE goes. The only difference is, and maybe my memory is going, but cavalry can attack and move away. This is irritating if there are 4 enemy cavalry units then they can all attack one unit in the same turn by using this method. Mages also have a 1 range, not 2, and the priority system isn't split into melee and magic--it's light (units) > magic > heavy > light, etc. Gunners destroy everything.

There are also more units in this game than in at least FE Sacred Stones; dragons can become one of 3 kinds, mages can become one of 3 kinds, there are a lot of different rogue/assasin types, archers, cavalry, etc. I haven't even scratched the surface so far.

I like the story so far, it's pretty decent.

All in all, if you're looking to scratch the FE itch this is a great game to do that.

9/10.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm a real fan of the old school, tactical RPGs such as the Advance Wars series. Given the high ratings SoW gets I thought it would be a sure bet.

At times SoW can be fun, but the fun is mixed in with so much frustration that I can't recommend the game. I enjoyed playing the first few battles but the frustrations greatly outweighed the positives and after completing about half of the campaign I gave up.

One big issue with SoW is the interface. Just give the game a go for an hour and see what you think. Don't assume the interface gets less annoying as you get used to it, for me, it is always getting in the way of my enjoyment of the game. So much so that I find I don't explore various options the game offers with skills and other upgrades because the process is so cumbersome.

But a bigger issue for me is the enemy AI, which can be described simply as "Attack the weakest opponent, regardless of whether it makes sense". Your weakest squads are always getting pounded, resulting is massive save scumming. The enemy will often be able to send 4-6 squads against your weakest squad. Losing a squad is very expensive, necessitating a reload.

Compounding these issues is opaque combat rules. Why is this squad doing so well, how do the units interact etc. - no game mechanics are detailed. So more save scumming to test out options. Making this even more painful is that you only get one in battle save, often I would get myself in a bind and would have to restart a battle.

Yet another issue is game difficulty balancing. I played on the recommended, normal difficulty, with perma death enabled for the first ten battles. I managed to make it with no squads lost (you don't want to lose any as it can become a death spiral). Then the perma death option made the game far too frustrating so I disabled it. Then by the 12th scenario the difficulty made massive save scumming a necessity. Worse, even when a squad is killed and you realise you are going to have to reload, you can be faced with a five minute wait as the enemy AI plays out the rest of its turn.

I strongly recommend Spellforce Conquest of Eo if you are looking for a great turned based strategy RPG.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI REALLY wanted to love this game, but I had a few major problems with it.
- The maps tend to be pretty boring with few interesting ideas or challenges. They are laid out in such a way that the path to completing everyone with the side objectives is painfully obvious, no strategy required.
- The units are too powerful. I only played it on normal, but the difficult was a complete joke with 3-4 main units that can take the entire enemy army.
- The most daming of all and the real reason I didn't stick with this game: the writing is horrendous and feels like fundamentalist Christian propaganda right down to the "might makes right" mentality of our MC and christ-styled resurrection arc. It felt like a sunday morning christian cartoon level of writing. If that's your jam, enjoy, but I hated every single cutscene and it's the main reason I didn't want to replay the game again or be excited for the sequel even though the army building mechanic is really fun.\t\t\t\t\t
