\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA fantastic Fire Emblem style game with plenty of its own ideas. So good I played through twice!

Gameplay:

If you have played Fire Emblem or Final Fantasy Tactics, you will be right at home with the grid based strategy map structure of the game. What really makes Symphony of War stand out is the squad system. Instead of just controlling 10 or so individual characters, you control a bunch of different squads consisting of multiple units. The game opens up quickly and allows the player to experiment with different squad compositions to fit their own playstyle. Want a tanky squad that never dies? Slap some sentinels on the front line and put a few healers behind them! Want to erase enemies from the face of the planet before they can counterattack? Put a bunch of mages or siege cannons in a squad! How about a mounted squad that can weaken an enemy and retreat to safety? You can do that too!

Over my two playthroughs, I experimented with tons of different squad compositions and had tons of fun refining and tweaking them over the course of the game.

I would suggest avoiding the permanent death option, though. My first playthrough was on the normal difficulty setting with permanent death on for the entire playthrough, and it really felt like the game wasn't balanced around it. It felt less like the old Fire Emblem games which were balanced around losing units forever and more like a Pokemon Nuzlocke challenge. Possible, but not the intended way to experience the game.

Story:

The story is pretty typical for this genre of game. There isn't anything you haven't seen before, but some moments are done really well. The story felt a bit rushed in the final few chapters, but it was never actively bad or a drag to read.

DLC:

The game has paid DLC as well. My first playthrough was done without the DLC, and it was excellent. With the DLC you will get eight new chapters and a handful of new classes to experiment with. The new maps flesh out the stories of some of the side characters and provide access to some of the new classes and artifacts. While not required to enjoy the gameplay or the story, some of the new classes are really fun to play around with, especially the necromancer and risen classes. If you aren't on a budget, I would recommend picking up the DLC as well.

Overall a fantastic game, and I can't wait to see what the sequel has to offer!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHaving finished this game, i can safely say that I've not felt this level of nostalgia in ages. It feels good to have played this game; to dive into its systems and truly learn how to run through it with well-executed strategies. I whittled away entire days figuring out how to properly pull off a mission, and even now i look back with new knowledge i'd bring to a new play through with zero hesitation.

Symphony of War is a love letter to those of us who played Ogre Battle, Fire Emblem and Final Fantasy Tactics, pitting you, your army consisting of squads of customised units against a varied selection of enemies. Thinking things through pays off, and you can customise your difficulty from the get-go, meaning if you're new to this kind of game you're not facing a massive skill wall.

I mentioned customised units: Well, let me paint you a picture of my roster: I had a squad of mages linked with bodyguards running around. Almost all of them were units i myself recruited from the market, trained and turned into whichever class i wanted. Unit doesn't have something you want? There's items to customise them.

From units, we go to squads: Each squad has 9 spaces. Only one unit doesn't take up 1 space, and that's the DRAGON. It takes up two. To continue: There's one leader, and up to 8 units accompanying said leader. As you'd expect, you've got a bevy of units, ranging from a large selection of staples a la knights, rogues, swordsmen and women, and paladins, to the ranged selection, with bows, early firearms and artillery cannons also making an appearance.

Despite the presence of units that seem powerful, it's all balanced to an excellent degree: Cannons preparing to decimate your line? If you have a cavalry unit able to hit them, or a squad of rogues ready to flank them, you can decimate the cannon units without them firing a shot in return. Got a massive cavalry charge headed your way? Spears, magic and other means can be used to melt them.

And melt them you will: The developers of this game did an amazing job with the effects, though some can be a bit underwhelming..which is the nature of the beast: You can't make everything as glamorous as a rain of fire, or an artillery barrage.

When it comes to map variety, i am also pleased, though there is one gripe i have: As there are traits for fighting in multiple map types, i had hoped to encounter said types a bit more often than i actually did, making said traits not that useful. Otherwise, the inclusion of weather (which can MESS UP your gunpowder units) and night time missions means you have to adapt to all manner of circumstances.

The story is gloriously nineties, as many of us who've played this can attest: It feels like a plot you'd play on one of the older consoles, but doesn't feel outdated in the least, even if some of the twists can be seen a mile out.

All in all, for a game i played for 50 hours at the time of writing this? I think it is money well spent.\t\t\t\t\t
