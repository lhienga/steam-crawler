\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is an amazing game that combine both the best from Fire Emblem and Langrisser (Squad system).

-The story is fine.
-The characters are really good (Specially when they start to wipe out entire enemy armies; P.S: Diana MVP).
-The music is neat, some themes are just great for the key moments of the campaign.
-The maps offer both challenging and beautiful layouts with all sorts of enviroments where most of your characters and squads can be used one way or another, so you never feel like there is a squad you can't use (Hell, there are maps in the early-mid game where you can fit 15 of your squads, so you might aswell create as much useful squads for your army as you can)


But the real meat of the game is the gameplay (duh), the game presents the units on the map as an unit per-se, but each one of these units on the map represent a squad of soldiers, featuring a Squad leader (Which acts as the head and can influence with certain traits) and the soldiers which can be any unit available in the game.

By customizing your Squads between the maps, you can modify which "role" that Squad going to partake in the battle, for example, adding more Heavy Infantry will lead more towards a Tank Squad, meanwhile adding more Archers or Mages can lead to a Damage Squad according to your needs. There are also Light Infantry units which specialize on taking down squishy targets, Cavalry units which are great for plain open maps, and even Flying units if you have the chance to do so.

One can spend so much time thinking about how to make the ideal Squads that suddenly half or an entire hour goes by.
And then, when those Squads start cleaning off the maps of enemy units, it's so satisfying.
There is even more depth in the game than this, but I wanted to talk about the Squad system because it's just genius, easily a 9/10 game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFire emblem, very crusty in places, but unmistakable. It's synthesizing elements from several titles and injecting it's own take on things and overall works extremely well and scratches that itch I've had since 3H.

It takes the whole squadron+leader angle and doubles down on things. Every single unit in a squadron is a pawn to develop and can die (and be rezzed, for a fee). I'm on chapter 10 and my roster is north of 70 individual units spread across 15 squadrons I field for a level.
Combat plays out like an auto-battler. Some units might go for the backline first, healers in the squad attempt to patch things up, some units have traits to funnel attacks onto them. It works well. Highly flexible class system and the squads really lets you develop a roster that is *yours*. Since you can shuffle units between missions you are very easily able to keep everyone at par and explore builds as you'd like. No more XP vacuum issues.

BUT, it does also mean you have a *staggering* amount of menuing to do between missions at times. I mean, this is some terminal gamer brain levels of menuing. If this genre is catnip for you though I think you'll power through, but beware ye who enter.

Otherwise, while the art in some of the screenshots (character portraits) doesn't reflect the current polish, the pixel-esqe art is where the visuals stand out. Story has been forgettable, but also I can't exactly defend older FE on storytelling. Music varies from "unremarkable but serves it purpose well" to some bits I've stopped playing to just listen through the whole loop. SFX is very GBA era.

I'm no where near done with the game. But even the first ~18 hours justified the purchase and has been the best persistent-unit-style TRPG I've played since before COVID.\t\t\t\t\t
