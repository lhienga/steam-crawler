--
It takes me back to Shining Force 2 on the Sega Megadrive which captivated me as a child with its secrets and unit development. People compare it to Advance Wars or Fire Emblem mechanically, but there are far more parallels to SF2, and it has a different atmosphere that is more nostalgic to me and which kept me playing a lot longer than I expected to.

The writing is simple and a little incongruous with the characters in many cases, especially as it features a lot of modern idiom, though there is a naive sweetness to it. It really does give you the sense of someone just doing their best for a labour of love, rather than the pedantic polish of veteran designers (that's not a bad thing either!).

Features I liked:
- Faction points and tech tree gave incentive to clear episodes cleanly, as did the sub-challenges.
- The arenas gave resources to fill gaps, and the token exchange system was neat.
- Customisable traits and affinities add a good level of squad optimisation (although there could be more diversity or specific unit boons, as one tends to lean on the same traits.)
- The side dialogues does create some cute character development. If this was better scripted and the relationships added more complexity to gameplay it would make a big difference. My suspicion is that the target audience is older, so some more mature themes could be explored here (oi, get your mind out of the gutter).
- The music is surprisingly on point.
- The UI functionality and flow made sense. That is more than I can say for many games these days.

Things that need work:
- The writing and dialogue is very basic, immature eve, and it leans hard on old tropes. It gives a strong impression that the script was written by someone young, which is ironic given the whole aesthetic emerges from games that are 30 years old. This is arguably good in a kind of nostalgic way, but the people who would be nostalgic are also a lot older now and likely have more experience with nuanced and compelling stories.
- Class diversity and balance was pretty good, but could definitely be refined so units can become more specialised, allowing for more tactical and strategic complexity.
- Heaps of artifacts are basically useless.
- No meaningful secrets: One of the things I loved about SF2 was that there were obscure things to find and hidden characters. It was very gratifying to hold on to these things and then figure out how to use them for later rewards. That mechanic shaped the way I manage inventories to this day (I better hold on to this in case it does something cool later!)
- Very linear story. There is no exploration. Though I admit this makes sense in terms of a world proceeding within a particular timeframe.
- Would love to have seen more tactical map combat types: AOE attacks and spells, counters, combo attacks (remember Suikoden?). There is so much room for exploring cool stuff here.
- Legendary hires are cool, but should have more unique properties.
- More artifacts. More whacky properties. More legendaries.
- Exploring specific characters with more sidequests with story-based limitations would be awesome (eg. can only use X units or units have Y conditions, or Z actions deactivated). There is only one side quest section in the game, which makes it stand out as a bit of an anomaly.

A lot of my criticism has to do with "more and better of the stuff that's already in it." rather than saying the fundamentals are flawed or should be completely overhauled. If there was a kind of "grown up" sequel to this, that developed the tactical gameplay as well as the story-writing, I think it could be an enduring classic.

If the developer has enough of a fanbase, it might be worth allowing for player-made modding and content, depending on how much work it is to implement.

Overall, this game is kind of cute and worth playing if you like the genre. I was extremely surpised that I devoted as many hours as I did to it (though I am ravaged by the guilt of having put things off to do so).\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA very solid Tactical RPG game. A must play for fans of Langrisser, Shining Force, Fire Emblem, Ogre Battle alike.

STORY
This story was decent enough with the classic good vs evil plot stuffs. Though there are a few points of questionable plot twists. Characters were generally likeable. 7/10

COMBAT
The combat is a major selling point of this game. In good old fashion, you command many squads made up of different troop compositions to fight against decently smart enemy AIs in traditional turn-by-turn combat with alternating ally and enemy phases. You can take advantage of unit class counters and terrain advantages like most other games in this genre. There are a lot you could do here, be it magic wielding sorceress, fast charging knights, tanky heavy infantries, good all-rounder paladin, or fearsome dragons, along with archers, blade-masters, guns and cannons, etc. 10/10.

CHARACTER CUSTOMIZATION
This is another major selling point of this game. There are a lot of customization you could do for each squad. Each squad is led by a leader whose growing Leadership (LDR) attribute determines the number of units you can add to the squad. You can have up to 9 different units in a squad including the squad leader. Generally, you would put tankier units in the front line, followed by DPS units and healers. Back row units can be back-stabbed by assassin type enemy units, so there are extra squad building strategies to consider. You could build a full squad of archers on horseback that do hit-and-run tactics from range. You could build a formidable snowballing squad with tanky units along with a mix of physical and magical DPS units. A dragon takes up 2 spaces, so you could add 3 dragon riders plus 3 regular units to make a flying dragon team that bypasses all obstacles! On top of the large variety of unit compositions and class upgrade paths to play with, each squad leader can also be equipped with up to 3 different artifacts. Artifacts have different rarity with legendary and epic ones providing incredible power spikes! In addition, you could add special traits to boost up each unit's offensive and defensive capabilities! But there is even more. There is an entire upgradeable tech tree divided in 3 sections that further caters towards each player's preferred play style! In between each campaign, your army rest at a camp where you can purchase units, artifacts, and traits, as well as upgrading the tech tree. I must have spent more time upgrading units and customizing squads in the camp than actual combat. =P 12/10.

MINI-GAMES & PUZZLES
The base game did not have much mini-games or puzzles. There are some special achievements in each campaign that may require some careful planning and execution. I heard the DLC has more puzzles in the extra campaigns, which I have not verified at the moment. 7/10.

GRAPHICS
The retro feel of the graphics really bring back some nostalgic memories. The attack animations look fantastic. You could speed up the frame rate with hotkey on controller (or keyboard) as well, which is a nice Quality of Life feature. 9/10.

SOUND TRACKS
The sound tracks were solid, but nothing spectacular IMHO when compared to the epic Langrisser and Shining Force series. As the game progresses, more background musics are introduced that depicts different cultures in different world map regions. 7/10.

CLOSING REMARK
If you are itching for some strategy RPG gaming with classical turn based combat, a huge range of meaningful unit and squad customization, look no further than Symphony of War: The Nephilim Saga! An absolute gem given the significant entertainment value at a very reasonable price! 9/10.\t\t\t\t\t
