\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSymphony of War has everything you'd expect from an SRPG, including it's downfalls.

The Good: The army building elements of this game are quite addicting. If you enjoy min-maxing stats and customizing your strategy, expect to spend as much in menus as you spend in combat. The combat also feels smooth; as somebody who typically turns off animations halfway through a game, these felt breezy and non-intrusive. The named characters felt mostly unique and had the kind of meaningful interactions you'd expect from something like Fire Emblem. I also thought the Force Surrender mechanic was a very interesting addition to the genre that added purpose for weaker squads and also some additional tactical considerations.

The Neutral: I don't have any major complaints about the story, but I wouldn't say it's peak RPG storytelling by any means. The combat designs had occasional interesting decisions, but typically the battles were to destroy the enemies or seize some sort of point. The number of unit types was decent, but I wish there were some options for battlefield support through buff/debuffs.

The Bad: I played on normal difficulty, and although the game starts out challenging, the difficulty drops off quickly as you upgrade your teams. This happens a lot in most SRPGs, so I can't be too upset about it. I imagine it would be even easier if I had used up all of my arena tokens, as I could have probably grinded out quite a few more levels.

Overall, Symphony of War is pretty much what a SRPG fan should expect. It has some really fun mechanics to play around with, and a story that is just passable enough to not hold the game back.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThink Fire Emblem, but you can assign every leader a squad to complement their abilities.
Symphony of War was a very fun experience. The squad-building element makes it feel fairly unique among tactical games and gives a lot of extra variety. There's a large number of units you can bundle together and experiment with, as well as a wide array of items and character perks to really let you customize your units. Finding a squad that works for its given role feels satisfying as heck! The difficulty level feels about right (with permadeath on feeling like a solid challenge and permadeath off feeling leisurely without becoming dull). The story even takes a few interesting turns.
I have some knits I could pick: The portraits look really out of place with the rest of the game's art style; you get used to them, but these weirdly detailed renders sporting thousand-yard stares are a bit eerie for the first few chapters. I'm a little disppointed by the lack of direction you really have over your named character - them carrying your name of choice is more to make you feel affection toward them than to make them "your character". The 'tutorial' section, while better than whole wikis for many games, mis-explains the unit-typing a bit; it briefly explains that the most plentiful unit type will usually determine squad type and talks about priority, but fails to mention that light vs. heavy and cavalry vs. infantry are actually determined separately for this. You can, in fact, have a light cavalry squad with only a single light cavalry unit, provided it was a tie between light infantry and heavy cavalry broken by that light cavalry. And on unit gripes, in spite of the wide variety of units, there's only a single 'kind' of flying type, and several of its qualities make flying type squad design extremely limiting.
And again, these are all knits. Most can be easily ignored or gotten used to.
I highly recommend this game to fans of tactics rpgs. You will have a wonderful time. I'd also recommend it, though less-so, to fans of grand strategy - the squad-building element will probably delight you the same way hand-crafting an army composition would.\t\t\t\t\t
