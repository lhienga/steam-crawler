\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR: Entertaining gameplay with some depth, great sprite work, abysmal ending.

GAMEPLAY:

The campaign structure is very straightforward. You start a mission, get some dialogue, then get some more dialogue after the mission is over, after which you're sent back to the campaign menu where you start your next mission, buy new items, get new recruits etc. For the majority of the game there's only one mission to choose from, but near the end you get a couple side quests like saving a dragon, saving some children from an evil cult, helping out a party member with personal issues, stuff like that. You can also participate in arena fights, which you unlock with arena coins of different rarities that you can find during missions. Different kinds of coins unlock different arenas which yield different rewards. With bronze coins yielding the least and platinum coins yielding the most.

Balancing follows a rock paper scissors formula: heavy infantry beats spear infantry, spear infantry beats heavy cavalry etc. Every unit type has its strengths and weaknesses and they all fill a purpose, except for Light Cavalry which just kinda sucks.

Combat is relatively simple. Every unit has a maximum attack range, ranging from 1 to 3 tiles. You have multiple different ways of attacking, which have different properties, but to be honest the only ones I found to be useful were the aggressive attack which makes your squad more vulnerable but also deal more damage, and the normal attack which doesn't add any bonuses at all. Engagements work as the following: attacker>defender>attacker>defender. If a unit's initiative stat is high enough, it has a chance of attack once more at the end.
Every individual unit has an HP bar and squads themselves have a morale bar. Morale is based on multiple factors, but it's mostly determined your unit's health. A low morale unit deals low damage and vice versa. If you attack a unit with low enough morale, and your morale is comparatively high, you can give the enemy a chance to surrender. Surrendered enemies increase the bounty you get at the end of a mission.

There's also a tech tree which you level up by investing research points in (I couldn't figure out how exactly you gain points, as far as I know the amount of points you gain per mission is set and cannot be increased or decreased) . The techs are useful for the most part, especially the later ones because they unlock artillery and dragon riders, the two most powerful units in the game. It's also not overly huge or complicated, so understanding what does what and what you should invest into is doesn't take long. Overall a very solid mechanic.

Every general of yours can equip up to three items, which you can get from defeated enemies, find in chests, or buy at the store or bazaars (bazaars are special shops that only appear during some missions, that carry valuable items and mercenaries that you cannot get anywhere else). At first, items only give minor stat boosts, but later found equipment can grant powerful bonuses, which come at a cost e.g an item gives your entire squad's units health per enemy unit killed, but damages your squad at the beginning of every turn. Effects stack (as long as it's different items proving the same effect) and you can do some cool builds with the right gear, which gives the game additional depth and replayability.

Later in the game, around the halfway point, you get magic abilities which you fill up by doing damage and use out of combat, You get four them in total and they're all very useful.

Navigating the menus is a bit unintuitive, but you get used to it quickly and it's nothing that detracts from the game too much. I highly recommend playing with keyboard and just ignoring your mouse, it makes things a lot more comfortable. Or just play with a controller instead.


GRAPHICS:
Portraits are nice and expressive and the in-battle sprites look excellent and have very fluid looking and crunchy feeling attack animations. There's a bit of a clash between the cutscene sprites and environment tiles, and some of the effects look a bit wonky (mostly fire and artillery fire) but that's the case with the vast majority of RPGmaker games and doesn't really detract from the overall experience. Overall the game looks very pretty.

STORY:
Good God. At first it starts off with some political intrigue, which isn't very interesting, then it becomes a stereotypical hero story where you're part of the Chosen Ones and have to save the world, for which you need a powerful artifact so you can defeat the demon king, and to do so you must defeat an evil army of devil worshippers, this part isn't anything ground breaking but it kept me entertained. And then, then comes the last 5 missions.

This is where the writing sharts its pants to an enormous degree. One mission, the protagonist suddenly remembers that they're actually out for revenge and go crazy with bloodlust, intending on killing every single enemy on the map, so much so that you cannot control them in that mission and have to make due with the rest of your army. After said mission ALL of the MC's allies (including the MCs love interest, more on that later) turn against the MC, and say that they just cannot condone the MC's actions and need to keep them from becoming too powerful. Keep in mind, up until this point the protag has only fought either literal devil worshippers, or people serving said devil worshippers. The protagonist then spends a mission fighting against their former allies, which then give up and surrender. The MC chooses to spare them, and proceeds to the final boss, which the protagonist then faces with an entire army full of traitors, who were more than happy to murder them a minute ago. Wow.
With the final boss defeated, the protagonist then proceeds to go to the underworld to think about their actions or some garbage. Keep in mind this is the same protagonist who participated in missions to save animals and children and just killed not-Satan, who threatened to destroy the entire world, a second ago.

Roll credits.

Now, I'm not a big story kinda guy, for me gameplay matters more than anything else, but this ending was so ATROCIOUSLY bad, that it soured the entire 36 hour experience for me. I don't know what possessed the developers to write an ending like this, they should've just continued going down the shounen route and ended it with some generic "bad guy is dead, good guys are alive, everyone's happy"-ending. WHICH THEY DID, kind of.
After the MC has retreated into their cuckshed, you get an end roll which tells you just how happy everyone lived forever after. Then, after the credits, just like in my heckin' Marvel movies, you get a scene where the MC meets their love interest again, having left the underworld (because I guess they pondered their actions for a sufficient amount of time) and they ride off into the sunset together. SO WHAT WAS EVEN THE POINT OF THE WHOLE BETRAYAL PART OF THE STORY??? \t
I hope the developers do better next time. If you want to do a sad ending, do that. If you want to do a bittersweet ending, do that. But for the love of all that is good and holy, please don't repeat whatever this was.

ROMANCE:
It's there, I guess. You get to choose one love interest, one of which being the canon one, and then the protagonist's other party members all fall in love with each other. With the one the MC didn't choose getting together with a homicidal maniac, after you've beaten the game (no I will not elaborate). There's, like, three scenes where you interact with your special someone in the entire game and it never feels like there's any chemistry there. Neither for you nor for your allies. I feel like this was added just to appeal to Fire Emblem Three Houses fans, probably very late on in development.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game has a lot of things about it that make it fairly enjoyable, so overall, I would recommend this game, but I think you should go into the game knowing some things. By far, the strongest aspect of this game is the customization of your squads. There are a lot of different units that make it so that you can build squads that deal a lot of damage or that sponge a lot of hits. It is also possible to make squads that are mostly one type of unit, or that have a lot of variety in their composition, as long as you use them for specific purposes in combat. It's a lot of fun to come up with a wide variety of squads, and they are all generally useful in some way or another.

That being said, you need to go into the game knowing that the story is very much a JRPG. It is not particularly complex, and your characters will actively make the dumbest possible decisions a lot of the time. It broke my interest in the game for a while to see how dumb some of my character´s choices were.

In terms of unit types, the game suffers from the classic problem of different classes being gender locked. Female characters are the only ones that can heal, and male characters are the only ones that can use guns. Your character cannot change class, and you start as a swordsperson who does not really do a lot of damage until about halfway through the game.

That being said, I still enjoyed the combat of the game and the overall leveling up process of most of my other character units. The game plays a lot like Fire Emblem, so if you like that, you should try this out.\t\t\t\t\t
