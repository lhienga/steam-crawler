\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI know this will be a hot take and it's hard to know how to review this game, but I honestly don't think Baba is You actually works very well as a puzzle game at least in this form. The core conceit is of course undeniably genius, what a crazy and inspired idea for a game! For a while I had fun seeing what unexpected interactions you can make, playing around with more of a sandbox of words and getting a giggle when you turn yourself into a tree was captivating at first. The problem is you very quickly start seeing the actual game isn't nearly as creative as at acts, and the puzzles to me were mostly annoying slogs of brute force combos more than satisfying lateral thinking challenges

Stephen's Sausage Roll is also super hard, but I know what'll happen when I stick the fork in the sausage every time and throughout the game you learn different ways you can stick the fork in the sausage to achieve different goals, and you use a combination of strategies in increasingly complicated but related puzzles. This is not true at all in Baba. Mechanics change randomly and arbitrarily from level to level, mechanics rarely transfer, and new mechanics rarely build off old mechanics at all. This could work if it felt like a sandbox where I'm constantly given fun new toys to play with and many options to build my own solution, but that isn't the case. The game is very rigid and particular so instead of being excited about cool new things to try I was just exhausted that to start any new level I would have to grind out 30 more random combinations of words like I'm playing doodle god just to see what Baba would decide I'm allowed to do\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAbsolutely wonderful, truly unique puzzle logic game. Fun for adults, but also probably an engaging tool to teach children about logic and maybe the building blocks of code.

Some levels are incredibly frustrating, though, and a few times when I gave up after several hours of trying everything I could possibly think of and finally looked up the solution, my first reaction was, "AGHH!! i didn't know that was an option!!!". In some ways, the game might benefit from an instruction manual of how the rules you've encountered behave, but on the other hand, half the fun is just experimenting to see what happens and drawing your own conclusions from the results. In that way, it mimics the process of scientific discovery (from the ancient times, it seems, building up your own catalog of axioms and theories from the ground up); the game presents you with an alien material reality that is both inscrutable and behaves consistently, and then asks you to work backwards to see what makes it behave such (positing hypotheses, testing them, rejecting or consolidating them into a cohesive theory of reality), and ultimately allows that knowledge to reshape the world according to your whim (in this case, bringing about the "win" condition). So, great for developing out-of-the-box thinking that questions every assumption!

(Of course, in the case of *this* reality there is incontrovertible proof of an intelligent creator of this reality, as well as the comforting certainty that all these puzzles do, in fact, have a solution-- or, well, perhaps I simply haven't gotten that far lol.)\t\t\t\t\t
