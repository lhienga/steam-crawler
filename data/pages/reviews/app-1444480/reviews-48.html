Really clever way to explore and learn the fundamental workings of computation. Being a computer engineer, I particularly liked the starting from the NAND gate, and building all the rest from there. In general, I totally recommend this if you are interested in computer architecture!

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is one of the cases where I wish I could give a neutral review. I recommend this... conditionally. I am conflicted because I'm enjoying the "tech tree" progression of learning towards being able to build a general purpose computer from a very foundational understanding of digital logic gates, but I find the builder UI more tedious and distracting than other options available.

I am currently just getting started (I just finished the Full Adder puzzle), and I'm enjoying it and have already learned a lot. I am going into it with almost no prior knowledge of Digital Logic (I had just watched Ben Eater's basic intro videos on logic gates, D Latches, and SR Latches and gotten curious).

The puzzles are all quite simple and fairly quick up to this point, but I am already noticing a few things I wish were different or at least configurable about the interface:

1) You can only step forward in ticks sequentially. As I'm learning I keep wishing I could click on a particular state on the truth table and have it step directly into that state.
2) The arrows moving through the wires to illustrate which ones are high (1) is distracting and difficult to visualize. I wish there were an option to just switch them to a solid color across the whole wire with no animation, or to at least maybe make the animation a subtle pulsing glow of the whole wire at once. Another thing that would be nice is if you could have the low wires faded/dulled/partially transparent.
3) My review originally complained that wire placement is entirely manual. This was getting very tedious when I wanted to move elements around as you have to delete and redraw every wire. I got so frustrated with this that I just spent some time trying to figure out if I was missing something. Turns out that you can use multi-select to grab components and their wire connections. If this was ever explained, it wasn't clear. I don't think it was. Highly recommend having an obvious tutorial step for this.

I'm going to keep using this, but I'm already finding myself jumping to other programs that are less tedious to experiment with. I will gladly update my review if the UI is improved to make this feel cozy to stay in.

Update: I have gotten to Turing Complete now (there's still much more left in the tech tree). After the initial learning curve, the warts in the UI mostly become less distracting. I will still say that I really wish to improve the wire indicators as I've suggested. That would still be a big quality of life improvement.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR Rather than simply teach you the basics of electronic circuit design, Turing Complete's campaign mode tasks you to discover them. You don't need to build a plane or understand aerodynamic theory in order to know what a plane is. The only shining moment of Turing Complete is that it has a sandbox where you can build anything you want, but only if you know how to. The general layout actually would lend itself better as an introduction sandbox tool if the campaign was jettisoned. Not recommended and save your money.

Have you ever wondered how Mankind first discovered fire?

While I am no archaeologist, I'm guessing primitive cavemen probably discovered fire first by chance, like a bush or a tree on fire after lightning struck it. They found things that burned easy like wood kept that fire going, and then later figured out that scraping flint to produce sparks or rubbing dry twigs together produced fire. In other words, simple trial and error producing an observable and measurable result. A tedious process until Mankind advanced enough to put on record how to produce fire without every single soul on Earth having to reproduce those steps all by themselves.

Take trying to understand how an airplane flies for example. Each person would have to discover the scientific principles behind flight and the mechanical means to reproduce those principles, then through trial and error make an airplane that could fly. We don't need to do this. We simply know through reading about airplanes what they are and what they can do.

Imagine further if this sharing of discovery was not possible. We'd be lucky to make it out of the Stone Age, never mind having the Internet and computers.

However, Turing Complete asks you to do just that in order to understand how electronic circuits work. You are the proverbial caveman discovering things, rather than simply being told how things work because they already exist and need no re-discovery.

The first part I want to talk about is the campaign mode In this game. In it, you are a backward human taken captive by a super-intelligent alien who gives you the chance to parlay with more advanced civilizations. All you have to do is learn the building blocks required to assemble your own working computer. The approach taken by the game on you learn about these building blocks is what I have a problem with.

I've taken a basic electronics course on Udemy that simply tells me what, for example, an AND gate is. In that course, all I had to know about an AND gate is that has two inputs and one output, and the output is only switched on when both inputs are on. Easy. In Turing Complete, you have to DISCOVER the AND gate by taking lesser elemental components and randomly hooking them up until you replicate the expected truth table of an AND gate. I am not kidding you: the AND gate does not exist for use in the campaign later until you discover it through experimentation.

You will also have to do this for EVERY electronic circuit component in the campaign. You don't learn about it and later use it, you have to discover it.

What a hell of a way to learn things. We don't do this in our education system, or even when we assemble a bookshelf purchased from Ikea. There is no re-discovery. Knowledge is passed on. We're told what to do because it was already learned a long time ago and put to paper.

I might be more forgiving if this was a historical sandbox game where the protagonist learns how to build IC components for the first time, though it would probably still annoy the hell out of me trying to learn how to make things in Turing Complete. The story behind the campaign itself is somewhat weak. Humans already know how to make a computer, so why is this alien asking us to build a computer when the means to do so is fairly easy even at the IC component level? Is it trolling us or maybe thinking we really are that primitive and we have to somehow prove we discovered all this by ourselves?

There's also the part where the campaign world suddenly splits into two where events no longer dictate outcomes. What I mean is at first I'm able to test the circuits by clicking input nodes to generate output signals. You know, event handling. That gets taken away from me later where I can't directly test that way any more. The campaign controls the input handling and I have to guess (or should I say guess more) what my elemental components should be hooked up as in order to get the designated output. Time in the form of ticks becomes a new variable to my circuit design, and the fact I cannot create an internal circuit loop to simulate things like a clock (as I can in sandboxes like Logic World) is another hurdle that annoys me even more.

Moving on to the sandbox mode of the game, at least that has all the needed components available to design anything you want, but if you are coming into this with no knowledge of basic electronic circuit design, well you're still going to feel like a caveman confronted with fire for the first time because none of it will make any sense anyways.

And really, that's a crying shame too. The way the game is laid out via the UI and the sandbox by itself would make it PERFECT for an intro to basic electronic circuit design courses were it not for the "Discovery" approach. Replace the campaign with something similar to tutorials found on Google and run solely with the sandbox.

If you have no experience with basic electronic circuit theory, this is going to really make you sorry you purchased Turing Complete. There are other offerings on Steam, Google Play and the Apple Shop that will do a better job teaching you, and it will be far more enjoyable. I wouldn't recommend buying this.
