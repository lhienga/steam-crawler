\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI love the coding games of Zachtronics and Tomorrow Corporation, but this one hits a different spot. Certainly the most educational game I've played about CS - I feel like I understand much better how computers actually work, at a fundamental level, as a result of playing this game.

It's also very well designed as a game. By solving the earlier problems - which provide lots of nice eureka moments in themselves - you are actually building the components you will use to solve the later levels. This means that, even as your circuits become quite sophisticated and capable of complex operations, you know that they are all just combinations and permutations of NAND and NOR gates that you have assembled in a particular way. This is hugely satisfying, and getting to build a Turing-complete computer in this way, from scratch, is a payoff I don't think you'll find anywhere else.

Complaints about the lack of proper instructions are justified, to an extent. There are some levels where the brief just doesn't make sense, and it can take quite a bit of reverse engineering to figure out what the objective is. At the very least, it should be possible to keep ticking through new inputs even after a failed output - not even to debug, just to try to figure out what the conditions are!

At the same time I wouldn't want the devs to lurch too far towards accessibility and hand-holding, because it's partly the lack of explicit help that makes the game so rewarding.

Highly recommended.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOkay hear me out, I played ALL Zachtronics games, and I actually love this game too. But I cannot recommend it to others for two main reasons:

1. Removal of SR Latch / Flip Flop - I tried a previous version before paying for the game and buying it on steam and restarting, so I must say I'm EXTREMELY disappointed with the new introduction to memory. Presenting "Delay Lines" as a fundamental way to store 1bit of data is just wrong and bad on so many levels. If the implementation of SR latches is too difficult, I'd rather just explaining it to players and then skipping ahead with it as a black box.

2. Unskippable levels - This means that you must be able to sole EVERY single puzzle in order to advance to future content. This might prove challenging for less proficient or casual players who might simply remain stuck. A good solution would be to allow them to optionally "skip ahead" to the next section upon completing 90% of the missions of that tier, e.g. 18/20, that way they still have wiggle room to fail.

Once the above are addressed, I'll happily change my review and recommend this game, as I still thoroughly enjoyed it.\t\t\t\t\t
