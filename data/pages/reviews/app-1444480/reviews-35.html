\r\n\t\t\t\t\tYou do not need to be an engineer to play this game. I think it would be good for kids as well as adults. It starts very simple and builds on things that it has previously explained. It does ramp up a bit fast. You may need to go back and do the same puzzles a couple of times to move forward, but everything you need is here. It doesn't go into great depth. It tells you only what you need to know to progress in the game, not to be an electrical computer engineer, and moves on rather quickly. Some of the later puzzles, I may need to stare at for an hour and play with for another hour. But, I can get through them, and I am not an engineer.

I think this game is great for anyone wanting to get into computer science or digital design; adults and kids. However, I would have liked to have seen more early on regarding various types of latches and memory types and configurations. There are lots of ways to do the same thing. I think the repetitiveness of covering the same concepts in different ways may have helped some people. But it is a solid game as is. Overall, I'm enjoying this game. I started to play the game again from the beginning. I highly recommend it - even for people who just like a tough puzzle game - and it can get tough.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe educational portion of this game is awful.

In many cases, it simply tells you to build components without cluing you to how, almost as if they want you to stumble upon them by accident. Once built, they are rarely explained.
There are also achievements for getting efficient solutions, but no actual indicators in-game on how optimal your solutions are, so you can build wildly inefficient solutions that rely on incorrect assumptions/intuitions and the game will go "yep, that's right." The tech tree doesn't make sense as certain concepts are brought up way before they're relevant, and others are just skipped through. You go from simple logic gate combos to solutions requiring large and complex wire patterns seemingly instantly.
Nandgame is much better at coaxing out those intuitions while giving you the necessary guidance. This game seems like it was designed to be played by people who already know the answers- please do some QA testing.

The UX is also just bad. Placing wires doesn't feel good, moving components is clunky, and the component selector screen... why are the components so small and tucked away?! You have all that screen space. Why do the three-input AND/OR look the same as the regular AND/OR? Why not just make them any size? Besides the gates, the components themselves look clunky on the screen and have nonsensical shapes that are inconsistent with eachother. Byte and bit components/IO/wires are barely differentiated. Instead of lighting up wires when they have signal on them, like any other circuit software does, they have animated arrows which just look extremely unclear when you have dozens if not hundreds of wires in your solution. The level objective isn't displayed onscreen and you have to instead click a button to show it. The manual (which is too sparse) isn't visible in the tech tree for some reason. You can't test your solution past one tick unless it's correct. Middle click doesn't pick a component or wire.

It's also buggy. Going back to earlier levels, sometimes the inputs/outputs just disappear, breaking the level so it can't be completed again or used to learn.

Finally, and this is the dealbreaker for me, the fundamentals are wrong. Delay lines, really? Switches? These made-up components feel like they exist in order to cheat past this game's engine limitations, so you can imitate how a real computer works instead of actually building the real components. Please change this back, it's just wrong. You aren't building a real computer, you're building a solution to a puzzle with rules invented by this game.

All that being said, it's probably better than the competitors at being a game that lets you design computer components. NANDGAME is much better at education but this gives you significantly more freedom. I would really like to see this made into a legitimate product, but that is going to require an editor going over the campaign and cleaning the mess up.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA digital logic course masquerading as a puzzle game, its essentially Logisim: Story mode
It gives a fairly holistic overview of how computers function on a gate level, while being somewhat more forgiving than an actual course due to (generally) not requiring an optimal solution, and skipping over topics like boolean algebra or karnaugh maps. This also keeps it from being a totally suitable replacement, but honestly if you're planning on playing this game you probably already know something about digital logic haha (not that you couldn't play it if you didn't)
Most of the stages are well explained, and many have tutorial videos for if you get stuck, though a few levels are somewhat confusing on just what they are asking you to accomplish.
There's a few other minor gripes, like needing to flip through the 'storyline' text every time you check the level description, but as for what actually matters it does what it claims to.
Edit- After completing the first set of assembly levels, the game expects you to create a more complex computer, but doesn't provide nearly as much direction as for the first. Its occasionally difficult to understand what you are supposed to do, but I was still able to figure it out without looking anything up. It made me mad at first, but once I realized what they were asking the extra freedom it afforded me made me enjoy it more, actually. Though, ff you had great difficulty with the first computer, this would probably be borderline impossible to do on your own (Though you could argue the game 'ends' once you make your first turing complete computer.)
The final assembly challenges (of which I haven't done any of yet lol) seem to test programming skill more than just testing the functionality of your computer.\t\t\t\t\t
