\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's been a while since I wrote a review for this "game"/"software" (its both and neither in a healthy mix). In this time I added many play hours, referenced it in my Bachlor thesis and recommended it to some of my students (I coach a robotics class).
It's clearly visible that the developer puts a lot of effort into this product, I was impressed how many components and functions were added when I took a break for some months. I was even more impressed to see a recommendation I wrote in my first review to be picked up. Thats why I would say that the game is, even though the price rose after it's initial release, is very acceptable - IF your're in the target audience. If you don't want to know what is going on in the machine you're sitting in front of / next to, skip this.
The game covers everything you need to build a basic computer - handling data, mathematical and logical operations, and even more like stack and functions. As a computer science graduate it was easy to solve the levels. One of my students reached the byte comparision levels on his own, so up to this point the levels are well guided even for a newbie (with programming experience, but thats it). After that, there were moments where he had to reach out to me for advice. I would definetly add a level where the custom instruction maker is explained, the student didn't realize that it's available and had a harder time constructing the LEG architecture according to the op code specification.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThere is little more satisfying than solving a problem with an algorithm you designed on your own CPU in your own assembly language. The game is friendly and guides you from NAND gate to CPU, with a wonderfully dry humour (that does seem to disappear by the end game unfortunately). The dev is also super active, with bug fixes and tweaks coming out seemingly daily, although these have broken the game for an hour or so at least once.

It may not have the competitive score chasing of the seminal zachtronics games, but this game feels much more cohesive and focused with levels being linked and building upon each other, and the pay-off at the end is greater than a single puzzle level could be on it's own.

Plus, there's nothing stopping you creating your own CPU architecture from scratch and solving puzzles with that. If you're weird.\t\t\t\t\t
