\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you are a nerd like me, you may find the game entrancing. However, it is not a game per se. It's more like an (ideal) digital circuit simulator with a built-in puzzle set with an unforgivingly steep difficulty curve.

For those who have an EE or CS background, the game is about taking you back to class. Refreshing your memories about digital circuits and the fundamentals of digital computers with a hands-on interactive course. If, on the other hand, you know nothing about logic gates, then it's a "crash course" on how to build a processor from scratch.

But I hesitate to describe it as a course. It's more like being given a puzzle without explaining how the pieces fit together. Like reading a textbook, the game assumes you already gork about designing digital systems. Nitty-gritty details that are crucial to understanding how the solution works went unexplained. Perhaps it's intentional not to explain the key to the puzzle -- it's left as an exercise to the reader.

I can imagine that the game is brutally hard for players without experience fiddling with electronics. Akin to Stephen's Sausage Roll, you are thrown into the game without any hand-holding and have to figure out how the game works on your own. The solution to each puzzle is sometimes unique. A single mistake will unwind all your prior effort, leaving you starting from scratch again. But hey, at least it's not introducing gate latency, thermal noise and fan-out limitations, which are the real pain in the arse for actual digital circuit design. (Think about hazards. There is no hazard in this game but should you use real 74xx chips to build your circuits, chances are you will run into strange problems because your design contains hazards.)

Overall, I find the game absorbing. I can't ask for more but an adjustment to the difficulty curve. Maybe add a little box asking if the player is familiar with the subject, and more hand-holding stages for those who are just getting their feet wet.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI don't dislike the game, but think that it is, right now, in a bit rough shape QoL-wise and therefore have decided to give it thumbs down. Note that I have not completed the game yet, I have completed all Memory and Arithmetic (except the "store four bytes in this tiny space" level, more on that later).

The good first: The visuals are pretty, the music is great. The "story" is a bit dumb, but whatever.

I don't like that it sandboxes you so much. I.e., it's okay to give direction to people who would be unable to construct a computer without help, but I would find it much more fun to be able to "unlock" elements and once they're unlocked can be used in any subsequent level. Add a bit nonlinearity to that. It's unreasonable that a 3-AND is unlocked, but no other 3-input devices or no 4-AND, for example.

It is ENTIRELY unreasonable that we get a MUX early on, but a DEMUX which would be so immensely useful isn't available (yet, I'm assuming). This sucks.

The time-based levels where you have to speedrun twos complement: F off! Honestly. What crap is that? Who thought this was a brilliant idea? To people who do not know twos complement, putting them on time pressure sucks, because under time pressure they are GUARANTEED to not learn. To people who DO know twos complement it sucks because it's a stupid chore. I accidentally clicked the "do not force time constraint (accessability mode)" link and then couldn't reenable the timer anymore. Go figure.

Another thing, controls SUCK. Like, holding shift to mark a group is SO unintuitive coming from pretty much any EDA. Why? Sometimes, copy/paste will copy/paste the gates, but not the nets inbetween. What?

In some levels, the instructions are entirely unclear. E.g., the first level where you have to build a D-FF it isn't clear that there's an OE output. A bit more text.

Debugging also sucks, the testcases and input/output are sometimes displayed at the bottom, but many times are not. It's unclear what the exact error is.

To the visuals, yes they ARE beautiful, but they're also not of particular utility because especially with overlapping nets, it is quite difficult to figure out where's what.

Lastly, and this sent me over the edge and led to this "thumbs down": The "squeeze four 8-bit registers in this tiny space". What a crappy, crappy way of increasing perceived difficulty. It even acknowledges that it's going to be a mess. WHY? Is the goal not to get people going in design, instead of forcing them to adhere to some stupid pseudo-challenge? I completely get that this could be an additional challenge, make it a Steam achievement for crying out loud, but don't force me to do crap like that. It's lazy game design is what it is.

Please fix the QoL and I'll revise my review.\t\t\t\t\t
