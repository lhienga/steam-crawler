\r\n\t\t\t\t\tYou're probably reading this on a computer. But have you ever wondered how that computer worked--like, really worked?

Turing Complete is a game about designing a computer from it's most basic components--just a few simple circuits, 1s and 0s. You'll start with making logic gates (tiny devices that are the building blocks of computers) and design the various parts of a computer: memory, performing calculations, etc. The game gives just enough information to help you along, but the scope is always expanding--soon after learning to add 1 bit, you're adding 8 bits, for example. It's not as intimidating as it sounds.

Fortunately, the user interface is very friendly and easy to use. Visualizing your creations is aided by clear and colorful visuals.

I was gripped by the sense of discovery--and even ownership--in my solutions.

I have no formal background in Computer Science or Mathematics, and I was able to complete the game. If you're on the fence and have no experience in computer design, don't worry about it! In fact, it might make things even more interesting.

However, one criticism is that in some of the very later levels the designer uses terms that assume someone had background knowledge that was not communicated in the game. Feel free to check the forums or the internet in those cases. However, they are the exception, and I was able to create the first version of a working computer without any assistance.

I strongly recommend this game. I was completely addicted to it and cranked out these hours over a couple weeks. I expect that I'll come back to this any time I want to remind myself how a particular circuit or logic gate works, or even to see my computer--with all it's wires and blinking lights--and admire that I made a machine that is, ahem, Turing Complete.\t\t\t\t\t
