\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is basically two games... Actually I'm not even sure if it's one games? It's definitely most of one college course and like a tenth of another. It... It's most of Computer Engineering 101 glued to whatever the junior level class about Verilog is in your program.

I have literally no idea how it plays if you haven't already taken those classes, but I do know that several years out from having taken them, it was fun to go through a massively abbreviated version of them (without all the writing out truth tables by hand on paper). I'm going to assume you've also taken those classes for the purposes of this review, I'm sure there are other reviews for people who haven't.

It starts with the hardware part, you go through the ultra basics up until you have your first turing complete computer with a god-awful instruction set (like, only a little bit better than LC3), then it goes "you know... what if you... made a better instruction set?" and you do little tweaks to it throughout the rest of it. It flat-out assumes you're familiar with ideas like "I should make a bus for everything to sit on but only talk to when addressed!" but, when you are, it all flows very naturally and intuitively. I never felt like I was being asked something unreasonable.

When it transitions away from the hardware, it turns into an assembly programming class with a better-than-LC3 ISA (they call it LEG, I'unno) and asks you to implement some famous algorithms that you should've heard of and probably implemented in regular languages already, and it all culminates in what felt like an interview question.... but like a *good* interview question.

TL;DR: It's all the fun parts from ~1.5 university Computer Engineering courses without the crappy parts.\t\t\t\t\t
