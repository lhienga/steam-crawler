\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is one of my favorite games of all time. I've always wanted to learn *how* a computer actually works, and this game teaches you every single part by making you assemble it yourself piece-by-piece with parts you already assembled in previous levels. My only complaint is that I wish the developer would add the ability to easily reset your progress and start over. Either by adding save-slots or with a progress reset setting. After making it well past the Turing Complete level I wanted to start over and do it all again and the only way to do it was to move/delete the save data file and the schematics folder (which contains your completed solutions for each level). Other than that, this game is top-tier educational and is a good investment for anyone interested in learning about computers, even if you think you already know a lot. I'd love to see an expansion or a sequel where you transition from assembly, into creating your own high-level programming language and compiler. I think that'd be really cool.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is a great way to learn CPU design concepts while maintaining a careful balance between fun and work. Video games are appealing because they abstract work/friction/real world messiness away from the problem solving process to distill the learning process into short, simple, and cheap cycles of (1) understanding (arbitrary) objectives, (2) coming up with ideas, and (3) error correction, then rinse and repeat. Turing Complete does a great job of this.

Turing Complete can lean more towards work instead of pure fun and some players take that as an interesting challenge while others might not find this appealing. I wanted to experiment with Turing Complete as a way to learn some CPU design concepts without the work/friction/real world messiness. As a human full of contradictions and as someone who spends a lot of time building software, I wanted a CPU building simulator game that could help me build slightly transferable knowledge without reminding me too much real world computer programming. The trade off is that solving problems becomes more like work the more transferable or practical the skills are.

The campaign stopped being fun after completing 70% of the puzzles. I stopped after completing 90% of the campaign before experiencing diminishing returns for effort because the puzzle design no longer abstracted away work/mental friction. it stopped being a puzzle game and became interactive homework. The puzzles are not really that hard, but they became hard enough for me to abandon the game given how long it takes me (not others) to complete just 1 puzzle.

I highly recommend experimenting with this game to experience the fine line between video game and interactive computer science text book, and between fun and work. If you want pure video game fun, the game might not be for you. If you are reading this, then you are probably someone who is interested in experimenting with learning, fun or both.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHighly recommend. It perfectly scratches that Zachtronics itch. Coming into this game I knew a lot about how to code, but very little about how computers actually work at the lowest levels. This game struck the perfect balance between holding my hand and challenging me as it introduced me to the comp sci / electrical engineering fundamentals. For example I was never quite satisfied with the explanation of 2's complement negative int representation that was taught in my college classes, but this game explained it in a very intuitive way and let me play with the bits until it "clicked"

I used to be dumbfounded by people who could implement a computer inside of redstone or run doom on factorio circuits. After beating this game, I now understand how to build something like that!

A little rough around the edges as it's still in early access, but I didn't run into any game-breaking issues. I usually wait until games are out of early access before playing them, but this one is polished enough that I'd recommend it anyway\t\t\t\t\t
