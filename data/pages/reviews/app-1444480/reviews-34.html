\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBefore this game, I knew some of the logic gates, and how they work together, and how they're useful in the real world. However, I knew next to nothing about computers, or how they were made from just these primitive and basic logic gates. Now I have an innate intuition for how logic gates chain together, and some plans for building some computer components of my own in other sandbox games. Turing Complete is a powerful simulator with a silly premise and one of the quickest and most effective ways to learn how computer architecture works. Thanks to this game, I'll be trying to build redstone computers in Minecraft soon.

However, my one main criticism is that it's definitely rough around the edges with the training portion, and I've often found myself floundering around for hours and googling circuit diagrams (ESPECIALLY with the less than / greater than stuff) due to how little guidance it gives you sometimes. Games like this gotta find that balance between "challenging puzzles" and "impossible to progress without google", which Turing Complete only really struck on occasion. I do understand that this is still in early access though, and I can't really expect a finished, polished product with perfect onboarding for people inexperienced in computer science.

Additional notes: Music is lovely, premise and story is goofy in a good way (a tad rough around the edges but it's fine), visuals are nice, UI is good but could be better, performance is great.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis IS Early Access done right. The game has re-ignited my interest in microelectronics of forty years ago (science fair, Tandy kits etc.) and successfully re-taught me information that had become woolly over the last twenty-five years or so with that really lovely joy that comes from rediscovery of lost passions of the past.

One might ask why go through the NAND to CPU architecture learning curve? Why bother when it's all in black boxes? Why not just concentrate on the high level languages and just learn to get 'the job' done?

It's because it trains one's brain in a way of thinking; I believe even subconsciously -- yes like that prior word that I failed to spell correctly but tried before resorting to the spell checker. It aids problem solving in a wider context than just the knowledge of 'old early micro's' that is often perceived as as pointless as vivisecting a Frog for the last possible unknown's about a Frog's anatomy -- no Frogs are harmed and only electrons were perturbed -- So dive in!

My sincere gratitude to the developer and for a thoroughly wonderful use of ludology.\t\t\t\t\t
