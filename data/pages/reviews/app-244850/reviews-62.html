\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSpace Engineers is a borderline case of recommend / don't recommend, but after thinking about it at length, I am giving it a thumbs down. There are glimmers of fun to be dug up from the refuse pile, but at the end of the day, the game is more pain than enjoyable. Even the term "game" is a bit of a misnomer; it's more of a game engine somebody forgot to make a game out of.

Â· There are two extremes of gameplay; creative and survival mode. In creative you are just building stuff, but nothing you do matters since you're invincible and have infinite resources -- why build a mining rig when you don't need to mine, why build a spaceship when you can teleport everywhere? Survival mode on the other hand is an unholy mess of bad game design, from an irrelevant progression system to some truly torturous building mechanics.

Â· On that latter point, people sometimes call this game "Minecraft in space". It's vaguely true, but the blocks are 2 and a half times larger in every dimension (16 times the volume), too big to jump on, are plain and ugly, you have to assemble a single cobblestone block from 100 rocks and a bag of concrete, you can only carry 20 rocks at a time, and you have to stare at epileptic strobe lights for several seconds each time you assemble one. It's insanely tedious and all you can really build are rectangular rooms with nothing going on inside â€” unless you buy the decorative DLC blocks, then you can copy-paste the same bed, kitchen and lockers over and over again.

Â· Ah yes, the DLC. The game creators want you to buy the DLC. They really, really want you to buy the DLC. There are some 14 DLC packs, each containing a few cosmetic blocks so you can simulate having a toilet in your spaceship. As a reminder that you don't have these DLC packages, your build menu is full of these blocks, grayed out and taunting you every time you select something to build.

Â· Keen Software has some sketchy business practices. Space Engineers will repeatedly bug you to waive your privacy rights when launching it. They are responsible for Medieval Engineers, an abandoned early access game still up for sale at $10. People have noted a lack of concern for security, so if you play online this game may very well give hackers a way into your computer.

Â· For inexplicable reasons, the game keeps separate folders for your saves and mods depending on whether you're playing with an internet connection or not, either of which becomes inaccessible when you play under the opposite conditions.

Â· For all of the hours-long grind it takes to build things, it is insanely easy to destroy them in seconds. The physics bugs in this game will absolutely tear apart the thing you spent a ridiculous amount of time assembling. Multiplayer lag and desynchronization will leave you wondering why everything just exploded. Combat is a matter of who has the guns with the longest range, and the other side gets the pleasure of being torn asunder before they realize they're being shot at. The autosave feature seems designed to capture that moment right after your rover's suspension launches it kilometers in the air upside-down.

Â· The vanilla speed limit for any vehicle is 100 meters per second (224 mph). This can be modded up to about 500 m/s before the physics bugs take over. Contrast the SR-71 Blackbird at 983 m/s, the International Space Station at 7,660 m/s, or Voyager 1 at 17,000 m/s. For a game about space exploration, you move painfully slow. Yet the player is fitted with a jetpack which accelerates to 110 m/s with the lightest touch, often with lethal results. Ship-to-ship combat typically results in the NPC ship running away at maximum speed, and thanks to inertia, will continue drifting at maximum speed after its engines are shot out, rendering it unrecoverable. Even if not for the speed limit, overly abundant asteroids make extended flight in open space a bad idea, so you're forced to teleport from place to place using a jump drive.

Â· Contrary to its age and general lack of content, this game makes your GPU run hot. Spending time on a planet in particular will turn your PC into a space heater, which might be a plus in the winter, but not so much the rest of the time.

Â· Like most good games, Space Engineers has excellent mod support and the community has done an okay job of patching its flaws, but even with mods, it just doesn't feel complete or well-integrated. None of the mods really create an impression of being in a living universe where anything you do matters. Many mods are non-functional due to game engine changes over the years. Even finding mods that work well together is a bit of a slog.

Not to say it's completely bad. Once in a while you'll be flying through a canyon and you'll think, "okay, this is kind of cool." Scripting a cruise missile to follow the terrain of a planet, keeping itself aloft against the pull of gravity, can be fun. But, it's bad. For $5 or $10, sure, it's probably worth the price of admission, but not at full price, and definitely not with a full pack of DLC.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI appreciate that due to the rabid nature of the devoted fan base, I am going to be downvoted to oblivion, but this is the most aggressively inaccessible game I've ever played in my entire life. It's like the game doesn't want you to play it. Every single mechanic is as complicated as humanly possible and not for a single moment did one solid second of the game feel intuitive. Every mechanic is obscured behind a complicated, byzantine UI and a control system that never felt responsive or natural.

I want to like this game so badly. I watched the half hour of tutorials required before you can even play. I watched youtube videos. But this game is like the developers actively despise people and want to make their lives as difficult as possible. I just wanted to build cool spaceships :(
