\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really liked this game. Has a Pokémon feel to it, which I’m sure is inherently obvious with 10 minutes of playtime.

The positives of the game is that the story is pretty unique and weird in just the right ways; the Archangles are pretty funny, and generally their design is really unique and cool. I thought that the game was fairly well balanced as well, without any clear-cut “easy afk win” strategies (although I played on the hardest settings and didn’t experience much difficulty either. Felt like the hardest settings were medium mode). The Landkeepers are hilarious too, that whole subplot was one of my favorites.

As for the open-world element, it wasn’t my favorite. I felt like it had all the wrong elements of it amplified too much; I often had no real sense of direction and was just abusing wooden box physics to try to get to places I wasn’t supposed to be. The use of rumors to direct the player is cool in concept but often leads to having to hunt down whatever NPC holds the rumor you need. I think the story could benefit more from directing the player a lot more; it feels like chance that I mostly followed the correct direction to play in.

Overall, the game has a certain charm to it that makes it fun to play, despite any qualms I have. I haven’t done much post-game content yet, so there could be some interesting gems there, but I doubt it would change the tone of this review.

A solid 7-8, at least.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tReally fun Pokémon like game, based on duos and mid-fight fusions.

For the gameplay, the game is relatively close to Pokémon, where you have to record (collect) monsters by fighting them, the only difference being capturing a monster takes one turn and leaves the person capturing vulnerable (it sounds weird, but it makes sense once you test it and it also adds a bit of strategy to the capture itself).

You can also mix and match your monsters abilities with stickers that you can put on and remove at will, and each monster can have a certain number of stickers on them, different for each, at my level, it's usually from 5 to 10 abilites on each, allowing for a lot of possible interactions and strategies during fights.

Another interesting mechanic, that makes any monster you capture useful immediately, is that they share their level for their combat stats (but still have a separate exp metter to get new abilities or evolve). I really like this, as you can change your team to fit your needs more easily, without having to grind too much.

There is also a type table, with your usual types such as Wind, Water, Fire or Electricity, but also some more novel ones, like Plastic or Glass, and they all have pretty neat interactions for another layer of strategy.

The game, although I just started, doesn't seem too hard, but some fights can be challenging, especially Fused monsters or the major bosses.

There is also a bonding mechanic allowing your Fusions with your partner's monsters to be more powerful, while getting some wholesome stories and interactions with them.

The main story is fun so far, but I don't think it is the most interesting part of the game, far outclassed by the very solid combat system, though your interactions with other characters are always enjoyable (at least to me, so far).

It's overall a very good game that I recommend without hesitations.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tit's good and i do recommend it, but there's definitely a few problems. first, the good everyone mentions is rly good. the music is shockingly good, the monster designs are great, the characters are a little 1.5 dimensional but are still very enjoyable with overall pleasant art. when the game opens up, the world feels really fun to collect stuff in. the story is alright, it has some very charming subplots and the ending is good, but everything working up to the payoff is pretty shallow.

the main problem with the game, for me, was that it is too open ended. i feel like i stumbled upon almost everything i was supposed to do, which i think to some players that's great, but i didn't feel like i discovered anything, i just wondered over to the next open-world-collectible/miniboss. the world is very diorama-esc, which makes it feel very... game-y. again, this likely really appeals to some players, but it made it hard for me to feel emotionally invested in almost anything in the game, and it seems like the game wanted me to be invested. i never felt a narrative arc, no sense of escalation or pressure, just wandering around finding stuff until the game says i found enough stuff to unlock the final last stuff to beat the game. the villain is present here and there but in the most indirect ways, almost every goal of the game is just to gather more resources, do little platforming puzzles (that were fun enough, if a little stale towards the end), or go fight X thing. because it can be done so open ended-ly, they all have to just be kinda hanging out in plain sight, and i started to get serious open-world-game-fatigue.

this is not helped by the fact that the game tries to guide you via the level curve of certain areas, but by the midway point you can go most places as it is very open ended, which creates the problem that pokemon gold/silver had: the beginning is strangely hard as it tries to bottleneck you into the areas where you first level up, and then the mid to late game is suddenly incredibly easy once you start to outpace the remaining sections in levels. the post game it gets a little bit harder again, but strategy in the game is WAY more free-from than pokemon, which at first is really fun as you get to handcraft kinda broken strategies by holding onto all moves (stickers) and applying them to the appropriate CBs, with some pretty absurd (and not the best balanced) passives. However, this gets old when you realize the opponents do not have the same access to strategy, or else they too could create CBs that have like a 20% chance to instantly nuke your first starting CBs and maybe even you or your partner. so by the time you get to the end game, you will likely know a few nigh unbeatable strats if your CBs are in a remotely similar level range as your opponent.

overall, i'm critiquing the game a lot despite the fact that it is really pleasant to play, just because some of the core design detracts so much from what is good about the game for certain players. it's good to set your expectations accordingly. i honestly still recommend it to pretty much anyone: the music, the visuals, the area design, the side quests, collecting monsters, building your character, all of that stuff is incredibly fun. the game manages to be very funny as well. but if you aren't a huge open world fan, keep that in mind.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tVery fun pokemon-like game. The world is fun to explore, there's secrets and sidequests everywhere and fun characters.

The combat system is fun and allows you lots of freedom to pick whatever monsters you want and spec them out how you like. You can either run a mix of light and heavy attacks or some staple mid-range attacks you can repeat every turn. The element system is very cool, with each "strong" or "weak" interaction inflicting a status effect instead of just extra damage (but also grants you bonus AP to encourage you to keep exploiting it). Forms scale off your own level instead of being useless until you level them up, but they still gain extra ability slots and evolve if you use them enough.

My only criticism is that it's very difficult to tell which stickers (ie moves) can be used by which forms--there's some vague patterns but no consistent rules. You can select a sticker from your inventory rather than the form's movelist to see who in your active party can use it, but there's no guessing for forms that are in storage and no way to check when buying a move from a shop. Reskilling can be a bit of trial and error as a result. Also, when you swap party members you first need to swap whatever form they were using with a useless one from storage, which is minor but annoying.\t\t\t\t\t
