\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCassette Beasts is very clearly inspired by Pokemon, and while many games have come out in recent years that have been the same, no other has truly redefined the subgenre of monster capture RPG in the same way. Cassette Beasts contains a numerous variety of gameplay tweaks and changes that make it go from nostalgia bait to a true delight, which is especially ironic in the best way because the aesthetics and basic gameplay style are very much designed to pander to nostalgia!

First off, instead of capturing the monsters themselves (raising questions about dogfighting), you instead copy their form down and gain the ability to turn into them yourself, which itself means that there are two health bars, your human form and whatever monster form you have active. Losing a monster form results in the excess damage hurting your human form, as well as leaving it open for an attack until their next turn; you can either lose a turn and swap out to something else, or risk it all for one last attack, and if your human form loses all its HP, then you lose an active monster slot until you recover at a campfire or the pokemon center equivalents. You also leave your human form vulnerable when trying to record a monster form, which makes the act even more dangerous.

Second, type advantages are no longer simple rock-paper-scissors, but rather, type advantages and disadvantages inflict buffs and debuffs unique to each matchup, allowing you to pull off some crazy stunts by attacking your own teammates or forcing enemies to attack a specific teammate, as well as encouraging type variety in your own party even when building it to fight specific types. And because you can have up to two party members out at one time, the strategies and teamwork pokemon games almost never let you use outside of multiplayer become a staple part of battling here.

Third, each monster form can have its moves removed from it and then reapplied to another monster, turning every monster you gain into a portable TM dispensary- these moves can then be removed and reassigned as much as you like, but each move is its own item, so you're very much encouraged to level up as many monsters as you can to gain access to more unique move pools for the monster forms you prefer.

This and more (including a variety of side quests and content, unlockable out-of-combat abilities, an extremely silly yet intriguing story, and puns galore) await you in the island of New Wirral, and I highly suggest you check it out!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is my new quintessential mons battler, coming from the background of a very enthusiastic Pokemon junkie. I've tried others in the genre, but this is the only one that's stuck. All the pieces of the game - mechanics, story, characters, music, and art - range from good to excellent on their own, but they reinforce each other throughout. The music sticks out, especially, which feels key for a game rich with musical references and a main gimmick surrounding cassette tapes. The soundtrack is worth picking up on its own.

Mechanically, I was initially concerned by the absence of some mechanics I anticipated from the genre, especially species-based passives and same-type attack bonuses (STAB). They ultimately weren't missed - the mon species are plenty distinct with signature moves and varying move compatibility, and skipping STAB creates much more versatility in movesets. Fixing on duo-battles is a winning position for the game, as it seems to create a good space for strategy without too much complexity, and it plays well with the companions from a narrative perspective. The type interactions are complex, but in-battle prompts and almost exclusively single-typed mons mean there's little need to memorize a type chart. Interestingly, the interactions aren't about extra damage - hitting an ice mon with a fire attack would change its type to water, and following up with a plant type attack on the now water type would seed it with a leech effect that steals hp for the attacker's team. From a strategy perspective, the mons tend to naturally fall into a few different roles, and easy type changes and plentiful walls (similar to Pokemon's Substitute) make for a unique and interesting strategic battleground.

Much of the rest of the game can be summed up by the word "cozy." You spend the game traveling alongside companions who are fundamentally good people, through a world that's colorful but just a little washed-out, to music that's just chill. The 80's aesthetic isn't anything I have a particular affinity for, but it works extremely well in context (and it is placed in context.) Wandering the town and talking with the people there, companions and otherwise, has an inclusive, diverse, feel-good vibe. The story is also a tale of human imagination and resilience, a refreshing deviation from the conservation-minded Pokemon set. I'm entirely sympathetic to climate- and ecology-oriented messages, but avoiding that depressingly current topic to focus on a timeless story of human foibles and the strength to overcome them fits better with the emotionally-dense, largely positive-minded presentation.

None of which is to say that the game can't be serious or even somewhat creepy when it needs to be. Battle music is period-inspired rock and roll with some indie influences, and pretty expertly composed. It also shifts into a more intense vocal mix for critical moments in combat - a determination based on the player's decision to engage the limited-use fusion mechanic, so it doesn't feel out of place on push-over bosses (not that I found any), or miss out on difficult standard encounters. There are also a set of main-story boss fights that have an entirely different audiovisual landscape, completely at odds with the rest of the game. The visual changes and musical themes that go with these foes underline their role in the story. It's not particularly groundbreaking, but it's all so well done that it jarred me out of gameplay a few times to notice how everything was tied together.

At the time of writing, I've just finished both branches of the main story (to twin heartwarming conclusions), and haven't done much past that. However, I'm reliably informed that there's plenty of post-game stuff to do. The game just received a free update (again, at the time of writing) with new zones and mons and announced both a future multiplayer update (including trading and battling), and a paid expansion. I'm sure I'll be coming back whenever a need a hit of a good mons battler, or just some good vibes.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tVery good fun! Good pixel art, able to access the elemental chart during combat, wide variety of beasts to turn into (personally I think the fusion bestiary is just going to be impossible to finish; and you can't finish the regular bestiary until you beat the game. It is just ONE though). I enjoy playing this game on the Nintendo Switch as well!
Q: So is this another Pokemon-Clone?
A: No. Similar mechanics, but instead of dealing extra damage they add an extra effect. Like adding a damage over time effect or turning them into another elemental. Like you can use Fire on Plastic to turn into poison.
Q: The major bosses are kicking my butt! What am I doing wrong?!
A: The damage over time effects, such as: Leech, Poison, Burn, or Electrify do percent damage and will be the most useful! Unlike in the Shen Megami Tensei, they are immune to status downs; like reduce attack or defense. You can put them to sleep as well! Also watch their Action Point gauge! Putting up an Elemental Wall will take the full force of the attack and deals reduce damage to Fusion.
Q: Are attack/abilities set in stone?
A: No! You can peel the stickers (what the game calls attacks and passives) off and hand them to other Cassettes! Not every Beast can learn every move, but it is fun to mix n match while developing your favorite strategy for each Beast. Fun Addition: Sometimes moves can have different qualities and effects. Like a basic range attack that can ignore walls, or (much more common) a status or buff lasts an additional turn.
Q: Any possible love interests?
A: Weird question, but the answer is yes! You get partners who travel with you and there is a 'romance option' (expect the dog, SPOILER). You will need to defeat a major boss related to their quests to unlock their Hearts that max at level 5. You can also have level 5 heart WITHOUT romancing them. You can be their friend instead!
Q: Are the partners LOCKED into their unique beasts?
A: No! You can also change out their beasts! They do get a bonus to their unique beast, but it is much better to have a beast with better type match up to the boss fight or fusion encounter than having a 10% bonus.
Q: Can you get multiples of the Unique Beasts?
A: Yes. Spoiler: There is a flying Island at the upper left corner of the map that has the chance to unlock your partners' beasts.\t\t\t\t\t
