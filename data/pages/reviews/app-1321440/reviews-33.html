\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAn extremely good and interesting monster tamer when you become the monsters you catch!

Pros
- Has one of the best soundtracks in any smaller scale game I've ever heard, I 100%'d the game a few months ago and still listen to several of the songs regularly
- Great monster designs, with several having interesting evolution methods
- Easy to swap around movesets and create fun builds
- The combat has mechanics I haven't seen in a fair number of other monster tamers due to you having an NPC partner to play off of
- Easy to cheese combat with certain setups (great for endgame grinding if you're an achievement hunter)

Cons
- Personally low replayability for me, wasn't much to bring me back after 100%ing
- The bulletin board in the post game is an insane grind that doesn't even feel worth it for the rewards you get
- A few puzzles are misleading/confusing
- Easy to cheese combat with certain setups (can remove all challenge from the game)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDo you like Pokemon? Into action, adventure, grinding, and exploration? Well, if you like any or all of that, plus an extra heap of nice music to go with it, this may be the game for you.

+++Pros+++
+ The overall concept and most of the execution is great
+ The game looks decent and the music fits it
+ No crashes or bugs encountered so far
+ Varied selection of monsters, stickers, and companions to customize your play
+ Almost a casual game, but you can get yourself into trouble if you don't pay attention

---Cons---
- Would like a bit more variety with the music - each monster form has its own unique music since they are "taped"
- At the outset the pacing seems a bit slow & grindy, as you have to repeatedly return to a rest spot to heal yourself; you can only carry a limited number of items that heal/repair your monsters/tapes, but not you - the number carried can be upgraded eventually though

===Conclusion===
While I haven't played any Pokemon games in over 20 years now, I doubt the formula has changed so much that my experience with it would be irrelevant. This game is cut from a similar cloth, but gives you more freedom to play around with your monsters.

Monsters can evolve after awhile, but you also have the ability to customize them via the use of stickers that you apply to the cassette tapes - the higher their level, the more sticker slots they have. Then, you also have the ability to temporarily fuse you and your companion's equipped monsters into a unique monster - prior to playing, I thought you could permanently fuse two monsters to create a new one that joins your party, but that isn't the case. Too bad, as I would have loved a breeding/fusion system that allowed for that, but oh well.

I wasn't sure what my reaction to this game would be before playing, but I'm pleasantly surprised. There are a few things I'd like to see done differently, but overall it is quite fun. Oh, and it plays flawlessly on the Steam Deck, so there's that.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCassette Beasts is a very fun turn-based creature collector RPG. What originally sucked me into the game was the air of mystery surrounding the setting of the game. The music especially the town music and the night music really help you feel like you are lost in some unknown land away from home. Right at the start you wash up on the shore of an unfamiliar land and meet your first party member who runs you through the short tutorial and sets the stage for what to do next. After the initial tutorial, you are free to roam the land and take on quests at your own pace.

I enjoyed the freedom to explore the word at my leisure and prioritize quests that interest me. Do you want to explore a strange cult or a haunted mall? You can do one, both, or neither and still progress through the game's main story line. There are some key quests that you'll need to do to reach the game's ending, but you can still make progress on exploring the land without doing the quests because recording certain beasts with your cassette player gives you special abilities to better traverse the landscape and reach areas that were previously unreachable.

My feeling about the combat in cassette beasts is that it is very free and respectful of a player's time. It is free in the sense that you are free to use any cassette beast without having to grind it up to a certain level to be useful because the cassettes you use scale off you and your teammate's level. Each tape can be leveled up five times. Some tapes can be remastered into stronger tapes after five levels and each level awards you with a new sticker that you can attach, remove, or reattach to any compatible tape. This is how Cassette Beats respects a player's time.

The game showers you with stickers. You can buy them from randomly rotating stocks at vendors, you get them as your tapes level up, you get a random sticker every time a tape levels up at the five star cap, and rare shiny bootleg cassette beats give rare stickers with additional attributes. You can then mix and match these stickers on any compatible tape to develop your own strategy and builds. You can even stack multiple copies of the same sticker on a tape for devastating results. You don't need to worry about losing stickers by trying out a strategy that turns out to be ineffective because you can always peel the stickers off a tape if it doesn't work out without losing those stickers. That is a huge benefit for players really looking to explore different play styles in the end game or for players who just need to change up their strategy for beating a certain boss.

I've thoroughly enjoyed my 153 hours of this game at the time of this review. I'm looking forward 153 more with my friends once the multiplayer update is released. I hope to be playing with some of you! Feel free to reply with questions if you want to know more about the game!\t\t\t\t\t
