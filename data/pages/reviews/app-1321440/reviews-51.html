\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo this is a monster collection RPG with a few big twists. First of all, rather than collecting and training monsters, you're playing as something of a shapeshifter, who can turn into the various beasts on the Isle of New Wirral by recording them in the wild and then listening to those recordings on your portable cassette player!

You find yourself in New Wirral after falling through a dimensional portal while sleeping (I spent a good portion of the game running around in my pajamas before I learned how to change clothes, giving the experience a vaguely 'Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy' feel), and are quickly brought to one of its two only active settlements by a young lady named Kayleigh, who is one of the Rangers that have charged themselves with protecting Harbour Town from the dangerous beasts that dwell on the Island. You quickly learn that the population of Harbour Town, and the rest of New Wirral, is made up of people from alternate versions of Earth, which leads to a lot of entertaining dialogue and some fun cameos from famous historic figures (There seems to be an emphasis on Philosophers and Scientists in particular for these).

Kayleigh quickly persuades you to try out for the Rangers yourself, and the early game is largely a matter of you exploring the Island and finding and challenging the various Ranger Captains to prove your worth. You soon learn of a major and mysterious threat to Harbour Town and its people, but I've gotten too far into spoiler territory as it is.

Exploration is handled in a somewhat Zelda style, with certain of the beasts enabling different means of travel once you add them to your repertoire. This does make some areas gated off initially, but I found a few spots were clever use of powers could get you into higher level areas a bit early, if you were clever and persistant.

In the course of your travels you continue to collect recordings and gain new forms, though you can only have five active at a time, and also gain new companions, though only one will travel with you at a time for most of the game. They all have distinct personalities and stories, and one of them is even a cameo on the part of a well known literary character! Romance is an option, and the game does not bar you from making any matches you might wish in that regard, with the understandable exception of the dog.

Combat is turn based, and offers another twist on the monster collecting formula: Once you have established a friendship with a companion, you can fuse your forms together to make a more powerful, hybrid beast! This is not something restricted to the player, and battles against hybrid monsters, known as 'Rogue Fusions' and 'Fusion Swarms' are some of the most rewarding in the game!

I really enjoyed this. The plot is reasonably straight forward, but still contains some interesting twists, and the writing and characterization are both super solid, with a lot of fun had with the ideas of so many people being native to alternate realities, and the fact that no one truly understands how the shape changing that is possible in New Wirral actually works.

If you enjoy monster collecting games, or if the idea of a Zeldaish game with turn based pokemon like battles sounds appealing, you should give this a try.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCassette Beast is a charming game, created with passion and love and it shows.

The monster designs are fun makes you curious into what creature they turn or what you might get what interesting combinations you can produce with the fusion system. The buff/debuff focussed type system allows for more tactical and creative gameplay. The chance of getting "uncommon/rare" moves with random buffs added to them keeps you excited for the level ups and the ability to just take a move from one monster and give it to another makes sure you can keep your favorites always decked out with the best stuff.

The overworld is diverse and fun to explore, filled with various little puzzles of all kind for you to solve. As the game progresses, more traversal options are unlocked, making the exploration easier, quicker, and more fun.

I just have one negative comment to give. One might be interested in unlocking every unique content, finishing every unique quest. If one wants to do that it will require grinding, at some point in the postgame it may end up taking a huge amount of time and it won't be very enjoyable as one will do one of the few random quests of "go x kill y", "give A item B", etc... and there will be no interestig reward during that time. I doubled my playtime of the game in the postgame and most of it was just the same boring quests/fights till i could finally unlock the last unique quest. Which makes things a little sadder is the fact that after you finish the last unique quest, the grind might get become interesting because of the "mechanic" locked behind that quest.

Overall even with that little critic, it's still one of my favorite games and it captured me until i finished it and even then i was left wanting more of it.\t\t\t\t\t
