\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAn incredibly ♥♥♥♥♥♥' game the likes of which hasn't really been seen in this format - a cassette-based format. Genuinely really fun, really sweet, really vibey - excellent music, excellent graphics, excellent concepts, excellent tastes, excellent multiplayer. Can't wait either for eventual online compatibility and the upcoming DLC. This is genuinely far better than Pokemon due to its themes and overall depth of philosophical critique - I'll be listing some of my more specific examples at the end of this post (copy/pasted from elsewhere), but I especially want to pay shoutout to the inherent liminality of this game that extends far beyond its story, setting, and vibe. Overall the feel you get at the start is "this entire thing is a familiar place you're visiting but can never return to. The entire thing feels like a nostalgic trip and yet still all at once wholly new and fleeting." And it goes so far beyond that the deeper in you knead and dig.

Even outside of the anti-capitalist themes.

Couple all this with the very nature of the gameplay itself - recording and remastering your beasts. You record the data of a beast, and then you get to merge with it. The data of that beast grows in the tape's film with time alongside you, and each beast is implied to have a different sensory experience when merging with them. They are not only your friends existing in a dream-like state that you fuse with, but they exist in a physical data form in the cassette itself. To top it all off, you get to customize them to your heart's content with stickers, making them the best they can be. It nails the abstract dreamy vibe by pulling it all down to earth in a physical, allegorical, nostalgic way.

Now here are some specific examples of these themes of liminality, going outside of your scope of experience, as well as the surreality of the game:

About the main Archangel you find at the start of the game, touches on what they actually are: Morgante is the archangel of rebellion, and used to work with Aleph in order to create devastation. Up until Morgante and you rebel against Conquest and devastation itself. Not even speaking of the sheer presentation of them and the other archangels and how they use different graphics than any of the other characters, essentially a 3D model within a 2D sprite-based world, alluding to the idea of 3D being greeted by 4D gods/concepts with actual form. A crossing of dimensions. It's even more apparent this is the case when you take into account the description of the Cube.

In regards to the companions you meet and befriend - or romance, though you can only romance one character that you get to level 5, not all of them, keep in mind. But all characters can be gotten to level 5. It even goes as far as the characters' own issues - like Felix harmonizing with his past works. Viola harmonizing with the fact she is someone else's fictional character. Kayleigh harmonizing with the fact she has committed mistakes and built a house that harms others. Eugene harmonizing with the fact he is empty without purpose and drive. And Barkley... well. He lost his original owner when brought to the world and is likely why he can use Cassette Beasts. His owner probably was supposed to but he ended up getting it. Most likely Barkley is from a world where those cassettes are common, possibly they were on a walk when they were transferred. And Meredith herself harmonizes the fact she's pretty introverted and tends to not live life to the fullest, eventually coming out of her shell and forgoing the NEED for materialistic things but still enjoys them.

The Merging and Fusion mechanics themselves and how they relate to the themes of empathy, harmony, liminality, and sensory experience of life itself: It also touches on the themes in how it describes the merging process - either the sensory aspects of fusing with your beasts (which themselves are DATA contained on a cassette tape, data that grows alongside you and "your data", and the sensory aspects of fusing with other people.

There are even implications that while plastic, steel, poison, etc. are seen as "impure" types to a psychic/electromagnetic type like Astral as it's called, it doesn't mean that those things are necessarily bad. The main case in point being the Sweet starters that can either be alchemy or a candy machine - both can heal or bring joy, despite being poison type and steel type respectively.

In the end this is a game about harmony, liminality, friendship, romance, connections of all different kinds, data, dreams, dimensions, empathy, crossroads, decisions, and knowing in life who or what your actual enemy is. And you get to befriend everyone and everything along the way, in essence, except for your one true enemy.

Enjoy the game, I know I did.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tReview in short:
"Cassette Beasts has a better story, theme, world building, level design, soundtrack and ideas than any Pokémon game in the last decade. An extremely solid game that goes beyond being just a 'Pokémon-like'."

Info on how I played:
* Played on PC
* Played in single player

Pro:
+ Amazing OSTs
+ Simple but effective character creator, with everything (also name and pronouns) that can be changed any time during the game
+ Optional Quest Log
+ Special types interactions
+ Visible enemies on the world; no random encounters
+ Original beasts' designs and names
+ Difficulty settings
+ All cassettes in the team gain exp
+ Original mechanics (capturing beasts, world movement skills, fusions, ...)
+ Good level design with shortcuts; well done open world with Metroidvania mechanics
+ Interesting world building
+ Good characters
+ Post-game content
+ Huge replayability thanks to special New Game options
+ Unbelieavably huge amount of unique beasts sprites for fusions; each pair of beasts has one for a total of more than 16k

Cons:
- Cannot remap joypad's controls
- Captured bests go automatically to the "box" and cannot be added directly to the party
- Cannot add pointers or markers to the map
- Cannot skip or speed up animations, making battles against many enemies incredibly long
- Completing the bestiary requires heavy RNG-based mechanics and some unclear ones
- Bad handle of sorting/filtering of moves
- Bad handle of re-ording beasts between the party and the "box"

Miscellaneous:
* Pokémon-like, but more JRPG
* Cassettes' level is tied to evolving; in battle, it is the character's level that matters
* The game is not all "happy and colorful", but has some relatively creepy parts and designs. It's NOT a horror game, mind you, but it's not a kids game either.
* Strongly altered status-based combat system
* [Minor spoiler] In order to finish the game, the player is required to solve a riddle which is "unique" to each game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHonestly, really love the game. The creature designs are fun, every remaster is a joy. Finding the game's version of shinier, bootlegs, is always fun and actually adds something mechanical to the game rather than just aesthetician. The open world aspect was a bit annoying to me at first, but once you get your bearings it becomes a neat touch.
The game isn't without its faults, it has random hitches here and there in its frames, there's awkwardly placed loading screens. And, personally, the look of the world isn't my favorite, I'd much prefer a full 2d style. And thr battling can be kind of hard to grasp at first, with each type not only being strong against others, but also applying different status effects to the other creature or yourself.

Even with the negatives though, I highly recommend the game.\t\t\t\t\t
