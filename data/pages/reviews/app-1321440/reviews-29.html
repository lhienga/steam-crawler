\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCassette Beasts is a phenomenal re-imagining of a tired formula that went so far above and beyond the standard for its genre that it can scarcely be compared its inspiration's more modern entries. It not only satisfies the persistent craving I've had for a creature collector that doesn't suck eggs for the past ~7 years, but it distinguishes itself through its innovative-yet-familiar combat, its wonderful soundtrack, and its absolutely incredible art direction.

+ The design of the monsters draws from a wide body of myth, folklore, and everyday items and animals, and the bosses are some of the most visually compelling material I've seen in ages. The general visual style of the game is unmatched. Seriously, if you're on the fence about the game and don't mind minor spoilers, look up the Archangels.

+ The soundtrack is stellar, as is to be expected from a game featuring cassettes as a core component. It's a good mix of very chill video-gamey lo-fi tracks and absolutely banging battle themes, with a bit of ambiance and other stuff sprinkled in. The gimmick of (very minor spoilers) fusion adding (shockingly great) vocals to the otherwise-instrumental battle tracks was a fantastic idea well-executed. I don't know music theory too well, so I can't really communicate why the soundtrack is good, but take my word for it: it is.

- The story isn't great. While the setting is A++ and the lore is pretty good, the actual plot doesn't feel terribly interconnected. A consequence of the game's open-world design is that there isn't gradual progression so much as a few chunks of it, ultimately feeling pretty clunky. Not to mention, your character's motivation is pretty weak and never elaborated on. It ended up creating a level of disconnect between me and my avatar that only really became evident in the endgame, but it did detract from my experience a tiny bit.

+ The characters are good. We don't really get that much interaction with them, given that they're not the primary focus of the game, but I really enjoyed the quest or two for each we did get.

- The player character has pretty much nothing in the personality department, which didn't bug me all that much when playing the game, but in retrospect feels like a bit of a missed opportunity, given the existence of relationship mechanics. It feels more than a little odd that you spend all this in-game time building relationships and falling in love and such, but I ultimately know nothing about my character as a person. There *is* a watsonian explanation for this, with it being insinuated that you, the actual player, are comparable to the lovecraftian Archangels, and the avatar you control is more of a person on a stick than an actual human, but this is literally never elaborated on, raising like six questions and answering none.

+ The open-world, while not immense or groundbreaking in any way, is well-designed and generally enjoyable to traverse. The way it opens up incrementally doesn't feel arbitrarily limiting, as is the case with many other similar worlds, but rather is an effective way to combine a linear level-progression system with a world that's fully-traversable from the get-go. You don't feel unable to explore when the game starts, but you'll feel like a movement wizard by the postgame. And from a less gameplay-focused perspective, I loved the setting of New Wirral on a conceptual level. The idea of a island inhabited by space-time-displaced but otherwise ordinary people, combined with the "power-of-thought" magic system, was very well-suited to the genre.

- I will say that while I'm a sucker for superbosses, the postgame does leave a bit to be desired. Once you finish up the ~3 postgame questlines there isn't all that much to do, unless you're interested in the game's shiny-hunting equivalent (which is a bit more fun here, I'd say, but not generally something I'd recommend to casual players). The harder (read: grindier) postgame achievements were a bit of a slog, and I think it was a bit unwise to lock a whole (admittedly not that important) miniboss variety behind like 20 hours of grinding a resource. Still overall an improvement on contemporary Pokemon, but I do wish there was some sort of Battle Frontier-type subgame.

+ The game offers a robust selection of customization options, most notably adjustable difficulty, variable level scaling, optional nuzlocke rules, and a randomizer mode, the latter two of which require beating the game to unlock.

~ The fusion system, while a fun concept implemented moderately well, can trivialize fights intended to be difficult quite handily, even if you're using monsters not specifically built for fusing. When making use of fusion optimally, as opposed to only using it as my final form like John Darksoul, only one or two postgame battles caused me any real trouble, and that's along with the fact that I didn't use healing items while in combat. I haven't experimented with harder difficulties yet as of writing this, so take this with a grain of salt, as it might be a personal gripe I could have very easily remedied. Also, for such an otherwise visually-outstanding game, a lot of the fusions look... not super great. This didn't really detract from my experience, and it's totally understandable given how damn many there are, but it still warrants mentioning.

+ Aside from the visuals, the leveling, stat, and skill systems are what stand out most to me here. You have 3 levels, one tied to your avatar, one to your partner (who is mostly functionally identical to you, but with their own slightly different stat spread), and one to each of your monster cassettes. You & your partner's level are the primary determining factor of your monster's stats, in conjunction with which species of monster you or your partner are currently transformed into. The level of a cassette also affects its stats, but not nearly as much as its stats or your level. The primary function of cassette levels are:

a). Reaching level 5 (the stat increase cap for cassettes) enables many tapes to be "remastered", which is comparable
to evolution in Pokemon

b). Leveling tapes is (at least in the early game) your primary method of obtaining "stickers", which are essentially
your monster's moves and abilities, which can be exchanged between compatible monsters.

The brilliance of this system lies in its conduciveness to the use of a great variety of monsters, with little need to commit to a set "A Team", as is the case with most other creature collectors. If a monster levels up and gets a move, and your immediate thought is "Wow! That'd be fantastic on Skrunkum, the Space Tardigrade!" there's a pretty good chance you can just take it from the monster who learned it initially and give it to them. (Compatibility varies, for obvious balance reasons.) Essentially, you can use any monster you've recorded and have them be effective with little to no grinding necessary. There are very few, if any, very bad fully-remastered monsters.

In closing, Cassette Beasts is a fantastic creature collector, both for longtime fans of the genre and newcomers. Its system mechanics are cleverly designed to allow for an elasticity in playstyle unmatched by nearly anything else I've seen in gaming, never mind its own genre. Most of my issues with the game are relatively minor, and certainly didn't stop me from 100%ing it (though I wouldn't recommend doing that yourself). If you're dissatisfied with Pokemon, looking for an experience similar to Pokemon, or just in the market for an RPG of respectable-but-not-immense size, then I can't recommend Cassette Beasts enough.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFantastic, fantastic, fantastic game!
To compare this to Pokémon is to do Cassette Beasts a disservice. Pokémon feels to me as though it has lost its heart and soul. This game plays not like a Pokémon made for kids or to print money but a game made for all ages, and a game made out of love and passion. I'm so ashamed of myself for sleeping on this for so long. I bought it back when it came out and just never got around to playing it until now. Ironically enough, I kind of put it off a bit thinking it would be “yet another Pokémon clone.”

I'll be transparent and say I haven't finished the game, but I'm on track to doing so. It runs well enough on Steam Deck and utilizes the Godot game engine. My playtime has been split between playing on my laptop and playing on my Steam Deck. I leave it set to 50 Hz on Steam Deck, and leave all other battery settings at their default values. Turn off half rate shading if you're using DeckyRecorder. HRS seems to cause some sort of flickering in footage. May cause epileptic episodes if you open footage without realizing you've left HRS on. Steam Deck does get frame drops, and they are noticeable, but due to the nature of the gameplay, they are not game-breaking. I did get some crashes, and I think my issue was limiting the wattage to 13 instead of leaving the limiter off. I have been unable to recreate the crashes after setting wattage back to default. I don’t have an OLED model Steam Deck to compare, but the fans ramp up quite a bit on the old 512 GB LCD model Steam Deck.

Cassette Beasts is 100% what I would call a wholesome and cozy game. It's got some creepiness and some cuteness. It’s got a good amount of diversity. It’s got some heartfelt moments, and some good potential for character development. There’s been some really clever moments that made me go “Huh. That’s interesting,” as well. I think I still have quite a bit left to do, and I've been stumped a couple of times. The game could do with some more hints or giving more guidance on what you're supposed to do next at certain points. It just kind of started to slow down at one point, and I ended up wandering around until I stumbled into what I was supposed to do. This actually happened a couple of times during my play sessions.

There’s some really good music in certain locations and boss battles of this game as well. I was in a cafe at one point and was suddenly hit with some music that took me aback for a moment. I wasn’t expecting that kind of vibe from this game going into it. As a music lover, I can’t help but love the record-themed stages. The first boss encounter had a really good song, as well. Not quite the kind of music I was expecting from this game, but definitely not disappointing by any stretch.

I find the fusion and cassette/recording mechanics to be really interesting. They’re a good twist on the monster collector formula. Type matches and mismatches, abilities, buffs, and debuffs all feel really good to me. They’re fun to play around with, and they make sense in this game’s world. I really like the way the leveling system is implemented as well. I never felt like I was hindered by the beasts I wanted to use. Your beasts level up, evolve, and all that good stuff. However, your player character also levels up. That’s the interesting part. There’s a lot to play around with here, and a lot of interesting twists on the monster collecting genre. It’s really not “just another Pokémon clone.”

To just describe it as “Pokémon but better” or something along those lines is to do Cassette Beasts dirty. It’s so much more than that. It’s a love letter to what made those classic Pokémon and Zelda games great. It wears its inspiration on its sleeve, and it does it in the best way possible.

Pick this one up. Buy it on sale if you want, but I think it’s worth the full asking price.

Edit: I beat the game. I cried. I'm now playing the DLC. Cassette Beasts is definitely worth the asking price. So much so that I've gone back and purchased their previous game, Lenna's Inception, as well.\t\t\t\t\t
