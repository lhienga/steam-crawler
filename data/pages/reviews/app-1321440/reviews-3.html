\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNormally, I will purchase games that have native Linux distributions to support my chosen OS platform, even if I never really enjoy them, as long as they have some redeeming feature I enjoy. I was not prepared to enjoy this game as much as I have.

While very Pokemon-esque, the combat mechanics are dissimilar enough that it takes some learning to get these correct. It has the type chart, which is not terribly complex, similar to Pokemon games, and it has moves that the monsters can learn, hit points, statuses, etc. This, however, is largely where the similarities end.

The moves seem far more balanced than those in Pokemon. Sure, some are better for starting, but even some of those 'basic' moves can be enhanced to allow other attributes through being more rare. Moves can interact with each other, and even trigger each other, in ways that are semi-intuitive, so even if you don't know all the rules, they sometimes make sense. Oh, and a note, a monster can have up to 8, depending on how 'evolved' they are and how high of a 'level', and each has an 'action point' cost instead of power points, and APs build up slowly so you aren't going to have to deal with hyper-beams on round one (most of the time).

The story, though, is very interesting, as well as the questing mechanic. It seems to have a lot of replay-ability. I have not yet finished the game, so I am not sure if 'breeding' the monsters is a thing, but the basic monsters are all identical in stats so you wouldn't get much from doing so anyway. I am impressed with the story, and will recommend this to anyone who has a Pokemon itch but wants to scratch it in a whole new way.\t\t\t\t\t

  • Gameplay: 10/10. Best battle system I've ever seen in an RPG. This is a game that really understands that for a turn-based RPG system to work well and remain engaging, and not devolve into what I like to call a "press-A simulator," you really have to have at least one other useful party member, and you really have to have buffs (that are powerful enough to actually be worth the turn they cost), and it helps to have some customization potential. Even though you can only have two active party members at any given time in this game (counting the protagonist), it still manages to place the greatest emphasis on party cooperation and synergy that I've ever seen in an RPG, and that's immensely to its credit -- not to mention, thanks to stickers and bootleg monsters, the customization potential is practically endless. The battle system stays engaging even after you've hundred-percented the game. I have literally nothing left to do in the game and still grind the end-postgame enemies just for the pleasure of it.
  • Graphics: 8/10. Obviously not mind-blowing in the tech department, which is why this score isn't higher. But the reason it's as high as it is is because, for what they are, the graphics just look good. Gorgeous pixel art, vibrant and beautiful color palette, reasonably decent character portraits, fun and memorable monster designs, stylish and theme-appropriate menus, impactful and communicative visual effects, and there's a certain decision made about mixing styles in a certain very specific situation to violate our expectations to achieve a certain very specific effect, and I won't spoil it, but that specific decision is just brilliantly done, really conveys what they were going for. I won't give the game credit for coming up with individual custom designs for each and every one of the couple tens of thousands of fusions, because actually, they didn't. The devs have said the designs aren't AI-generated, which is true, but I think people may have taken the wrong message away from that. They aren't custom. They're procedurally generated, the procedure in question just isn't AI. What they did is, they sprited each and every individual body part that any monster can contribute to a fusion, and then they combine those body parts with a trivial non-neural algorithm comparable to putting an outfit on a paper doll. I will say, given that's how they do it, it's very impressive how they managed to sprite the body parts intelligently enough to make all the fusions look reasonably seamless and representative.
  • Audio: 11/10. The monsters have good battle cries, the sound effects are dramatic and immersive (which is a weird thing to say when the graphics are retroesque and third-person top-down like they are, but it's true, the audio really ties it together), and most of all, the entire soundtrack consists exclusively of complete and utter bangers. I listen to it while I work sometimes. It's so good.
  • Story: 8/10. It's well-written. It's not the most poignant and brilliant writing ever, but it doesn't have to be. It's just high-concept enough to be thought-provoking, with just enough character and soul to be compelling, and otherwise it stays out of the way. It's actually remarkable how well they managed to write it given how open it is. To put it in perspective, I'd say it gets less points in story than Undertale, but more than Stardew Valley, which, for a game that's mainly about the gameplay, I'd say is fairly high praise. It goes without saying that it's certainly leagues better in the story department than big corporate monster-tamers like Pokmon or even Persona.
  • Overall: 10/10. The mean score based on the above is actually 9.25/10, but it gets an extra 0.75 bonus points for possibly being my actual favorite game of all time. I only have a couple more-played games in my library, and one of them isn't even a game, it's a game dev tool -- and that's over the course of my entire life, whereas I discovered this game less than a year ago.
