\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCassette Beasts is a wonderful game that does a great job of capturing the feeling and aesthetic of Gen IV Pokémon, with a battle system that is focused on taking the basic concepts of combat in Pokémon and expanding them in intriguing, unexpected ways. Tied together with a light story that essentially functions as a framework in which the game operates rather than being the driving force, this game is both cozy and challenging, simple and thought-provoking, and a great time from start to finish.

It really feels to me like the developers have a great deal of appreciation for what makes Pokémon work so well - one of the things that really annoys me about some romhacks and fangames is the sense that the developers of those always think they can do things a lot better than Game Freak, whereas with Cassette Beasts, it feels like it simply takes inspiration from the basic framework of combat and goes in a fresh, interesting direction. The core of battle is type interactions, which are a lot more varied and diverse than in Pokémon, relying on basic logic for you to ascertain how elements will interact with each other. I was discovering new interactions as late as the final part of the main mission, and I'm still not completely sure I've seen it all.

Party management emphasizes experimentation - monsters don't have their own individual levels, instead your player character levels up directly, giving every monster a common base power upon which you can build, and making all of them at least a little bit usable in battle from the moment you acquire them. In place of levels, monsters have a separate leveling system that grants more move slots and default moves every time they level up, though movesets are completely customizable at will via the use of HM-like move stickers that are infinitely reusable. It all just feels very thoughtfully considered, more about creating a satisfying environment for player experimentation than, I don't know, being difficult for difficulty's sake.

The story is sparse, and the somewhat non-linear mission system can sometimes leave you feeling a little directionless, but all it takes to get on the right track is to just open the mission list, pick a mission, and start pursuing it. Eventually, you'll narrow the list down to just the core path, and doing the other missions as much as possible first will allow you to properly scale up to match the increasing difficulty of the main mission. The art style is very, very charming, using sprites for characters and 3D models for the environment - very Gen IV Pokémon, as I said before. There's an unsettling aspect to some elements, too, hinting at a darkness at the center of the world you're thrown into, and the variance in art style for the characters related to that core mission is one of my favorite things about the game.

This is a game where it feels like every element comes together extremely well, in the service of ensuring the player enjoys every aspect of it as much as possible. It's not perfect - fusion feels like little more than a gimmick, there's an ambiguity to some missions that makes it hard to figure out what exactly to do at the mission marker, you can inadvertently sequence break and get rolled pretty easily even with your partner party member warning you when you're underleveled for an area - but it does a lot of things well, very well, in fact, and it's a game I had a blast with from beginning to end. Worth it at any price.\t\t\t\t\t
