\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCassette Beasts is the newcomer in the recent slew of monster-taming games, a genre that has mostly been content to follow the ropes and tropes set by a particular market leader. But I’m happy to report Cassette Beasts carves a new path through subverting expectations and refining the genre in ways you might not even realise you want.

Let’s start with the main event – the combat. Cassette Beasts’ combat is focused on ‘energy,’ a resource that needs strategic management and preservation. Every turn you’ll gain a fixed amount of energy and any decent move has an associated energy cost. Between those powerful attacks your turns will be spent playing defensive options or doing mild chip damage. It’s reminiscent of the sort of resource management you’d find in tabletop games and interesting tradeoffs naturally emerge, even in wild battles that would feel inconsequential in other RPGs.

These interesting play mechanics are all enhanced by Cassette Beasts solely featuring Double Battles and dedicating a significant proportion of its moves to enhancing or protecting your team mate. The combat possibilities are dizzying and the best indicator of those possibilities is in Cassette Beast’s equivalent of ‘Gyms,’ which are not based on monster types but each instead showcases an entirely unique battle strategy. It’s great stuff.

I also never stuck to a single strategy thanks to team experimentation being made trivial. Experience persists across all your monsters and attacks can be outright transferred between them (so long as the receiving monster’s movepool accepts it). Once any final evolution was reached I swapped them out, meaning I was able to progress both the bestiary and storyline without falling behind the level curve, creating a rewarding sense of progression.

Said story is well-planned - a somewhat sombre reflection on nostalgic escapism and those themes will resonate with the target audience. The execution is serviceable, is filled with late 90's charm, and the boss battles along the way are a stylish highlight. However, I do think more sidekick-focused quests would have helped flesh out the characterisation, as the game lacks that immersive ‘ensemble goes questing’ feeling you might desire in your RPGs. I think some of that comes down to a lack of “quality time” you get to spend with the cast, so keep those expectations in check.

On the topic of characters I’d say ‘creature designs’ are typically the weakest point of other monster-taming games, but Cassette Beasts bucks the trend by drawing from a range of inspirations while weaving a consistent design language throughout. The spectrum goes from ‘adorable’ to ‘edgelord’ while always maintaining a unique ‘Cassette Beast’ feel. Shoutouts to ‘Djinn Entonic’, ‘Brushroom’ and ‘Elfless’ (my beloved).

My only major gripe here is navigating the overworld, there’s significant traversal stutter when moving at pace and the visuals are just about adequate (in comparison to the excellent monster sprites). It also takes a while to unlock your full navigation moveset and the interim does feel like something is ‘missing’ which makes for a bad first impression. Keep at it though, once you unlock the Bulletino Dash everything will start to click.

So, to summarise, Cassette Beasts may be the monster-taming newcomer but imo it’s the most interesting of the lot. Serious effort has gone into identifying and challenging genre staples and using them as opportunities to innovate. I’ve kept details about a lot of that polish vague because the best bit of Cassette Beasts is in the surprises and delights you’ll discover along the journey. To any fans of turn-based battles seeking interesting yet accessible gameplay then Cassette Beasts is the one to grab. Highly recommended!\t\t\t\t\t
