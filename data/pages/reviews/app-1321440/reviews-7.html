\r\n\t\t\t\t\tVery enjoyable game with a lot of interesting mechanics. Seems like an excellent start to a series.

Some feedback to the devs:
* Loved the setting/story/narrative progression.
* Reining in the world scope and keeping the side-quests pretty simple clearly helped the main quest be as great as it was.
* I really like the idea of type advantages/disadvantages implemented, but I feel like it still lacked some spectacle. It seems like you were very against damage adjustments, but that made types feel almost entirely unimportant in most situations.
* The custom game mode stuff seems to downplay the game's excellent monster and world design, but maybe I'll try the perma-death stuff.
* A lot of it feels a little too accessible:
- Recording shouldn't save monsters from defeat.
- The fact that the glass angel monster showed up over and over after I failed to catch it made the awesome first encounter feel less awesome after the fact.
- Exp share led me to basically just use the starter and the goth girl starter, exclusively.
- Catching 'em all was a too easy.
- Player level has too much affect on battle outcomes. I never became attached to any one monster and I did very little sticker management because it was all too balanced in this way.
- I generally liked the move cost system, but the lack of limits on moves, a la PP in Pokemon, made some battles drag on for way too long without anything interesting happening.

So in all I thoroughly enjoyed your game and I look forward to a sequel, but once I wrap up the Steam achievements the game just doesn't have enough replay value because the mechanics aren't impactful enough and it's too accessible.

But seriously, thanks for 52 very fun hours of gameplay and the coming 10ish that it will probably take me to wrap up.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhile the Pokemon-inspired elements are pretty visible, I wouldn't call this an actual Pokemon clone. Cassette Beasts is more of a classic RPG, an open-world game in its way. It gives you a bunch of Main Quests and Side Quests, hints on how to get overworld skills, and then lets you loose. It's not a Metroidvania either, I think, but the primary roadblocks are either enemies to defeat or skills you don't have yet (like swimming, etc.). There's nothing blocking you off from an area except your base ability to get inside. Nothing is stopping me from tromping around the snow zone, but my ally helpfully warned me that the local monsters were probably too tough for us.

My only real problem with Cassette Beasts is the sometimes janky controls, and most notably the platforming. The good news is that the plot-relevant platforming is on the easier end, and usually isn't in an area rife with respawning enemies to drag you down.

Aside from that, CB has good music, decent art, and a really intriguing story. Everyone has either washed up on this interdimensional beach or descended from people who have, and not everyone is from the same universe. Your character decides to try and find a way home, and uncovers some mysteries of the island in the process. One thing I like about the story is the mentions of various new areas and things cropping up periodically, so it feels less like a Chosen One situation and more of happening upon these things while doing our own Main Quest. You can do things in any order you like, pretty much, especially once you unlock most of the overworld travel skills. Each ally has a Personal Quest line to fulfill, increasing friendship and giving you motivations to do different things and visit different areas.

Mechanically, the battles are really interesting. There is a level of 'mon-collection, but it works differently than maybe any game I've seen before. "Capturing" a 'mon results in the player getting a baseline copy of it - a tape - that then the player or your partner can transform into. The game is really good about letting you try to capture a 'mon without killing it, though there are downsides if you aren't careful.

Elements work partly in a Super Effective format, but the game gives even more incentive to pay attention because moves have different effects depending on types. For example, hitting a Water type with a Fire move grants the Water 'mon the buff Healing Steam, which does exactly what it says. On the other hand, the Fire 'mon taking a Water hit will Extinguish it, tanking its damage output. You can eat damage and race to finish a fight with a type disadvantage, but the smarter move is to swap around to get the best lineup for the situation. (You can also attack allies or yourself to trigger Buffs the same way.) This makes for a really interesting, dynamic system that keeps the player on their toes. I don't want to describe the entire combat system for 5 paragraphs, but suffice to say they found a pretty good balance that definitely evokes Pokemon types at times, while still feeling fresh and new and relevant to the current setting.

The player and your partner, as well as certain foes, can also Fuse - creating a dual-type 'Mon with the stats and moves of both parties, and combined AP gauge. This has its benefits and drawbacks, and makes for a really interesting option to spice up boss battles or create special encounters.

Lastly, a really neat thing about the combat system is that transformed humans - the player party, and opponents - have a separate health gauge. Overflow from knocking out 'mon HP will affect this, and it's possible to shut down an enemy with a strategic, concentrated maneuver, preventing them from pulling out their next tape. It's a neat way to reward players for thinking ahead, without making battles impossible if you miss it.

There's way more that I haven't gotten into, but suffice to say that I'm having a really good time. CB is an okay pickup for Pokemon fans - it's not unlike[/i a Pokemon game - but I will say that the general vibe is very different from a core Pokemon game, which is why I want to stress the inaccuracy of the "clone" claims. Honestly, it feels closer to Digimon in some ways, but I know less about that series than Pokemon, for what that's worth.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA better version of pokemon made for PC! It has similar pluses and minuses, but is generally better

Pluses:
+ AMAZING cozy-epic rock soundtrack
+ Nice aesthetics and pixel art
+ Fun gameplay
+ Some bosses felt especially great
(gameplay, compared to pokemon):
+ Easier capturing
+ Easier upgrading (you can easily swap beasts, particularly for "upgrade them all" achievement)
+ Easy swapping of beast skills
+ Sorta balanced (unless you abuse a few broken interactions). You can't just annihilate enemies by 2x or 4x damage. And there is no single-element gyms (aka water gym) to be predictably destroyed by counter-element (aka grass)
+ Difficulty can be adjusted
+ No animal slavery as the part of the world setting (you transform into beasts, instead of forcing others to fight)
+ While there are “only“ around 150 beasts, there are ~16000 fusion combinations of them, that, they said, are not made by an AI. That is something

Minuses:
- Grindy in post-game
- Characters and story are nice and cozy, but rather simple\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI don't leave reviews often so I'm not quite sure how I should structure this, but I will try my best.

Cassette Beasts is a very charming game, with nice character and monster designs, and a combat system I am quite fond of. They were clearly inspired by some of the earlier Pokemon games while making this, but I don't think you could consider it a "clone" or a "rip-off", it truly feels like they were inspired by them, and then made their own game from that inspiration without relying on it or having it feel cheap, which is not an easy thing to do. The story is compelling, the character designs are good, the art style is pleasant to look at, the dialogue is amusing but still takes itself seriously when it needs to, and the combat system is easy to get the hang of but not so easy that it becomes boring. The platforming is a bit rough, but it's certainly not the worst thing either, and I'm fairly certain that a majority of the difficulty I had with it is due to the fact that my hand is a bit messed up at the moment, but I figured I would mention that anyways since the controls are definitely a bit off sometimes, messed up hand or not. Obviously this is all my own opinion and it may not be the game for everyone, but it certainly is the game for me, and I'm having a wonderful time.\t\t\t\t\t
