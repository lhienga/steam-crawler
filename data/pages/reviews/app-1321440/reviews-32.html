\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCassette Beasts really scratches an itch that current mokepon either cannot or wont.

The monster designs are really fun and I adore the pixel graphics.
The environments take a mixture of 3D assets with pixel designs that make for a very unique map.
Having the option to develop relationships with the other characters you meet is really great especially when there's a benefit to doing so in game.
Gaining overworld abilities based on the monsters you record is a ton of fun.
Unique enough combat system that benefits from slow burn strategies.
Fights can actually be challenging and not just bulldozed.
The story is pretty lackluster but there is plenty here to make up for it.

Overall, I would definitely recommend this to anyone who is looking for a monster taming game. I really had a blast.

Your moves are attachable to your monsters, and can have rarities and bonus effects, giving you not only the ability to swap to a new favorite monster much easier-you can take your strong moves with you-but also lets you make some wild and wacky strategies with all the freedom you get.

The soundtrack is an absolute selling point. By a huge margin. The idea they use of having the vocals come in when you use the game's unique battle mechanic is absolutely genius, and ramps the soundtrack up to one of the defining features of the game almost instantly. It's an unforgettable feeling when it first happens, and can make truly memorable moments all the more memorable. When you're on your last legs, about to die, at less than half health with only two mons left, and you desperation fuse...and the lyrics kick in. ♥♥♥♥. There's a ton to enjoy here. An absolutely stellar creature collector, and one of the best that I've ever played, I wholeheartedly recommend it without hesitation. Nab it and have a wonderful time, it's a ton of fun from start to finish.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo let me start by explaining that while I do not currently have a lot of time logged on this game here on Steam, I originally bought it on switch and have sunk well over 300 hours into it there. Because of that this review is based largely on that console (but the game seems to run even better on PC anyway, so hopefully this review is still helpful for you).

Overall: 12 / 10 stars for this game. I have been a gamer since the mid 1990s and I can honestly say that Cassette Beasts has *easily* landed in my top 5 games of all time, hands down (and probably my game of the decade if I'm honest). In an era where publishers constantly push out huge titles with excessive hype and increasing prices while delivering often broken, uninspired and just mediocre products, this unassuming game has reminded me what it is like to play a game made by an extremely talented and passionate team that chose to let their creation do the talking; not some corporate marketing department.

as background, I bought this game (originally) over the summer to fill the gap while I awaited Bethesda's latest hype dream of a game. I found Cassette Beasts on sale for a little under $20 and decided it looked interesting enough to try hoping it'd provide a something interesting to play while I waited for the AAA title I was looking forward to in the fall. I enjoy monster collectors and while some are better than others, I figured the price was worth the 'risk' and maybe I'd be happily surprised with it being a solid entry into the genre like some others I've found over the years even if it was 'just another monster collecting clone'.
Well... Wow. Just... Wow. First off, this is not a clone: yes, it's solidly in the monster collector genre, but obviously designed by people that love this genre and took careful notes of various flaws or un-enjoyable parts of earlier entries in the genre who decided to not only improve on those that came before but to throw the gauntlet down for those that dare come after. This game exceeded every expectation and hope I had for it going in. By the time I finished the main story, my partner had already gotten themselves a copy after trying the game out on my system, and I had bought this steam copy as well both to support the developers who (for a rare change in the industry these days) put out a product I felt was worth far more than they asked for, (and also to have a copy on my computer for when my handheld eventually dies or just for when I'm feeling like escaping into the world of "New Wirrel" when sitting at my PC -and honestly if there's ever a version put out that doesn't require a service to run I'll probably grab a third copy just in case Steam goes offline someday).
In fact, while I've had no interest in going back to the AAA title I had been looking forward to since a few weeks after it came out, it's Cassette Beasts I'm *still* reaching for when I want some video game entertainment 6 months later.

Every time I thought I'd finished thinking "wow" with this game another thing popped up that left me in awe of how a small indie team could put out something so enjoyable and with such depth as this. Things as small as the solution to the main story arc not being a single thing (My partner's main story had a different solution/destination for the end chapter than my playthrough had for instance), to the fact that this game's "Shinies" (Bootlegs as they are called here) not only come in *various* bootleg forms, but have mechanical differences to them (not inherent advantages, just differences). The fact that you can choose game options to play a new game with *completely* randomized types and locations for creatures which provides a huge amount of replay-ability on top of what the initial playthrough offers, or the fact that the story and setting felt like something out of the mind of a mad-genius, just coherent enough to feel like it fit together and belonged in the world, and just incoherent enough to keep reminding you just what an alien place you're in (not to mention allowing for nearly limitless story telling potential in any future DLCs or sequels the developers choose to create). And even when you feel like you've found all there is to find, there's an elusive mysterious NPC lurking somewhere you've never seen, or a hidden area you stumble upon thinking "there can't be anything here".

I had originally been a little apprehensive about some of the more innovative things about cassette beasts such as players turning into creatures themselves or the fusion system.
I quickly realized that there seems to have been very careful thought and intent put into these decisions though based on flaws with many of the flagship entries in the monster hunting genre. -the character transforming into the monster for instance? The way Cassette Beasts handles stats, the majority of the stats actually comes from your (and your companion's) character themselves with only some of the stats coming from the type of monster you're battling as. This felt weird initially just due to not being the 'norm' but quickly showed how genius this is in the way that it makes finding new creatures you like not force you to decide between grinding them up in levels or sticking with the battle hardened team you already have because since your character levels in CB; not your monsters, swapping monsters just changes the flavors of your party, not its overall ability. The way these devs have done this, they have ensured that there's a lot of strategy involved in Cassette Beasts as well though. Types have advantages and disadvantages (and some affect other types but not in an *inherently* negative way), which have a definite impact, but unlike the flagship monster collectors that make it nearly impossible to lose a good type matchup, Casette Beasts doesn't give the advantaged monsters 2x or 4x power increases or 0.5x or 0x resistances. Instead buffs and defuffs tend to apply for the type matchups which do add up and can definitely turn battles over time, but they still require some strategy (if playing on a harder difficulty at least -did I mention you can choose a difficulty in this game) and do not become a snooze-fest just because you have a good type matchup at the moment.

This game does have some flaws here and there of course. The main one I am thinking of is that the "stickers" you collect (Think monster moves from other games in the genre, but that can be mixed and matched and reused as desired), don't always stack correctly (you might have 2 rare moves with the same names and same added benefits that don't stack in your inventory and take up an added slot). There are a lot of inventory slots for stickers but *if* you play for a very long time this can be a minor annoyance.

I feel like there's a lot more I could say about this game (and there are probably a few flaws I'm forgetting at present) so I'll try to swing back by and update this eventually. But if you're on the fence about this game? My recommendation is to get it. It would be amazing value even for $50 and it sells for less than $20.\t\t\t\t\t
