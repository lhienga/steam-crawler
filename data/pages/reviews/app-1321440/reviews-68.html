\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCassette Beasts, a game that unabashedly channels the spirit of Pokemon with an 80s inspired theme, is a mixed bag of delights and missed opportunities. It does everything so right that you can't help but feel like it's missing that one thing you need.

Soundtrack (10/10):
Let's start with what's undoubtedly the game's shining jewel - the soundtrack. It's a bona fide masterpiece. Every note, every synth wave, evoke this modern 80's feel. I found myself irresistibly bobbing my head to every track. The developers have nailed the music, and it's worth the price of admission alone.

Combat System:
Cassette Beasts introduces a mid-level combat system that attempted to set it apart from Pokemon. With 2 vs. 2 battles, unique type matchups, and a generous roster of abilities and creatures, it offers the potential for deep, strategic gameplay. I won't spoil what really makes the combat stand out but once you find out, I'm sure you'll be just as impressed as I was.

Here's the ugly - Cassette Beasts is often too reminiscent of Pokemon, to its own detriment. It's as if they slapped a fresh coat of paint on the classic formula and called it a day. While the battles offer challenge and strategy whereas Pokemon games can be played by a goldfish, the game sometimes feels like it's playing it too safe in terms of innovation. It's nothing like those blatant rip off games that flood the app store. It's evident that a lot of care and time has gone into this project. It is different enough to make it worth the experience. Any fan of the genre will find themselves tiring of the repetition about part way through.

One notable pain point is team management. As you progress into the mid-game, changing your team becomes a chore. You are nudged in this direction where you want to train up every cassette beast you catch and it turns into bringing 1-2 beef cakes and the rest are evolution subjects that you haven't really invested anything into. Not only that but there isn't a whole lot of breakup from the exploring and the fighting. It gets repetitive quickly. You'd end up facing each creature once to catch it and fleeing from the rest. I don't have the solution for this common issue, but Cassette Beasts definitely had this issue.

Storyline:
The story might come across as cheesy, but it adds a dash of charm to the game. It doesn't overwhelm, but it keeps you engaged as you explore every nook and cranny of the world. The game runs into the issue of being open world and not being forced to spend very much time with any companion. Then it expects you to care about these people when they're really just bait while you try to record new tapes.

The open-world design offers freedom and discovery, which is both a strength and weakness. It's fun to explore and stumble upon the various nooks and crannies but it can also lead to moments of aimlessness. Staying focused on the main objectives can be a challenge.

Overall Rating (7.6/10):
In summary, Cassette Beasts is a game of highs and lows. While it occasionally leans too heavily on Pokemon's formula and doesn't offer much reprieve from the combat, it delivers engaging battles and an exceptional soundtrack. With some tweaks to team management and a touch more originality, it could achieve greatness. Despite its imperfections, I wholeheartedly recommend Cassette Beasts to fellow monster collector enthusiasts. It is worth its value and I'm not saying that just because its soundtrack alone justifies the price of admission.
