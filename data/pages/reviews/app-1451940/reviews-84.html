\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWish I could give this one a middle thumb, but I enjoyed more than I disliked, so it gets a thumbs up.

I think the biggest issue is the fact that money is a non-issue in the game. I get that the appeal is more about quirky humor and the morbid curiosity of what you can do to Angel, but once that wears off, there isn't much to get invested in. You don't have to worry about algorithm changes, more popular streamers stealing your audience, needing to dual monetize with video content alongside the streams. The game has a decent excuse for money to be a factor with sponsored streams, but these are mostly played for laughs and are almost non-choices if you want to reach 1,000,000 followers. If you already achieved a major following, the chat messages don't change in meaningful ways, where you get messages like how they've never heard of Angel before, or that she's got potential even if you've already finished the campaign essentially.
A more personal gripe I have is that if you want to treat Angel like a person and reduce her stress and darkness to zero, the game ends rather than Angel being a more stable person who happens to stream. She just quits, and you don't know if she went back to school, got a different job, or just reserves herself to living with you. The game comes across as a fantasy of many somewhat true stereotypes of the internet, but is very different from the truth. It also feels like the only reason you control Angel as her boyfriend is to dehumanize her more so that you as the player don't feel bad doing messed up stuff to a clearly fake person. There could a social barrier here that I don't understand like if this was a common thing that happens to Japanese idol girls, but it didn't sit right with me that she acted so robotic to the point I couldn't suspend my disbelief anymore. The (Un)Happy and normie endings were the closest I saw her become a somewhat believable person, but it came too little too late.

For some stuff I liked, the adaptive music (especially in the first half) was great. It's already a kind of stressful experience, but once Angel gets extra stressed, the music gets so manic and all over the place that you as the player will want to calm her down to save your ears and head. The different activities healing and damaging certain stats were all pretty accurate to real life like the couple's therapy, doom scrolling, dinder, sleeping, and spending "quality" time with Angel. While I have a problem with the unrealistic manic personality of Angel, the commitment to it with certain game mechanics was charming. Things like her leaving you if you don't respond to enough texts. If she already loves you, she'll force the sex option or bring you to her parents. The only way for her to find self-worth is in therapy, otherwise she has a varied amount of ways to spiral in hating herself, dissociating, or hurting her body in numerous ways. I haven't even seen 1/4 of them, but I'm sure they only get more deranged the worse you treat her.

Overall you'll find a few hours of a somewhat interesting take on the very niche subject of e-girls, but it feels stuck in a fantasy that doesn't capture the reality in a convincing way if you engage with the mechanics past the surface level. I don't regret my time with it, but I'll probably never replay it or hurt Angel more just to satiate my curiosity for the other endings.\t\t\t\t\t
