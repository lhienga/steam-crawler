WHAT JRPGs USED TO BE. WHAT FANTASY OUGHT TO BE.

As I write this review I'm at hour 82 of this little piece of liquid joy, and I'm still on "act 2." I can't sing enough praises for this game, all I'll say, and I'm totally going there, is that while Baldur's Gate 3 was made with "big boy money" and may be bigger and prettier, Sea of Stars beats it in writing, world building, and soul... which to me, are all that matters in an RPG. Sea of Stars is a love letter to the JRPGs of yore, a longing to simpler times, times more innocent for the industry... much like the fantasy genre itself, and it shows in the colorful pixel art, music that takes after OG Yasunori Mitsuda, and world building that fills you with wonder every step of the way.

As per the old high fantasy formula, Sea Of Stars is a zero-to-hero story of two kids who happen to be the latest generation of Solstice Warriors, an ancestral order created with the mission of exterminating monsters called "dwellers" which, unlike other monsters, not only don't age, also keep growing as long as they keep eating, so it's Solstice Warriors' duty to exterminate them before any of them grows into a World Eater (TL;DR, kids fated to save the world)... in this case, though, they're at least young adults... and against formula (and much to the delight of anyone tired of alocentric carp being shoved down our throats), the protagonists not only are not in love, also THERE'S NO ROMANTIC PLOTS IN HERE. AT ALL. ZERO. ZEULCHE. NADA. (sorry, horndogs... but about time we got some aro representation in here, don't you think?).

Graphics-wise, the game goes for old SNES looks... if some disgruntled Nintendo engineer had shoved all the extra-chips of a 16bit arcade board on a SNES. It pushes 16bit paradigm close to its limits of all you could visualize on said paradigm, providing rich vistas just at the right time and surprising "mock cut-scenes" for Ultimate Attacks, only one step from reaching Monsterboy's "perfect pixel" level (not quite there, but almost). In addition, key moments of the story enjoy fully-animated sequences in reminiscence of Playstation's old remakes of Lunar: Silver Star Story and Blue Star Story (granted, Sabotage couldn't grab a hold of Kadokawa Animation like old Game Arts did for Lunar, but it's still a most decent job).

Music wise, if you're old enough to have enjoyed Chrono Trigger, you know what to expect, and the music blends with the art style to perfection to create an atmosphere of pure magic for old Gen-X gamers like me.

What really takes the cake, though, is the world building... and again, I'm going THERE: Sure, Baldur's Gate benefitted from half a century of accumulated dnd lore, but that is exactly why I have so much more respect for Sea of Stars... SoS created a full fantasy world with unique cosmogony whole-cloth, no borrowed settings and no more help than the shoulders of giants they stood on to see farther than most. As a matter of fact, if I had to compare, I feel SoS is closer to Exalted than to DnD. It's exactly the breath of fresh air anyone sick of vanilla fantasy needs. It's not more of the same, it's not another Johnny Come Lately. In addition, and something I hope becomes SoS' contribution to the RPG genre, is their twist on the tired old narrative auxiliary tool know as "codex": Rather than just hoarding text snippets with all bits of world building the devs couldn't manage to fit into the game's active narrative and that you have to remove yourself from said narrative (see, opening the menus) in order to read, SoS ads a "travelling magical historian" NPC for your party, and every time you come across a "codex entry," the historian (who happens to be disarmingly adorable) acquires a new tale to tell you when the party sets camp... and Teaks is an AWESOME storyteller.

Again, nothing against Baldur's Gate 3, in fact it's still a game I want. Sea of Stars just happened to be the game I needed.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA fun, classic style RPG that's light on the difficulty (on the default settings) while still making you think about which moves to use and when.

As somebody who loves my 80+ hour long RPG's I do enjoy every once in awhile playing through something that won't take up my life for the next month and Sea of Stars is exemplar in that aspect, it has a fun battle system that feels like the Mario and Luigi games mixed with Octopath Traveller (timed attacks and a 'lock' mechanic to prevent enemies from making their move) an interesting world design that is fun to travel and explore through and a story that whilst is pretty standard for a good 70% of the game really throws some good twists that surprised me.

Art direction is phenomenal and most of the music is really fun, Eric W. Brown, Vincent Jones, Yasunori Mitsuda and Celtic Metal Dude put together a phenomenal soundtrack.

The game is also super friendly to achievement hunters, unlike most RPG's, the developers specifically designed the achievements to all be possible in a single play-through, with the slight exception of one which may be possible in one play-through but is far easier to do in New Game+.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEver since it appeared on the Steam page as well as seeing the trailers, I was dead set on owning the game after it was released. And boy oh boy the price was so worth it. Let’s have my review, shall we?

Pros
– Reminiscent of a Classic : Chrono Trigger , to be precise. The fights and the story (kind of), but it doesn’t just idolize the classic game. It gives you a unique story and gameplay inspired by the game, which is, in all honesty, pretty dope. I would hope Sea of Stars will continue to pave the way for innovation.

– Innovative Gameplay : Being a turn-based fan, I kind of loathe real-time gameplay that requires skill and precision. Thankfully, their rhythm-like innovation to the turn-based gameplay grew on me, and with the adjustable difficulty in the form of perks and disadvantages, you can set your pace to your heart’s desire (except for that one achievement). It also perfectly embeds regular attacks and spellcasting with the limited inventory, enemy cast canceling, and a wide array of mooks and bosses, which makes each visit to each dungeon a unique experience. Another thing to note is how I liked that the currency isn’t given to you in a spoon-feeding fashion, and it feels authentic and realistic even in a fantasy setting.

– Soundtrack : In addition to the “Night and Day” function, they have managed to evoke vibrant day journeys and calm night strolls through their music. As well as fitting for the dungeon or town, the music helps in pulling you into the game, creating an immersive environment perfectly blended with the pixelated sceneries.

– Visuals : You can see and feel the passion the developers have put into this game. The sceneries feel unique and immersive, the interaction with the night and day to the environment, and smooth and varied animations of the characters and NPCs, blend well with the soundtrack, giving you a trip to the past with a more vivid look. The cutscenes are also animated, and they contrast well with the pixelated overworld.

– Characters : I love the characters, they all seem natural. You will connect to them as you go along with the story. Each of them has their quirks and personalities, and it is fleshed out well and does not feel forced or flat. The NPCs are also a delight to see, as well as the certain dialogues you encounter. And thumbs up to the shapeshifter, their forms look rad!

– Story : I’ll admit, I dismissed the story as generic the first time I played it. This game flipped my expectations by not only shoving the characters and the world’s story in your face but you are allowed to observe the story and feel the emotions they have portrayed. You feel happy when they are happy, and you feel sad when they are, and that to me speaks volumes about this game. To the Storymaker, I salute you. You and your team had done a great job in delivering this story to the fullest.


I think this game is perfect in its own way. But if I have to be nitpicky and a prick, then here are my few gripes on the game:

Cons
– Gameplay and Story Segregation : I find this common in 2D games I play. Interchangeable location of the scar ? Sign me up NOT.


– That one achievement : I do agree with them, boss rush needs to be added. It’s just me though.

– A few tweaks on Teaks : I love Teaks, don’t get me wrong, she is fine. The Lore Grimoire does need a few more, like tracking on how many recipes you still haven’t cooked or a world or enemy glossary would be nice, befitting a historian.


Once again, kudos to the team for making such a nostalgic, immersive world into a reality through a video game. I hope all of you are doing well, and I am looking forward to your future creations!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTLDR:
-This game is all looks and no depth.

Maybe I am being unfair as an old JRPG Veteran and coming of playing the absolutely genius Chained Echos earlier this year but I couldn't bring myself to play more than 10 hours of this game. While Sea of Stars has a lot going for it on the surface it ultimately falls short of delivering a good experience for at least regular RPG enthusiasts. I guess I should start with the obvious strong points: The game looks absolutely stunning, not only are the graphics beautifully handcrafted, also the creature design and sheer number of small details that this game offers are a sight to behold. The music is still pretty good and gives you that nice retro gaming vibes of the 90s JRPGs which we all love. Sadly that's about it. In all my 10 hours of playing the characters did not gain and depth the writing was rather stale and seemed uninspired. The story was flat and there was nothing which amounted to much to keep me playing. That said I did stop before the first major encounter that apparently the game was building up to. Which brings me to the absolute weak point of the game. Because not only does Sea of Stars fail to hook the gamer early on with interesting characters or a compelling story to keep playing the core gameplay loop is boring and repetitive.

The battle system is one dimensional and rudimentary at best. The various skills of the characters of which I had six in total at my disposal in the first 12 hours do not offer any synergy of strategic depth. Every battle is the same and over fairly quickly. The combo system, which on the surface might have been a nice addition doesn't really play into the rest of the battle system either since the battles are usually over before the meter is filled. Everything feels like there had been some good ideas somewhere, but nothing really connects and due to the little variety, the enjoyment of the battles subsided within the first 2 hours of the game. The 8 hours left I was just grinding along to see if there was more interesting stuff coming down the line to justify my time. Sadly, the lackluster battle system ultimately wore me down, maybe before the game might get better in writing, story and who knows even battle system. But from what I have gathered online maybe complaints about the game are analogues to mine, leaving me bummed out.

The game could have been so much more. The battle system if it had only been interesting could've elevated this to a beautiful game with lots going for it. But from my perspective it is sadly only nice to look at and nothing more. I really wish I could like this game, but it left me feel utterly disappointed.
