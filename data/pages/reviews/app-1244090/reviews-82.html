Sea of Stars

Content and Replay Value
\t
Sea of Stars is a turn-based RPG. With that you understand what you’re getting gameplay wise. What it does differently with it isn’t much BUT it does an amazing job of paying homage to the inspirations they draw from (i.e. Super Mario RPG, Chrono Trigger and Illusion of Gaia).
\t
Should you get it?
\t
I believe Sea of Stars has two selling points. The strongest one being a well-polished 16-bit style turn-based RPG. That alone feeds into a community that chomps at the bit for a good addition to the storied genre. The second is continuation on the sleeper hit “The Messenger”. This game does an amazing job in developing an interesting world within that universe. If either of those factors appease you, it’s an easy get.
\t
Overall Verdict
\t
Sea of Stars is a great game that takes pieces from its past to form something unique and beautifully vibrant and appealing to the eyes. It has enough intrigue to keep you wanting to know more, and for those who played The Messenger, interesting history and world building aspects that allows studio Sabotage to construct narratives in this world. 8/10

Setting & Writing:
Sea of Stars takes place in what’s just called “The World”. Within the world you play as the two solstice warriors Zale and Valere who have the power of Eclipse Magic. This magic allows them to fight back the major forces of evil called Dwellers. Dwellers are the minions of the Fleshmancer, an alchemist who has achieved pseudo-godhood. Through the power of Eclipse magic the Solstice Warriors fend off the Fleshmancer’s minions in an attempt to keep balance within the world. This is supposed to be the way it all works. Within the history of the World, the Solstice Warriors are on their backfoot. If they cannot fight back the Dwellers, they transform into a World Eater (self-explanatory). Zale and Valere are a rare case within the world where the Great Eagle has delivered Solstice Warriors twice in a row. Through rigorous training and mentorship, they are prepped to fight off the last surviving Dweller until the unexpected happens.
The overall writing and script in Sea of Stars can come off a bit cliché. Although, once it traps you in that thought it breaks free with twist that sometimes I didn’t see coming. This was a strength in Sea of Stars, its’ implementations of successful twists that compel you to want to keep playing. Even though some of the characters didn’t hit for me, what did hit was the in-game historian Teaks. Throughout your journey you find artifacts and trinkets that give you insight into the world. Is this type of mechanic new? No, but in a vibrant setting it continued to make it convincing to continue giving it my time. The history gives you an idea of the grand scheme of all that’s happening around you. It works well as a supplement to the characters you meet and the places that you go.

Gameplay system & Bosses:
Sabotage does a good job of making Sea of Stars feel tough all the way through. Not once did I feel invincible like you do in some RPGs. What you lack in raw power is made up in one of their gameplay gimmick called locks. When battles start, your enemies have timers. Every so often above these timers are locks which are in the form of various damage types your party is able to cast or deal. The goal is to ‘break’ these locks to prolong your turn (if you break all the locks) or lessen the damage of the next incoming attack of that enemy. Once you’ve grasped the idea of how this works, it’s pushed to the next level in boss fights. The intricate lock combinations presented by bosses forces you to think about your line up and forces you to utilize all team members. Luckily in Sea of Stars once you push past the initial three-party members, you’re able to swap them within battle without using a turn. This adds some aid to the player when breaking locks. One of the systems Sabotage studios took inspiration from within combat is the use of team-up attacks. The team up attacks can be used once a meter is built up by attacking, or further increased by successful timed attacks to do more damage and timed blocks to prevent a percentage of damage. Once initiated these team up attacks can have added effects on to the massive damage they do. They can even hasten lock breaking. There are toggleable gameplay options as well that can enhance your experience (in the form of aid or restrictions if you desire more of a challenge) called relics. Overall Sea of Stars does enough to make you think about all your battle decisions which in turn keeps you engaged as a player.

Quest, Mini-games & Challenges:
Sea of Stars starts off linear, driving you from point to point. You will never get lost on finding your objective and that’s good. This game gives you some freedom eventually, but its main goal is to push you through their story. Along the way there are some minigames that allow you to take your mind off the growing threat and side missions that help give you useful, but not game breaking, items. While that’s all well, the main driving factor in this game is the main quest to save the world. Everything else is just icing but doesn’t do much in developing places and people outside your party. Off the top of my head the only side missions worth meaning were two of the end game side missions. That isn’t a knock though! Sabotage studios achieve their goal in keeping all the important bits in the main quest while also feeding the player any world building and info through the collection of artifacts with story by Teaks.

Miscellaneous (art, music, etc.):
Sabotage studios leans into a beautiful vibrant 16-bit art style to show off their product. Maps and areas do a beautiful job of telling background stories. Whether it’s the magical town Mooncradle where our story begins or the cursed town of Lucent where the Solstice Warriors are tasked with facing their first Dweller. Sabotage further leans into the retro style by having a well composed soundtrack that harkens back the days of old-school SNES RPGs. These tracks enrich the mood of the places your party visit.

The over-world is so convenient to explore. You basically never stop opening shortcuts through the less populated areas, and as someone who loves collecting everything I can in a game, it feels so courteous to the player and their time. This isn't even mentioning how thoroughly detailed every inch of those over-worlds are. It sounds small at first, but being able to climb and hop adds an unreal level of dimension to each location. I never stop combing every inch of a room until I know I got everything I could. By the time you reach the endgame, you'll have built this laundry list of things you've been staring at all game on the world map wondering "What IS that thing?" Once you make it there, oh man, it's a state of euphoria that only games like these can give you.

The battles are consistently engaging thanks to the timing for more effective attacks & blocking on top of the combo moves you get between each party member. The game's systems encourage you to never spam the same attacks over and over again. There's a beautiful loop where you'll never default to that mindless state of spamming your strongest move until you win. I also love that basic attacks refill your MP. I tend to have the opposite problem where I never want to use my MP because I'll have to use an item to refill it. I always freak out and worry that the one time I get too comfortable I'll hit a boss where I need that precious resource to make it. Sea of Stars makes it such that you never need to have that worry because, worst-case scenario, you can spend a couple turns doing basic attacks and you'll be able to use whichever spell you need to. This isn't even crediting the game for how common save-points/camps are, which is very much appreciated.

I'll admit, I did end up letting some of the objectives in the game's plot pass me by. I greatly enjoyed the characters and the game's sense of humor, but I swear the list of MacGuffins you'll have accrued by the end will make you scratch your head trying to remember what on Earth it was for. The thing is, even then, the game knows how to use its characters to evoke strong emotional beats off exactly as intended. I can barely even call my lack of investment in the plot (i.e. the things the characters are doing and why) a fault of the game because the story of a few friends setting off on an adventure to save the world always works, it just does. The number of creative ideas on display in the game's story is something best left for one to discover on their own, I dare not spoil the many surprises here.

Sea of Stars is full of surprises. It may take inspiration from the RPGs of old, but the careful way Sabotage Studio curated and blended those mechanical inspirations together ends up giving you something entirely new; something unlike any other RPG I've played before. I hope this game sells well and is talked about in the same vein as the RPGs that inspired it. I would love to play another game like this one, and after playing it, I would love to find another excuse to give Sabotage Studio my money and support.\t\t\t\t\t

Great experience. I loved the art, the story, and even the combat eventually grew on me. It is kind of simplistic, and it gets annoying sometimes that you cannot break some locks, but the overall feel is solid, and the class fantasy is there. I love exploring and 100%ing games, and this one scratched that itch perfectly with the true ending and all the side stuff collecting, ability unlocking, and gear improving. Although it did feel quite linear at times, and you are locked by something almost the whole game, the true exploring is best left just before the final boss. The story was quite nice, and a few surprising plot twists kept it fresh all the way to the end, which is quite rare. There was not much of a character growth, but they did grow on me, and I liked every single one of my crew which is also rare. It would be a solid 10/10, but there are some annoying bugs here and there, and some of the achievements require a tad bit more effort because of them – looking at you cook “all” recipes at least 1,000,000 times.\t\t\t\t\t

- Insanely good sound design

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'll caveat my recommendation by saying that you'd be very well served by getting the bundle with The Messenger and playing this along with that.

Beyond the story itself, the writing for the main characters are perhaps my singular major qualm. There's a issue writers have with 1.) putting characters they are making in danger and 2.) making their characters have problems. This doesn't have to be debilitating, or chronic, but real people are not constantly a single way. Put another way, just because every single character in the game is -saying- that they just gee gosh really love this one singular guy does NOT make us as players or as audience members actually like the guy.

When it comes down to the characters very specifically, they pretty much unilaterally have one-dimensional approaches to life. If you're going to name your main characters and have them speak, you should really give them some more personality. This sort of reaches into basically ALL the characters too though.

So yeah, there's a massive mary sue problem at play here. A mean, not only in the colloquial sense, but I mean the literal definition of what a mary sue IS shows up here and it's a drag on every thing else.

The -Story- (capital S story) is fine. Structurally, it's taking you places and you're doing things. Just, again, not entirely super compelling.

Music is great, no complaints. Better near the end, very good near the very very end.

Mechanically, they have some of the more annoying tropes that show up, such as not getting major traversal abilities unlocked until the end of the game, but that's slightly more of a consequence of the main problem: Pacing.

I mean, this game is big for the studio size. I legitimately was hoping to get to the end of it already by the time I was nearing it. It's like having taken just a bit too much on one plate and getting overfull. The ironic part here is that it's NOT due to the nature of the content itself, but rather the approach to how that content is laid out. If the framing mechanism operated a little differently, I could have imagined fitting in a good chunk more into the game comfortably.

It's an imbalance that's happening here. There's a LOT of time spent with our mary sue, but there's sort of a lack of tethering other characters and major story events to the world at large. Imagine having a lot of scenes that are the emotional or functional equivalent to FFX's 'laughing' scene and you get what I mean. As a creator, you should really be limited the unnecessary filler emotional bonding scenes in favor of the more pivotal story beats that break characters down a little. If you can't do that, we end up with whole lot of 'uplifting' scenes with kind of no reason to exist.

So yeah. I would say to the company: Get yourself another writer, preferably one that is capable of breaking characters down a little more. What you've got isn't bad, but what you're missing is making this harder to go through than you'd expect.

Side tangent: Don't split stories between games, even if they are shared in the same universe. Any given piece of media needs to be able to stand completely on its own.\t\t\t\t\t
