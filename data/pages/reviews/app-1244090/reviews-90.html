goes to show you how little you can "rely" on reviewers and influencers...the masses are rarely right.

yes, the game looks great
yes, the OST is brilliant, even if you don't like the particular tracks: the production quality and mixing is wonderfull

but that's about where the ups end.

there's little in the form of character- or progression-mechanics. this is one of the best examples why i think complexety is a must have for every rpg, beeing it jrpg, crpg or whateverthef*ckrpg. you level up and can chose between 4 different additional boni (next to your stats automaticaly increasing), then there's a tiny and i do mean tiny equipment system...and that's about all there is. you get "relics", but frankly most of em are downright cheats-enabling and that's kinda how they are implemented: accessability options for people who just want to go through the pretty enviroments.
rpgs need some mechanics to play around with, the more the better. heck even souls-likes need some sort of character building, min/maxing, etc. . what Sea of Stars offers is just to little to keep you from getting bored.

then there's the combat that got so much praise. i find it horrid. imagine playing a game like Chronotrigger, but ever mob, every encounter, every action needs you to Sekiro-parry...and i'm not beeing dramatic. then there's enemies having cast-turns, the only way to break these turns is by using specific attacks, kinda like a QTE, especialy when you think about the timed button-presses for attacks. the game hints early on that all of this is optional, which is utter nonesene. unless you are using the accessability options all these Sekiro-mechanics are mandatory. this grew old very fast.
enemies also only respawn on loading a save file or completely leaving an area (loading screen). which makes grinding just annyoin. there's a rest mechanic (camp), which probably should just respawn mobs..but again: the game is mechanicaly so bereft of depth that it doesn't realy matter. it's a QTE-combat system...and i'm realy not a fan.

the characters and story are okay. these elements are always subjective, but i found em enjoy- and servicable.

but the most important thing about a game is the actual gameplay...and this is sadly where Sea of Stars completely falls apart for me personaly and why i cannot recommend this game unless you want to hit the "cheats" and just run through the game once.

in a day and age with games like Dragon Quest 11 or Tales of Arise most of these gimmicky 2D-nostalgia clones just forget to put in interesting mechanics and character building...which is just sad.

a real progression, min/max or character building system and a more classical core-combat gameplay and this game could've been a banger; but it is definitly not.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSea of Stars tells a beautiful story of personal development, in the form of an RPG, with mechanics that are a mix of Final Fantasy, and Legend of Dragoon, with the ability to time attacks, defence, and abilities for bonuses and extra affects, amongst a couple of extra mechanics to flesh out gameplay, which I shall not spoil.

There are some reviews that are upset about the loose ends at the end of the story, but those loose ends are the point. This is a story about life, first and foremost, in a high fantasy setting, *life is like that*, and there is some hidden stuff at the very end of the game that you can spend just a few extra minutes to actually take the time to listen to, which says as much.

To be quite frank with you, I hate games that take all of my interpretive agency away from me. They're fun stories, but they're not great stories. Great stories are both fun, and leave you with something to think about at the end. They stick with you and let you ruminate about the deeper meaning behind the whole story, the reasons reasons certain design elements exist, certain decisions were made with the story, why the characters act the ways they did, until you finally figure it all out, and the lessons the story actually taught you ring clear. You don't grow as a person if you're not given the room to think for yourself, and that was most certainly the teams aim here.

I applaud the whole development team, for not only making a fun game that had me hooked from start to finish, but for being brave enough to tell the story they set out to tell, without sugar coating the ending - the ending is almost perfect. I hope, with all my heart, that they are willing to lean into their emotions more next time, if they ever decide to produce another game. To have the courage to make a Magnum Opus, instead of a Swan Song. 9 out of 10!, I would change nothing about this story, except to lean a little heavier into the subtext, and each character's emotional drive, so that you don't have to leave an ending 'Easter Egg' that spells it out for people. But I appreciate it all the same, it's perfect in it's own right, it doesn't get too heavy, it says what needs to be said, and it's clarity is motivating.

I don't know you, but I love you, you beautiful bastard!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA great tribute to old school RPGs. The artwork across the entire game is the best I've ever seen. Visually stunning, tight animations, diverse backgrounds. Musics fits well throughout the whole game although I wish the battle music would stop cutting in on the BGM during certain areas of the game.

The battle system is very unique and requires you to put a lot of thought ahead of time to make sure you have mana, and that the characters you need have their turn available to break the locks, like solving a puzzle. The death mechanic is also done well in my opinion, but feels too easy since they revive with half their health. Timing hits and blocks is rewarding and doesn't feel like a chore or a waste of time doing it the entire game since there are a ton of enemy timing mix-ups.

Characters and story are very basic and an extremely weak part of the game. I was certain so many characters would show up again later to develop more on their story but they're just never seen again.

The worst part of the game for me was the linearity of the entire game. At pretty much no point in the game did I feel like I had options on different paths to explore or take besides going to a lake to play a fishing minigame before I continued on with the main quest. Even after you get the boat every island you can travel to is basically blocked in some way until you continue with the main story. And the dungeons are especially bad. There are so many missed opportunities to make cool dungeons that were complex with puzzles that weren't just for 5 year olds (there's even a picture puzzle during the game where you have to swap the pieces but you can just see the borders of the 8 outer pieces). The game even has some of these too near the end of the game with the solstice shrines which introduce new puzzle mechanics (why were none of these in any of the dungeons earlier on???).

Game is way too easy even without using any relics. I do like the system of having a limit of how much food you can bring with you at a time though.

Equips were implemented terribly. Every equip you "found" (99% of the time it's just in a chest on your way through a linear dungeon or area) would always be an upgrade and besides the two main characters sharing equips, every equip was unique to a certain character so there would never have be a thought. Just equip and go.

The non-true ending to the game is also absolutely terrible, but if you want the true ending then you have to spend a bunch of extra time doing monotonous backtracking to finish all the tasks so that you can redo the final fight with the true ending.

Overall there are still a lot of things to appreciate about the game. Do not let my problems with the game deter you from buying it if you think it looks interesting. Chances are you'll still enjoy it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSea of Stars is simply a joy of a game to play. From the moment you set off on your quest you are captivated by the the storyline and the sentimental throwback to 16 bit RPG games of old. I'm in my fifties and was immediately transported back to those classic games I enjoyed and played as a student on the SNES and PC. The developers have clearly done their homework and taken the best elements of games such as the early Final Fantasy Series, Chrono Trigger and even Zelda (a link to the past) which although did not feature turn based combat has clearly influenced some of the interactive gameplay, puzzles and almost pefect presentation. I binged it from start to finish, with no interruptions from other games for close to a month. The graphics and animations are on point and whilst they push the 16 bit credibility, it would take a seasoned developer to know where they have gone beyond the capabilities of those machines. Then there is the soundtrack. Kudos to the developers for onboarding some of the creators of those earlier games to ensure you are distinctly aware of the mood of the moment whether it be exploring the diverse array of environments, playing fiddle to the emotional feelings of the storyline or the gravity of the boss battles and charcter arc progressions. At the end of the game, I was left with a true sense of appreciation toward the sheer amount of effort the developers had put into recreating some of my favourite gaming experiences. I therefore highly recommend the title to anyone that has either not experienced the creative innocence and fun that these older titles delivered or those like myself that mourn their almost complete disappearance from the current gaming scene.

With such words and respect, you might expect that Sea of Stars steams it's way into one of my top turn based RPGs of all time, however it's not perfect and misses that mark by the slightest of margins but in several distinct respects that I think the developers could easily correct with some minor tweaks;

1: NPC behaviour and sidequests
Encounters with NPCs throughout the game (and there are many) offer dull dialogue and don't really add much to the story although you feel obliged to endure them all in the search of side quests. When you do find sidequests, there is no intuitive way that I found of tracking them, monitoring your progress or tracing them back to the NPC from which they originated to collect your rewards. The map is too simple in this respect, focussing on where you need to go to progress the story and lacking the detail to give you sufficient clues as to where you need to return to. The opportunity to mop up sidequests only reveals itself late on in the game by which time the rewards on offer do not enhance your charcters ability beyond those you've already achieved on your journey and simply provide you with items to sell.

2: Combat challenge
At no point in the game did I feel that I was in jeopardy and could lose a battle. The fact that it was only late in the game that I worked out the monsters were telling me the actions I needed to perform to disrup their magic attacks (so I never used them) and at a relatively early stage of the game my lead character received an overpowering amulet that reduced battles to a repetitive formula that always resuted in victory. Restoring character stats at the end of every battle, the ability to swap new characters in/out of boss battles with complete stats and a larder full of replenishing food saw me complete the entire game without losing a single fight, reloading a previous state or needing to revisit my boss tactics. The original games of the genre were filled with frustration, dying numerous times before you hit the right blend of characters and tactics to take down the enemy.

For these reasons I felt more like a sentimental tourist taking a wonderful journey through a genre of game that I thought I would never see again but would only really revisit by replaying the original games (or their modern reincarnations) themselves. Maybe I missed something but I can't see myself replaying Sea of Stars yet I have replayed Zelda3, CT and FF3-7 numerous times and got something new from them with every return visit.

As I said, I think this is something the developers could easily correct with a patch or two so here would be my tips;

1: Do not reset player stats after every wandering monster battle to encourage them to manage their food and character contributions continuously.
2: Restrict players to select three characters ahead of each boss fight to align character skills with those needed to take the boss down
3: Encourage and balance the pursuit of side quests throughout the game, improve the map to make them easier to track and deliver rewards needed to progress further in the game

The above changes would transform the game from a sentimental celebration of games gone past to a true contender that I would happily revisit howvever I can't deny the pleasure the game gave me and therefore am happy to give it a solid, smiling, solstice warrior shining 8/10.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tToo long didn't read version: This Chrono Trigger/Mario RPG game is nearly flawless. Buy it. Buy it now.

Long version: Lately I've noticed my nostalgia has been keeping me from playing triple A titles that cost $80+ and have ad campaigns that really hype up the game. More often then not these triple A games aren't worth the price, they don't keep my interest long enough to finish the game, or they end up being forgettable in the end.

This game checks every box that I expect from a game.
Just looking at screenshots or the trailers can give you a rough idea what to expect. If you clicked on this game and have an interest in RPGs such as Chrono Trigger or any of the Mario RPGs then this game is going to blow your heckin mind.

Pros:
- Graphics. It looks gorgeous. I haven't played a game with this style of graphics that doesn't use the same assets over and over again in a long time. Every new area has a fresh look to it. Never looking like the area before it.

- Music/Sound design. My heart bleeds for this soundtrack. I regret not buying the OST at the start. I thought after finishing the game that I could find the OST on my spotify, but was sorely mistaken. There is so much soul and emotion to the soundtrack. You can tell the sound design team is very passionate with how music and sound effects should line up with scenes.

- Story. It isn't the end all be all of stories, but it captivated me until the end. Many twists that had me gasping or just going "OHH DANG" so there is something here to enjoy. I might not be giving the story enough credit, but it's good. REALLY good.

- Gameplay. It's not difficult, but I did die a few times. You can make the game difficult with special items you can find along the journey that add challenge and also award the player so that's good for the "masochists" out there. I thought the difficulty was welcoming. Dying isn't fun all the time. If you have played games like this before then you likely will experience it how I did. If you're more casual with games then it might be the perfect kind of difficulty for you. Again dying is not fun. In some games it can be a learning experience (Souls like games), but in this one it just feels good to defeat enemies or bosses with ease.

- Length: It says on steam it took me about 70+ hours to beat it. I did almost everything in this game. I also might have left the game running idol for a few hours so I would say this is roughly a 50-60 hour game if you want to 100% it. Depending how much back tracking you might have to do if you weren't thoroughly looking around for treasure chests from the start of the game.

NOW THE CONS!!!!
- Backtracking: Okay I'm ranting now so be prepared. ALSO SPOILER WARNING!!!! Gunna try keeping that on the down low but just... be careful reading this



----------------------SPOILER RANT START-------------------------
So you beat the game. Then you find out it says "The End?" on the final screen. So obviously you need to do end game stuff. And you do. Because this stuff is HEAVY STORY stuff. Not just like achievement hunting stuff , but story that really should not be locked behind the following requirements.

You are assigned to do tasks for the party members. Each character has a specific task to complete before you can get the TRUE ENDING so it becomes required. Some of this stuff you can do before beating the final boss the first time, but after you beat the game you must reload to the last save and do some tasks that can't be accessed yet till the end. So most of them are easy enough to do. But there is one notorious task that kept me from considering this game to be perfect.

You must collect all 60 hidden Rainbow Conch items

I had to look up a guide for this. I am so upset that STORY BEATS WERE LOCKED BEHIND A WALL BECAUSE OF THIS TASK. If it was just for a super weapon then okay, but it is required to see the BEST ENDING and that rubs me the wrong way. I cannot believe this idea was considered by the team that made this game. Maybe I just suck at exploration, but I cannot say this game is perfect simply from this flaw.

------------------SPOILER RANT OVER---------------------

- Conclusion: I love this game. Easily my favourite game of 2023. I am surprised how well it turned out and the impression it left with me. I love games that I can think about almost every day. Whether it was from story beats or from listening to the soundtrack, a game that keeps you from forgetting about it in a positive light is a very good game. A lasting impression is important, and I believe this game is going to stay in my heart and memories forever.

Buy this game.
Buy it now.\t\t\t\t\t
