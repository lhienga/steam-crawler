\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe timed attacks kill the fun for me entirely. The animations are definitely designed to mislead you. Your characters charge to the enemy then stop. Then wait. Then attack. This isn't engaging, it just slows combat down.

For people who loved Super Mario RPG, it might be your cup of tea. If aren't a fan of "press x to make your attack work properly in a misleading animation" then this might be one to pass.

The fundamental problem with this type of QTE combat in turn-based RPGs is that it believes that turn-based combat can't be made interesting, so instead it tries to distract you from the combat with button presses that add no depth, and simply demand extensive memorization. A better game would iterate on its combat system and high level design to shave down unnecessary fights, make the combat system itself more engaging, or streamline the combats to at least make uninteresting ones quick. A strong turn based RPG will deploy a mix of all of these approaches. Sea of Stars only minimally attempts the reduced combats as compared to most SNES RPGs with endless random encounters.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLike the developers, I too have magical memories of playing Chrono Trigger. Renting it at my friend's house and going back again and again. I was mesmerized. You can feel the love that they poured into this gem. Wonderfully modernized. Beautiful, magical.
Chock full of YES!!! moments.
Battle system is perfectly balanced. Every single battle is fun. I never find myself feeling tired of it. Most other jrpgs have me rolling my eyes from the repetition, and randomness, but not here. Canceling an enemy move is super satisfying.
Music is awesome. I love all the battle music, and the pirate pub renditions are amazing.
Visuals are a real treat. Gorgeous hand drawn art. Very tasteful cg supplements. Super impressive animations.
Brisk is my favorite place in the game. I return regularly. So beautiful.
Wheels is SUPER FUN. I always turn it up; the little mechanical sound effects are adorable.
One quirk. I played most of SoS with an og SNES controller, and 95% of the time it's perfect. There are just a couple button mapping issues, which you can really modify in settings. I'm not even sure how they could address this but I thought I'd mention it. Since the game is such a love song to the 16 bit era, compatibility with old controllers would be sweet. Maybe Boost and Game Menu could be combined.
I love the story and all the characters. Very engaging, well written, poignant, funny, clever.
Played this game for over 50 hours, being pretty thorough, when I beat the last boss. Enjoyed every minute. The credits roll, and they let me continue playing to get all the rest of the end game stuff. 65 hours total now, nearly 100% completion, and I can wholeheartedly say, DO IT. Get every one of those conches. This game is a 10/10. You knocked it out of the park.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tExcuse me as I GUSH! I did not expect to love this game this much. Truly scratched an itch I did not know I had.

In this review, I will delve into the game's exquisite pixel art, expansive world, rich storytelling, unique playstyle, flexible difficulty modes, and its roster of lovable characters.

Stunning Pixel Art:
One of the first things that will captivate you when you enter the world of Sea of Stars is its pixel art. It's nothing short of a masterpiece. The attention to detail in every environment, character, and spell effect is simply awe-inspiring. The game effortlessly combines nostalgia with modern design, making it a visual treat for gamers of all ages.

Expansive World:
The vast world of Sea of Stars feels like a living, breathing universe. As you embark on your journey, you'll traverse lush forests, dive into mysterious underwater caves, and explore mystical temples. The sheer scale of the world is mind-boggling, and it's a testament to the developers' dedication to creating an immersive experience.

Rich Storytelling:
The narrative of Sea of Stars is a true gem. Filled with magic, ancient prophecies, and a touch of humor, the story is engaging from start to finish. It's a classic tale of two young heroes tasked with saving the world, but the execution is what sets it apart. The lore and history of the world are intricately woven into the gameplay, enriching your sense of purpose and connection to the game's universe.

Unique Playstyle:
Sea of Stars doesn't shy away from innovation. The game seamlessly blends turn-based combat with real-time elements, offering a unique and highly enjoyable battle system. The combination of light-based and shadow-based abilities adds depth and strategy to the gameplay, making each encounter an exciting challenge.

Flexible Difficulty Modes:
Whether you're a seasoned RPG veteran or a newcomer to the genre, Sea of Stars has you covered. The game features flexible difficulty modes, allowing players to tailor their experience to their skill level. You can ramp up the challenge for a more hardcore experience or scale it down for a more casual, story-focused playthrough.

Lovable Characters:
The heart of any great RPG lies in its characters, and Sea of Stars delivers in this department. The protagonists, Valere and Zale, are instantly endearing, and their budding friendship is heartwarming to witness. Along your journey, you'll meet a diverse cast of characters, each with their own distinct personalities and backgrounds. Their development and interactions enrich the overall experience and make you genuinely care about their fates.

In conclusion, Sea of Stars is a captivating RPG that deserves a spot in every gamer's library. It's a shining example of the magic that can be created within the confines of a video game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI wrote a long, unedited, spoiler-filled rant to a friend of mine and copy posted it on the forum. If you've already played the game and want to know the gritty details of why I'm giving this a thumbs down, then check it out here:
https://steamcommunity.com/app/1244090/discussions/0/3877093932373922940/


The spoiler free version of my rant is that I went into Sea of Stars coming from The Messenger, and my high expectations were disappointed.

The production values, especially the music, are great.

The combat, while really tightly designed, lacks variety. This starts to hurt as you approach the mid point of the game.

Most disappointing though is that the story is trite and full of narrative shortcuts. The potential seeps from the wound this creates like pus, and it's unceremoniously wiped away and discarded.

It's an entertaining game, and usually I would give a thumbs up for that, but it fails the standard that Sabotage set up for themselves with their previous game. With a bit more planning, this could have stood tall with the classics that it clearly draws inspiration from. Sadly it ended up being yet another derivative indie game, and as a result no amount of production value is going to prevent it from fading into the void of the forgotten.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWith 5 hours into Starfield and a dozen or so into Baldur's Gate 3, I stopped playing both of those games to play Sea of Stars, if that says anything about how much I liked it.

I haven't quite beaten it yet, but I'm close, currently avoiding the final area and going back to get all the things I missed**, but otherwise it's mostly finished.

TL;DR - I enjoyed it a lot.

The pixel art is vibrant and upbeat, though the character portraits and faces in general seemed a little jank. Monster art and overall animation of everything is great.

The story is interesting and takes a few fun twists. Some other reviews have commented that the two main protagonists are bland and might as well be silent self-inserts - almost like they forgot that's exactly what Crono was.... it was choice, and you can choose to like it or not, but I don't think it was an unintentional mistake by the dev team to characterize the protags the way they did.

Biggest flaw in this game is the balancing of combos in the battle system. For your party members to perform combo attacks, combo points are needed. As you use combos, your character's ultimate attack gauge (limit break) fills, and combos also help you "break" enemies' attacks by hitting them with particular different damage types in the same turn. That sounds really cool, and it could be, but here's the rub. The rate at which your combo gauge raises is so slow that one of two things happen. Either, one, you can't get enough combo points in time to break an enemy, or two, by the time you've solo-attacked enough to earn any combo gauge, the enemy is already dead. This could have been a great system, but it goes almost totally unused other than boss fights. I get it, they are powerful attacks, and should be earned to some extent, but I'd like to use them on more than just bosses...

I never played much of The Messenger, but I might go back to that one soon - from what limited amount I played a while back the controls were tight.

Anyway, good game - Sea of Stars earns a solid "$34.99"*

* I rate games by how much I would pay for them (even if I spent less)
** Didn't use a guide until I got to the end, then used a guide to find some cool stuff I missed so I wouldn't have to play the game all over again for a few 'chievos.\t\t\t\t\t
