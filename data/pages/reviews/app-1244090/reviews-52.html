\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game asks a lot for $31-35, and it does not go above and beyond what other indie games offer for $20 or less. I would recommend against it for that reason alone.

The graphics are fantastic; just looking at the preview media tells you all you need to know in that regard. There is a wide variety of environments, and the animations of both characters and enemies are top notch. Unfortunately, the rest of the game does not hold up to the visuals.

Combat is alright; well thought out, but limited in challenge. It's fairly simple, there are no status effects for example, only some abilities that push back enemy turns. Between this and breaking weaknesses causing the enemy to lose its turn entirely, fighting bosses tends to be less dangerous than normal enemies, and when they do actually get a turn it is very unlikely they can wipe your party unless you haven't been healing. The "tap button to continue attacking" abilities like the boomerang are necessary for breaking in some situations, but personally I felt like I was wasting time bouncing 20+ times and preferred to use other abilities for most of the game.

The music is, surprisingly, a mixed bag. The contributions by Yasunori Mitsuda are solid, but that accounts for only ~10 pieces of ~200 (including the 20 second game over track...) The rest of the score by Eric W. Brown and others has some equally solid pieces, my personal favorites being the regular and boss battles, and most of the Docarri/water related music. In other cases, the texture of some instruments does not mesh well with the rest of the piece, or the disparity in quality between tracks is just too much (switching from sailing to flying always kills me).

Exploration is painful, especially backtracking, because aggressive enemies will always chase you even if you're at the endgame looking for chests in one of the first zones of the game. You cannot escape from battles, so you waste less time going in aggressively instead of trying to walk around them and getting caught anyway. There are collectibles called conches which, when turned in, grant various items of mediocre to decent usefulness. You meet the NPC who takes them about halfway through the game, but if you want to turn them in anytime after leaving that area (until endgame) you have to take a fairly long walk through a previous zone - both ways. There's also only tiny hints on what each item is ("something useful", "something shrewd"), and most of the later ones are very disappointing.

The characterization and story is the weakest part of the game by far. (minor, non-specific spoilers for the whole game) Zalere and Vale have basically zero personality; Garl carries it for a few hours, but eventually his outbursts and interruptions get more annoying than endearing, and there are more characters written in this way; so committed to their gimmick that they get worn out. There is at least one part in the game where characters do something drastic that makes absolutely no sense other than to keep the plot going. There is also way too much foreshadowing; throughout the first 2/3 of the game, about as much of the story that you/the characters find out for themselves is hinted or outright told to them. After that point came an actual surprise, which was probably my favorite part of the game - the setting shift and details like the music were really well done. However, this did not extend to the ending.

Overall, I would recommend waiting for a sale if you are really interested; it does not distinguish itself from similar games that are half the price, except in looks. It was OK to play through, but it was not a fulfilling experience.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHi everyone,

SoS is definetely my personal game of the year!

Short summary when I was recommeding the game for my brother:
- clever turn based combat
- really nice graphical art style (a little like a beautiful mix of Stardew Valley and Crosscode)
- very catchy soundtrack (again reminding me of Stardew Valley - in particular the track playing at the ponds)
- gameplay reminded me of Zelda (Link's Awakening) and Golden Sun (riddles in the game but most of the time less complex than in Golden Sun) - reminding me in a good way!

More details:
I love the graphics-style and the soundtrack. I found my mind "playing" some of the songs - most often the boss battle theme - inside of my head and then started listening to it on my phone while not playing (I really rarely do this with gaming soundtracks).

I liked the story with some really nice little twists (and bigger ones :D) and liked the characters, too. However it is a "light" story - not difficult to follow and I did not grow as attached to the characters as I did in some other games (such as Detroit become Human, to name one example). Character wise I would say that the two main characters are not the star of the show. ;-)

I wish wheels would be a real game (this is a minigame in the game that you can play at several locations).

And for the combat system, the addition of user actions/timings/button pressing during turn based combat is a real benefit drawing you more into it. I really liked the system in Vampires Dawn 2 (turn based combat and the speed of characters was displayed as a gauge - faster characters filled the gauge quicker and so could attack more often) I was hoping that this mechanic would return in a game as it made you having to decide fast even though it was turn based as in a real battle as the enemy is not waiting for your action. SoS is not giving you that feeling but lets you take part in the precision (timing) needed in combat in fun way.

The game was never too hard to beat and you can tailor the difficulty to your needs using artifacts. And if you liked collecting conches in Zelda - Link's Awakening - you will feel at home (without having ro dig through the whole world ;-) ). And this was not the only part reminding me of Zelda.

If you like the sound of my description then don't miss this gem. :-)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm not sure what is happening with indie pixel art games but I cannot recommend Sea of Stars, just like the earlier released Chained Echoes. It's crazy to me that both these games have the same fundamental flaws, did Sea of Stars not look at that game and try to learn something?

First, the story is just awful. The characters are incredibly one dimensional and show no depth or growth and instead stick to one archetype. Everyone is also so incredibly cheerful that it's obnoxious at times - I'm not against positivity but seriously the characters don't need to be written for a G audience. The story also is riddled with plot holes like it's trying to collect as many as it can for no reason other than to waste your time. The ending is terrible, it builds to this huge main villain the entire time and you don't even fight him.

The combat is also incredibly weak. It's fun for the first 2 hours, but the game shows its whole hand right away. There are like 1, maybe 2, new abilities per character for the *rest of the 20 hour game*. Some of the abilities feel like they were designed to waste your time as you have to keep clicking buttons to use them, and not in a fun way like paper mario.

The game's strengths are the art and music. Both of these were fantastic but really the game is nothing more than a shiny turd. The entire experience is a slog to get through and I really wouldn't recommend anyone buying it and wasting their time with a disappointing ending.

I'm not sure what's so difficult about hiring a writer for these kinds of games, both Chained Echoes and Sea of Stars had the exact same problem. At the very least, they need to hire an editor because the game is riddled with grammar issues that constantly took me out of the experience.

I would love it if they learned from this experience and hired an actual game designer for the gameplay and a writing staff for the story. The audio and video team did a fantastic job and don't need to change anything. We constantly rip on AAA studios that produce shallow games that are nothing more than graphics without any substance, why shouldn't we do the same here?\t\t\t\t\t
