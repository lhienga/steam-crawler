\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA great ode to classic JRPGs like Chrono Trigger and Super Mario RPG with enough fresh mechanics to keep it from feeling like a complete retread. Lots of variation between different areas throughout the game so you won't feel like you're fighting the same enemies over and over again.

Lots of negative reviewers here complaining about the main characters being generic seem to conveniently forget that many classic JRPG protagonists are wooden planks with 'I want to save the world' scribbled on a piece of paper stapled to where their face is supposed to be. If you, like this subset, expect this game to transport you back in time to when you were a kid playing your first JRPG ever, you will probably be disappointed by what Sea of Stars has to offer.

If you have more realistic expectations, you'll probably enjoy this game a lot.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tMaking your game like another much more famous game is always a perilous gamble: You're going up against industry professionals and trying to make the second time around with something just as good as the first time. Sea of Stars perfectly walks that line by taking just a little bit from its inspirations and then filling out the rest with its own world, and I highly recommend it.

Story: Original and full to the brim with personality. What I originally thought would be a puffed up, twee story that couldn't take itself seriously turned into an adventure that had me invested every step of the way. I genuinely love these characters– even the bad guys.

Graphics: Gorgeous. You don't need me to tell you, anyone with eyes can see it. If you really want to unlock the beauty of this pixel masterpiece, I recommend downloading ReShade and using the CRT Royale plugin for that authentic look.

Music: Banger after banger. I bought the soundtrack because there were TOO MANY good songs stuck playing in my head during and after my playthrough.

Gameplay: Moving around is standard JRPG fare, with lots of little improvements. The battle system, however, is where it shines: It's basically Chrono Trigger with timed hits, MP that recharges on melee and a number of additional layers that make every fight unique. Enemies cast devastating spells that you can interrupt by matching their 'locks' with the right types of damage, but the clock is ticking down every single character's actions, which is where you really get to the meat of what makes this game so satisfying to play moment to moment. By the end of the game, you won't need to make every timed hit, but you will need to have your roster's movesets memorized and be planning ahead to make sure they have enough MP to pull off those clutch lock breaks. There's even more to talk about but honestly it's just as fun to see unfurl in front of you.

Accessibility: I didn't look at any visual or audio accessibility elements, but I can say that on the difficulty front, they have plenty of very granular ways to make the game easier or harder. Personally I felt like the default difficulty was just about right.

In Conclusion... what can I say? It's a 10/10 for me and I'm not the kind of person who plays very many RPGs. You owe it to yourself to experience this game.\t\t\t\t\t

Charming and heartwarming take on JRPG genre

This game won't make you 7 years old again, but it can restore some of your youthful sense of awe and wonder, which is the next best thing.

A superbly crafted game, Sea of Stars manages to nail down what feels so comfortable and nostalgic about playing JRPG, and bring fresh ideas while paying tribute to the titans of the genre. If you've grown up with the classics of the golden era (Chrono Trigger from which it takes copious inspiration, Final Fantasy, Secret of Mana, Golden Sun...), you'll be right at home.

From outstanding and jaw-dropping art to engaging exploration and fighting gameplay, to a storyline that, after respecting the clichés of the genre (propheties! Heroes school! Evil sorcerer who wants to destroy the world!), decides to take on more risk and novelty, there's plenty to like with this title which is a love letter to the genre.



The real hits
(+) Phenomenal art direction, from stunning pixel-art to best-in class character animations. Each one has its own way to run, walk tight ropes, swim, climb, stay idle, and its own attack rhythm.
The anime cutscenes were surprisingly good and didn't feel out of place compared to normal gameplay.

The music is consistently cute, with a few killer tracks (Elite encounter, mountain path, assembly line, frozen peak, Garl's theme...). The pirate band playing in inns was an outstanding idea.

(+) Some great features for tactical combat such as boosting skills with live mana which allows even the boring Attack & Regen phases to pave the way for future attacks.
Timing attacks and blocks, while challenging at first, makes the game rewarding as more blocks = less damage = more mana return = more time to actually play the game.
If you're brave enough, you can activate relics that put even more emphasis on mastering the mechanics and make it impossible to brute force your way through.

Instant character swaps, along with combos and ultimates , make the late game much more enjoyable than the initial hours.

Oh, a whoever designed Moonerang needs a raise!

(+) Garl the Warrior Cook is a loveable, wholesome and memorable character. He's the best friend you ever had, compensating his lack of divine powers with sheer heart and enthusiasm, always facing adventure with a grin on his face, he is unsurprisingly everyone's favorite. One of the best designed support characters I've seen.
I also enjoyed having a resident historian, Teaks, tell stories around the bonfire with a soothing voice effect.

(+) Relics that can be triggered on and off are a creative and flexible way to fine-tune difficulty that go beyond simply multiplying HP and damage. Having the option to have insta regen after each fight turns the game into "story mode", while Artful Gambit and Double Edge drastically raise the stakes of respecting the mechanics.

(+) The movement set of climbing, walking on tight rope and along ledges, using the graplou is enticing and, combined with the good dungeon level design of puzzles, day / night cycles and shortcuts, makes the non-combat screens engaging.

(+) Streamlining gameplay solves many issues that traditionally plague the genre: very little time on inventory management, few consumables to hoard (there's no status to cure, and cooking is built-in in a flavoursome way), no endless interruptions of random encounters, no plethora of confusing and anti-fun status effects, can't charge up Ultimates on basic enemies in preparation for a boss, all characters stay the same level even if you have your preferred 3 etc.

While at first I was disappointed by the lack of depth the game offered, I later realized that this deliberate choice also makes it possible to avoid getting bogged down by these non-essential and traditionally frustrating considerations.



Mistakes I hope won't be repeated for upcoming DLC

(-) Hiding a true ending behind "Gather all hidden collectibles" is not only a game design cardinal sin, it showcases that the game is not built for backtracking. At some point, you're stuck following the croaking of an unhelpful parrot, wading through legions of screens you already completed, being interrupted with level 1 encounters. Horrible experience during these hours which felt like a chore.

(-) Limited combat depth. Apart from timing hits and blocks, there's not much you can do to "git gud" or outplay the enemy. While the Break system starts out promising, you'll realize that it throws combinations at you that cannot be countered in the available time, and the Boss spells are much more devastating than their regular attacks.

As a result, you'll find yourself relying more and more on the "Delay turn" abilities, and hoarding your mana to counter their casts, which promotes a reactive playstyle instead of what the devs seemed to want to encourage judging from their "how to play the game" tips = attacking at full mana is a waste of regeneration indeed, but casting a spell leaves you open to not having sufficient mana to counter the cast that will put your entire party at 1 HP.

Disappointing as the Lock system was a good idea, poorly executed.

(-) So much character building focused on Garl that Zale and Valere seem bland and interchangeable by comparison. Would have appreciated to see more character development as you pass some of the game milestones, and have the 2 main characters respond differently. For example, Zale could have felt more affected by the darkness within. After all, aren't they supposed to be as complementary as the Sun and the Moon?

(-) Limited window for customization, which is restricted by the low numbers of spells, scarcity of available equipment, and roulette type stats increase during level-up. Items, with a couple of exceptions, are direct level-ups, there's no real interest in swapping out equipment based on the situation.

(-) Wheels is a poorly designed minigame and whoever decided to make us play against an Assassin / Priest combination made me want to gouge my eyes out.\t\t\t\t\t

The music was good, as well as the visuals and there was enough progression with new abilities in and out of combat that always made you feel like you were progressing. For completionists, I don't think I could recommend this however as reaching the level cap is an absolute slog, even with the bonus EXP item that is in the game. If you don't care about level capping however then give it a try! The story was great and while I didn't agree with some choices, I could at the very least understand them, looking at it from the characters point of views. The true ending is a bit bitter sweet, and I'd like to head cannon a bit of a tweak to it honestly but over all I can't complain. I'd give the game a 8.9/9 out of 10!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI finished the game and, unfortunately, my initial review still holds true.

The game works best when you are exploring, searching for secrets, solving puzzles, fighting monsters, immersing yourself in the world and letting your imagination fill in the blanks. This is when it really reminds me of the JRPG games of the 90s.

The writing isn't all bad. I enjoyed the stories that Teaks tells. However, the dialogs ruined any kind of atmosphere that the game had going for it. This was the most frustrating part for me.

The images tell such an amazing story full of happiness, mystery, darkness, sadness, hope, friendship, betrayal. This game truly could have been a masterpiece.

Initial Review (14.8 hrs at review time)

The graphics are fantastic! There are some really neat visual effects. The music and sound design is good. I enjoy the combat mechanics and the puzzles. The gameplay is solid. I like the fantasy world and its lore. The story has all the trademarks of a good RPG!

What really bothers me, is that everything is written so bland. The characters look interesting, as if there might be something more to them, but there isn't. The enemies look intriguing at first, but then they seem nice and friendly. I know that there is conflict in the story because I can see it on the screen, but when all characters act so happy-go-lucky, or mildly inconvenienced by what is happening around them, I don't feel it. What's at stake here?

Things happen, characters deal with it. There are a few interesting bits here and there but they are resolved rather quickly and unceremoniously, instead of leaning into them, like a real adventure would. It just seems so inconsequential.

Maybe that's the point. Maybe the bad guys aren't the bad guys. Maybe what the good guys are doing is pointless. But if that's the case, why am I playing this game?

If you are looking for a light-hearted and easy game to fill some time, Sea of Stars does a fantastic job! Otherwise, you might be disappointed.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFor starters, yes this is a decent game. I can see why it won Indie Game of The Year and its certainly a game thats worth playing, but I wouldnt suggest it to someone at full price. While I had an ok time with the game there are some pretty fatal flaws I have with the game that stop me from giving it a thumbs up.

The Good

  • The pixel art is probably some of the best I have ever seen, games gorgeous
  • QOL: Backtracking for main story content has been good. Compared to older JRPG's where you have to walk 9000 miles one way just to walk all the way back, this game largely gives you quick trips back to the start of areas
  • The mix of active and turn based combat , while gimmicky, does spice up the gameplay. Its nothing amazing but its satisfying to learn every boss\\mob pattern and act on it for your benefit

The Bad

  • The story. I get the true end is the actual end, but the base ending is just not it and feels really lackluster. If your goal is to leave the base ending as open as possible in the hopes that people will finish the true ending after, it had the opposite effect on me.
  • Character progression in terms of the story is really quite bad. From the start of the game to the end of the game the cast doesnt really evolve. Sure, things are slightly different but by and large despite all the events in the game the cast feels very samey from beginning to end. I slide this in with story, the writing definitely needs work.
  • Character progression in terms of stats on level up is also pretty bad. You cant really build characters the way you want because the stats you are provided every level up are either random or inconsistent. Say you want to build a tanky unit and have lots of health: Well, you cant really do that because you cant guarantee a health upgrade choice for every level. Apply this to basically any other build or strategy you'd like to employ to create a specialized character for a roster. On top of this, at least in my playthrough, levels largely felt irrelevant due to the games difficulty. As such, it didnt really feel like the choices I made during my level ups or the levels themselves really mattered which feels super bad as a player.
  • The base difficulty of the game is really, really easy. Not just easy by JRPG standards, easy in general. I get there are modifiers to make the game harder for yourself but in my head the base experience is the developers intended gameplay experience and its honestly just too easy to feel rewarding. Given how characters auto revive after a certain number of turns its just simply hard to lose in general. Characters are also very powerful in general so you'll rarely feel backed into a corner even if someone manages to die.

Overall, I can see why it gets a large amount of praise. The game is reasonably short, looks fantastic and is easy enough for a majority to complete if they actively try (although less than 30% have completed the base game per steam achievements). Its certainly not a terrible game by any stretch, especially when you consider all the shovelware RPGMaker games that flood Steam weekly. However, given the issues I have with the games core experience (story, progression, and difficulty) I cant in good faith give it a positive review.

My verdict is this, if you like JRPG's, pick this up at a discount. It doesnt even need to be a massive discount, 30-40% or more and I think its worth a shot. Full price however for me is a no go.\t\t\t\t\t
