\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'll list the bad stuff first, because that list is much shorter.
Bad:
Dialogue portraits and cutscenes could have used a bit more polish. Compared to many other aspects of the game, these two seem to lack the same quality. It's a bit jarring given how beautiful the rest of the game is.

Music is repetitive. Tends to repeat and become stagnant. Could have used more tracks.

Stuttering/skipping frames commonly. Doesn't happen on the overworld or in battles, just in roaming gameplay. It could be my computer, but I find that hard to believe given other more intensive games run flawlessly.

Puzzles are rather easy. This could be an intentional design choice, but compared to the combat, the puzzles are incredibly simple. This is not a knock on their game design, just a personal gripe (grain of salt).

Timed button presses for a few attacks/moves are not well telegraphed. They include an item that is supposed to help with this; it only shows if you HIT the timing, which only seems to solve a problem that, by that point, no longer exists.

Good:
Spritework is beautiful.
Undoubtedly the best interactive turn based combat I've experienced.
Characters are interesting and have some depth.
The story is interesting and keeps you invested.
World design is beautiful and varied.
Large amount of content.
Boss designs are phenomenal.

TL;DR Conclusion:
9/10
Easily one of the best JRPG's I've played. I cannot stress enough how this might be one of the best games I own. See above reasons. However, the minor inconveniences and little details keep it from being a 10/10 in my book. If you are looking to buy this game: If you like Chrono Trigger, Final Fantasy 1-10, or Golden Sun, I can all but guarantee you will like this. Buy it. IF, however, those are not your style, I'd still recommend getting it on sale. I don't think you will be disappointed.

Recommendations to devs for future consideration:
-Increase quality of portraits and cutscenes, (really just the portraits will do). The artstyle is wonderful, but the cutscenes are choppy, and the portraits tend be a bit lopsided and derpy.
-Increase amount of tracks, or have variations of the same tracks (same melody, different pitch/instruments, etc.) and spread them out. Having a few different battle themes would be nice, just to spice up encounters.
-Maybe rework the artifact that helps with button timing. I had to look up how to do a few of the timed presses, because I had no idea when in the animation it expected me to press the button. Mending light comes to mind. Could have it blink your character red if too early, or blue if too late. Then it's a game of hot and cold until you find the timing.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSea of Stars is a masterpiece. For anyone who loved Squaresoft RPGs in the 1990s, this is the game you have been waiting for. The most obvious point of comparison is Chrono Trigger - from the world map design, to the combo-based battle system - but across the board, Sea of Stars is a love letter to the 16-bit era of RPGs.

However, it doesn't feel so retro in its design, making a wide range of updates and improvements to systems and control in ways that make it feel modern and fresh. Character movement is a joy, with intricate, layered environments that let you wall climb, grapple, shimmy across ledges, and find high points from which you can jump down through breakable surfaces, and all the while it feels quick and responsive. Sometimes when revisiting older JRPGs, I find that the movement feels somewhat stilted, but this game completely avoids that, contributing to a sense of freedom and freshness that encourages exploration. On top of all of that, the game has a variety of puzzles, often centered around navigating the intricate environments as well as making use of certain abilities, all of which are well designed and provide an extra layer of depth in navigating the world.

That bring us to the battle system - a source of a lot of contention for many JRPG fans, as many companies have struggled to update the classic turn-based formula in ways that feel more engaging, with some abandoning it altogether in favor of real-time combat. Sea of Stars better than any other game I've played offers a turn-based battle system that maintains engagement and momentum by adding a range of other elements. As one example, enemy spells generate a "Lock" for several turns during casting that can be broken with the right combination of physical and elemental damage types - using the right abilities and bringing in the right characters to break the lock adds a sort of puzzle-game element to the combat.

In addition, Sea of Stars adds rhythm and parry mechanics, where precisely timing a button press during attacks or while defending can either boost or reduce damage to your benefit. It then further expands on the concept with special moves that can land dozens of hits, as long as your timing is sharp. Beyond that, it also takes inspiration from the most flexible turn-based systems - such as the easy party switching of FFX - so when you come up against a challenge that requires a certain character to address, you are able to switch them in immediately without losing a turn. You'll find yourself quickly switching characters to break certain locks, deal the right damage types, make optimal use of "boost" charges, and so on, with nothing feeling tedious or slow. The overall effect is a dynamic and rewarding battle system that offers a fresh take on turn-based combat through a thoughtful combination of mechanics.

With all these strengths, plus beautiful environments, great characters, numerous side quests and collectibles, a surprisingly fun minigame available in each tavern, and a general level of polish that equals or exceeds the best JRPGs of yore, Sea of Stars is the complete package. Most incredibly, it's a resurgence of a classic genre, that despite being heavily influenced by the past, somehow never feels outdated. This is a great game and a must-play for any JRPG fan.\t\t\t\t\t
