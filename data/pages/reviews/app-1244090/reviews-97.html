\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA very good classic JRPG. I will say I thoroughly enjoyed my time exploring this world and the combat.

Is this game the second coming of the SNES JRPG filled with hope, love, and wonder? No. But as someone who loved the Messenger, this is a great follow-up. I'd call this a solid 8/10 type JRPG. If you like classic JRPGs, this is a must play.

Also, huge points for someone using the idea of "breaking" from Octopath Traveler. Makes the combat so much better.

I do wish the combat were a little bit deeper, and the story a little bit more cohesive, but it does everything at worst, quite well, and most of the time, very well.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tsea of stars is the most okay game iv'e played this year. the combat system, the story, the world, it all just feels so bland. there's like one fun fight and one good character, and absolutely nothing remarkable in between them. i can't think of better way to describe my problems with the plot than "things just kinda happen". your'e told go somewhere, and then something mildly interesting but usually unimportant happens, and your'e told to go to next place. i also feel like the game just can't decide what tone it's going for. if you've played the messenger, the previous game by sabotage, sea of stars is like that just often enough to prevent any sort of serious tone from emerging. the writing is also very bland, which for the most part prevents you from getting any sort of emotional attachment. the protagonists don't have any personality whatsoever besides the typical "hero" traits. but then occasionally, they'll talk about something that had happened in the past where they acted totally differently, but you just feel really confused because as previously stated, they haven't been characterised to have any personality at all in the actual events of the game! on to the combat system, each character only has about five different thing they can do in battle, and although there's technically a bunch of different combo moves, you barely ever actually get to use them. there is no synergy, no builds, and barely any strategy to battle. again, if you've played the messenger, you'll remember the quite frankly brilliant way that game was structured. well, sea of stars is completely linear. how 88% percent (as of the writing of this review) of players enjoyed this game will forever remain a mystery to me. i wouldn't buy sea of stars for 10 bucks, let alone the 34 they're charging. if a jrpg is what you're looking for, please try octopath traveler 2 instead.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBland Story - 4 hours in and I've yet to meet the antagonist. Two kids are off fighting in dungeons because that's what they were told to do.

Gameplay is not balanced - I like the button press timing thing, that's novel. However, I think the "party members don't die but are just knocked out for 3 turns" is a sign that the gameplay is not complete. it's a cop out because the way the turn counter works doesn't allow you to develop a cohesive healing strategy that includes enemy pattern interruptions. They need to add complexity, it's too simple to carry the interesting aspects.

Animations - a little weak but not too bad.

Graphics - fine, environment is great.

Level Design - overly simplistic. I don't get the sense that i'm trying to weigh resources to get through. It's just stock up on food and plough on.

Play crosscode instead, far far better game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSea of Stars doesn't take enough risks. I mean this in nearly every aspect of the game they've presented here. The storytelling doesn't escape the tropes of its predecessors, the combat mechanisms don't offer a large variety of decision points, and the exploration is linear and narrow. Despite all of this, Sea of Stars is one of 2023s best games, even when you take into account the stacked year we've had when it comes to video games.

While I have my complaints about the game, I cannot stress enough that I ultimately enjoyed my time in its world. I enjoyed it so much that I 100%'d the game (at least in terms of collectibles and endings, never going for that super late-game achievement unless I crave monotony). Sea of Stars excels because its foundation is from the golden era of gaming: the 90s. Every sword slash, item grab, character expression, soundbite, text box...resonates this nostalgia that doesn't let go until the credits are rolling. There was a lot of love that went into this game and it clearly shows. The visuals are the best in the business; every new location a treat, same for the monsters and bosses you'll encounter. Of course, the music is outstanding, pumping the game full of memorable moments even if you're just jumping off ledges or walking a tightrope.

However, it is this hesitance to stray away from its inspirational sources that I think makes the game fall flat in a lot of regards. The combat, while animated smoothly and hitting all of the minimum markings for what makes an RPG good, doesn't do anything unique. Each character has 3-4 abilities they will be using for the majority of the game; some offering useful utility, some being used every single encounter multiple times because it's that good (looking at you, Moonerang), and then some that will never see use in a million playthroughs. You'll see these animations or button pressing mini-games hundreds of times throughout your run, and by the end of the game you'll just be bored with it. Leveling up doesn't help with this either, as it only offers stat upgrades rather than changes to these abilities. An evolution of the skills would have gone a long way to making your options feel more diverse and impactful.

Then there's the lock system that the game introduces to provide more strategy during combat. Enemies will have certain damage types listed above them, and if you hit with the corresponding moves before their attack goes off, you cancel it outright. On paper it sounds like a fun puzzle with a rewarding result, but that's just not the case most of the time. Certain damage types are only available to your characters after the first round of combat, but you'll have enemies with locks on them with those types during the first round. Effectively, some locks can never be broken no matter what, which is frustrating. There's also a decent amount of the game where enemies have locks that cannot be broken simply because you don't possess the character necessary to do so. You can ignore most locks in the game, spam Moonerang, and just win.

Exploration is oddly one of my biggest gripes; it's linear. There are some secrets paths, don't get me wrong, but a vast majority of the areas in the game require you to press forward until you've cleared the area. Maybe you'll press A a few times to hop over a gap or climb up a vines, but you're just moving forward in a straight line. There's never multiple paths that lead to the same area, never a puzzle in navigation, never anything more than mindlessly pushing ahead. For some, this is enough. For me, if you're going to have these beautifully lush and pixelated areas for me to explore, let me explore them with open-endedness. I'd rather they just give me a single straight road from place to place because it would at least have the benefit of saving me time.

Then there's the story. There are some spoilers, so be warned. The game's initial premise is simple: you're from a town, you're born with special powers, you're supposed to use these special powers to kill a world-ending monster. You travel from location to location, meet unique and funny characters, come across challenges along the way, then finally are about to kill the monster. So far, so good. Nothing out of the ordinary, and the dialogue is precious and charming. There's a twist early on and suddenly killing the monster isn't the only goal: you now need to kill more monsters. Cool, awesome, point me in the direction and that's where me, my sword and my magic, will be going. It's at this point that the game does something actually worth mentioning: it kills off a major character. The one game to make me actually cry this year and it's Sea of Stars. I applaud the team for making this decision and adding weight to a rather feather-like story. Wipe your tears, adventurer, there's monsters to slay.

But...all of that motivation to push forward and save the day to honor your fallen comrade is quickly snuffed out. The ending lacks any real resolution to the story as a whole. Sure, we killed the monster(s) and brought peace back to the world, but it happens so quickly without any punch that I was sure it was a gag at first. Then, out of nowhere, our heroes feel this pull towards the stars and now there's even MORE monsters to kill, this time they mean business! And just like that, our main heroes fly away into space to battle these beasts, leaving their allies and me alone with nothing but a sour taste in our mouths. It's even worse when you unlock the secret ending, which is about the same except for our fallen ally gets resurrected. There's so much to hate about that decision. It takes this great risk and then doubles back on it. How anyone thought either of these endings were satisfactory are beyond me.

Sea of Stars delivers on what it promises: a nostalgic game that wears its inspiration on its sleeve. The formulas it uses are solid and concrete enough to provide a game that is beautiful to look at, beautiful to listen to, and a joy to breeze through. It doesn't do anything outside of the skeletal structure of RPGs that is groundbreaking or interesting, but ultimately I think I may be too critical of its elements and systems simply because I expected it to learn from games of yore and improve on their faults. For the casual gamer, or for those who love collecting things, this game is something you should play immediately. For others, who are intrigued but not sold by its charm, buy it knowing that it will not be the heralded champion of RPGs, nor will it ever be.

8/10\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCopypaste: The Game with choreographed battles.
It's plain boring, no depth to the gameplay or level design, it just has no soul whatsoever. Comparing this to Chrono Trigger or any of the old goldies is an insult to the genres to me.

It does have very well made graphics and some fancy 3FPS cutscenes though, and not to miss the "quirky indie dialogue".

To me, a failed attempt of plagiarizing several other majorly successful games, with only graphics being the true originality and big selling point. The game just doesn't make sense to me gameplay-wise.
Basically it feels like art students made this with a guidebook telling exactly how to make a game, and taking a little bit too much inspiration from Golden Sun, The Legend of Zelda (The older ones), Chrono Trigger and other games of that era. Inspiration from those games is an amazing idea, but when it's a straight copypaste it gets a bit iffy.

Nothing has any branching options, everything is streamlined, feels like every battle (Especially bosses) has 1 (One!) solution, unless you fancy taking some heavy damage - Which ultimately doesn't matter because you technically have infinite healing, even without the relic fully healing you after every battle. Just nothing matters, there's no brainpower required at all.
To me, it takes away all the fun when strategizing and thinking is taken away from the game, and it just feels like it caters to those who never played a game in their life, alternatively hit their head real hard as a kid.

So if you want linear gameplay with no thinking whatsoever required, and only do things when the game tells you to do it instead of using your own head it's probably a good buy.
Story seems decent so far at least, and the environment is gorgeous, just extremely lacking gameplay and the obvious cringe "quirky dialogue and 4th wall breaks" at times.

Don't recommend as a game, but as an interactive movie with mediocre exploration it's probably fun for some.\t\t\t\t\t
