\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFirst off, I'm going to split this review into a couple of different sections, so here goes nothing:

Backstory
In April of 2022, I found this game via a Tiktok video of the fishing mini-game. As an aspiring game dev myself, I found it bore a very striking resemblance to a fishing mini-game akin to one I'd had in my mind a few months earlier, so I decided to look into it more and learned about the 'overworld' exploration (e.g. a 'small' version of the character walking between locations), another idea I'd had floating around! I laughed about it, because what is an original thought anyway, but I kept the game in the back of my mind, checking their Tiktok every now and again to see if there was anything more to the game. In the lead up to the games release the advertising felt a bit.. weak? It focused heavily on the visuals of the game or talking about Moonerang and it made me worry there might not be any substance to the game other than the, admittedly, beautiful art style. As such, I kinda put the game on the back-burner to see what the reviews were like then kinda forgot for a while.

Recently I lost my closest friend very suddenly at 25. He was a 6"7 gentle giant who cared more about helping his friends than looking after himself with a passion for food, cooking and especially cooking for others. As such, I decided I wanted to buy a game to cheer myself up. I was in the mood for an RPG, started browsing steam, couldn't find anything. Went through recommendations, still nothing... Then I remembered my wishlist; more specifically Sea of Stars. I figured, what the hell, why not.

The Actual Review
Oh boy. I started the game and met Garl. It was like my friend had come back to life and been put into a video game. The intro to the game was.. rather slow if I'm entirely honest, but I stuck with it because I wanted to see where it goes. Boy am I glad I did.

As soon as combat became involved I immediately fell in love with the game. I didn't expect much but early game, every fight felt threatening, like if I ignored locks or didn't strategise there was a very real chance I could wipe. I liked that a lot and despite the absence of any real story, I was enjoying myself a lot.
Now, as the game progressed, the combat felt less and less appealing to me, but still not boring. It found new ways to reinvent itself with new characters, combos and eventually ultimates; for me personally it never got stale but the threat felt lower as the game progressed. Still, nonetheless, very very good combat system and pacing of mechanic reveals. 8/10

However, as the combat lost its initial lustre to me, the story began to pick up. Several different locations, plot hooks and story notes get revealed to you in a very, very good slow burn style. I can say now after finishing the game that every piece of information given to you, except How and why Kleeshae's crew are aware of the Sea of Stars being their end goal, but not her true identity, nor any other real information about how they met her, how she got to this world etc. is relevant in some way or another. Those very well written stories you hear about through Teaks? All plot relevant in some way or another. The pacing of the game feels just like, well, a Moonerang. Slow to start, but once it gets rolling it's all about the timing, and boy does the story hit them Bouncy achievements. Expertly written story, incredible pacing all wrapped up nicely with some very unique characters, both villains and allies alike. 9.9/10, the only reason it's not a 10 is due to the pacing of the 'ending', even if the true ending made up for it tenfold.

Characters. Well. Major spoilers follow for this section but I'll do my best to write a non-spoiler part after. Garl's story hit me like a best-friend shaped brick. His sacrifice to protect his best friends, how everyone treats him both when he's dead and alive resounded within me painfully. Seeing the main characters respond to his death, from them explaining how grief sticks with you, but how it makes you feel lucky to have had someone so special in the first place, to the funeral they hold for him, to even just the small comments the villagers make about how special he was; I cried like hell several times. It was like staring at everything I've been going through this past month and a bit. and now.. for the non spoiler version! As I briefly said in the story part, the characterisation of both allies and villains alike is pretty darn good. The MCs are very typical, being that they don't necessarily have a super strong personality other than "I am justice, I will do good" but it makes a hell of a lot of sense considering their background. Throughout the story, you get to see the flip side of how such an upbringing can affect people, both through Erlina/Brugraves and through some side stories that are told through various NPCs. For a story about fate, the characters don't feel wooden or "rail-roaded" at all. Even the major story NPCs who are canonically incredibly powerful and world-altering level strength don't feel out of place at any time during the story, as they too are as much victims of fate as the rest of the cast is. Each and every character has its place. Expertly written. 10/10

Now for the one that I think most people are aware of when it comes to Sea of Stars. The graphics. I'd like to say it speaks for itself, but there's more than just the surface level beauty to this game imo. There's a lot of small details that could very easily go unnoticed, from the beautiful distortion effects as you look at yourself through crystals, to how the UI changes in Serai's world, to the way the shadows flicker and move as you cast fire magic at your enemies, there's a whole lot of love gone into the art of this game. However, I do feel that a lot of the cutscenes were very jarring and out of place compared to the rest of the games style. There is however the [spoilers]Sea of Stars entrance cutscene[/spoiler] that is absolutely breathtaking and plays on the changing style immaculately. 9.5/10

Last but absolutely not least, the sound design. The music captured me very quickly, despite often being simple it never feels repetitive or in your face, and that's something that gets to me a lot; even in some of my favourite games. The thing that really got me, though, was the incredible idea that is the band. Playing their own version of the games songs, and how it changes when you talk to the instrument player to no longer include that instrument, what a simple but beautiful idea. The taverns in this game feel something more, and that's not even including how great Wheels is (I won't go into insane depth about it, just know it's a very well designed mini-game that I wish I could play against my friends). I will say, however, that at some points in the game the sound design is a bit on the buggy side. From jarring switches, to the fact the second moon ball in Valeres ultimate is incredibly quiet and the others are deafening loud, it can, unfortunately, quite often take you out of the moment. 9.5/10

Final Thoughts
SO. Overall:
Combat System: 8/10
Story: 9.9/10
Characterisation: 10/10
Art: 9.5/10
Sound Design: 9.5/10

I've never wrote a review for any game before, but this just hit differently for me. The design contains a lot of my favourite things; flesh shaping, wind mages, fishing and even some familiar soundfonts. It plays perfectly on everything I love, to the point I'd call it one of the best games I've played; and while that might be because of my own preferences, don't let that subtract from how insanely talented the team behind this game is. Play Sea of Stars!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn my 30 years of gaming I haven't played a single RPG like Sea of Stars.. I usually avoided party and turn based RPGs. But this game is amazing! The graphics are so pretty and remind me of my 16 Bit childhood, everything looks lush and vibrant. The soundtrack is great, the world design is diverse (forests, ice, oceans, deserts...) and there is always room to explore and find secrets. Even better, the levels are filled with little puzzles that are not too hard but very fun to solve. The characters are very charming and the story is immersive - I'm completely hooked. Personally I think that the devs really poured their heart and soul into this game and it shows in many places!

I also like the turn based fighting system a lot since it's not super complex but still challenging and it requires some planning ahead at times. When a plan works, it's so satisfying! Everything falls into place and feels just right. The game hits all the right notes for me, I'm really happy to play and the devs can be proud of themselves! I bought it instantly after playing the demo and I can only advise to check out the demo if you're on the fence. For what it's worth, I'm playing it on the Steam Deck where it runs beautifully.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game starts off with an amazing art design, some great music, and a fresh take on the Chrono Trigger style combat. However the combat will quickly overstay it's welcome as it is about 90% of the gameplay, and it never really innovates in a way that keeps it fresh. There is very little else here to relieve the built up resentment over how absolutely annoying the combat is. The story is compelling when it exists, but it does not exist enough to carry this experience. The wheels mini-game is similarly frustrating and time consuming as the AI will always have some gimmick that forces you to play one specific way.

There are a lot of positives with what they tried to do, but I feel like they failed to make it a good experience overall. I would only recommend this to people who have a high tolerance for the mundane.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSea of Stars felt like a nostalgia hug. To me was reminiscent of a lot of older games that I dearly love like Lunar 2 and Chrono Trigger. Somehow just from the trailer I knew I would love this game and having finished it I am quite pleased to have been correct.

Visually, the pixel art in this game was absolutely stunning. Each map was unique and appealing and I never found them boring or similar to another. I loved how there were so many different styles within the world from rustic villages, to futuristic sci-fi towns, to decrepit haunted manors. I felt that the character art and boss are were also top tier.

Combat wise I found the game to be very engaging. I enjoyed the lock system and it kept me from zoning out during battle as even some mob battles got dicey if I wasn't trying. This is probably one of the first RPGs where I've actively used items which was also a nice change of pace though I constantly was using Seraii's skill to give me more turns for breaking locks because the turn limit to destroy a lock was usually incredibly short. I loved the combos and that each character would be needed sometimes. This game encouraged swapping out characters and again, another game where I didn't just stick to my three favorite characters and leaving the others to rot in my back line. The options to make the game easier or harder were nice. I felt the boss battles were evenly spaced and had a good difficulty level for game progression.

Music wise it had a similar nostalgic feel and I really enjoyed the different areas themes. I felt they matched the atmosphere of the area and the battle and boss battle music was quite catchy so I didn't mind hearing it often. I could honestly listen to this soundtrack for fun.

Story wise is probably the weakest point unfortunately. First I want to say I still enjoyed it for what it was. I was very immersed. I cared what happened to the characters, I needed to see what would happen next, I cried. The game focuses heavily on themes of friendship and I was here for it. I felt like there was never a lull that dragged things down, I never found myself bored. I felt the games antagonists were also well fleshed out but didn't have a strong enough conclusion to their arcs. We got a hint of a deeper story but a lot was left unsaid. I liked each of the characters and my biggest gripe would be that I would have liked more emotional growth for the two main leads (they felt very interchangeable and had the exact same personality (though calling it a personality seems generous) - were both characters even really needed? Probably not.) and their general attitude/demeanor/values/thought processes never wavered. There was never any moments of doubt or uncertainty, just lining up each next bad guy and saving the world (as is their destiny - is this a spoiler? I don't think so). Overall it is very much also a standard hero's journey and wasn't necessarily unique but I still found myself invested anyway. While the story left a lot to be desired, it still had several strong moments and I really loved a lot of the cast and their side stories. Yet even saying this - I still had such a fantastic time, I still found myself loving it anyway. I will say the work to get the true end is worth it. The initial end is not bad (if a bit bland) but the true end has a few things that just made a difference. (Though I have a gripe about the true ending as well but don't want to include spoilers)

I wanted to also discuss the mini-game Wheels. I loved this game. It felt unique, it was fun, easy to understand I didn't hate myself playing it. Some rounds were challenging and it was honestly a nice interlude when I wanted to tackle it. It also was not required in any way so absolutely skippable if someone didn't enjoy it like I did.

Overall this game is very much my thing, and I absolutely loved it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSea of Stars is a great nostalgic homage to those classic JRPGs I grew up with. Particularly Crono Trigger. It's not really fair to compare it to Crono Trigger, it's an unrealistic standard for a game to live up to, but it's hard not to. This game isn't as deep or extensive as CT, but it pushes a lot of the same buttons, checks off a lot of the same boxes. The visual design, especially of the characters, is very reminiscent. And of course there are lots of little nods, references, and Easter eggs. I spotted one reference that I instantly recognized, despite not even so much as thinking about it for decades. Made me laugh.

So, on to the game:

The story revolves around Zale and Valere, two Solstice Warriors, people born on the summer and winter Solstice, respectively, with magical powers tied to the sun and moon, whose purpose is to find and defeat Dwellers, powerful monsters who can only be killed by Solstice Warriors, and only during an eclipse. Dwellers are creations of the Fleshmancer (kinda silly name, IMO), an evil being that has wreaked havoc on their world in the past.

So your standard quest to save the world from evil. New and interesting allies are met, enemies met and defeated, plot twists, side quests, and a big final battle, complete with a regular and a "true" ending. Also a New Game+, because an homage to Crono Trigger wouldn't be complete without one.

The battle system is pretty polished, and engaging. Combat is turn-based, with your characters being able to fight in whatever order you want. Three characters active at a time, but you can swap them out at any time without penalty. Each character has a physical damage type (blunt, blade) as well as a magic type (solar, lunar, poison, and magic). Normal attacks cause magic spheres to drop that can be used to apply the character's magic attribute to their physical attack, or power up their special attacks. Normal attacks also regenerate a small amount of magic points. As combat progresses, a gauge fills up to three combo points that allow two characters to join together to perform special attacks that mix their attack types.

This is all important due to the "Lock" system. Besides normal attacks, enemies sometimes charge up special attacks. When this happens a chain of icons appears above their heads indicating specific attack types. If the player manages to hit it with all of those attack types before the monster's turn comes up, the attack is canceled. This adds a bit of strategy to combat, as you have to think about what characters you want to use and which attacks to use, and in which order. Sometimes it's just not possible to break all the locks, due to lack of MP needed for a special attack, or lack of time before the attack launches, so you have to decide to try to weaken the attack, or take a more defensive action.

There is also a timing element to combat. Pressing a button at the right time can cause extra damage to an attack, or block an enemy attack, reducing damage taken.

So this is not a fight-on-autopilot-button-mashing kind of RPG.

Each character has one type of weapon and armor which periodically gets upgraded. For the most part, the only upgrades new equipment give you are damage and defense improvements, with a few exceptions. I would have liked to see more weapons or armor with special attributes. Characters can also equip two normal accessories that apply bonuses to themselves, and one special accessory that applies bonuses to the entire party. There are also "Relics" you can buy or collect that alter the game play somewhat, such as making blocking easier or giving you a lot more HP to make the game easier, or drastically reduce your HP to make the game more challenging. The party can also carry up to 10 consumable items that can be created at campfires, the game's version of resting/save points.

There are also a few mini-games. Fishing (gotta have fishing in an RPG, right?) which isn't really challenging, and gives you cooking ingredients and contributes to a collecting quest. A Quiz game where you answer questions about the game for small prizes. And "Wheels," a strategy game that's actually pretty fun. Most of these give small rewards and the main reason for doing them to completion is to work towards the "true" ending of the game.

I didn't really intend for this to be an exhaustive review of all the game's features, so I'll just stop here. Long story short, this is a great game that any classic console RPG fan will enjoy.\t\t\t\t\t
