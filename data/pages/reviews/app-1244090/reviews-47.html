\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSea of Stars shows a frustrating amount of potential. TLDR: I recommend SoS to anyone who is drawn in by the gorgeous soundtrack and visuals, and are willing to put up with somewhat amateurish writing and unbalanced combat.

Sea of Stars' strengths are truly exceptional. The presentation is undeniably stunning- the retro style shows even better craftsmanship than Sabotage's last game, The Messenger. The combat lock system is interesting, and the visual variety of enemies and locations is impressive. Exploration and puzzles are generally engaging enough, if rarely challenging. The connections made to The Messenger's world are interesting and deeper than I expected. Minigames like Wheels and fishing, stories from Teaks, and sailing are a fun detour from the core gameplay. Overwall, SoS is crafted meticulously, and made with clear passion. I would also say the length of the game is just about perfect.

However, because of how great these elements are, the limitations of the game stood out to me more, and I want to spend most of this review discussing these because player feedback might help Sabotage understand what didn't work as well.

The combat would be a good starting point- it's not bad, but it became tedious about halfway through my playthrough. This is a combination of a few issues, but I'll focus on the Relic system since I haven't seen much conversation around how it impacts the experience.

If you choose to ignore Relics entirely, the game will be braindead easy. Because of this, I played most of the game with the Tactician Relic added in a recent patch, which makes combat more challenging, but SoS is clearly not balanced around this relic. Timing attacks is essentially required in order to break enough locks to survive, and because enemies with locks take much less damage, fights tend to go on longer than they should. This means that you'll probably end up wasting a few minutes in fights with enemies who have no way of killing you. Other Relics must be purchased from stores, and determining which I should use was challenging. Some are clearly broken, literally reducing damage you take by 30%, but these are listed by others that lower shop prices by 10%. It feels like Sabotage didn't know how to make SoS both accessible and challenging, so they shoved an unsorted pile of Relics in the player's face and told them to figure it out. I would have liked a more clear and limited difficulty selection for a more balanced game, the trade off would be easily worth it. Other issues like skills and combos becoming repetitive and the final bosses being letdowns are worth mentioning, but you can read other reviews if you want more on that.

However, my biggest issue with Sea of Stars is the writing. To be clear, I think the larger worldbuilding is done well enough, with the connections to the Messenger and deeper lore really carrying things. Sure, there are some nonsensical fantasy tropes and plotholes, but It's the character writing and dialogue that really drags the game down. While Chrono Trigger wasn't exactly Shakespeare, the overall story was compelling and tightly written; characters generally had arcs with enough depth to stay interesting, and this was all through a rough translation from Japanese released 25 years ago.

In contrast, the characters in Sea of Stars don't really change over the course of the plot. The main protagonists are impressively bland- in Chrono Trigger, Chrono is basically a silent self-insert, meaning bland but never annoying. In contrast, Zale and Valere are in almost every conversation, stating the obvious and talking about how much they love Garl. And Garl is almost worse- he's the most blatant Mary Sue I've seen in a long time and the universe revolves around him. Some of the other playable characters are interesting when they actually get some room to breath, but everyone is too busy talking about how great Garl is. I think a bit more conflict and distinction between protagonists would have gone a long way toward giving these characters some distinction. It never feels like the party disagrees on anything, nor do any of the characters develop or change their opinions as the story progresses (if they had any to begin with).

Without spoilers, I'll add that a significant amount of the game's dialogue and plot felt like a rough draft by a non-native English speaker. It's far from the worst I've seen, but there are noticeable typos, unnatural sentences, and distracting jokes that just don't land. For their next game, Sabotage Studio really needs to hire a dedicated writer, or at least an editor to make some of the conversations and events flow a bit better. In less story-focused genres or games (The Messenger) this is less of a problem, but when half the runtime is narrative, you have to put in the dev resources to polish it properly.

All of that said, I have nothing but respect for Sabotage Studio and wish them the best of luck in future endeavors. I hope they can learn from what worked well, and what didn't, and finally put together the masterpiece they seem close to achieving next time around.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA great JRPG style game with bopping music.

I'm of the generation that lived though the golden age of JRPGs, Chrono Trigger, Final Fantasy 4-7, Breath of Fire 1-3, etc. The fact that pretty much all of them got sequels is a testament to their popularity and quality, but the first one in that list has always mysteriously been lacking one aside from the, imho, lackluster Crono Cross (that was so different it might as well have been a completely different series).

Sea of Stars is a proper successor to Chrono Trigger, though it doesn't quite hit the mark in terms of story quality. It's alright, but it doesn't feel that personal. The devs have spent a lot of effort trying to make us care about the people in this world, but honestly... it didn't really click for me. It may have something to do with the two main protagonists being larger-than-life chosen ones from the get-go, rather than roles they grew into. The many antagonists you meet really aren't that special either in terms of character development, not even the final bosses of the game (that despite the true ending leave several giant plotholes behind).

What the game DOES have, though, are really awesome boss battles and kickass music. Composer Eric W. Brown has gone above and beyond to make the the combat tunes real head boppers, and in each dungeon I always look forward to what cool boss will they let me rave out to next. The animations are something to behold, with sway and motion perfectly accompanied by the beats of the music, making each encounter a treat (despite some unskippable attack cinematics slowing things down, having watched them a bajillion times already).

There's plenty to of collectibles to find, a fishing minigame (because all JRPGs must have them by law), a steampunk:esque boardgame and gorgeous environments to discover.

It's not a flawless game by any means, but well worth the asking price if you're into JPRG-style games. Do keep in mind, however, that it's a _very_ slow starter. It'll take quite some time before the action kicks off, and even longer before it really gets into gear.\t\t\t\t\t
