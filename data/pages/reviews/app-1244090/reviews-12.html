\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSea of Stars was a game that I had my eyes on for a long time, and it delivers the classic JRPG experience that I’ve been chasing for years. It features highly interactive gameplay, something that turn-based games tend to lack, and rich interwoven story arcs which make it all the more compelling. However, Sea of Stars does drop the ball in a lot of important ways which ultimately makes it less of a standout than it could have been.

Characters (5/10):
The protagonist of Sea of Stars is Garl, and no-one will be able to convince me otherwise. He has the most character growth, the most compelling motives, and simply has the most charm in the game, period. In his supporting cast are the two (supposedly) real protagonists who have no noteworthy personality to them whatsoever aside from “heroic”. They do exactly as they were told when they were children, and have no deeper reason for existence other than to be Mary Sues. It’s a tragedy that the game centers around them just gaining more power and going around fighting stronger enemies to save the multiverse.
Regarding the other playable characters, they are generally written well, with self-consistent motives and some traits and abilities that give them some form of unique identity. There is one exception, however: the overarching antagonist. He is another villain who seems to be evil just for the sake of being evil, which would have been fine had the game not tried to come up with some nonsensical justifications for his actions. Inconsistent characterization makes him constantly seem like he’s out of character. Overall, there’s a roughly even number of perfect characters and awful characters, so it averages out.

Gameplay (4.5/10):
The gameplay of Sea of Stars was overthought. It features many puzzles and parkour elements that the player is required to solve in order to progress along the main storyline, as opposed to saving these for optional treasures and side quests. When kept in moderation, it adds some intrigue to an otherwise boring gaming experience. However, Sea of Stars was far too heavy-handed in this regard, and overstuffed the main game with these puzzles which makes traveling around incredibly frustrating sometimes.
Each character’s skillset is also incredibly simple with only 4 abilities, which also detracts from the strategic element that all the best JRPGs exhibit. On the flip side, the lock system, the combo system and the interactivity of attack and defense in this game do force you to concentrate and have good reflexes instead of just using a skill and dozing off, making the game require some skill.
However, this isn’t to say that the gameplay in Sea of Stars is wholly bad. Having puzzle elements does work in its favor when it comes to the side content, and the world exploration makes the game more immersive.

Art (9/10):
The art and music in Sea of Stars are phenomenal. Visually, the world design is very aesthetically pleasing in the nostalgic 16-bit style. Not to mention the skill animations, particularly the ultimate attacks, which are great. Sea of Stars also features awesome music which is always enjoyable to listen to. There really isn’t much to say; the artistic elements are self-evidently excellent.

Story (9/10):
Despite its weak protagonists, Sea of Stars manages to spin a compelling, complex, interwoven story of multiple characters, most of whom are actually interesting. It also brings in the concept of multiple timelines and universes, which it executes very well. The story begins as a simple tale of three adventurers setting off to find a way to help their mentors, before learning that not everything is as they once believed and that they would have to search outside of themselves. The arc of Aephorul and Resh’an in particular stands out as a tale of the things we sacrifice to chase our dreams, and of the principles that we hold onto no matter how seemingly irrational. I personally liked that the True Ending gave purpose to a seemingly useless key item given at the start of the game; it’s incredibly poetic.
As a side note, though, the phrase “use magic without using magic” is incredibly awkward and stupid sounding.

Overall, Sea of Stars is a unique game in the JRPG genre with a fascinating universe and lore which is certainly worth looking at, even though it has some elements that disappointed or annoyed me.

Final Score: 27.5/40 (Good)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSea of Stars is now my new favorite video game.

There are so many things to love about this game. I absolutely love the personalities and art styles of the characters, as well as the design of the levels and bosses. The story, while a little confusing at times, is extremely captivating and all falls into place in its own time. Discovering the new parts of the map and unlocking different abilities and powers kept the game interesting and it never felt "grindy" at any point, especially since the level ups always felt to happen at just the right times. Even the mini games like wheels, fishing, and going around finding all the extras to complete the achievements was still a good time and didn't feel too tedious. This game made me feel genuine emotions of all kinds, whether it be happy, sad, suspense, excitement and everything in between and that's what I loved the most about it. All in all an absolutely wonderful experience that I highly recommend to anyone. 10/10 about to go play it again.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRecommended. This game was so much fun. It promises nothing and delivers everything. Completed my first play-through at 30 hrs. Haven't gone back over for completion-ism yet.

My only criticism is that the last 20% felt like it was packed with a lot of filler.
Manual back tracking, areas that you literally just run through with no combat or puzzle interaction and a stairwell in the final dungeon that would give MGS3's ladder a run for it's money...

That is however, the only real bad point that I can find.

OST is AMAZING, i've never had so much fun bopping my head to boss battle music. It's like someone took my pokemon nostalgia and modernised it.
The turn based combat system feels great, it has a few nuances to keep it interesting and not just melee, time your healing, melee repeat.
The characters are fun and enjoyable, if a bit cheesy.
The (pixel) art direction is incredibly well done, although on a modern, high pixel desnsity monitor... I found I had to run the game in 75% windowed mode for everything to not feel 'stretched'. No biggie.

100% worth a buy for your library.
