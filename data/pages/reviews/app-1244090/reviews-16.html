\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEdit: Got the true ending. Cribbing so much from other JRPGs, it does get significantly better later on. I wrote the below right before the game popped off. That said, I do think it's still more or less true, but as the game goes on, you get a lot more boss fights and they're really cool. Meduso is basically what I described below of a battle that's just the Lock mechanic used ad infinitum. It has this neat effect of "If you don't solve this puzzle, you will take a ton of damage and die. If you do solve it, you will be granted a stream of combo points and ultimate abilities which you will need to solve this next puzzle, and on and on". Those fights where you're just chaining from one combo into the next are where the game really shines, and they're basically the entire back half.

Gonna be upfront and say that this style of game is catnip for me. I do really like it, but as I'm playing it, I often feel like I like it more because of the technical aspects than any "fun" I'm getting out of it. There are RPGS with better combat, better pixel art, better music, better writing, etc. This game has never seen a swing it didn't want to take though, and that means you get music where you're often like "meh", and then you fight the first boss and the song Encounter Elite doesn't leave your head for 2 days. Literally hearing the hook while I'm typing this. The combat is similarly a mixed bag. Most turns are boring and strategically shallow which is pretty bad for a game like this, but I like the Locks. They force you to switch into this mode like, "OK, this character will do this which will set up this character to do this, which will give me a combo, which will break the Lock". But the combat isn't defined by that and it's weaker for it (in fact, if you get good enough with the "repeat" abilities, you can safely skip any strategy. I've been told this changes as the game goes on, but like I'm 10 hours in).

The lore and aesthetic are spectacular. There's no qualifier there, it's just very good and cultivates this idea that you're in a world fundamentally shaped by magic and that you are a legend in a world of legends. My fic brain spun up so fast and hard, which is honestly a good enough reason in itself to play a game.

So, ya. There's a bunch of cool ideas in here. There's also a lot of cruft. I'm enjoying the things I'm taking out of it more than the game itself. If that's something you think you'd like, you'll have a great time. If not, wait for the sequel 'cause I betcha it'll be incredible.

*Also, some of the stuff is Canadian references I don't get. Which isn't a complaint; it's not in the way or anything. More like if you're Canadian that's another potential positive for you.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI can't say much that hasn't been said about this game before, it just hits you right in the heart with a combination of wonderful nostalgia and brand new ideas to make this a very fresh, yet very familiar experience.
Almost every set piece of this game has something that can be identified as coming from another game before but the more you play it the more you realize that it's not just a copy or "inspired by" these games, but rather it feels more like a natural evolution of what the retro games that inspired would look like if they were still made today with their original design philosophies.
The gameplay is a bit lackluster at times, I would've prefered a larger pool of abilities and skills for the characters, but its not a big stain on the game and sometimes it even allows you to lay back and just enjoy the story and the atmosphere the game brings, which is its biggest forte and the best part of the whole experience imho.
Additionaly, there's a lot of rewards for people who completed The Messenger and have a keen eye for references and connections between both games, it's not a mandatory game to be played before this but it certainly enriches an already amazing experience.
All I can say is that I eagerly await for next Sabotage projects and hope they're as good as this one.\t\t\t\t\t
