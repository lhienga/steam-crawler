An extremely bland story, paper-thin (even "cardboard" is too charitable a word) characters and awful dialogue are coupled with gameplay mechanics that were ripped off from games going back almost 30 years, alongside a world/level design philosophy with no regard for crafting a cohesive, or even sensible, game world. Overall
I found this game to be quite fun. There were some very pretty scenes and areas throughout the story, and the gameplay was engaging. The only parts that I found lacking were the story (I found it a little flat, without much driving me to the next stage besides having nothing else to do) and the main characters (there didn't seem to be much individuality to the two main characters). These are minor complaints, however, and are more than made up for by other things.

Gameplay
For the most part, there are three levels to the game:

  • World Map
  • World/Stage
  • Combat

The world map is where you spend the least amount of time, and there are only a few puzzles to solve. Mostly, this is just where you go to get to the next area. The World/Stage is where you'll likely spend most of your time exploring and solving puzzles (more on those later). There is a lot to do at this level, but I found myself rushing through a lot of the linear sections that didn't have as much to discover. Finally, the Combat is what I found most engaging, and where you'll spend the other significant portion of your time (more on this in the next section).

Generally, I found the gameplay rewarding, if not very challenging. The only major complaint I have is the minor tedium of navigating the world map when you need to backtrack, but this is mostly solved in the later portion of the game.

Combat
In my head, I compare the combat in this game to Paper Mario: The Thousand Year Door, since I grew up playing that a lot. Everything is turn-based, and you can time your attacks/defends in order to get a better result. Unlike Paper Mario, enemy actions having a count-down that decreases every time you take an action. There are ways to increase their timer, and "blocks" that you can break by matching attack types in order to cancel their action altogether. In my opinion, this made the combat much more engaging, as I sometimes needed to hold back resources to break attacks when needed, or had to use attacks I might not have otherwise to avoid taking massive damage.

The combat abilities have a decent variety in this game, with multiple physical and special types, as well as a few different mechanics to strengthen or prolong attacks (times presses/holds). Beyond the base increase to damage/defense, this rewards players that have good rhythm or reflexes. The enemies are likewise varied, and have different mechanics or timings for the player to learn.

Puzzles/Treasure
I found most of the puzzles in this game to be pretty simple, either because they are the same type you've seen multiple times before or they are just a quick progress check for world-abilities (skills you can use outside of combat (think Metroidvania-style)). There are three of these that you gain as you progress through the story, and are often used to hide treasure in previous sections of the game that you'll have to go back to. However, while the puzzles were not difficult, I didn't ever feel annoyed at them because they were'n typically tedious: at most, you waste a minute moving a block around on the floor.

For the treasure, I found most of it on my own just playing through the game and checking the corners of areas. Nothing is too annoying to spot, but a decent amount is locked behind abilities you won't have yet, as I mentioned before. This was my only real gripe, since I dislike having to backtrack for no reason; my preference would be for the game to give me a reason to go back through those areas a part of the plot after I have obtained the necessary abilities.

Characters/Environment
As I stated in the introduction, I found the two main characters to be a little same-y during the main story. While they are given a decent back story and some typical hero-characterizations, I'm left wondering why we were given two main characters except to fulfill the Sun/Moon plot device. I would have liked them a lot more if they were treated as foils in their personalities, and if they didn't always react the same way to everything. That being said...

The truly enjoyable characters in this game are the sidekicks and enemies. The developers did a pretty good job making all of the characters you pick up throughout the game stand out from each other. I found myself attached to two characters in particular, and was fully engaged in their parts of the story as I went through it. For the enemies, there are some that are jokes, like the DJ, and others that are just funny, like the zombie that (in my headcanon) accidentally deletes itself from existence every battle. The main bosses are pretty cool for the most part as well, and their soundtracks are fantastic.

For the environment, I found this game to be excellent in the environment and in the character design. There are some gorgeous views throughout the story, and the world-building ties into the decaying structures and leftover ruins quite well. The characters all look great, and the enemy design (especially the bosses) can be very, very cool. To tie it all together, the game has an excellent soundtrack that is at times incredibly peaceful and at others exhilarating.

Story
The story is unfortunately where I have the largest issues. While I liked the world-building and the general idea of the plot, I found it to be very linear and a little flat. It could have benefited from some side quests to break things up, but we really only get to a few at the end. You're also pretty solidly railroaded for the first 5-10 hours or so before the world map opens up a bit, but even then you can't go anywhere you want until unlocking a late-game ability.

Something I did enjoy, however, was a tone shift 1/2 to 2/3 through the game that I found very interesting. For a lot of the game, you're told that the Big Bad is so horrible and you just really have to beat him. Then, you finally find out why the game has the title it does, and you find out why the Big Bad is so very bad. This marked a renewed interest in the story for me, but I just wish the game had more moments like this.


If you read all of that, thank you for your time. I would recommend this game if it's the type of genre you've enjoyed in the past. If you have an interest but aren't sure if it's for you, give it a shot once it goes on sale. If you have no interest in this game, I'm not really sure what you're doing here.

Best,
