\r\n\t\t\t\t\ttl;dr: I literally cannot recommend this game enough. This is the first game I would gladly pay 79.99 for. I've gotten less with more money.


You know... I've been sitting here for about five solid minutes trying to think about where to begin, and honestly this game has left me speechless. All of the praise, the awards, and the compliments I'm about to throw into the pile are so well deserved I believe nothing I say below is going to give it justice. It's literally a swan song to all of the RPG's I loved as a kid utilizing the best of every one of them. For those who may not know, I'll point out these mechanics when I bring them up, at least the ones I know confidently.

Everything about this game, from the story, to the way you traverse the world, to the combat and puzzles is damn near flawless. While I can't say everyone would agree with that or feel their experience matched mine, I will say that if you have any love for RPG's at all, this is a win for you undoubtedly. I would genuinely call this a must buy.

The sound design is captivating. The musical score is definitely top notch and up there with the best of them on a bad day. The music and sound effects are all just stellar. They definitely put a ton of love and care into this, and the remixes they did from their other games soundtrack are also amazing, and very nostalgic for those who've played The Messenger.

Enemies are usually visible on map, and encounters begin very similarly to Chrono Trigger, and they did an excellent job with the creativity on encounters. combat is turn based combat very much influenced by Super Mario RPG (timed attacks, timed blocks timed inputs for skills etc.) with a twist in that you can choose who goes in what order for your party, it isn't based on character speed or anything like that. You also have full use of all of your characters in your party at any time which I thought was a genius improvement to the usual system RPG's use. It's very purposefully and excellently done as it pairs perfectly with the fact they utilize a "lock" system, where certain attacks are telegraphed and if you attack the enemy with the correct types of physical or elemental damage you cancel their turn. It can be very complicated and sometimes anxiety inducing when you get used to the concept and realize there's turns where you just don't have the correct moves to cancel.

They provide easy and hard mode in the form of "relics" you can collect which are basically all a bunch of toggle able passive game play changes. (i.e. a tactician relic making the game far harder, and a baby mode relic that makes your party nigh impossible to hurt let alone kill until end game.) +many others.

There isn't really an emphasis on leveling or even really equipment to a point. They were cool and didn't often have merchant items in chests to make you feel like you wasted $$$ on that upgrade. I never had to grind, but I also never avoided fights.

The stats part of leveling up and what not are simplistic, and when you level up you can choose to boost stats which also is identical to the way you do it in SMRPG. A base set of +'s to health, str, def etc all different each level up, and you choose another stat to boost like + to mana, or + to magical defense etc. Rudimentary but also useful in the fact they basically allowed you to customize most of your characters based on how you used them vs optimizing, though the characters themselves are pretty easy to build in an optimal way by just checking their base stats. I did whatever the hell I wanted and it worked out fine. I didn't play the tactician difficulty though so YMMV.

Skills are earned via story beats throughout the game. Every skill is useful and there's really no characters beyond the two main protagonists that are objectively better than the others, beyond element use or lack thereof. This is also punctuated again by their use of being able to hot swap party members on the fly to augment their lock combat system. They also added combos ala Chrono Trigger as well. These you get by Solving puzzles/beating certain bosses etc.

They take this a step further and also Give you ultimates that you use once you've grown your combo meter by doing combos..

The only other RPG I've played that's created such a tight skill system that pushed you and encouraged you to utilize everything from basic attacks to all skills and abilities regularly is ASTLIBRA. (I also reviewed that game, and gave it as glowing of a review. Stellar.) It's so well done and 100% improves on the systems of it's predecessors. Kudos to the team for coming up with it, extremely creative and well executed. I mean literally, imagine the best systems from Final Fantasy 7 to Chrono Trigger to SMRPG. It's all genuinely a step up from all three of those games' combat systems, combined..

With the graphics, I believe we have reached the pinnacle of pixel style graphics with current technology, and in various forms (such as the 2.5d with Octopath Traveller.) Either way, it's excellent in all forms. From the abilities used, to the enemy sprites, the character sprites, all of it is excellently done, and very creative. ALMOST no enemy recolors, and even those have like an additional feature change.

There is a ton of detail in the environments and each one felt very unique. Given they utilize a more active movement system as well it lends to very creative zones with verticality and they take advantage of this in very clever ways introducing shortcuts and making use of the perspective for secrets and traps all throughout. When you add all of the puzzles as well, the best thing I would compare it to is like CrossCode Medium-Lite. (Another well reviewed game by moi.)

The story is pretty gripping. I feel like they did an excellent job blending mature and light elements to the settings, stories and characters that it makes everything feel genuine and worth investing in. Everything from the overarching plot, to all of the side stories, and the Easter eggs are all extremely well made. Every single quest had a good premise, and felt worth doing. They even made their Collectathon quest for Rainbow shells easy to tolerate by utilizing mechanics that help point out if you're missing something in a particular part of the map and making the chest for that particular item unique and eye catching even when very well hidden. Fishing is super easy, but who doesn't like fishing?

Secrets abound in this game but nothing is miss-able on a single play-through*. They make it easy enough that it's not tedious, lets put it that way. *You have to "beat" the game twice to officially see everything, but only the final boss is repeated beyond a couple extra new tasks that are enjoyable

Overall this is a game I would place along with the greats of the past, such as Earthbound, Many Final Fantasies (shout out to 1/5/6/7/8 and 14!), Xenogears, Chrono Trigger, SMRPG, etc. No cap. This game is worth more than they're charging honestly compared to some larger games out there.

Also, for the rant at the end:
I heard you. Hope things are awesome for you!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSeeing as it won Best Indie at the Game Awards this year, it might not really need my little review, but here goes. Sea of Stars is really quite a lovely take on the JRPGs of the mid-90s (read: not just Chrono Trigger). It may be a slow burn at first -- I'm seeing this becoming a theme for Sabotage Studios -- but once underway, and especially a few boss fights in, the challenge and intrigue start to mount. (Some minigames in the early going could have helped with player engagement). In battle, timed attacks and other button inputs on loan from Super Mario RPG are a great way to keep things lively, and the strategy component adds to the fun factor; however, there are sequences with far too many battles (Maelstrom, I'm looking at you). Exploration and puzzles are a pleasure, as is the downtime in between, where one can enjoy the very Quebecois tradition of folklore being read around the fire, plus other quests and a roulette war game. It's a self-aware game, and though it gets a tad overly serious at times, it still holds some genuine surprises (mad props to the devs for the cutscene that channels Phantasy Star IV); despite this, there are some serious oversights. The first is with the music, some of which is just... bad. There's a glaring lack of cohesion between tracks that sound like a MIDI file c. 1996, and the ones with the obvious signature of Yasunori Mitsuda (which, for the record, are outstanding). The second is the English translation. I highly recommend you play in the original French if you can, as in the English you'll see signs of hasty word-for-word-ness in places and a general lack of refinement. I hope this gets some polish in a future update for English-speaking players, as it's actually really important in a game with so much text. On the whole, I really do like Sea of Stars, and I recommend it to basically anybody.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI wish there was a neutral review option on Steam.

I wanted to enjoy SoS, drawing inspiration from Chrono Trigger and others with its impressive animation, music, and a nostalgic story tone. However, its attempt at innovation falls short. The 'magic without magic' system feels underdeveloped and visually obfuscated. Special moves are theoretically good but become tedious due to relentless enemy spam and lengthy animations. The timing-attack, enjoyable in other games, feels repetitive here, with poorly telegraphed enemy moves causing frustration. Even the dual/triple tech system from Chrono Trigger is poorly executed, as building combo meters is rare in random encounters, and both individual and combo skills lack variety.

Despite visual appeal and style, the combat simply fell short for me and couldn't sustain my interest in the rest of the game. In the end I wish it simply copied CT more and tried to innovate less, as none of the new ideas here seem to really add anything to the gameplay.\t\t\t\t\t
