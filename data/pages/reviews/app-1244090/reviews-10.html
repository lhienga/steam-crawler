And this game really helped me feel better.
So thank you.

I have very few things to say that I didn't like (a bit too grindy, writing was not as solid as I would've lliked) but honestly it did so many things so damn good I am pretty amazed.

Story-wise it was cute, but had potential for a lot more (especially for the main charactrers). They're not bad, just a bit boring.
It has a lot of persionality otherwise, everything in the world feels alive, you know and tought every corner of the game, personally.

It is one of the best adventures I've played and I love this game. If you are a classic rpgs lover, you will enjoy this. As a game overall it is great.
If I felt a strong theme, maybe? A coherent firm that mattered, that connected, I might have considered it near perfect. But it is incredible regardless.\t\t\t\t\t

If you can suspend your disbelief and allow the game to work its magic on you, I really think you'll be in for a good time.

I've gained all but two achievements, with about 93% completion, but I've found 100% of all collectibles, treasures, and items. The only two achievements I didn't get are one for cooking every dish in the game (I just couldn't be arsed) and the other for beating 10 bosses with a certain end-game relic (it would've required me to do new game+, which I wasn't keen on doing after just playing for 50 hours).

Some random thoughts on the game, in no particular order, by rough category:

The Combat

I'll come right out and say it: I don't typically like JRPG combat. It often fails to engage me and just feels like repetitive busywork that I'm slogging through to get to the next cool story sequence. That is not the case here. Sea of Stars has multiple unique twists on the traditional JRPG combat formula, to the point that it doesn't even feel like a JRPG anymore -- the flashy combo abilities, Mario RPG-style timed blocks and attacks, the 'lock' system, and the ability/attack charge-up system all force you to think instead of mash buttons.

The lock system is the main stand-out here. In Sea of Stars, when enemies (Any enemy, not just bosses) are charging up a special move, 'locks' will appear on screen above their head, along with a number indicating how many 'actions' (Not turns) you can execute before that special goes off. The tactics come from figuring out the right combination of abilities, spells, combos, and characters to use to break those 'locks' and interrupt the spell. You won't always be able to, and that's actually one of my main critiques of the game. Even in scripted encounters where you only have specific characters, the game sometimes throws locks at you that are genuinely impossible to break. However, you usually at least reduce the damage based on the number of lock sigils you bust, so it's still worth trying in many cases.

Figure out the right combinations of abilities, spells, and attacks to use to stop an enemy from unleashing their deadly (or just annoying) ability on you, and be rewarded by interrupting their attack and skipping their turn entirely.

The Visuals

The pixel art and pixel animation in SoS is world-class, in my opinion. I don't think I've seen it done better -- at least, not substantially so -- in any other game of its kind. Animations are smooth yet retro, and both the environments and characters are wonderfully detailed. The lighting is gorgeous, and adds a modern twist to the aesthetic.

The Music

I thought the music was quite beautiful, but I don't have much else to say about it. There were maybe 3 or 4 tracks that I felt really stood out and made me go 'Wowwww...', but the rest were just cool, or pretty, or whatever. I dunno, I'm not very good at describing music in games. I just know when it's done REALLY well, and I don't think, for the most part, SoS is best-in-class in the audio department -- there is no true 'Corridors of Time' here, at least in my opinion.

Completionist-Friendly

Sea of Stars is a very completionist-friendly game. At one point in the game, you can get a parrot that will squawk at you on the world map when you click on a region to tell you if there's anything you missed there, and what exact sub-region you can find it in. This, combined with a guide from IGN or YouTube, makes cleaning up collectibles in the post-game an absolute breeze.

At one point in the game, you also unlock a game-changing way to navigate the world, which is a MAJOR QOL bump for completionist-type players that hate having to trudge through the same areas over and over to find something they missed.

Simply by being thorough, you can find the vast majority of treasure and secrets in the game on your first playthrough -- and then the post-game can just be a few hours of clean-up for anything you missed.

Additionally, because of the way encounters are handled (there are none of the random JRPG encounters that I've grown to despise over the years), you can choose to avoid many fights if you wish; simply by running past enemies. This is great for completionists, because it means you don't need to slog through the same fights again and again, far past the end of the game, just to find everything.

The Ending

I... Was not satisfied by either of the endings to Sea of Stars. It's a real bummer, because I enjoyed the journey so much, but let's just say there's something I really wanted to do in both endings, and was unable to for reasons I can't understand. They left me wanting. They were NOT terrible, to be clear... Just not amazing.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is a love song to everything i loved about classic JRPGs. It feels like the best parts of Lunar:SSSC and Chrono Trigger, while having a fresh story that feels like it should be kinda cliche, but is engaging. Sea of Stars is letting me play it at my own pace, and there are in game 'Relics' that let you adjust the difficulty settings to what you prefer. There's one to boost hp, there's one to lower shop prices, there's even a relic to boost enemy HP and make the battles more difficult and strategic. If you're into that kinda thing :P

I'm casual, I play games to relax and have fun, I'm enjoying the easy settings because while battles and levels aren't exactly useless, it's the puzzles and the exploration that i'm really enjoying. It's the characters interacting with the world, it's the absolutely gorgeous world itself. The Story has already thrown some curve balls that I've enjoyed XD.

The art in this game is a delight, a treat to see. I saw one reviewer ( I think on YT) mention that all the artwork was unique, and if it's not I haven't noticed. I've been looking, and every level and setting seems to be not only beautifully done, but there aren't a lot of repeated assets. It's the kind of attention to detail I don't think big gaming studios could do these days. In exploration I've already discovered at least two mini games, which I feel like if I end up growing burned out on the rest of the game, I'll be able to play with the mini games to chill and refresh. Backtracking isn't exactly high priority at the stage of the game I've made it to, and I'm pretty sure i'm nowhere near the end, but due to obligations such as work and sleep, I've had to limit myself in how long I spend playing. I wish I didn't have to XD. This game is a treat for those who enjoyed games like the classic Final Fantasys, Chronos Trigger, and Lunar: Silver Star Story Complete. Even if for some reason the game starts slacking off in the later stages, I consider it well worth it for the joy this game has given me so far. I've been exploring every map and figuring out the puzzles, and finding little rewards, a hidden treasure chest or some ingredients to cook with, when going out of my way to explore the whole area. And the Water effects 8D ... this game is so gorgeous. I don't think I can stress that enough. :3
