\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWith no ground control intercept, you'll spend an hour or more just searching for a target, and in VR you're at a strict disadvantage vs people with higher resolution monitors that can spot you more easily from further away (along with difficulties IFFing). Tank crew battles are superficial at best, leaving self-propelled tanks such as the SU-152 suffering from a crippling reload with an overkill gun, and no suitable building targets.

I found one server with in-game voice chat (SRS), frequently with no one on, which meant coordination was impossible. There are maybe 3 decently populated servers, typically running scenarios of different years, meaning you might log on, only to find no server running a scenario that even has the planes you want to fly at that time. The allied side has 1 fighter that can catch the majority of German planes which vastly outspeed the allied, which due to the aforementioned problem, means German players will engage you 1-2 times, and then simply outrun you because they know they can. Compared to DCS where even cold war aircraft have primitive heat-seekers that can punish someone trying to run away in a straight line, this has you crawling after the german player for tens of minutes, by which time another 3 german fighters will jump you. With no coordination, there are no formations of allied bombers with their fighter escorts (what these were designed for), meaning the german side has a steep advantage in this game, which is to all extents and purposes, a glorified War Thunder VR team deathmatch.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA long review from a 15 year combat sim veteran.

OK, So I've been playing IL2: Pacific Fighters since it came out in 2004. At the time, I was in middle school. I still have it installed and fly regularly in that sim today - its a classic and an absolute must have to this day for flight sim geeks.

That being said, I've wanted to fly the new one since Bodenplatte came out. I'm an American, so flying at least some American heavy metal is a must for me, not really because of 'Merica but more because over-armored over-armed pieces of steel have informed my flying style, and so I tend to not do as well in other types of aircraft. I didn't buy it for a long time because of minimum system requirements, which is the first heads up I'll give new players - getting this game running well takes some decent equipment. Also, while the game will let you fly with the mouse, note that the two major multiplayer servers require joysticks; and tbh you're not getting even a fraction of what this sim experience has to offer without at least a cheap stick like a logitech extreme 3d pro or something similar. If you're new to sims and want to make that choice, please be aware that if its not a twist rudder stick\\has rudder pedals, don't buy it, you need all three axis.

Going on - new user experience, as a very experienced simmer... is.... horrible. This is part and parcel with flight simming in general, when doing something as complicated as simulating air combat at any decent level, there are complexities which make picking up new titles frustrating. Finding what I needed setting wise was a long, frustrating process, but after a few days, I got everything with my logitech G940 settled.

And once I had.... WOW!

This sim is on an entirely higher level than its predecessor as far as bringing the particular quirks of an airplane to life. I spent some time trying to get my toebrakes to work on the Yak, for instance. Couldn't figure out why they weren't working, went down a deep rabbit hole and updated my sticks frimware etc. only to find that RUSSIAN PLANES DON'T HAVE TOEBRAKES! They use a lever that differentially applies braking according to rudder input. This and many other things that the previous title in the early 2000's glanced over are all present, and it feels amazing to learn these intricate details of oil cooler, radiator, water injection systems etc as you go from aircraft to aircraft. Startup as well can be quite complicated if you go for the full realistic settings, and even the quick start you have to know a few things to get the engines going.

Overall I'd say that this simulator approaches or exceeds the "study level sim" benchmark for all the aircraft I've flown so far. So much so that I quickly forgot my aircraft bias and fell in love with the Yak series 29.

Another note on the UI in this sim - you can view the "specifications" for each aircraft in the menu's, and will see the stalling speed in normal and landing configurations, best climb rate (though Vy itself is not listed, unfortunately) and other paramaters that are important to know and effectively fight with a plane right there in front of you. No need to test fly endlessly to build your own charts, the devs have the info right there. This shortens the time needed to learn how to dogfight with new planes a lot.

Flight model + damage model is amazing. Hit detection can be an issue with high ping servers, so pay attention to what you're getting in the server menu.

The online play has two main servers as I mentioned before, one of them is pretty good at making sure theres at least one airplane on each side people with only the base game can fly, the other isn't, but you can always get into a rather well populated experience at almost any hour of the day. People on the servers seem to be pretty decent at following rules of conduct and helpful if you run into something you don't understand. Great community!

If single player is more your speed, the single player career mode is one of the best "what its like to be a fighter pilot" modes on the market today. This isn't surprising considering this simulators lineage. Those DCS fans considering this game may want to get it just for that feature alone. I haven't made myself a commanding officer in any of my campaigns yet, so I don't know how much control you get over setting objectives, strategy etc. if you're in command, I don't think it comes close to the squadron commander mode in Mighty 8th though (in IL2's defense, neither does any other simulator made since, with the exception of the X-32\\35 JSF sim that came out in the early 2000's). Single player mode will be hampered for experienced pilots a little by pretty predictable AI, personally I'm underwhelmed I think the AI in the orig was a little more capable.

Overall, if you're a WWII aviation fan and want to really get to know these iconic airplanes this is a MUST HAVE. Be ready for that frustrating getting-to-know-you phase of a new sim, and when you're pounding the keyboard because something doesn't work remember - there might be historical reasons why what you're trying to do isn't working out. Startup in particular is a learning curve for those used to a more arcade-ish experience like the original IL2 had or WarThunder. Overall though, once you get past that initial shock to the system, this sim delivers and does it in spades!\t\t\t\t\t
