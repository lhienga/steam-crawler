\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIL-2 Sturmovik can certainly be quite expensive with all DLCs, but luckily the game is on sale quite a lot throughout the year. But believe me, it's totally worth the money.
If you've played War Thunder before, and you're done with people who one shot you with mouse and keyboard while you're using a joystick, this game is perfect for you.

In comparison IL-2 Sturmovik is a bit low pased, and less arcadey. It's important to learn how to fly each airplane, as each airplane works a bit differently. The easiest airplanes just have throttle, while the engineers did all the work for you (like the real deal), and the hardest airplanes require you to keep an eye on your throttle, RPM, mixture and oil/water radiators and temperature. Be sure to check out forums and youtube to educate yourself, and trial and error can get you far too, especially if you get the hang of it. It can be pretty rough, but the journey in the end will be worth it, as it's very satisfying to shoot down your first enemy in a multiplayer server.

Damage systems are also amazing, and you will ♥♥♥♥ your pants if you've been snuck up from behind and hear bullets explode in your canopy. You can black out from a heavy hit, only to wake up a few seconds later while seeing the ground spiral toward you while hearing your ears still ringing from the shock. Or, after the initial hit, you realize that the airplane has become a bit more noisy than usual, only to see that there's a giant hole in your cockpit.

Flight mechanics are also very realistic, as far as I can tell. Your ailerons can be shot off, making it impossible to roll. However, just like a real airplane, you can still roll the aircraft using your rudders and make it back home safely, if your wingmen saved your ass that is. Similary you can lose your tail, making the aircraft uncontrollable and forcing you to bail.

I highly recommend spending some time familiarizing yourself with the aircraft you like, and teaching yourself how to navigate without using GPS before hopping onto multiplayer. Most multiplayer servers don't show your position on the map (default button is O), so it requires you to do some VFR navigation. From there, expect to travel some time before finding action, or the action to find you. Keep your eyes open and keep scanning the horizon as enemies often look as small as pixels on your screen. When you find an enemy, try to stay in a favorable position, and wait for the right time to place your shot, as you won't be able to reload your guns. And pray, pray that there's no one that snuck up on you that will require you to get a fresh set of underpants!

Another tip for multiplayer; some people can seem to be a little bit toxic to new players, but don't give up! We all have to start somewhere and with my 156 hours I wouldn't dare call myself a pro. Berloga can be a good server for you to have an arcade like experience, allowing you to face other players in dogfights without losing team resources. Simulation servers often have a limited supply of aircraft available so unless you think you can help in the virtual war effort, it might be a good idea to gain experience in servers with unlimited aircraft. :)

Another thing I highly recommend, not just for IL-2 but for simulation games in general, is a TrackIR.
Delanclip is an affordable option, which works perfectly on this game too. It will allow you to look around in your cockpit and behind you using your head.

Also, joystick. Although there is mouse control for this game, a lot of servers require you to use a joystick. I won't be judging you if you use it, okay maybe a little bit, but this game just feels amazing with a joystick.


I hope to see you in the skies!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOne of the best PCVR experiences I've had. With a VR headset (Quest 2 and virtual desktop) and HOTAS (T1600M HOTAS) the immersion level is really high. High resolution cockpit textures make all the instruments clear and easy to read, despite them being in foreign languages.

I've not got an insane amount of hours in the game like most other reviews and actually I gave up on this before trying it again with a VR headset and that is when the sim finally clicked for me. Which is strange because I thought off the get-go I would love this sim considering I enjoy WW2 history (especially the Eastern Front and enjoy old warbirds).

The moment this all really came together for me was in an early career mission I was piloting the BF109-G2 doing a bomber intercept sortie. By this point I had learned the basics of flight control in the 109-G2 and was almost dangerous. Well, anyways, my small squadron of four fighters intercepted the targets somewhere over the Stalingrad area and began our intercepts of the targets. We successfully repelled the bomber force but during the combat a lucky gunner had struck the engine of my fighter and damaged the oil system. This caused the windscreen to become caked with a cooked black ichor and gradually got worse and worse as we returned to our airfield to the point at which visibility was significantly reduced.

That's when it dawned on me "wait, im playing in VR, I can pop open teh canopy and peer around!" So, I backed off the throttle some, and began a descent to low altitude. Once I felt it was safe I popped open teh canopy and poked my head out to see where the heck I was going.. But, the sim had other ideas! the oil spewing from my damaged engine was now splatting all over my goggles and I still couldnt see squat!

Somehow, I managed to navigate half blind back to the friendly airfield and began my approach for landing. While on base approach I noticed the air-raid sirens blaring from the airfield and thought "huh, thats strange" and then the reason for those sirens became dangerously apparent! Somehow a couple of Russian fighters had appeared out of the blue and were attempting to engage my significantly slowed, shot up, and lone fighter! Flak began bursting around the scene as the ground support opened fire and I instinctively throttled up to try and gain airspeed, engage in a climb, and fight back.

Flak took care of one of the fighters while I began aerial maneuvers to fight off the remaining plane. All the while still with my severely obscured visibility. I was able to land enough lead into the opposing plane that it broke off and raced away. But by this time I was now several kilometers away from the airfield. Once again I had to limp myself back and attempt to land half blind and with a ticking time bomb spinning the propeller.. Not sure how I managed to get the bird down, but I did. Coincidentally this was the FIRST landing that I managed to maintain acceptable approach speed and altitude and proper application of the wheel brakes which resulted in a safe landing. This was truly an awesome experience that had my adrenaline going and for that brief moment I had never felt more immersed in a sim before.

If you've got the patience to spend a few hours learning the basics and the hardware (HOTAS/VR) then this is a must have.

The sim base game routinely goes on sale and can be had for a VERY affordable price, so what are you waiting for?!\t\t\t\t\t
