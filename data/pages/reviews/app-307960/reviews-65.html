\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI must admit, I almost didn't purchase IL-2: BoS due to the mixed reviews it had received. When it went on sale a while back, I took a chance on buying and I'm quite glad that I did. I played the original IL-2 series for years... from the original to Pacific Fighters to 1946. Now, the IL-2 Great Battles series, beginning with Battle of Stalingrad, feels like a natural progression from the original series. It was a relatively accessible sim back in the day, and while the complexity of things going on "under the hood" has increased, the sim remains forgiving. Multiple assist options are available to build confidence before venturing into the realm of "full real"

Some points compared to IL-2 1946:
The graphical fidelity was obviously increased as well as audio, it looks good and sounds good.

The flight dynamics were improved as well as individual aircraft system modeling... the developers have gone to great efforts to do each aircraft justice.

The damage modeling was improved as well, and even more complexity to the model is in development.

Now, one of my original complaints for the Great Battles series was the AI being passive and not really interesting to fight at all compared to the much older IL-2 1946. But this too has undergone continuous improvement over multiple game patches. The AI now has a good range of capabilities... both in defensive and offensive maneuvers and tactics and gunnery skill.

Another minor criticism is a lack of 4-engine bombers, (IE: Targets), but this is forgivable due to the time and resources necessary to fully model such aircraft and their systems.

IL-2 has received support from the developers for years and continues to be improved upon with feedback from the community.

Now, I have seen many complaints regarding pricing and I feel the need to address this issue. The major modules typically include 8 flyable aircraft that are meticulously modeled, a large map to cover the area of operations and a career campaign. For the price I believe this is a reasonable offer especially considering that each module's content is essentially at the level of a stand-alone game. Compare this to another flight sim that offers a *single aircraft* for a similar price... with campaigns, maps, and even ground targets sold sperately. Yes, the other sim has clickable cockpits, which is nice... but when I am in battle, do I really care about that? \r\n\t\t\t\t\tWell, this is a difficult one. The game isn't bad, but eh... well read on.

This is a package that does not work out of the box. There is no tutorial and you're going to be sitting there scratching your head in confusion and frustration trying to get your controls set up properly initially. This is for a number of reasons, not least because many of the keyboard binds for certain commands (like the airbrake) are mapped to something ridiculously unwieldly like left shift or left alt + whatever. But fine, it's manageable, right?

Remapping keyboard binds is a pain in the backside because the settings menu will not even tell you if a certain key is in use when you map it to a command, nevermind what command that key is mapped to. So therefore, if you want to change anything, you have to add a keybind, check the entire controls list in 6 different sub-menus for a conflict, delete that conflicting keybind and then rebind that to the same effect... and so on. For a game with this many things to map out, where the default bindings for a conventional joystick like mine are hot garbage, this is pretty daft.

As for the actual game itself, there are quick missions, which let you make simple engagements with a few drop down boxes and let you adjust the circumstances (i.e whether your flight is pursuing, taking head-on, altitude, time of day, etc). These are eh, well, fine. It's kind of like just pasting aircraft in the Arma Editor with simple waypoints. There's no context or build-up so it's kind of flat.

The career mode is the complete opposite extreme, where you can and will be sent on bomber-escort missions, which even when supposedly sped up by 8x with the time dilation (which doesn't really work, but anyway) can easily amount to sitting and doing absolutely nothing for half an hour. Mission starts, you press the autopilot key, the autopilot flies you in formation for 15 minutes in a weaving pattern above some bombers until they reach the target. Then the same happens on the way back. Sometimes you'll get intercepted by enemy aircraft.

And as with any mission, sometimes the enemy don't show up at all and this is entirely deliberate by the dev's own admission. In which case you just wasted half an hour of your life that you'll never get back for no other reason than the game wanted to rub in your face how authentic and historically accurate it is.

You can't save the game state. There are no checkpoints. If you quit the mission while your aircraft is over enemy territory your career savefile is now over and you are "captured." If you speed the game up, the volume cuts out, so you won't get an audio prompt that the enemy have actually appeared this time. The only visual indication of enemy contacts approaching is a brief radio message on the bottom left of the screen and a small red arrow around the hud. The game doesn't pause if this happens.

This is incredibly dumb game design.

But, the actual dogfighting, flight mechanics, graphics, aircraft models, etc is enjoyable and robust. The part that's supposed to be fun is actually good, which is kind of important. It's just that the rest of the package is severely lacking.\t\t\t\t\t
