\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI bought this despite having an inadequate PC for max settings. That said, I've made it playable, if not pretty. It also works with my 20 year old stick, which is a plus.

My first impressions (having played other flight sims, including the original Sturmoviks) are good. Everything feels very realistic, from the flight model, to the cockpit details, damage modelling, and ballistics. The sound quality (engine, weapon, wind and turbulence) is excellent.

So far I've only faced off against average-quality AI. It seemed adequately (for its level) competent, but a better test will be at veteran and above.

My only complaint so far is the common one: lack of plane variety. While I'm aware of the extremely narrow audience in flight sims and the necessary DLC system to make any game like this profitable, I still dislike the base game fighter selection. On the German side, the 109-F4 (an excellent version of the 109, thankfully) is nearly identical to the only other German fighter, the 109-G2. Same armament, largely similar handling and speed. On the Russian side, while the Yak-1 is essentially Russia's low-altitude equivalent to the 109, the LaGG-3 is quite awful, and in my opinion, a waste of space in such a limited roster. The Russian joke about LaGG initials was something roughly translated to 'Guaranteed Lacquered Coffin'.

Overall: clearly a great game, just lacking selection. Buy the base game on sale to see if you like it, and then invest in the DLC.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhen it comes to flight simulators and this is a WWII military flight simulator, so to speak, one needs to be able to distinguish between two major aspects, one being game play, the other, flight simulation.

I have not used this much, so I cannot say much about the entire game play, however for the little time that I used it, I can make a premature review on the aspect of flight simulation. I have been aware of this game for quiet some time, however I use various other flight simulators for pilot training purposes, such as X-Plane 11 with VR and Microsoft Flight Simulator X/2004, mainly these two, which are great tools for flight training when one cannot fly in real.

I eventually got down to buying this on a major sell. Don't get me wrong, I do think this game is worth the full price, however I was in no rush to buy this as I already have the mentioned tools that did cost me some money (£55 for X-Plane 11), so I was kinda waiting for this sale, that arrived. I got it for just a penny short of £10.

Played it for less than an hour and I think this is a very good and realistic enough flight simulation of the amazing WWII planes. Obviously, you need to set the setting to most realistic, to begin with. I have never flown in a real Me-109, hence I cannot tell you literally how realistic replication of the real machine this is, but the actual visual feedback of the motion on the ground and air, does appear this simulator is more towards realism than not, despite I would say that X-Plane 11 is more realistic and no wonder as it is also used commercially in professional flight simulators (the big cages that train airliner pilots). The fact that I was able to land this plane and ended up toppling this plane over is a sign of realism, as I had no prior training in landing tail wheel planes.

To conclude, if you are looking for a WWII flight simulation that aims towards realism, I recommend this to you. If you are looking to just have some fun playing a game and are not interested to fly real aircraft, then you probably better buying some arcade type of combat flight sim, like hhmmm, Call of Duty, the ones you can "fly" in. Call of Duty is a joke, I mean, this game is for kids, to begin with a soldier carrying heavy equipment and a machine gun that can weight up to 10kg and to be able to jump in the air over a meter and to keep on doing that over and over again without ever getting tired, is a JOKE. I guess, some people like to waste time on jokes. This game is not a JOKE. Enjoy it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAn all around good combat flight sim. A bit expensive for newcomers like me, but I found it is worth it when on sale. Joystick is a must though.

**Edit after playing 40 hours of the game:

The dlc practices for this game and for current gen simulators in general is excessive. But quite frankly all the time I put into this game was pretty much with a single plane (the f4) on the main game (battle of Stalingrad). I had a blast the whole time. I haven't even finished the career yet and I do plan on playing multiple careers with the other planes. So I do find that even with the base planes from the main game, there is still a lot of value there. Also, the general comments on the career missions being a bit boring sometimes are true. But I do like the balance of calmer missions and heart pounding missions.

I also played a couple of coop missions with friends. You need to be able to port forward which is just as much a pain in the ass as it was back in 2004, but the hours it took to get it working was really worth it. Some of the grand scale battles that are possible in this game are really fun with a couple of squad mates and you can even multicrew the basic tanks in the base game (check out the burning steppe coop mission on the main forums). I also can't wait to check out the tactical air war server for dynamic multiplayer missions.

All in all, even with the base game there is a lot of value here and if you like learning something that is quite difficult (ie landing a plane or shooting those damn "why are they always faster and turning like crazy" Yaks) and if you keep in mind that this isn't an arcade game where you can just get in combat without any practice (or without any joystick), then this may be the game for you. It's a sandbox, so you kind'of have to make your own fun. I am just surprised how much fun I have had with just learning this one basic plane.

As simulator games go, this one is pretty high on my list... I just hope it will get more combined arms in the future ;)\t\t\t\t\t
