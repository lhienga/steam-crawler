\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's a decent racing game. It has a lot of good tracks and car controls feel great. It's way better than some other games I've tried so far, but it's not something I'd recommend.

Controls in UI are a mess. The same button copies and deletes in different context and there's no undo. It’s frustrating as hell. Two different buttons are used for accepting when starting a race. I keep entering the wrap editor instead of starting the race. The person responsible for that should be fired.

Speaking of wrap editor, the idea is great, execution poor. It's great that there's a way to make cars look custom, but bad controls in the editor are a nuisance and make it difficult to create a cool wrap design.

Despite the wrap editor the cars don't feel personal at all. You unlock them for free. There’s no sense of owning them. No upgrade system, no tuning, nothing. Unlocks are too often, making them feel worthless. Getting showered with new and new cars (sometimes several at a time after finishing a single race) prevents you from getting really familiar with them. It feels like you just try one car once and then yeet it away because there are so many others. Then you forget which one was actually a good one and which had a way worse handling.

Equipment is useless. EMPs and spike strips miss, road blocks block only you, helicopter misses and hits you instead. It’s frustrating rather than fun. It kills the joy.

Unnecessary cutscenes before starting a race are long. Even when skipped, you have to wait because there's a couple of seconds of a cutscene that cannot be skipped. It adds up when retrying the race over and over to get the gold and becomes very annoying.

Lack of any sort of upgrade system or story makes this game pointless to play. There are no stakes, no goal. Progression doesn’t feel rewarding at all. You only collect meaningless medals. Once you've unlocked the unlockable things all races feel the same, only with artificially inflated speed to make you pick other car with better stats.

This game doesn't have what I'm looking for.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'd gladly defend the original Hot Pursuit as best realized modern Need for Speed to date. It's hard to believe that the franchise was in an even worse state than it is now. After ProStreet and Undercover failed to maintain the momentum of the Golden Era™ (2003-2006), the series was close to being scrapped. Publisher EA panicked, scrambling together additional studios. Need for Speed became both a simcade and a Nintendo-exclusive racer for a little while, giving godfather studio Black Box an extended development window to get their ♥♥♥♥ together (they didn't). In hindsight, this entry might be the reason the franchise still exists, but I'd rather visit the alternative timeline where the team in Guildford stuck to Burnout.

Criterion's Fate

Making video games is almost impossible, simultaneously dealing with upper-management makes it a nightmare. I don't know too much about how Criterion's Hot Pursuit came to be, but the end-product is magnificent. Following tough years, the franchise was finally revitalized and brought back to the original vision with additional, literal weapons. Simplicity is it's strongest asset, but that doesn't mean there's nothing beneath the surface. Criterion's mantra at the time consisted of making games that were easy to pick-up, but hard to master. This was Burnout's secret ingredient after all, explaining why Hot Pursuit followed the same recipe.

Playing it all these years later, having revisited Criterion's previous games, the resemblance to Burnout 3 is obvious. From the presentation of events, scoring system, event types, routes, progression, attitude towards cars, handling, crash-cams and even the signature Takedown-feature, slapping the Need for Speed name on the box would seem strange on the outside looking in. The truth is that Need for Speed had always centered around the driving fantasy. Fast cars, beautiful environments and pursuing cops were the pillars of the franchise, before Underground introduced me to Lil' John, telling me to 'skeet skeet skeet' to... something? I did not know, for I was a kid.

Having played Hot Pursuit 2 on the GameCube back in the day, I couldn't be more confused with the title when it first got announced in 2010. Still, this looked promising. Everyone's favorite developer of racing games was leading the charge to bring Need for Speed back. Commercially and critically, Hot Pursuit exceeded expectations, meaning Criterion would remain developer of the series for the foreseeable future (lol) and expecting that Black Box would deliver something similar a year later (lol).

Continued Curse

The 2020 Remaster is alright, in the sense that I don't feel it's existence is justified. There are a couple of new features though, bringing a Livery Editor, revamped UI, additional required storage space and anti-aliasing. The inclusion of the DLC cars is cool, but this still is the same fantastic Hot Pursuit from almost fifteen years ago. The graphical updates are minimal, only having noticed additional lens flares and shinier cars, but I get that this Remaster serves a greater good.

Stellar Entertainment has previously remastered Burnout Paradise, providing similar improvements. That one didn't really get any additional features, so seeing the Livery Editor here was quite shocking, positively speaking. Both games run on the fantastic, yet antiquated Renderware-tech, which, I feel, makes the addition more impressive. While I am curious about their next project, I pray that the development of both remasters mean that Stellar have the experience and knowledge to bring back old-Criterion's style of racing, forever separating it from Need for Speed.

As the difference in quality between the original Hot Pursuit and Black Box's follow-up couldn't be any bigger, the series basically felt like Burnout for the latter third of it's lifetime. While Need for Speed has identified itself, yet again, in urban car culture, the question remains if the current Criterion is physically able to ditch the infamous brake-to-drift driving-model, first introduced by their predecessors in this legendary, cursed title.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAfter having played both the original and the Remaster, I can confidently say the original is better in some aspects:

The original is much more stable and you don't encounter as many bugs as you do in the remastered version (clipping through objects, re-spawning after a crash and then your car floats for a couple of seconds, random freezes on modern hardware due to the mess that is the EA launcher, etc.)

Graphics-wise, no improvements, not at all a remaster. In some cases, it's actually worse (rock textures look worse for example). At least they managed to keep all the licensed songs in the game, unlike other developers (I'm looking at you, Rockstar).

It feels more like a quick cash grab than anything, considering it's normal price (30 euros at the moment of writing this review). Since the original isn't available to buy anymore, if you consider this game, pick it up while it is on sale. Otherwise, sail the high seas.\t\t\t\t\t
