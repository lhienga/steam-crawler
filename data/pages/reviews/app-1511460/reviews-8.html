\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDespite this game being listed as "full release", it plays and feels like early access.

Its a city builder with complex traffic and resource flow, yet lacks basic tools to control them. A good example is storage - you can construct a "warehouse" to store your resources, but this warehouse doesnt distribute said resources back into your network. So you can use stored resources to build stuff, but if something requires that resource for production, you cant use it - you have to create it all over. Its the easiest thing to fix, and its so dumb that its designed this way.

Traffic control is a nightmare. I have plenty of experience with games like Cities Skylines etc, so its not an issue of "you just need to learn how to control traffic". No, its an issue of "The transport AI is so ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ dumb, it will create traffic jams even if you give it absolutely perfect roads". And if you decide to micromanage, prepare to have to manually click every single crossroad because there's no way to save or carry over your signal settings.

And dont even get me started on upgrading roads or rails. It seems the game flips the coin which direction the road will be when upgraded - meaning if you have a two-sided road that you replace with one sided, it will often interweave back and forth on the same road, resulting in entire road being unusable and you having to dig it up and redo from scratch.

Oh and the "deletion" tool, to remove unwanted buildings? Not only does it not have any confirmation box, it also is "see through". What do i mean by see through? It means that if you try to delete a road, and there's a house behind the road, it will delete the road AND the house. And, once again, it wont ask you for any confirmation. It will just delete it.

There's no voice overs, no ingame events, no story. The background music consists of a single tune that keeps looping and honestly drove me insane.

All in all, this would have been a promising game, if it was early in development. But for a full release? Absolutely not recommended. If you have an itch to scratch, go play Factorio, Dyson Sphere Program, Cities Skylines, Anno or literally anything else.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe idea of the game is fun but there is no agency or challenge in the game. As you have near infinite resources every problem is solved by just placing more buildings with no downsides. power is a main resource but is as infinite as concrete and steel.

Game crashes often when upgrading roads or placing long pipe runs.
The tooltips are not new player friendly and looking for icon descriptions can take a bit. (devs said they will be working on this)

Traffic is never an issue since you can have your populous locked in a 1 way suburb that only accepts deliveries to upgrade the homes. (people do not need access to the industrial buildings they just work with a number simulation)

The biggest gameplay downsides are trains. Trains should be able to replace most roadways and enable long distance districting and distribution but station management is horrendous. you can't edit a route without deleting and re-adding multiple stops. the stations cant be upgraded only deleted and replaced.. which also breaks all the routes. tracks are somehow only 1 way which for most applications in game and the real world are not useful. most of my trains only go to 2 or 3 stations so needing a looping track for everything is cumbersome. it also forces the stations to have way more tracks than they need as there is no left / right toggle for the station you get both 1 way directions even if you only use 1. there is also some bugs with stations or trains where a building is pending a stack of deliveries but the items just stay in the station and the building will never get items again. (might be affected when adding or removing trains/ routes)

the distribution center and collection center didnt make much sense to me and both were pretty buggy from what i expected to happen with traffic.

I like that this is a simple city builder but it doesn't feel like you are barely surviving on this planet, resource scarcity and management is the core component in games like this and even the management here is just a slider that randomly disables a % of specific industrial buildings to save people or power for another task. design and types of buildings are great but the core gameplay loop doesn't evolve from spamming buildings.

positives are: buildings work with each other if they are close enough to get bonuses, that is very cool. the resources mostly manage themselves with each building having internal buffers for import and export. overall progression make sense (though most is everything unlocks too fast)
no real issues with building or pathing.

in EA Devs added new stuff and fixed a ton of issues but i don't believe it is finished yet, so im excited to see what gets changed in the next 6 months\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI want to like this game, but it is so ... boring. No challenge at all.

- Building cost is minimal and the building is instantly build. So if you produce to less of a ressource: Just build another 10 factories instant, you always have enough space.
- If you have traffic problems: Just build another more direct road, you have enough space.
- The game is a number simulation. There are no immversive animations or interaction e.g. you see no people. Ever. The people don't have to drive to work, so the distance of a production building doesn't hinder it's production output.
- No upkeep costs. Really?

The game is so boring it all comes down to: Produce more, so you can have more colonists (I don't see any!) so you can build more factories. The only thing that happens on the screen are moving trucks...
If you make a game no challenging at all, at least make it beautiful to look at. But the graphics are so minimalistic and there is just nothing you can look at.

If you want a real surviving Simulation on another Planet, I can recommend Surviving Mars. Beautiful Animations, very good immersion and realism.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI bought this because I loved the developer's first game, Founders' Fortune, and although it's a very different game and IMO neither as charming nor as addictive, it is similarly well executed. At first glance it's Anno 2070 without the culture wars but there's a much more complicated logistics side to it, and a huge technology tree. Managing and distributing resources could easily become quite tedious but this game seems to get around that well, and the automation of deliveries using roads, trains and cargo gondolas works nicely, with about the right level of player intervention. Other than the occasionally wiggly rail lines it did what I wanted it to do. The user interface is intuitive enough but there's still a bit of a learning curve. It would be good it if was possible to expand stations without having to demolish them first, but that's my only real complaint.
As with any game like this it's probably just a case of working your way up the technology tree and building every kind of building then moving on to another game. There's nothing particularly ground-breaking about it but I think it's good for several hours of gameplay, and it seems fairly priced for what it is.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game has promise: the systems seem to be there, with more than enough complexity and options to make for an interesting city-builder that's focused more on puzzling out optimization than trying to make things pretty and livable.

The main issue is that the presentation is at best bland, and at worst frustrating. There is a lot of information missing in the UI, a lot of places where tooltips could be a lot better, or where the game could tell you what things are missing. Most buildings don't have proper tooltips, so I'm still confused what a "distribution center" is supposed to do; things you haven't yet unlocked appear in the buildings menu anyway, but you can't mouse over them and see what research they need to unlock them.

Basic things like resource icons actually telling you what they're called is not there. And I spent a good few minutes looking for grey rocks on the light brown map, because there seems to be no way to just get an overlay for natural resources; you'd think the game would be nice enough to highlight iron for you when you're trying to build an iron mine.

There is an overlay for producers and consumers of a specific resource, but I've found no way to access it, except by just finding a building that makes that resource (can't do it from the consumer side) and clicking the little button from there; with all the overlay menus you'd think they'd put this in there too.

And for overall production balancing, there is a fairly nice menu that has a list of what goods are produced, what is lacking, etc. But you have to keep opening and closing it, since it's kind of in the way otherwise... but you also kind of want to see that information all the time, since balancing production is kind of your main job, at least early on.

I ultimately gave up when trying to build a curved road, and for some reason every time I connected it to an existing road it got weirdly inverted. All I wanted was to connect two bits of road in the middle of nowhere by a nice curve so it wasn't a weird angle between two straight lines... but apparently that was too much to ask for.

Hopefully they'll polish up all this stuff and the game can become actually enjoyable to play... though honestly, they really should have done that during Early Access.\t\t\t\t\t
