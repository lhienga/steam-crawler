\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTLDR version:

If not for the multiplayer with my friends, I would have refunded.

Reasons:
- Too many bugs and crashes for a triple-A game
- The main antagonists are the most annoying and unlikeable characters ever
- Map design and some of the UI is a step (or rather, a giant leap) back from previous games
- Devs clearly thought 'more is better', and couldn't have been more wrong
- Attempt to appeal to broader audience, potentially alienating original players

Long version:
By far the buggiest AAA game I have ever played. I even waited 4 years so that all the stupid DLC was available in a nice bundle instead of having to spend double what you paid for the base game, and yet it is still full of random bugs and crashes, even some that were already reported on forums in 2019 and 2020.

The twin villains are, much like real life influencers, extremely annoying and just general ****s, so I suppose they nailed that. Getting to finally shoot them in the face was probably the most satisfying thing in the entire game, but unfortunately that is not saying much. Getting to the end is just tedious and boring, in no small part due to the map design. Whereas previous games had a fairly good distribution of fast travel points and shops, here most levels have only one set of those at the start, and that's it. Respawn points are spread out well enough, but that is but a meager consolation.

Which brings us to the next issue, the devs' attitude that 'more equals better right?' (Spoiler: it does not). The maps are usually enormous, but extremely linear. The lack of regular fast travel points means you have to slog through all the enemies again if you ever have to come back to that map. Incidentally, those enemies also respawn a lot faster than in previous games, which adds even more to the tediousness. They have tried to fill in the massive maps with all sorts of side missions and raid bosses, but variety is rather limited.

Furthermore, the product description contains the phrase "..., packing bazillions of guns and ...". As soon as you see that sort of stuff in marketing, you just know they needed more filler to act as a disguise to make it seem like something impressive. If you look beyond that though, you'll quickly realise it is a very thin veil indeed.

As for the UI, the thing that bothers me the most are the rotating numbers for what a particular item is worth. Whoever decided that was a good idea must still be living in the 18th century, because nowadays, ain't nobody got time for that.

There is however a good explanation for all of those issues. Development efforts were obviously focussed on adding a reference to every. ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥. Second. Of. Every. Borderlands game. Ever. Including quite a few obscure charaters whose names and faces I could not remember, nor care less about as they add very little, if anything, to the story.

Final point of criticism is not for the devs, but for the management and share holders, as I am guessing the decision to makes changes to previously established conventions is mainly their doing. I don't think adding female enemies, notably psychos, is necessarily bad, but it is rather remarkable that it happened in these sensitive (aka"woke") times. Same with the 'Midget' enemies. I understand some may find that term offensive, so changing it could make sense (although 'Tink' is a stupid replacement in my opinion, I suggest something like 'Mini Psycho' or 'Pocket Psycho'). Perhaps these things should have been implemented from the start, as taking them away now sort of feels like losing some of the rude charm of previous titles. And question remains, will it make those offende people suddenly change their mind and buy the game, or will it only alienate the original player base?

I wouldn't call myself a Borderlands fan by any means, if it weren't for the friends I play multiplayer with I probably wouldn't have even touched any of the games. But if there ever is a proper Borderlands 4, I think I'll pass, multiplayer or not.
