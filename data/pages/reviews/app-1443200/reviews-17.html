To be clear - the protagonist is the most sane character in the whole game. Which is to say, she's pretty messed up from a load of trauma her family and peers have bestowed on her. She's also not a Good Person. But as a teenager, she's developing coping mechanisms, which just happen to include drug abuse, manipulation and sarcasm.

You're not meant to wholeheartedly agree with the protagonist on everything, but if you have any empathy in you, after finishing the game you will at least see why she would behave the way she does. The game manages to be both lighthearted and heavy at the same time. If you're not having a small existential crisis after finishing the game, you either weren't paying attention, or you did have gym coach walk into the dressing room to harass you and you did your time in therapy already.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tClass of '09 takes place at a typical high school in the eastern United States during 2007 to 2009. You play as Nicole, a student who is openly sociopathic, manipulative, dishonest, and self-centered. Having had to move again recently, she has to get used to this new school but finds that it is full of students, teachers, and administrators that are no better than she is.

This game is a visual novel which means it is more about reading the story. You can make choices along the way that dictate the course of the story. There are a fair number of choices to make and there are ultimately fifteen different endings to achieve. I would estimate that it would take around three or four hours to see everything that this game has to offer.

To start with some of the positive aspects of this game, I thought that the voice acting is outstanding. This game is fully voiced and I found that all of the voice actors did a superb job of portraying their characters. While the voice acting typically sticks to the various stereotypes of the characters, such as the jock in Kylar, the anime nerd in Jeffery, and the valley girl in Jecka, it still works well. The voice actors were able to excellently add to the personalities of the various characters with their work.

The artwork was rather mixed. As is generally common in visual novel games, the design of the characters sticks to a standard anime appearance which does not really stand out as either terrible or exceptional. The background art is mostly nonexistent as it consists of little more than blurred photographs. There are only a few CGs in the gameplay itself, though most of the endings have their own artwork. One good aspect of the artwork is how most characters, especially the students, have multiple outfits which is a nice touch and not something that I usually see in visual novels. By far the worst part of the artwork is the sheer laziness of the design of the female characters. Nearly all of the female characters have the same porn star physique and suffer from same face syndrome.

As with most visual novels, the main attraction is the writing and characters. This is where the game really fell flat for me. While I can appreciate the fact that this story is supposed to be a brutal satire of the some of the worst aspects of the high school experience, I found that its depiction of this through comedy was so exaggerated and over-the-top that I just stopped caring and it quickly got tiring. Nicole is a character that is awful because of the traumatic experiences that she has had, but she still has almost no redeeming qualities and the only reason the player to supposed to root for her is that most other people she meets are even worse. Every character is intentionally written as being a horrible person, whether it means a pedophilic or racist teacher, an uncaring principal, a fetish-obsessed nerd, among others. But it just becomes tiring when every storyline and joke is shock humor. One storyline involves a teacher being outed as a white nationalist, but by the time I had gotten to this storyline I had already sat through literally dozens of jokes about rape, pedophilia, drug use, and so on, that I had already gotten tired of the game's repetitive tone and the jokes had no bite anymore. When everything is meant to be shocking, nothing is. I do admit that I had a few laughs while going through this, especially with the excellent voice acting giving the often ridiculous lines a fun reading. But these moments were too far and few between to make it overall enjoyable.

I also find it odd that one of the selling points of this game is that it is explicitly a period piece taking place in the late 2000s. This caught my eye as I actually was a high school student during the 2000s. But I found that this aspect was barely utilized with little more than the general lack of smartphones, use of the word "gay" as a general insult, which was common at the time, and a couple of references to public figures prominent at the time like Barack Obama and Soulja Boy. So much more could have been done from this perspective.

Finally, I must also comment on the technical aspects of this game. This has to be one of the worst designed visual novels that I have ever played. It is standard in visual novels to allow the player to progress the lines manually with autoplay as an option. Here, there is only autoplay with only options to either pause or go back. Why not implement standard visual novel features? Sometimes I want to wait before moving on to the next piece of dialogue for whatever reason. Why not give the player this option?

In conclusion, I wanted to like this game because it has an interesting premise of something that could make for great satire and comedy, especially for a period piece taking place in the late 2000s. There are some positive aspects such as the excellent voice acting. But this experience fell flat with its lazy art and overreliance on shock humor. It is a cheap game and might entertain you for a few hours, but it is not something for everyone.\t\t\t\t\t
