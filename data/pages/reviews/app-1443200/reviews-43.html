\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCrass and unrelentingly funny, Class of '09 is a game that has stuck around my head-space a lot longer than I expected, especially for something that I picked up on a whim because of some gameplay clips on YouTube. This is entirely thanks to the incredible dialogue, terrific voice cast, and distinct protagonist being able to create some of the most striking and memorable scenes from a video game I've played in recent years.


There's a fear here, like many period pieces, of the game risking being alienating because of how dependent it is on its setting. But despite being 10 years too young to be a class of '09er myself, the dialogue perfectly and naturally evokes the feeling of a North American high school of really any time in the last 30 years. This isn't so much to do with the slang or tone of the language (though both of these help), but rather the decision to just have characters talk about the most random ♥♥♥♥ at any given time. Plenty of conversations in this game are just people saying stupid things because they were chasing some impulsive thought in their head. This offers no service to traditional story elements like plot or character development, but does perfect the setting by accurately reflecting the high school trademark of "no tact" and "no ♥♥♥♥♥ to give". It somehow manages to create an odd nostalgia in me for a time that never existed, that didn't really exist for anyone if I had to guess; the situations in this game are so out-there I'd be concerned if they accurately reflected anyone's high school experience...

...but there are enough elements that you will relate to! That you see, and grasp, and say "That person's just like X" or "God, teacher Y did that at my school, yikes"! These short straws of relatability sort of trick your brain into thinking this was your high school experience and creates nostalgia over that.

Basically, this game makes me wish I did drugs in high school.


Enough praises cannot be sung about this game's voice cast, which is I'd argue solidly makes the best delivery of casual conversations in any game ever. I see of lot of disbelief online over how high-profile the voice cast is here, especially for what is at first glance a rushed VN with still character portraits. But when playing it's clear the creator cared very deeply about the audio experience of the player, so it makes sense that a lot of effort was put into finding good talent for the game. There's no music, but the speed of dialogue is so fast and natural that it would be distracting if it was there, just like any real conversation. Instead it's a barrage of great voice lines punctuated by the rare sound effect that naturally fits whatever the characters are doing in the scene and adds to the experience. The only sound I don't like is this weird "boom" that sometimes plays when characters deliver a "heavy" line; it completely removes you from the game, feels needlessly unsubtle, and sticks out all the more so because it's (I think) the only non-ambient sound effect.


As much as the misplaced marketing would have you believe otherwise, Class of 09 has a very typical VN story structure: you make choices to get different endings. The underlying stories are good, the style feels closest to South Park, entirely verbal setup-punchline comedy alongside some crazy ♥♥♥♥ happening. All of which POV character (and self-proclaimed abysmal ♥♥♥♥♥) Nicole sort of just apathetically shrugs off.

However, all stories are not created equal here, because while every path is guaranteed to have at least a few funny scenes, your choices might lead to inferior play experience. Furthermore, they aren't really meaningful choices that Nicole makes in the narrative, and instead are more ways of artificially branching the story because the author thought "it would be cool if this happened". It's very rare that you can predict what direction a choice will actually take the story since most major story beats and new plot elements happen outside of the few snapshots where you're given control. Some choices cut the game early and give you an ending just as you were getting invested, seemingly solely in an attempt to shock the player. This use of choices as a game element as opposed to a narrative element leads to both some good and some bad.

In some ways this is honestly a bit of a non-issue since I think most people who touch the game will play all paths anyways (it only takes ~3 hours to see every unique line of dialogue). But it is worth bringing up as a criticism since it makes the overall experience lean heavily on surprising the reader with sudden endings. The game also has a little bit of an immature "edge" to it; making morally sound decisions and generally being a good person will net you the worst endings, and vice versa. I don't hate this take for a high-school aged protagonist as cynical as Nicole, but it is a "gotcha" that gets weaker every time, and it leaves some predictability towards the general conclusion of your current path once you've caught on. In general, I do think the comedic elements in Class of '09 are significantly stronger than the dramatic ones.

The up-side to this is that Nicole has 100 times more personality and 100 times more agency than like, any VN protagonist, ever. The true "anti-visual novel" element of this game is not that you can't date people (or even that you reject them), but that the game tries it's best to challenge your connection to Nicole. The emotions while playing verge on uncomfortable sympathy. The player inherently roots for Nicole most of the time because of all the ♥♥♥♥ she has to take (oh boy does she take some ♥♥♥♥), but also is held hostage to witness Nicole just genuinely be an awful person outside of their control, and often off screen. There's a bit of a chicken-and-egg thought-experiment between Nicole's awful life and Nicole's awful personality that the game presents this idea as it's central theme, but it's complicated by the fact that this isn't all happening in one timeline. The game's protagonist operates so foreign to your wishes, even when you make express choices for her, that it solidifies her instead of you as the driving force of her own story. This is such an out-there move in a genre known for its self-insert OCs, that I have to respect it.

SPOILER OF THE FINAL ENDING
The only other character explored in any depth is Jeffery, who will send you an anonymous message after every new ending, and a video message when seeing all of them, that kind of acts as a conclusion to the game. Personally I don't like Jeffery (not that you're meant to), and I was kind of baffled by this decision to have his worldview explained to you as a core narrative element, insofar as to make it the final talking point of the game. I understand of course that we're meant to understand his ideas as partly fallacious, Nicole herself shuts them down in the end. However the game seems to want to place a credence in Jeffery's thoughts that makes me deeply uncomfortable.

Ultimately though, this isn't a bad thing. There's a sort of poetic irony behind making a seemingly one-trait and nothing character talk about how it feels to be treated like nothing. It challenges Nicole's narcissism and misandry and even if she is left unfazed, you are affected. I honestly wish Jeffery had an equally unimportant role across all paths to drive this idea home extra hard. You're left needing to wonder what a Jeffery protagonist game might have been like. As Nicole says: "I guess we'll never know".


Note that this game deals with some really heavy themes, and so carries most trigger warnings you can think of. It's not as emotionally impactful as the sequel - this one does feel more for laughs - but it can still really sour your vibe for a few hours, so make sure you're in an decent mood when starting it.\t\t\t\t\t
