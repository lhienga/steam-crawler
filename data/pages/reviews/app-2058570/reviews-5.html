\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you enjoy turn based rogue-likes, this is one of the very best. This game can be very difficult, but it does reward careful planning and decision making. Compared to Rift Wizard I, there are some much needed quality of life improvements (you can now see upgrades to spells before you purchase them!) as well as a whole slew of new enemies, spells, skills and entirely new features like equipment and pets. The new equipment can add a lot of variety to your runs, so even builds with similar spells can end up playing quite a bit differently depending on what equipment you have.
One of my favorite aspects of Rift Wizard is that the entire game is unlocked on your first run. You don't need to cast 10 magic missiles to unlock void beam or any of that nonsense. This can be daunting to brand new players, as you have hundreds of spells and skills available to you at the very start. However, if you don't want to read every single ability you can always just pick a school of magic and stick mainly to it - many of the synergies are fairly intuitive and a run can be fun even if you don't get to the very end.
I also really enjoy the retro art style, music, and sound effects in this game. The visuals are clean and intuitive, and the level generation adds a unique flavor to the levels you will beat on your way to the boss.\t\t\t\t\t

The structure is very similar to it's predecessor - Fight dudes on a randomly generated floor. Collect points. Learn Spells. Go next. What is new here are some of the nuances of the rules and a couple new systems. \r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhen I purchased this game I expected it to be just a fun little one off indie game that id enjoy for a few hours but the game is incredibly addicting though and fun, the build, level and enemy verity is insane which makes this gem highly replayable.
I adore games with simple graphics when done right (CDDA is one of my favourite games of all time) and the sprite work is done well enough that I can usually tell what im looking at first glance, the effects and sounds though simple give the feeling of such impact, the gameplay loop of freely being able to build your wizards spellsets and passives and choosing which portal to progress through is the perfect mix of strategy and characters builds mixed with the healthy kind of RNG in levels and items and the depth of the enemies is actually really neat, they are more then just sprite swaps they are almost all unique with different attacks, abilities, resistances, lore and underestimating them can end your run, such as, running into a level full of ghosts when your damage is all physical and ghosts have 100% immunity to physical damage, meaning you have to pay attention to what portal you choose to go through. Honestly when you start the game you might be overwhelmed by the amount of choices in spells and passives, but pick a build archetype like say firemage and build it out and you'll be experienced enough to start mixing spells with a few runs.

I can actually only think of one gripe which is easily fixable, there's summons in the game, random summons and summons that can summon more summons which is fun and all but it makes it real hard to tell who tf is a friendly and who is an enemy without hovering each individual in the room, the simple fix id recommend is some sort of tiny UI element over friendly sprites to know at first glance.

Games a gem, in addition its early access done right because the game as it stands could easily be a finished game with a ton of content but the dev wants to add three times the content to an already really fun game, straight 8/10 could be 10/10 by the end of EA.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've only played for about 3 hours so far but I can already fully recommend Rift Wizard 2. The balance changes make the game much more fun than the first game (and the first game is quite fun) since you don't have to worry so much about conserving your resources.

In the first game I would generally avoid a more difficult stage even if it had some items I was interested in since it was very important to conserve health and spell charges. Now if you see a really tough stage that has some great items it might make sense to give it a try because even if you come out of the fight barely alive you'll be restored to full health and spell charges for the next stage

This same balance change also has some very fun implications for builds. In the previous game I would rarely use spells that only had 1 charge since you would need to spend a mana potion to use it again. Now you can use your extremely powerful spells at least once per stage.

The new equipment system is a nice addition that gives a great incentive to choose more difficult rooms to get the items you want and it goes a long ways towards giving each run more variety.

I love every change made so far and will be excitedly watching for the new content. Keep up the great work Dylan.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'll probably write a more in-depth review at some point but wanted to chime in and say that Rift Wizard 1 is one of my favourite roguelikes to come out in years. What's excellent about it is it evokes the feeling of playing a high-level wizard in Nethack without, y'know, having to carefully grind for hours to get to that point. You're slowly building and developing a neat little toolkit of spells and hopefully choosing the right path through the dungeons to set yourself up for success.

The possible spells and upgrades and combinations feels nearly limitless and Rift Wizard 2's departure from the first game is a small but novel deviation (now you're looking to synergize with your gear, so while before you had a small pool of very powerful spells, now you're reacting to what you find and finding synergy that way).

It's a slick bit of design and while there's a lot of similarities in art style and UI between the two games, once you get some time with both, they really seem to be very different systems.\t\t\t\t\t
