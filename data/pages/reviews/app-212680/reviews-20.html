\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWould I recommend this game? Yes*

*While this is a fun and rewarding experience with lots of different ships, upgrades, and strategies, it's not going to be a great game to you unless you're fond of losing a lot. I've played almost 100 hours and won less than 10% of the games I've played.

FTL is an RPG focused on operating and improving a warship over the course of a journey across the vastness of space, bumping into various opportunities and threats. There are many things trying to kill you, a few things that might help if luck favors you (which isn't often) and otherwise will try to kill you, and once in a great while something you encounter is actually helpful (or just pretending to be until it lures you into a trap to kill you).

It's very satisfying to have a decent ship and crew put together if you can survive the early game and find a combination of equipment that synergizes at all. Unfortunately, you have no way to save your game in case you run into an unsurvivable random encounter on the way to the end game, like an ion star frying your engines, shields, and weapons faster than they can charge, guaranteeing an hostile ship can obliterate you without any opportunity to resist or flee. At that point you start the game over from zero and have to repeat the process with no chance of ever playing that interesting set-up you had to the endgame. I don't like when games give you the opportunity to save scum your way to victory by ramming your head against the wall over and over until you can brute force your way to victory; it takes a lot of the fun out of the game when death isn't at least a setback, but it's more frustrating when a game gets you invested and then flips you off for trying. A satisfying middle ground would be if the game autosaved at the start of each sector marking the path of progression towards the end.

One of the great appeals of rogue-likes is that no two adventures are the same. Indeed, if you start a new game, you're going to get a randomly generated series of forking options of locations to visit. There's no way of telling exactly what you are going to find until you go to a location. There's no guarantees you'll find a shop when you need one, or that the vendor will have anything you want or need. All of it is totally randomized. This means you have to improvise until you've got something together which will work, enduring the enemies coming at you or running away.

Fleeing in all but the worst situations is a bad decision, because you have a limited number of encounters to pick up resources from and combat only yields material when you destroy an opposing ship or kill all its crew. Thus, evasion is not a viable strategy.

The variety of options given to players for combat are pretty satisfying- teleporters you can use to board an enemy vessel, beam weapons which inflict massive damage when shields are down, ion weapons to disable systems and crew temporarily, lasers for practical direct damage, missiles which can penetrate shields, inaccurate but powerful kinetic flak cannons, hacking modules to deactive systems or cause them to reverse function, mind control to force enemy crew to help you, bombs that can be teleported anywhere, a great variety of probes serving all sorts of functions, a cloaking device for avoiding enemy fire, and specialized anti-personnel weapons to clear ships without damaging systems to net a higher resource salvage quantity. A multitude of options are there to craft many very satisfying and functional strategies, but good luck getting the same set up twice.

Now there's perhaps one important consideration I've yet to cover: of all those cool strategies I mentioned, there aren't many which are viable for winning the game. One of the best playthroughs I had was teleporting onto ships and tearing them apart with a crew of four high-damage mantis corsairs. Come the final battle, teleporting onto the enemy ship ceases to be a viable option for a variety of reasons, not least because their starting crew outnumber your maximum by 250%. Using ion weapons isn't particularly helpful either- if you can't deal a lot of damage quickly, you aren't going to get anywhere. Beam weapons also aren't any use until their shields are knocked out, for which you need either lasers or missiles. And offense can't be your only consideration; you need to be able to survive their assault until you can beat the enemy's flagship. You've got a maximum of four weapons (usually less) and three drones (usually less). The only way to win is find a decent combination of lasers, missiles, and drones to hurt the enemy faster than he can hurt you (and abuse the hell out of anything else that can give you a slight edge like teleporters, hacking drones, system upgrades, the stealth system, and mind control). Even then, you must master the art of timing to hit at just the right window to destroy the enemy.

Oh and did I mention you have to fight the enemy flagship 3 times? Because you do.

The only way I've found to consistently endure three rounds with the enemy flagship is to find a hull repair drone and use it to fix your ship between the first and third stages. You aren't guaranteed to find a shop containing one along the way and may not have the resources to purchase it if you do find it, so that automatically dooms many runs to failure.

If there were no final boss (or only one stage of combat against it), there'd be a lot more ships you could use that would stand a chance of victory, but all too often the cool set-ups you find that work for 99% of the game are instant flops against the one enemy you know you'll be facing in the end and therefore aren't even worth trying in the first place. I have to give credit to the devs, this is a well-thought out series of ship designs, crewmen, systems, and upgrades with a lot of potential and the game has an amazing randomized gauntlet of encounters to test those combinations against, but with compliments come criticisms. There is no save feature, so if you find a crew and ship you like, there's no way of replaying the game with them if you die to a BS encounter. The final boss is punishingly difficult and kills creativity like a bug zapper does mosquitoes.

I do recommend this game, but there is an asterisk there for a reason. Only players who love optimizing the heck out of their ship are going to enjoy this, and it is the sort of experience where you have fun for two hours and then rage quit and don't touch the game again for two years because you remember just how easily you can lose everything and how annoying that can be. This is NOT a casual game.\t\t\t\t\t
