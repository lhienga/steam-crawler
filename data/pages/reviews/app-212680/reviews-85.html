\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRoad to 100%: Game #15

The replayability of this game is fantastic, as with any true Roguelike. I found myself actually taking my time with FTL while achievement hunting, choosing to see runs to fruition even when it was clear I had passed the threshold for unlocks (such as when hunting for the Crystal Cruiser). There was always a new build to explore, a new way to attack, a new way to challenge yourself without altering the difficulty of the game. Two of my favorite builds were Rockman Fire Boarding Crew and Ion Anti-Oxygen. Speaking of difficulty, FTL has a very distinct Easy Normal and Hard, which can prove to be very difficult (lol) for a lot of studios to design. Needless to say, I agreed with each difficulty.

The combat is tight and intuitive. The 3 weapon types feel balanced and no single weapon ever feels too overpowered on either side. Luck only adds a slight edge to the combat (dodges / fire / breach), but never determines the outcome of a fight. The rules always felt very clear. Essentially, the main objective of combat is to prevent as much damage as possible to your ship. You don’t have to win every fight, you just can’t ever lose. Your ship can achieve this with a solid defense or a quick offense. It was a lot of fun to play with several different combat styles to see which was the most effective at this objective.

I also loved the variety of engagement required for each battle. With certain ships, all you need to target is shield and weapons systems; with others, it can require a specific order of operations and a keen attention to timing depending on your build. Some fights you can lean back and watch the fireworks; others will have you optimizing like crazy, pausing every half-second to precisely plan your attack. Overall, the system / subsystem / crew mechanics are very cool!

I thought the storytelling in FTL was so simple, yet so charming. The flavor text at each beacon provided little hints into the lore of this universe; my imagination would run wild as to what mayhem was happening on the planet’s surface / atmosphere from such a small description. No story, character, faction, or event ever felt out of place. And most importantly, the lore plays as much of a role in your experience as you allow it to!

I also want to talk about the music separately, because it is a masterpiece of its own. I don’t know who this guy Ben Prunty is, I literally just looked him up, but his did such a wonderful job of capturing an essence of space-time cosmonaut adventure and convey it through his music. It all sounds so “space-like,” I really don’t know how else to describe it. Each track so distinctly places you in part of the galaxy with a accompanying ambiance as you sit at the first beacon of each sector; a feeling of being chased, a sense of adventure, or the foreboding weight of space as you stare at the eerie void in front of you. My favorite aspect of the sound design was how there was a second “Battle” version of each song that would play when in combat. Before you even get a chance to read the beacon info, the drums start to kick in and you know that a conflict awaits you.

I don’t have a single negative thing to say or a shred of ill will towards this game. FTL is both self-aware and self-confident. It knows exactly what it wants to be, no more no less, and delivers. Thank you Subset Games for another fantastic time!\t\t\t\t\t
