\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRedoing my review after winning 1 game on normal (14 hours). Revising after 19 hours because this game is such a miserable experience that I need to be able to effectively warn you all of how unfun a game it is and dig deep to find its many issues and verify them through experience:

- The game gives the enemy cheats in the form of boosting their dodge, accuracy, weapon effect chances (burn, breach, etc.), repair speed, and the effect chance one is significant as you will find your whole ship rapidly being engulfed in flames if the enemy has even a single missile or bomb weapon.
- Any event that involves the crew checking something out results in you losing 1 crew member, it seems to prioritise whoever has the most skill levels. I tried each event several times and it always resulted in the death of the most skilled crew member. Technically you can just learn to keep an eye out for them, but that just makes them traps for new players and empty spots for those who are experienced.
- The enemy does not need to have a free pilot to FTL jump, they can do it the millisecond they look at the room, I learned this when my mantis boarders took the pilot room and the ship jumped with them both still on board and the only remaining enemy on their way to fight them. I had expected holding the pilot room with an army to mean they couldn't drive.
- The game prioritises enemy ships having at least 1 missile weapon, this weapon type is a problem as it ignores shield and hit for high damage while also causing fires and breaches, this is especially painful since enemies have massively boosted status chances. Your own missiles use a resource to fire and miss frequently, the enemy has no such limitation, causing frustration and unavoidable damage, sometimes just 1 missile weapon can spell a game over if you miss enough. Post victory update: Missiles are overrated on player side, I won without even firing 1 missile, still completely unreasonable when the enemy has them.
- This game makes you feel hopeless against the RNG due to the high chance to miss, but interestingly you feel empty when you win, because if you win, odds are it was a trivial fight that you won because RNG let your shots hit and/or they just didn't have missiles.
- There is no room in the game for skill expression, as this game is entirely luck-based.
- According to reports, the final boss requires a very specific setup to be able to beat, so that adds 1 more level of RNG to the equation. Post victory update: I won with 3 weapons, 2 blasters that fire 3 shots each, a pike laser, stealth, and a mind controller. I say 3 because the rebels refused to let the 4th ever actually be used
- The AI gets access to some ridiculous weaponry even early game, at one point I lost after the first couple jumps to a ship that could launch 3 missiles at once as a barrage, each one started fires so the ship was quickly shut down, enemy effect chance is a pain.
- The game's RNG can sometimes give you nothing several times in a row, and the enemy can run away in battles which pairs annoyingly with the enemy getting massive dodge bonuses, and the rebel fleet is always encroaching so every node with nothing is 1 step closer to losing the game to bad luck.

In summary, this game is not skill-based like other rogue-likes, it is a fancy slot machine game where the odds are heavily stacked against you. Its fans will claim that the game is super easy and requires knowledge, but knowledge is worth barely anything compared to luck, you are still at the mercy of RNG.

There are 2 things I will say about the game that are positive though.
- The soundtrack is very good
- The final boss is very honest, the least RNG-driven part of the game, it's just a gauntlet of game mechanics so it's skill-based. I beat it first try.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t"FTL: Faster Than Light" brilliantly captures the essence of the rogue-like genre while propelling it into the territory of space exploration and strategy. This game, developed by Subset Games, is a masterclass in balancing challenging gameplay, strategic depth, and replayability, making it a beloved title among fans of the genre and a personal favorite to many, myself included.

From the moment you launch FTL, you're thrust into the role of a spaceship captain, navigating through a galaxy filled with danger, diplomacy, and the unknown, all while being pursued by a relentless enemy fleet. The game's premise is simple yet captivating, offering an endless array of possibilities and adventures that make each playthrough as thrilling as the last. The procedural generation of galaxies ensures that no two journeys are the same, constantly challenging players with new obstacles and opportunities.

What truly sets FTL apart is its intricate systems management gameplay. Players must juggle resources, crew assignments, and ship upgrades while making split-second decisions that can mean the difference between victory and defeat. The game's difficulty is notorious, yet it never feels unfair. Instead, it encourages learning and adaptation, rewarding strategic thinking and creative problem-solving. This delicate balance keeps players coming back for more, eager to face the next challenge head-on.

The pixel art graphics and atmospheric soundtrack perfectly complement the game's tone, creating an immersive experience that's both nostalgic and refreshing. The simplicity of the visuals belies the complexity of the gameplay, proving that a game doesn't need cutting-edge graphics to make a lasting impact. The music and sound effects enhance the tension and excitement of space exploration, making every encounter and discovery feel significant.

FTL's storytelling is another highlight, weaving narrative elements seamlessly into the gameplay. Each encounter and mission adds a piece to the galaxy's rich tapestry, allowing players to create their own unique stories of heroism, betrayal, and survival against the odds. The game's minimalist approach to narrative leaves much to the imagination, inviting players to fill in the blanks and become truly invested in the fate of their crew and mission.

In essence, "FTL: Faster Than Light" is a testament to the power of indie game development. It demonstrates how creativity, passion, and attention to detail can create an experience that resonates with players long after they've closed the game. Its success has undoubtedly left fans, myself included, dreaming of a sequel.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFTL offers easy to understand game concepts, yet proves them to be not that easy to master. As a commander of a starship you will commandeer your crew and ship, navigating through many diverse sectors, encountering many varied alien races on your way, most of whom will be hostile. The game emphesises tactics and resource management as during the countless encounters your ships systems will become damaged and unoperational. It will be up to you to delegate the tasks to your crew to repair those system long enough to live to tell the tale.

Despite its simple graphics, Faste Than Light provides tons of fun and challenge, leaving you with a sense of progress, achievement and a taste for more each time you die and must start over as this is a rougelike game where progress cannot be saved mid-game. This provides an additional challenge, forcing the player to choose their battles more carefully. I very much enjoy playing this game and recommend it to all fans of sci-fi themed tactical games.\t\t\t\t\t
