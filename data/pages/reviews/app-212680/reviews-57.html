Organic & Familiar

There will be a TL;DR at the end.

~

I remember playing this game when it first came out.
I remember playing it a few years ago.
I remember playing it last week.

It feels like a title that's truly been around for awhile, but at the same time - it's always a breath of fresh air when you boot it up.

There's an aspect to the game that is greater than the sum of its parts.
On the surface, it's a very simple action-strategy game with a space setting - where runs can be quickly doomed and restarted, or a stroke of luck can have you glued to your screen for over an hour at a time.
But there is a layer beneath it that is more significant.
Deeper than the warm and inviting art assets and comfortably simple presentation.

It is the intermingling of both familiar and fantastical elements that create an experience that is both familiar but also outstandingly universal in its invitation.

I've tried to get friends and family into Star Trek, Mass Effect, World of Warcraft, Dungeons & Dragons, and other such titans of fantasy in the Tolkien and Space genres. But there is more than mere resistance, in trying to get newcomers to consume new content, it is often a trial in realizing how difficult to digest your own interests might be. Mass Effect throws a million new things your way and expects you to balance a litany of mechanics, Star Trek has untold thousands of alien races with robust cultures and scientific jargon that takes awhile to learn and appreciate, Dungons & Dragons has hundreds upon hundreds of layered mechanics that have to be learned and memorized, and World of Warcraft was made by Blizzard.
There's really no winning.
It feels like a lot of sprawling fantasy titles that allow us to escape into the stardust of imagination are so ambitious and inorganic that it takes significant digestion to understand and embark with.

FTL: Faster Than Light is different.
While it gives players great insight into a new world of alien races, unique technology, political ideology, and many other such delights - it presents these aspects in a way no more complex than what can be expected for an infant to understand.
It's a lot, but never too much to be difficult to understand - moreover the clear visual differences and simple gameplay mechanics make it extremely easy to pick up and play for just about any skill level.

My imagination ran wild while playing, and I put more than enough time into the title over the years to appreciate what it has to offer.

It is refreshing, warm hearted, and creative in its approach. It is not shy enough to drift into the dark corners of the galaxy and remind you that man's inhumanity to man is not lost in this projection of a distant future - but it also isn't so immature as to crush your hope, and at times rekindles your faith in others.
There is good, there is bad. There is simple, there is complex.

When you boot up the game, you are fully immersed in it.
When you set the game down, there may be frustration on a failed run or joy with a hilarious conclusion to an otherwise unfavorable setup - but no matter what you will be satisfied with what you have played.

After so many years, coming back to FTL is like setting my sore eyes upon a familiar vista and drinking in its elegance and beauty. Something I do not have to strain to enjoy, and something that is not too harsh or saccharine as to spoil the appetite.

~

TL;DR
7.5/10.
FTL: Faster Than Light is a Very Good game.
Like bringing you back to your childhood sandbox, it draws your eyes to the great beyond and sparks that dying ember of imagination buried deep down inside that you had feared was forever lost.
And remember, you can never trust those shifty rebels.\t\t\t\t\t
