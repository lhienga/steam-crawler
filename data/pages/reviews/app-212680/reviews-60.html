\r\n\t\t\t\t\tReview from my diary
FTL is one of my favourite games. I think it’s really special. Trolley problems in space, I have seen it called. I played a lot during my last visit home. One of the few games I have put more than 100 hours into, which really seems mental, when I think about it. I love the moments in FTL, where despite its humble graphics, I feel like I’m part of a space opera. I recently played a game, where my heroic Mantis team boarded the enemies. These two warriors were my crew’s best weapons, their only chance of victory. Of survival. As the mantis’ would beam back to the ship following the first attack, they would be at death’s door but their work wasn’t over. They needed to be healed before being sent back behind enemy lines to finish their massacre. The rest of their crew would have to hold their breath. Literally. There was only enough power to give juice to either the medical bay or the O2 system. Like the scene at the end of Total Recall, my crew would be oxygen-starved, looking to their computers for salvation. They watched their messianic mantis’ health increase, hoping this happened before their air ran out. The rest of the crew needed the mantis’s health to increase as they were their only chance of success. The boarding mantis’ were my ship’s main offensive weapon. Mantis’ death meant crew death. They had to risk their own safety to give life to their saviours. The crew had to sacrifice for their survival.

Sometimes called a Startrek episode generator, FTL is full of moments like this. Each player has their own story. This game can take you to space\t\t\t\t\t
