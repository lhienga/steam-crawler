\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFTL: Faster Than Light is a searing emotional odyssey through space, following a band of misfits who progressively become friends and grow as people in fighting for the same cause. Subset Game takes a commendable creative leap in choosing the player character: I would never have imagined assuming the role of a spaceship would be an effective vehicle for storytelling-- but it is. It is a story where you choices matter, where your moment-to-moment gameplay has actual impact on the plot, which only makes the experience all the more immersive. Throughout the story, the player character is constantly forced to make decisions for which a clear-cut morally superior choice does not exist. Even if, on the rare occasions, the situation confronted has a morally upright answer, the player will still have to balance between the life of others and themselves, for story choices have serious gameplay consequences which can impede the player's progress. The themes and messages that the developers explored here are only as powerful as they are because we tackle them in the interactive media of video games: it would have been significantly less poignant if a TV show or a movie or a novel told us that, sometimes, what seemed good on the surface -in short term- may have lasting negative repercussion, instead of us facing the dilemma of letting slavers go or killing a ship of slaves ourselves. All of these great things are enabled, backed, by the stellar world building: the writers have brilliantly given us the illusion of a galaxy of infinite possibilities, of endless wonder, of millennia of history, of socio-political intrigues where, in reality, there are only a limited, although not to say narrow, amount of possible outcomes, a very shallow history and not much background lore. At the end, the game also has multiple endings, but the canonical ending, statistically speaking, is that all of the characters you have been watching and developing and inventing little stories for are blown into smithereens because you, the player, is too much of an incompetent ass who failed to manage their missile economy.

In hindsight, I might have been playing the game wrong.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFirst of all. I do love this game. This game is just freaking awesome.

But why is this so?
For me there a three main points for that:
- a) game play mechanic
- b) story telling with a)
- c) it does not get boring (because of a and b)

Gameplay:
Straight to the biggest achievement of this game, the game play mechanics.
They are so many unique and different mechanics in this game working perfectly together.
I would categorize them in following ones: Offense, Defense and Crew.
And nearly everything you can do in this game can affect one or all of this categories at once. E.g. Oxygen. low Oxygen is a thread to your crew (mostly) and does effect your defense capabilities. But it can also be used as in offense strategy in many different ways. Same counts e.g. for fire and etc.
And this is something what makes the game so great, the mechanics do work, they are plenty of them and can be used in so many different ways. Even me with more than 400 hours play time does not know all of them. Of all the mechanics they are maybe 1-2 I do not like to use under any circumstances, but for the most part everything you can do make sense and is very useful under specific circumstances.

Story telling:
Each run feels different, each run as his heights and lows. Not the game tells the story the game play and your decisions tells them and each run tells something different. Close fights, fleeing , deaths of crew, funny strategies and etc.

Gets never boring:
Mostly effected by the game play itself, but also based on the RNG the game force you to play it differently each run (depends on what the games offers you). And this is something I do not understand why so many use the RNG as a negative point. I do understand it if a random event can destroy a run, but this is 99,9% not the case in FTL. It just force to play your given cards.
Playing in the easiest difficult I complete ~90% of my runs and 9% of fails are related to mistakes from my side.
And the fun part starts when the game starts to throw difficult situations. (Fire on ship, destroyed Oxygen System, shielding do not hold, boarding crew on ship and etc.) This are the moments where the game starts to shine! Even in the worst situation there is a solution (even if it means to flee from fights). And that is very satisfying to solve such situations successfully.

Played this a lot and also get all achievements (rarely something I do) does make the vanilla game for me to less challenging.
For all people out there with the same problem. There is a solution for this => Multiverse (MOD).

Andy maybe one last thing. This game runs on every machines, good playable on the steam deck and is perfect for short and long sessions.\t\t\t\t\t
