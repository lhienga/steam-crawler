\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFar Cry 5 is an open-world first-person shooter with a slightly tweaked Ubisoft formula. For me, it’s a decent game, but nothing special in the end. This time, Ubisoft brought the setting to the United States, specifically Montana. And it is beautiful in any of the 3 main regions. This time, we’re fighting against a religious cult that took hold of the region as their own state.
The story is alright. This time, you play as a customizable, silent protagonist. In good Far Cry tradition, it has memorable villains, especially the leader of the cult. Too bad he doesn’t appear enough. Each of the 3 regions is controlled by a sibling of the leader, and they’re all twisted in their own way.
Speaking of which, the game gives you the freedom to play the story in whatever order you want. After an introduction, you have the freedom to play through the 3 regions however you want, since their stories are self-contained. The game suggests starting with a specific region, but you can leave that one for last, or even cycle through them and play them at the same time.
One thing that I didn’t like is the story progression system. The way it works is that you need to complete activities, like missions, to fill up a progress bar. Once you reach a certain point, the next mission involving the leader sibling is unlocked. So far, so good. However, once you reach that point, you are forced into the mission. I understand that it may make the game feel more organic, but I like to explore at my own pace and didn’t enjoy being forced into the missions.
The map is big, but not too big. The size feels appropriate, although it’s mostly mountains and forest with a few locations spread out. However, even in remote areas, things are always happening, like cultists taking prisoners or dealing with the wildlife. Unless you liberated a region, since most cultists disappear after that.
There’s no mini-map; instead, you have a compass similar to the newer Assassin’s Creed titles. Another way to make the game and exploration more organic. You can still access the map at any time, but it starts with fog and there’s no easy way to reveal locations. You’ll need to explore, rescue prisoners, find maps in outposts and other locations, or look for road signs. That I enjoyed; it felt like Ubisoft took some risks, instead of revealing everything by climbing towers and buying maps, littering the map with icons.
Gameplay is also alright. It does what an FPS should do but doesn’t innovate that formula. You shoot cultists and wildlife with weapons. Weapon variety is lacking, and while you can customize them, it’s limited to changing the skin, adding a scope, a suppressor, and a larger magazine. Some weapons also support different firing modes and ammunition, but that’s it.
Being an FPS, it’s much better to play on a keyboard and mouse, but I found that activities like driving and fishing feel better on a controller. If you have both, I suggest switching up. It’s awkward, but I preferred playing this way.
There’s also a standard progression with skill points to unlock perks, and a basic crafting system. My favorite element is the addition of party members. You can recruit allies to help you in combat, and they’re actually useful. Especially the 9 unique NPCs you need to unlock; they offer a solid selection of skills. There’s a perk that allows you to have 2 of them at once, and in a way, that can break the game with the right people in the right circumstances. They can even revive you.
While I played the entire game solo, there’s also a co-op mode and multiplayer with Far Cry Arcade, including a map editor. From what I saw, there’re still some people playing this mode.
The season pass also gives you access to 3 main DLCs. Hours of Darkness is a mini-campaign set in Vietnam, and in a way is an homage to the second Rambo movie. It’s a way to experience the main game mechanics in a smaller package. Lost On Mars has a similar structure, but taking place on Mars has futuristic weapons, more jumping verticality, and the enemies are alien arachnids. They are annoying and make combat much harder. It’s also longer than the first DLC. Dead Living Zombies is a set of 7 short levels where you’re fighting zombies, mostly at close range. It also has a score attack mode with some unlocks to the main game. Getting the best scores is hard, though.
Overall, I think the DLCs are a mixed bag, but if you enjoyed the main game, I think you’ll enjoy them for the most part. Time to talk about the Ubisoft shenanigans. Obviously, you’ll need to use Ubisoft Connect, which at this time has problems like not being able to see your stats and achievements in-game. Especially the achievements; they unlock correctly, and you can see them in the desktop app, but there’s no notification when you unlock one.
And of course, there’re microtransactions, although this time they aren’t too bad for a simple reason. Everything you can buy with premium currency you can also buy with regular in-game money. Of course, this means you have to grind that money in-game, but nothing is exclusively behind a paywall.
To conclude, Far Cry 5 is another Ubisoft open-world title, with only a few significant changes. It’s a good game, but not great. But it’s usually cheap when on sale, and I can recommend it at that price.\t\t\t\t\t
