\r\n\t\t\t\t\tUnfortunately, the fifth attempt at Ubisoft's open-world shooter doesn't bring anything new at all. The story is just boring at times and doesn't offer any innovations or clever surprises. It's enough for a picture-perfect but generic shooter. The potentially interesting scenario in the rural province of the American Northwest moves too much on the well-trodden path of its predecessors and gets lost too quickly in main and side missions, which can hardly be separated from each other and (with a few exceptions) always play the same. You won't find an exciting, thrilling story with meaningful NPCs, a cleverly devised plot and charismatic ingenious villains Ã  la Vaas Montenegro. If you buy a helicopter with the appropriate arsenal, you can master the game in the last third with almost no effort. But probably the strangest features of this game (maybe in the entire series) is the "Resistance Barometer", which fills up after e.g. hostage rescues, damage to cult property or other actions that harm your enemies. When it's full, a mission sequence starts, sometimes completely skipping story missions and making them unplayable. The skill tree is also completely inscrutable and illogical. Ironically, the game proves the ridiculed prepper scene right, which is also somehow quite amusing. With FarCry 5, Ubisoft has milked its cash cow way more than it should have and delivers a visually beautiful, but half-baked ill-conceived game. Why so many gamers are now self-satisfied with (expensive) lower mediocrity is a mystery to me. I would choose FarCry 3 & 4 over this part at any time.\t\t\t\t\t
