\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is a hidden gem of a rogue-lite containing aspects of Enter the Gungeon with a Monster Hunter-like boss chase at the center of its gameplay loop. The general idea is similar to EtG as you collect weapons (holding a max of two at a time Master Chief style) and buffing items which increase your overall power. The room to room clearing is also similar to other games of its kind but with the added twist of the boss being available to fight from the beginning. The boss is spawned at the start of each area with its health bar locked which means you have to explore the dungeon to unlock its health bar and earn weapons and items to increase your power. The player initially starts out as the mouse in this cat and mouse dynamic but you quickly grow in strength as you explore the stage. Once you have grown sufficiently in strength you take on the role of hunter and hunt the boss that moves between the rooms of the dungeon. This game is refined to an insane degree and the replayability and variations in its guns is extensive. The art style is also a pleasure to behold with fluid animations to round out the package. Highly recommend buying and supporting the devs on this one.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tVoidigo is up there with Nova Drift, Vampire Survivors, Atomicrops, and Risk of Rain 2.

Really I might put it on a pedestal with Nova Drift, I think I enjoy it that much.

The gameplay is excellent (highly replayable for its difficulty, or for ridiculous builds and silly "cosmetic collection") but, more importantly... this game OOZES with personality, and such a unique personality I can only describe as "some particular really funny friends who know how to make each other laugh with absurd humor made a roguelite together."

Voidigo presents dry, coy, dumb, absurdist, and surrealist jokes (kind of tinged with "esoteric internet humor" like, aren't rats and snakes just kind of inherently amusing in a certain light; aren't fishes and bugs underrated dudes?) harmonistically. It feels like the devs are birds, placing in your hands lots of weird nice things, and sometimes a cartoon bomb (a punchline).

This makes the road to technical mastery of the game, or exploration of all its content, feel less bland and lonely than it often can in games. I'm reminded of Dicey Dungeons, which rewards you for understanding the nuances of every enemy and item in the game, and leads you into this understanding by making them inherently, pleasantly amusing to behold.
