\r\n\t\t\t\t\tVoidigo holds its own in the realm of roguelite shooters, carving out a niche through its strong aesthetic and smattering of creative twists on convention. Rather than a dodge roll, players can defend themselves by jumping over threats, then stomping on enemies heads to stun them & bounce back upward into a chainable bunny hop. It feels a little sluggish with the un-upgraded movement speed, but the satisfaction of I-framing through attacks and bounding across a series of now-stunned enemies helps the game sink its hooks in immediately, and things spiral delightfully from there as your build develops. Theres a litany of upgrades to trigger additional effects when stomping heads or buttons on the floor, which typically spew some mess of damage upon your enemies, be it an explosion or countless ravenous bats or snakes gnawing foes on your behalf.

This chaos extends to most other upgrades you acquire over a run, including automatic emplacements, summonable companions and effects that proc when reloading one of the many bizarre, cartoon-logic weapons. Those run the gamut, from a nervous, personified banana peel which you callously squeeze to launch fruit projectiles, to a pair of pistol-nunchucks shooting with each swing, or a big CRT that fires staticky phosphorescence from its screen. While you can only equip two at a time, youll typically have a high turnover rate throughout a run, since upgrades often charge you another item or some max health. How often the game asks you to prune your build may be an acquired taste, but the resulting fluid, impromptu playstyle is worth the friction.

Unfortunately, this meandering does tend towards homogeneous (albeit fun) builds by the end of a run, which typically revolve around some combination of stomping, shooting and slashing enemies to spawn a swarm of various bouncing projectiles. However, you and your build only bring half of the excitement, as Voidigos distinct level format adds another fun wrinkle. In a slightly Monster Hunter-esque fashion, each stage has a formidable boss lurking in one of its 8-or-so rooms, waiting to be stumbled upon. If it werent enough of a threat unto itself, the boss health bar is broken up into locked segments, each protected by one of the handful of beacons strewn throughout the rooms. If you were lucky, maybe you were already able to stomp most of them, leaving the first 80% of the bosss health vulnerable to your onslaught. Often you wont be so fortunate, instead being forced to frantically scour the map for more beacons in order to progress while being hounded by the massive beast, not to mention the numerous regular enemies that still remain. Its a thrilling game of cat & mouse that can even invert, depending on your performance, when a boss burrows to the safety of another room. Either way, this madcap hunt keeps things frenetic and fresh over countless runs.

This is ultimately a game of simple pleasures, opting for a lateral meta-progression system of unlockable starting gear, rather than a Dead Cells or Hades-style power ramp, a decision which is fully to its benefit. The whole experience coheres tightly when you surrender yourself to the flow of overstimulating light, sound and kinesis, rather than getting precious about theory crafting or meticulously tactical play. Voidigo may not quite have the rock-solid fundamentals to unseat Nuclear Throne on its own terms as the reigning champ of the subgenre, but all these idiosyncrasies culminate in a game that feels utterly distinct, and one I heartily recommend.\t\t\t\t\t
