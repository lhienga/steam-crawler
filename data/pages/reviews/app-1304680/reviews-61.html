\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSpectacular mix of silly without being cheesy or dumb.

Satisfying game play - difficult enough that you get satisfaction from completing things and you'll get frustrated on occasion, but not to the point of putting it down.

Haven't found any permanent stat upgrades, instead you start with 1 character, 1 gun, 1 ability and as you play unlock new starting characters/gear/etc with very different and unique play styles. Some weapons are clearly better than others but you get so many thrown at you it's viable to just keep swapping weapons as you go and find the one(s) that work with your character and how you want to play. Many upgrades as well start turning you into a very powerful character by the end.

Low HP game - think 2 to 4 major hits (twice that in small hits) and you're dead. On the other hand, health potions are fairly abundant so while you're going to get hit often as long as you're not reckless you'll be healed up.

Can get chaotic but the bright pixel art is brilliant to look at. Very well done style.

Biggest complaint so far - please add native 3440x1440 / ultra wide support. The game stretches to my screen every time I open it so I have to reset the resolution on every open. Only takes a few seconds but still frustrating. I'd love to see higher frame rates supported as well, not capped at 60.

Haven't tried coop yet so can't speak to that, but this seems like it'd be a fun game for it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've had a pretty positive experience so far. I did pick up a few nitpicks here and there, though. Maybe the developer could address some of them:

The tutorial could be better. It gets all the info across, but doesn't do it in a very engaging way. Adding an NPC to it like enter the gungeon, or making all of it be part of the mini-dungeon at the end of the tutorial would probably give better first impressions.

No borderless fullscreen.

Default audio device didn't auto-update after changing it in windows settings, had to restart.

I often have a hard time finding the current area map in the tab menu since it kinda blends in with whatever's going on in the background.

No way to experiment with starting builds in the hub- you have to go in a game and exit to main menu. Makes bulk-unlocking noticeably less impactful when you can't try everything out at once without going through this tedious process.


As for more major issues:

Upgrades desperately need interactions with eachother. Once you pick up an upgrade- that's it, you've seen all there is to it. It noticeably hinders the replayability of the game. Risk of rain 2 has proc chains, isaac has stacking modifiers, etc.

There are also a lot of duplicate upgrades split up between each upgrade category, e.g. "melee attacks are on fire" or "bullets are on fire" or "your stomps are on fire", etc. I'd honestly rather these types of upgrades all get combined together into a single upgrade as to give upgrades more overlap between playstyles, because as it stands getting an upgrade that doesn't cater to your current build sucks & is way more common than in other roguelites. Then the hole left behind by these filler upgrades being taken out could be filled up with upgrades that interact more with eachother and aren't relegated to a specific playstyle as to not make an entire weapon branch obsolete by the end of a playthrough.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tVoidigo is the best new twinstick roguelite I've seen in a long time. It has, in no particular order:

- Really clever character designs. Your character selection drastically changes how you play and how you value pickups. Playing Dr. Fusion (the literal sun, in a mechanical suit, wearing sunglasses and board shorts) means that your projectiles now orbit around you at fixed distance rather than shooting forward. The other characters are similarly inventive and playing each one is a blast.
- Fun and lovingly designed weapons and powerups. Most of the things you wield are alive, and wriggle around in your hands with as much character as your actual character. Find a legless skeletal trumpeter who fires jiggly music notes, clears the bouncing bones out of its trumpet when it reloads, and wheezes exhausted when it runs out of ammo. Just like the characters, the level of quality throughout the set is consistently high, and they all feel great to use.
- Boss hunting mechanic. Scattered around the map are Beacons that need to be activated. Activating two beacons summons the boss, but it can't be killed until ALL of the beacons are activated. Picking when to engage the boss and when to run from it (and having it chase you around as you hunt for the last beacon so you can kill it) is a mechanic I haven't seen before in this genre and makes engaging with the bosses feel relevant throughout the level instead of just the end.
- Craftable pets. You can spend your currency between runs to semi-randomly build weird little guys armed with the weapons you find. They show up as recruitable companions in your runs and talk nonsense to each other in the hub area. It's a really cute mechanic and my hub is getting really full of them, please send help, I can't bear to banish any of my deformed murderous children.

Overall Voidigo is already a polished and engaging product, despite still being in EA as I write this review. It's obviously learned lessons from the other major games in the genre, and in my estimation is just as good as Nuclear Throne and better than Enter the Gungeon (mainly because it doesn't commit the cardinal sin of having mostly boring guns, come on...). Don't miss it!\t\t\t\t\t
