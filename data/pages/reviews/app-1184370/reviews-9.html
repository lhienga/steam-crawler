\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've loved most cRPGs based on TTRPGs, all the way back to SSI's Pool of Radiance, so I want to like this game, but its hard to.

Pathfinder: Wrath of the Righteous is based on the Pathfinder TTRPG. Pathfinder is (if this game is any indication) a dense and complex rule set. This makes it a perfect candidate for a cRPG, which can manage the multitude of calculations for you. Though, this version of Pathfinder comes with a Lego truck's worth of supplemental material. This density of rules immediately creates an unneeded barrier of entry, not just for casual cRPG players but even for veteran TTRPG players. Character creation alone can be overwhelming. When how your character progresses is so crucial and the diverse minutia of options so great, it is small wonder that players just give up at character creation and do a web search for guides that tell you the mechanically optimal way to build a character; though, rarely how to play them.

This issue could be alleviated with a solid tutorial and a UI with tool-tips and feedback mechanisms. Unfortunately, those are not sufficiently robust here. For instance, if you play an alchemist, there is no tool-tip to tell you how to change your default attack from bombs to anything else. You can similarly find your actions disappearing in battle. Sometimes your 5' action or move action just disappears or you get an extra attack. Some of these you can track down by reviewing what feats and other special powers you have, rather than by any feedback mechanism in the combat log, which only shows results, not the math that gets you there, thus failing to educate the user as to the game's mechanics. This can create situations where the player is unsure if "bug" or "working as intended" when something happens, like a character checking out for an entire battle; PCs & NPCs having ironically bad path finding on maps; only being able use and action to stand up if you also use an action to move (not just the 5ft step either), which means you can't just stand up and take an action like chugging a potion or punching a demon; and the disparity between clicking on a far away target to attack making you move and attack versus trying the same to heal causing you stay still, waste the spell, and not actually heal the target.

Related to UI, your visual range is tight, and I mean this in two senses. First, you cannot zoom out; this means that sometimes a target that you are shooting at, or vice versa, is off screen, which means you cannot see the roll and damage numbers, thus creating another problem for the user receiving feedback from the game. This issue also crops up when the screen jumps to the person whose next turn it is and they are off screen; it jumps so quickly that you won't know the results of what you just attempted beyond an audial cue. Second, your characters can only see so far, which would be fine if enemies couldn't see further, and even start combat with you when they are off screen, dropping you into combat mode with an inability to meaningfully act. This is exasperated when the enemy does nothing, forcing you to pick a direction and run into the fog of war to try and reveal them. In another instance on inconsistency, sometimes enemies whose allies are fighting you right now are just hanging out five feet in the darkness of the fog of war watching you squelch their friends.

Consistency of language can be another point of failure or at least misunderstandings. For instance, where many powers are spells, not all spells share the same characteristics, particularly between classes. This makes its way into tool tips as well, like crippled, which says that to remove it you need to visit a healer at a safe place, only they won't; you actually have to rest there instead. In an amusing language breakdown, alignment gets some interesting representation during dialog choices. Where lawful, good, & evil largely mean what you might expect, chaotic equals song, dance, making jokes, and generally enjoying oneself. It is almost as if the writers actively hate fun and are trying to make you feel bad for playing this game, a clearly chaotic act. Actually small correction, evil is almost exclusively represented by "rawr, kill," which is face palm inducing. Oh and if a dialog choice says that it requires a mythic path, what it actually means is that choice helps you unlock the mythic path. I know English can be a complex mess of a language, but this is why professional editors exist, people.

There's a ton of other mechanical little things, that range from issues with the underlying Pathfinder rules to the actual programming. These include such frustrating things as "why can't I bind something else to the A key?", "how does a crossbow get three attacks in 6 seconds (one round)?" and "how am I flatfooted (surprised) even though I initiated the attack?" At this point in the review, I'm sure some Pathfinder apologist is stewing on some counter argument, to which I say, look, if it was only one of two issues, cool, we overlook those. As a dedicated ESDF player, I have to look past a lot of keybinding issues in myriad, but the cumulative impact of all these issues weighs heavily, because unlike the bonuses in Pathfinder, all of these penalties stack.

Lets put the mechanics aside and look at the rest of the game. On a high note, the graphics are keen. The giant spider and humongous fly definitely left a positive impression when they first appeared. Your character's paper doll is a bit overstuffed, but that's minor. The available portraits look fine, and the game makes it easy to import your own. The audio gets the job done. I don't recall anything stand out, but also nothing horrible. I'd prefer to have more likes voiced, but that's not strictly necessary.

The overall story seems interesting. A giant insect demon shows up and ruins everyone's day at the faire. Then you start crawling through a ruined city like rats trying to survive and even fight back. That's pretty darn cool, and the way the game handles map movement/navigation and camping is solid. I felt it was better in the city than overland, but still solid. However, the game doesn't feel grounded. You don't have time to truly understand the world and what is going on before the world blows up, so although its clear that there are great stakes, they aren't any stakes that the player is invested in. Oh and the player's character is clearly a chosen one who gets to choose what kind of god they want to be.

This situation is not helped by the inclusion of joke characters. Rather than leaning into the atmosphere that the game tries to create, these characters yoink you out. Even characters that seem more inline with the setting can leave a question mark because the setting is never truly presented. There's a medieval Europe corner of the world with demons that you are dropped into, but then there's allusions to pirates, some kind of Asian that didn't actually study those cultures, animistic practices alongside deity worship, a token black woman for the sake of visual but not cultural inclusion, myriad sapient species that all get along, and outside nations sending mercenaries and crusaders to the area where the fight's happening.

Where this set up may be fine for a fan of the TTRPG that has a more full understanding of the setting, someone just coming to the game may feel lost, confused, or without eyeballs, as they rolled so hard they popped themselves out of the skull and are lost under the desk somewhere. Many of these issues could have been addressed through a simple prologue with a world map that explains the backstory. Start with a brief "here's the world of Pathfinder" and then zoom in on the history of the Worldwound and its crusades.

Overall? I bought this when it was discounted 70% off, and I feel I paid too much. The core of what it is trying to do is good, but the numerous issues just feed frustration, even when you're winning.\t\t\t\t\t
