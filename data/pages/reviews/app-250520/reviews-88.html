\r\n\t\t\t\t\tUnderRail is an isometric RPG with a heavy focus on crafting.

It's ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ fantastic.

Firstly: most character builds are viable on easy or normal, allowing new players, or those unfamiliar with RPGs to do well. On hard and DOMINATING builds must be finely tuned. The game punishes generalist characters, so a character adept at surviving higher difficulties will always lack something, encouraging multiple playthroughs with different.

You'll be using some combination of guns, throwing weapons, melee, and psionics. Each has an upside and a downside, and you're usually able to craft stronger version of what you can buy in stores, assuming you have the required skills.

In combat you have both action and movement points, same for enemies. Action points function similar to nearly every other turn based RPG. Each action you can take, such as firing a gun, throwing a grenade, or opening a door. Movement points are spent strictly on movement, but action points can be used to move as well. This allows you to spend all your action points shooting, then retreat to cover, or allows you to close a gap while still allowing you to shoot.

Most weapons have a percent chance to hit, based on the weapon, your skills, and character stats. A gun wielding character with low strength will do worse with sledgehammers, for example. Guns have different ammo types that do different things, armor piercing rounds will be more effective against heavily armored targets, while over penetrating unarmored targets. Shotguns hit percentage is per pellet, and they have a firing cone, meaning swarms of lightly armored enemies are best taken out with a high pellet count round, where fighting a single highly armored enemy requires a lower amount of larger shot.

Psionics are a little different. Just having the option to use them drops your health by a whopping 20%. For this tradeoff you get access to four schools of psionics, metathermics, kinetics, thought control, and temporal manipulation. Each school specializes in different things, with metathermics having direct, elemental damage. Freeze, burn, or electrocute enemies to death. Kinetics relies on direct, physical damage. You can stun enemies, create barriers, and augment your unarmed punches. Thought control focuses on debuffing enemies, making them run in terror or go bezerk and attack their former allies. Temporal manipulation allows for delayed damage and debuffs, allowing you to slow enemies or stack delayed damage for a one-turn kill.

Psionic characters can access any and all of these schools, but the number of abilities they have at one point is limited, starting with 2 usable abilities, up to a maximum of 8. Characters that use abilities from multiple schools will have the casting cost increased, encouraging you to specialize. Psi abilities are cast with psi points, which are refilled every turn from reserves, which themselves are replenished by a psionic inhaler.

I'm going to try not to spoil the plot, but you're a new citizen to south gate station. You start by helping with odd jobs that pop up as a result of a recent cave in. Fairly early on you make it to other settlements, and start to meet other factions that you can decide to align with.

Difficulty is a bit weird, there's certainly a learning curve but it spikes unreasonably at some points. No matter the build, proper use of traps and grenades can save your life.

Currently I'm playing a shotgun and Temporal manipulation build. I certainly die quite a bit, but much of it is my own fault (I walked into the same mine 4 times, dying each time!) Temporal manipulation lets me limit an enemy's movement and action points, allowing me to either stay in range with my shotgun, or retreat if necessary. Overall I'm really enjoying this combo, but as I said, nearly any build is viable on easy and normal. Assault rifle, grenades, and medium armor is a "boring" choice, but will help new players learn the ins and outs of combat.\t\t\t\t\t

It's overly meticulous and almost nothing is intuitive, from the controls to the combat. It's very archaic and while there are "old school" aspects this game mostly takes the negative "features" from past games into this one. Look at the complaints in the discussion board if you want an idea.

Game balance is also an issue, with the spike in difficulty being overwhelming. RNG is still a b*tch a lot of times. XCOM style 99% missing.

Be very careful of the items you have. Mission essential items can be dropped and easily lost with all the garbage and junk. I understand not holding the player's hand but this is aggravating and stupid. Divinity OS did a good job of rewarding the player for paying attention BUT this game ain't it.

It's a cheap game and I really want to like it but can't recommend it because the accumulation of flaws eventually tests your patience.

You will constantly die as you start to learn the game, and then die slightly less constantly after you have.

By the time I had hit my stride in Underrail, I was thinking about it almost every moment I wasn't playing it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA very obvious love-letter to my favorite games of all time: Fallout 1 and 2 - which sadly though is pretty rough to play and enjoy due to a lack of polish that affects the entire experience.

From poor UI scaling for 1080p and above to very subpar information delivery in a game that is going to dump a lot of information on you without as much as an in-game notepad to help you track everything (believe me, the in-game journal is not something you'll want to depend on for anything else other than a bare list of your tasks).

The abundant save-scumming and underbaked stealth system are two examples of seriously undercooked gameplay systems and game design that could be removed whole-sale and would have resulted in a better experience simply by their absence, had it been done early on.

Underrail also doesn't have the same quality of writing (dialogue, world-building, quest design...) that elevated the Black Isle classics from their primitive graphics, systems and interface to their positions as all-time-classics. Underrail is trying really, really hard but it falls a whole collapsed mineshaft (or two) below that gold standard.

When you are comparing a 2015 to a 1997 (or 1998) game and you can easily tell that the newer one is the more unpolished of the two, it forces me to give this a negative review. Thankfully, it's not as unpolished as say, Atom RPG (an even more obvious love-letter to the early Fallouts) and is very serviceable but overall, it just brings the entire experience down.

I bought this at 60% off and with the DLC at 20% discount. At this point, I would say that this is a barely acceptable propostion - if I had gotten both at 50-60% I would not regret the buy.

Us fans of old-school RPGs (which tried to adapt the tabletop experience into video-game format rather than just bring a few "RPG elements" into other video-game genres) have to be content with the few-and-far-between releases that we get. In that sense, I guess Underrail is OK if you're done with everything else the genre has to offer - but considering other modern cRPGs offer far more quality on all fronts than this one does, I'd say to leave it as a last option.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOften said to be a spiritual successor to the early Fallout games, Underrail is easily in my opinion the best modern crpg. This is made only more impressive in that it started as a passion project by a single man, although the game now has a very small team backing it. The best way that I can describe the game is as a '90s engine in a modern car. If a game that doesn't hold your hand, has no quest markers, and rewards tactics while ruthlessly punishing carelessness is what you're after, this is it.

Probably the worst thing I can say about the game is how much it constantly makes you want to restart and make another build.\t\t\t\t\t
