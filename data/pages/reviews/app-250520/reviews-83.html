\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL:DR

Beating the game's difficulty and learning the combat mechanics makes it worth it for those die hard players who enjoy a challenge. If this is the case, you may not be put off by the clunky UI and forced character progression. UnderRail is NOT worth if you require immersion in your rpgs.

Pros
+Atmosphere is great
+Character levelling and design has tons of depth
+Classic RPG feel of yore
+Decisions have consequences
+Complex crafting system

Cons
-Horrible UI (E.G. map, crafting, quest log, inventory)
-Non-engaging plot
-All characters sound the same and have similar personalities
-Tedious weight management system
-Exploring becomes a chore unless you have stealth
-Nonsensical quests and progression
-Almost non-existent quest log
-Shallow NPC backstories

I cannot recommend this game. I enjoyed it when I started, mostly because I was learning the ropes and was curious what I could explore next. The atmosphere and music made the game immersive. The combat system was engaging and difficult. Some red flags came up after I noticed the horrible dialogue. Nearly every character sounds like a juvenile delinquent, including you, the protagonist. The characters who don't sound like this are written cringingly bad or it feels forced. Some quests just seem odd and weird. The developers have added quite a few characters and items from various movies, games and pop culture. At first it was novel and pleasant but it started getting irritating, especially when you meet a character directly from a movie, who gives you a series of odd, nonsensical and immersion breaking quests.

His name is Dude in Underrail, which was directly taken from the movie The Big Lebowski - being played as himself. Everyone calls him The Dude in the movie. Lines have been directly quoted from the movie too. Great... The Dude's fantastical dreams, in The Big Lebowski, have been interpreted as real or alternate realities in UnderRail. He's a mad scientist in UnderRail that does not know any science yet he blindly leads you along a series of quests with out-of-place stoned dialogue. He just spits out a mish-mash of stoner dialogue. The writers of UnderRail seemed so focused to add pop cultural stuff they liked, they seemed to forget or not know how originality and creativity made stories great. For instance, the reason why The Dude was interesting in the movie was not just him but the characters around him too and how they interacted together. In UnderRail he's a badly written, boring, shallow(non-existent backstory), awkward, plagiarised, stoned quasi-scientist. It wouldn't be so bad if it was just him but it crops up more and more as you play. Unoriginal and boring.

Some quests and areas seemed half finished or have confusing/nonsensical logic to them, consequently, I couldn't finish some quests without looking it up online. There are missions or areas you cannot do if you do not have the right skills or are sufficiently levelled. There is no warning whether you should or shouldn't go down a certain path. If I didn't save often, I would have been locked out of the game entirely, because of bad quest and level design. I managed to complete this certain quest by cheesing it. Exploring becomes a chore unless you stealth it. Enemies will continuously respawn after a set time and because this game is turn based, it will just eat a chunk of your time if you revisit an area. It's even worse if you chose the Oddity way of getting exp at the start of the game: these fights then become doubly pointless and time wasters. They waste your time and you don't get experience, so the game forces you to stealth. The game becomes mind rumbling boring after a tedious trek if the only characters you meet are immature, all sound similar and are unoriginal. The rest of the cons I didn't mind, but immersion, to me, is quite important. This sort of game really needs good dialogue and plot. UnderRail severely lacks this. I get the impression that the writers didn't know what to put in their game so they just jammed whatever they could from whatever pop culture, game or movie they liked. Sigh...\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFirst impressions review
After 30 hours of playing underrail non-stop, I had assumed that there was something it was doing right. There's a tendency for certain audiences to label games as tough but fair, my first impressions taught me underrail is tough and unfair. The devil is in the details from how encounter starts force you to wait for a cooldown, or the fact that levelling up doesn't feel good because of the fact that you aren't progressing substantially enough to compensate for the difficulty you've had to undergo before reaching said level. This game was recommended to me as a spiritual successor to fallouts 1 and 2, and while there are sparks of what made those games great here, the failings truly show as a first impression when the several hour comparisons amidst the three games were like night and day. In underrail I was expending vast amounts of resources and quicksaves/quickloads for individual combat encounters that never lead to a reward that felt justified the hardships, whereas in the fallout games one's hard efforts paid off very satisfyingly even without much word of mouth advice guiding the player on where to go and what to do. Underrail doesn't get a recommendation the first impressions playthrough. I'll update this review after another playthrough of the game with more knowledge of it's elements.

20+ Hours review
So after specializing my character and actually paying attention to which attributes are better for my character, I did a second playthrough and found I had progressed miles farther in a fraction of the time. One really does need to take a playthrough of this game much slower than most cRPGs I've seen, and while I applaud it for having the player actively get involved with the mechanics rather than treat them as supplementary to the experience, I still did run into some issues. Being Encumbered is something I strongly dislike as a mechanic, especially given the fact there's only so much a player can do to mitigate the issue, and it makes investing in strength feel like a fruitless effort when the additional carryweight capacity isn't enough to feel like it's worth the point. Further thoughts on attributes are positive, I do like how significantly attributes can contribute to the strength of my skills, even if it means I'm still using a particular attribute enhancing set of armor because of the bonus it provides, moreso than for it's armoring capabilities. Once I got the hang of combat, I found it to be incredibly satisfying to have things setup as dominos in a sequence, and let it go according to plan. What really made this game click for me the second time around was when I was setting up a sequence of bear traps and explosives and realized that it felt as though I was playing deus ex again, perhaps not a 1:1 recreation, but enough qualities like careful emergent plans to deal with enemies and being able to bypass large challenges with a little bit of intuition was what ended up making me fall in love with Underrail. Underrail get's a recommendation, though maybe get some prior game knowledge unless you want to run the risk of a sour playthrough that has you learning what's wrong from experience before you can playthrough a "right" playthrough.\t\t\t\t\t
