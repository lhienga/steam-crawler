\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game itself has too many little inconveniences for me to really recommend that anyone plays this game. The psi changes are awful, but if you are a new player that doesn't matter. There are more than a handful of times where I have been temporarily soft locked by the game. You usually need to save scum to complete some combat encounters. If your build is poor, you simply need to restart the game, which can consume hours of time to do (not enjoyable hours, mind you, but repetitious, monotonous hours). The game has a skill system much like Fallout New Vegas, which is nice and simple. However, unlike Fallout New Vegas, if you misplace too many points, your build can become fairly nonviable, or at least inefficient.

The game is a hard, turn-based RPG, but not necessarily an exceptionally fun, turn-based RPG. The difficulty is rather on the nose rather than strategically thought provoking. In other words, the enemies hit hard and fast, so much of combat is crowd control. This is fun in its own right, but can become somewhat of a headache when having to reload a fight constantly, especially when the outcome is completely unclear. I can get stomped on my first attempt and then stomp the enemy on my tenth attempt. However, I never feel any more intelligent on the tenth attempt. Rather, I feel as if I just moved to the right pixel placement, rolled lucky enough for my attacks, or otherwise modified some minute detail of trial and error before busting my head through the brick wall.

Part of this problem comes from the level of RNG that is in this game. Many encounters can be won or lost by rolling the the dice on a 67% to hit. If you start a combat and you don't kill or stun the first target on the first turn, then that target can usually kill you or stun you afterwards (which usually leads to being killed anyways). Due to this, you can easily reload the save after losing 10 times, finally hit that 67% chance to hit, and steamroll the encounter. One may argue that if I am having to resort to save scumming, then there must be something wrong with my build, which could certainly be true. But even if that were the case, it still feels like terrible design that I can bumrush combats just because I'm lucky rather than skillful, or in this case obstinate enough to, again, bust my head through the brick wall.

Other, minor gripes*:
*The game does a terrible job of scaling difficulty between areas. The most clear example is this one base where all these enemies are of the same faction, but each zone has wildly different enemy difficulties. One area had six guys who all go down in 1 to 2 attacks, and then the next door over has a dude who has seemingly total immunity to mechanical (physical) damage and hits significantly harder than the previous enemies. I know this wasn't a boss level enemy also because the boss enemy, who is also immune to mechanical damage, was behind the door on the other side of the base.
*The game lowers your initiative when entering areas, which makes sense so you can't really cheese combat by entering an area at the top of turn order then leave before your turn ends. But this also happens when entering areas you haven't explored before. If you enter an area where you HAVE been, it lowers your initiative by 999, making you go last, whatever. But enter a new area, and it ostensibly only lowers it by 10, which is flatly silly on two points. One, it leads to the RNG, brick wall debacle of just reloading the save until your miraculously roll high enough and enemies roll low enough that the -10 becomes irrelevant. Two, why is your character the one surprised by bursting into a room. I can understand that maybe an enemy is being hyper vigilant, narratively speaking, but come on. This is a hyper, underground apocalypse where you are a one-man army I'm bursting into a room SWAT style, and I'm the one with his pants down? Just seems like a weird design choice to me.

The story is rather enjoyable and somewhat interesting. If you aren't dying for some deep long-winded anthology of the underground, you are in luck as the game is very terse with its dialogue and rarely overstays its welcome.

However, due to the gameplay I still should not recommend this game. There are other games that do what this one does better. If you played those other games and are looking for more, pick this one up and see if you can bypass what has bothered me so much about it.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWARNING: UnderRail HAS NO RESPEC OPTION! YOU WILL MOST LIKELY NEED TO RESTART MULTIPLE TIMES OR READ A GUIDE!

Without a guide, you can view the description of most perks beforehand, but it is impossible to anticipate how perks, items, crafting, etc will interact with the unknown enemies ahead. Saving and reloading is unrestricted, but it is incredibly easy to unknowingly make a series of poor character build choices that you only realize many hours later. You can theoretically overcome many tough situations, but a bad build will really drag you down.

The nature of the game would make any kind of respec option way too abusable, so the dev will likely never add one. I personally do not like reading guides for the games I play, but it's so neccisary for this game that I can't recomend UnderRail. However, if you're ok with that sort of thing, you may want to consider trying this out.\t\t\t\t\t
