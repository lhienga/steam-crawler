\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhen I first played this game, I really had no clue how or why I was doing anything, what to do, etc... My original character was a sharpshooter with no psi, and I made it pretty far - until I accidentally soft-locked myself and deleted that save (still a regret I have today).

I stopped playing for a good amount of time after that. In fact, it wasn't until watching Warlockracy's recent video on it (one of the first videos that comes up when you search for Underrail, fun fact!) that I realised some of the things I had been doing wrong, and where I could go right instead (like speccing into psi. Such a help in this game). I'm enjoying the game a lot more, and in the meantime I'm writing tricks and notes down that I figure out through the game to help remind myself of how I want things to go roughly.

This is a strategy roleplaying game through and through. If you don't want to plan ahead for a fight, potential load games old saves and be prepared to die a couple times to learn what to do - fair enough. This game likely isn't going to be much fun. What also stopped me from returning was remembering how much there is to this game and feeling intimidated, but by following the quests and adventuring when I felt like it, the experience became a lot clearer to me. Underrail is massive, there's so much to see and enjoy. I definitely recommend trying it, at least through the opening parts, to see if the game sticks or not. I'm glad I returned to this gem, however.

Looking forward to getting more hours in this game :)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you're a fan of RPGs employing turn-based tactics (stuff like Fallout 1 and 2, or Wasteland games) I'd recommend Underrail, provided you are fine with something more challenging. Combat has a good amount of depth, and the mechanics are very fun to play with. I would be lying, of course, if I said there wasn't much reloading - keep the quicksave and quickload binds handy.

There are lots of ways to create a build - each of them totally unique from another - which keeps replayability high. Crafting is also phenomenal, with lots of directions to take your weaponry.

Though it is combat focused, the game is written well, and the lore and setting are very interesting. If you invest in social skills, you'll find there often are ways to avoid combat via dialogue options. Quests often have multiple solutions to them which is also great.

Additionally, I've never experienced a bug in the game. Maybe the occasional typo, but never an actual unintended flaw in the mechanics.

Overall, Underrail is fantastic and definitely gets a recommend from me, but I can understand if the difficulty is too much for anyone. I encourage anyone who's a fan of CRPGs to give it a try.\t\t\t\t\t

The combat is tight and unforgiving, the dialogue + flavor text + lore is all on point (English is not the devs' first language, but you wouldn't know it, even when the game covers concepts like geology, pharmacology, anthropology, history, politics, the numinous, and the weird), and the mood and atmosphere are *chef's kiss*.

People say it's difficult, and it's not easy: There are no permanent companions, the RNGods will get you killed (quicksave/quickload are your friends), and having an understanding of what kind of build you want to make is important — the game is skill- and feat-based, not class-based, so it's a good idea to read ahead in the feat list and take note of the prerequisites you'll need to meet to unlock the feats you want. This is a pro, not a con, as the game gives you so many different ways to play from a combat perspective.

A lot of people on forums say you need to find a build online for your first play through; this wasn't true for me. I did my first run on Normal difficulty/Classic XP, and while I did read about how a few feats worked on the wiki (which you can also do in-game), I finished the game just fine. Looking back on it, that first character is an un-optimized, goofy mess, but I totally finished the game with him, and I immediately started another run using the Oddity XP system — I wouldn't even bother with the Classic XP system, as the Oddity system feels somewhat more like milestone leveling in a TTRPG and doesn't let you powerlevel too much.

The final criticism I hear is that the game is too long/a bit of a slog, with some fans quitting when they get to the final area of the game, or partway through the DLC (a worthy purchase for the unlocked feats and items alone). This is fair; my first run was blind and took 100+ hours, and the second time I finished the DLC definitely made me feel like it was overstaying its welcome. That said, I am not complaining about spending $15 for nearly 400 hours of fun.

CRPGs are experiencing a renaissance kicked off by Divinity: Original Sin and Pillars of Eternity, and with the success of Baldur's Gate 3, I wouldn't be surprised to see more developers playing in the space in the coming years. A lot of people compare this game to Fallout 1/2, and I think I get that since it's turn-based, relies on feats and skills, and has a sardonic sense of humor. That's where the similarities end, though: From a gameplay perspective, Underrail is in a league of its own. While Larian and Obsidian put out great games, and there are other narrative-rich titles like Disco Elysium and Torment: Tides of Numenera on the market, there isn't quite another game that scratches the itch of gritty, thoughtful combat and character customization the way Underrail does.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe only game I would consider a perfect continuation from the original Fallout 1 and 2 genre. I have never felt a game capture that traditional overhead gritty sci-fi rpg feeling like Underrail did and is a flawless spiritual successor. I went in thinking "Oh I've played the OG Fallouts and plenty of rpgs and games like this hard will be easy." and boy was in for a rude surprise. This game after a few hours started beating my ass. But I adjusted, I explored, and I realized where I should be going the same way Dark Souls 1 Skeletons taught me how. This game is not afraid to absolutely murder you if you take a wrong turn and don't listen. Also the game has on of the most unique xp and leveling systems I've ever seen. Oddity XP system is a must.

The game play and systems are so incredibly fleshed out and perfected. How the stat points interact with the skills and perks, the stealth, crafting, and combat all come together amazingly. I have no idea how they managed to balance out the game so well that I could see about 4-5 builds the game just naturally lays out in front of you and all work well (Even after I messed up a bit in the start). Coming up with any complaints about the game is pretty difficult and is just the tiniest of nit picks. Some perks sometimes felt a little lackluster while others were game changing and the number of things not explained that well when creating a character were some of the few Issues I had. Normal I'm sure is fine but on Hard and above you really need to have a build and your ♥♥♥♥ sorted or the game will mess you up. I learned that the hard way but overcoming it was incredibly rewarding and satisfying. You can play blind but there was so many times I got on the wiki to double check something or reverted a save after something I didn't like happened. Save often and have 1-3 backups to go back on.

Very excited to get the dlc and do another play through in the future. I'm really happy I gave this game a chance but only pick up if you really enjoy games like Fallout 1/2, Pillars of Eternity, Divinity: Original Sin, Wasteland, and games similar to that. Not for the very casual player but you can get through it fine on easy or normal if you don't want to min max and just enjoy the genre. There is no voiced dialogue and some of the text had me skipping it. They spend just a little bit too much time explaining things that don't matter when describing characters and some never shut up. Great writing but in a 100+ hour game it gets tiresome. 99/100 near perfect for what it is and what its trying to emulate just be ready to have a permanent wiki tab open.\t\t\t\t\t
