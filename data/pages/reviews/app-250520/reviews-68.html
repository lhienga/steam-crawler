\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSave scumming is a requirement even if you're running an efficient build.

Chances are good that unless you've positioned yourself for an alpha strike from stealth, your opponents will roll higher on initiative and stunlock you to death.

Even if you've perfectly sprung your ambush, there's still that off-chance you'll miss at a crucial moment or an enemy will get a string of lucky crits on you. Time to reload your game!

With certain enemies, getting the drop on them is borderline impossible without prior knowledge and specific tools. I, of course, refer to crawlers. Personally the only ways I've managed to not get ambushed by one has been to stealth throughout every cavern with a pair of motion tracking goggles, finger hovering above the enter key (combat mode) in anticipation of spotting their shadowy silhouette creeping towards me.

The other tactic is creating a no mans land of bear traps and mines, setting off a grenade, and picking them off as they come to investigate the noise. Which is utterly cheesy and requires you to carry around a bunch of heavy traps.

The barter system also leaves a lot to be desired. Sure it's realistic that a merchant might only be willing to buy one or two things, but it didn't really stop me from amassing wealth so much as it made accumulating it slow and utterly tedious.

Not to mention the tell, don't show method of introducing various factions. For instance, the Protectorate and Free Drones. Two factions that are opposed to one another. One wishing to unite all the stations under a single banner, and the latter being a resistance group that views the former as an oppressor.

I'm not really given any reason to be invested in ANY of the factions the game introduces to me. Sure, NPCs will talk at me about them but otherwise they don't seem to have much of a presence within the game world outside of scripted events. In fact it's ridiculously easy to lock yourself out of entire quest chains without realizing it.

For instance, a train filled with supplies vital to a settlement by the name of Rail Crossing gets hijacked and you're asked to investigate by its leader. You've also been asked by the leader of your home station to aid this struggling settlement.

You discover the train has been hijacked by the Free Drones because the much needed supplies were being delivered from the protectorate. You are given the option, with enough persuasion, to intelligently point out that the bandits are harming Rail Crossing more than they are the Protectorate by hijacking the train -- prompting them to realize you have a point, and peacefully depart.

Congratulations, you've just locked yourself out of ever joining the Free Drones. You HAVE to turn a blind eye to the hijacking of vital supplies, betraying the trust of the leader of Rail Crossing AND your home settlement for no tangible reason.

Another line of quests is also ridiculously easy to lock yourself out of. In fact, you have to do a multitude of quests in a very specific, "optimal" manner in order to be able to have anything to do with the faction. If you haven't satisfied these arbitrary and opaque requirements, you're locked out.

In fact, look up the "Guide Dang It!" trope on TVTropes. Yeah. That adequately describes the vast majority of quests in the game. For instance, if you're focused on the main story and trying to find a gang of scavengers important to the main plot -- nobody really gives you any information relevant to where they are other than them being somewhere in Core City.

I only found out where they were because I got sick and tired of searching the entire city top to bottom and started a chain of completely unrelated quests for one of the factions within the city, further advancement in said faction requiring you to do ANOTHER completely unrelated chain of arena fights until the faction deems you badass enough to give you better jobs.

Then suddenly the location of the gang I'd spent hours looking for was served up to me on a silver platter by my quest giver.

I looked it up, and completing these utterly unrelated quests is apparently the only way to advance the main plot. It's so ridiculously contrived that I could honestly laugh.

It's unfortunate because this game does honestly have a lot going for it that's unfortunately dampened by RNG, dull writing, and confusing quest design.

Edit: My hours in this game isn't an indicator of me secretly loving it, nor an indicator of lack of 'skill'. I was quite enamored with it at first, true. But the longer I played, the more the flaws stood out to me.

My advice to a new player: If you're willing to push past and forgive the frustrating elements of this game, have the wiki open on your phone/other monitor, and strictly follow a build stolen from the forums, then you'll probably find some enjoyment here. The game gets quite good after the early game, but note that the 'early game' is roughly 6-10 hours long.

Also I'll save you a few hours of running from merchant to merchant.

Batteries and bullets are basically a secondary currency and accepted by somebody in every settlement. If a merchant runs out of charons, then get them to pony up some bullets/batteries. W2C and JHP are more valuable per bullet, Plasma and Fusion cells more valuable per battery. Hoard accordingly to save on weight.\t\t\t\t\t

The Good
  • The world feels large and I appreciate all the effort that went into planning factions, NPCs, and locations. While some are forgettable, most of the world is intriguing.
  • Exploration is rewarding. You often stumble upon secret areas and new routes to old locations.
  • Graphics are gritty, dull, and often repetitive. You'll either like it or hate it. I thought the drab design added to the wasteland feeling of the world.

The Bad
  • Combat gets boring fast. While you learn new abilities and skills, fights often boil down to melee slug-fests, stun/frozen-locked rounds, or psionic spam-fests.
  • Inventory management slows the game down significantly. Crafting is often bogged down by needless encumbrance. You'll feel punished for holding onto ingredients to necessary to craft. Unless you pump nearly all of your points into Strength, too many items are simply too heavy to carry. Great for realism. Awful for the number of wasted minutes running back to storage.
  • Quest management is bad. Once you start venturing out into other areas, quest information becomes both overwhelming in number and underwhelming in usefulness.
  • UI and controls are clunky and poorly designed. Accidental movement will waste precious action points, unintentional inventory management may clear hot bars, and over-world maps are uninformative.

Final Thoughts
Underrail is a fun but flawed CRPG. It's clear that the designers are fans of early Fallouts and other 90s CRPGs. Nevertheless, I'm still recommending it because there's enough content and world building to make the game feel like an updated 90s CRPG.\t\t\t\t\t

However it is no joke you have to spend a considerable amount of time tinkering with your build and figuring out what feats are viable. This is a huge barrier of entry for alot of new people, especially since the devs have gone through considerable effort to ensure that no build reigns 100% supreme over the others.

Every battle requires keen planning (ESPECIALLY FOR STEALTH BUILDS), and Depot A is a filter. Seriously if you can't get through Depot A then you have to restart your build, it's entire purpose is to weed out the non-viable builds.

For this exact reason it can be hard to replay Underrail, you sink a considerable amount of time building each character and Depot A is something that you have to struggle through.

If you're willing to knuckle down, go onto the forums, and use the character builder, you can save alot of hours planning out a build before you get the reality check that is Depot A. However don't be too deterred from this, a few mispent points are USUALLY manageable, not feats though. However, seeing your character build shine is probably the biggest reasons I keep playing this game.

Solid recommendation from me.\t\t\t\t\t
