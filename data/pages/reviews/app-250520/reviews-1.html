\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAfter 12 hours of playtime (steam says 10.2 but it's quite literally 12h in total (1pm to 1am on the same minute)).

The biggest complaints i got so far is;
-It's easy to interact with the environment mid fight, it's sometimes clicking on a corpse or a furniture and it just wastes action points.
-When there's no enemies in combat with you, you often stay in combat mode. Which limits your movements and actions. (for some reason)-------it looks bugged
-Sometimes the combats are very luck based
-The texts in the game look a bit small
-You can't build around using everything or else they all sucks...i learned it the hard way after 10+ hours put into my first character...Which i sadly reseted and went with a new build because it sucked really badly. Combat was relying on luck on my side, which needed often to reload a save before a fight just to live through...It was just annoying.
-Spears are OP....I was using a combat knife on melee and made a new character who uses a spear and MY GOD IT'S OP. The block bonus is completely overpowered in melee combat. My 2nd playthrough is a high dex/agility build and it got a ton of dodge and 50% chance to block on melee. Like wtf i'm never hit on melee anymore. This is quite nice compared to the hell i had before
-Quite easy to get lost and there's barely any guidance (old school questing)-----An example is an area that goes through the railway and it took maybe 4 hours just to find the place i had to go. There's way too many roads to take.



Despite looking like a somewhat negative review...Don't let that fool ya. The game grows on you if you're patient with it-----The old school graphics aren't terrible but in my standards, they look like trash. But hey, that's how they used to be back then. It's like a late 90s game with a modern aproach on it's features and options. Which is perfectly acceptable in 2024

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tUnderrail is an amazing game that honors and elevates the spirit of the original Fallout games. This is a game that is not for the faint of heart as you will either have to pay careful attention to the dialogue all the way through OR be constantly reading the wiki (shame shame shame).

It's a hardcore RPG so you will be managing your equipment and inventory constantly. For example, to reload a gun you need to open your inventory, right-click the bullets, select the "reload" option and then select the gun you wish to reload. You can also add that ammo type to a hotkey if you wish but it'll still be a multi-part process. A simple reload key would be cool.

Leveling is very slow and it takes a long time for your character to feel powerful. This is a good thing as you're supposed to, as I did, be very overwhelmed by how big the world is and how fleshed out and dense the story/characters/cities are. But here's my main gripe with that system. The level 30 cap is too low. I'm already at level 29 and I'm maybe halfway through the main story, halfway through the first DLC and I've barely touched the second DLC. Progression will feel hollow once I'm 30 and there will be no incentive to get into fights, be a good guy, complete side quests or keep looking for good loot. I like systems where yeah, you can keep leveling for a long time but with increasingly diminishing returns. I might only get a 1% damage increase with shotguns or whatever but progress is progress and RPGs are all about building and growing a character within the game's setting. Hitting a ceiling halfway through the game is disappointing.

Another issue I have with Underrail is the purposeful obfuscation of the game's code which is intentional and designed to keep people from modding their game. This is an offline single-player game and if people want to customize their experience they should be able to. Especially so with such a rigid leveling system. Underrail already has loads of content to keep players busy and satisfied but modding potentially adds hundreds of hours of replayability. I believe the developers are doing themselves a disservice by shunning a large portion of the gaming community.

These are my only complaints against what is otherwise a fantastic gem of a game. It's not for everyone but as a guy who played PC games through the 90's up until today, Underrail is right up my alley.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHighly recommended if you enjoy and are familiar with crunchy turn-based RPGs. I have far more hours on this in the GOG version. On the surface this game looks and plays similar to the original Fallout games, but you should go into it knowing it is much more difficult and requires careful attention to the game mechanics. Bad builds are truly bad and there is no re-spec option, so tread carefully with character creation and restart if need be.

Combat is tough and abundant. Every human enemy has access to the same skills and equipment as you, which basically means every fight that isn't 1v1 is stacked against you. Non-human enemies range between furry cannon fodder and 1-turn instant death machines. Knowing the mechanics and going into fights with your best foot forward is necessary to succeed, and even then you're probably going to find yourself save-scumming certain areas. There is really no power fantasy here, there is the constant tension of a thousand different varieties of death being right around the corner, and the feeling of accomplishment/sigh of relief when you overcome the odds. Using the Oddity XP system and a combo of stealth and social skills will allow you to avoid a lot of combat, and will probably get you through the game a lot faster than using guns/grenades/wizardry.

There is an intricate crafting system that eventually allows for higher-quality gear than you can find anywhere else in the game. I am too simple-minded for this, and I got through the game just fine without interacting with it.

The story and world-building are well done and interesting enough to keep you going, but nothing to write home about. There are a lot of different routes to take through the main story and you'll definitely need a few playthroughs to see everything.

The UI is not great, but I've seen worse. The map is kind of terrible and only useful once you realize you need to be adding notes, but this sort of works in the games favor as far as ratcheting up tension as you stumble through trying to remember the "safe" routes back to a specific area. Fast travel exists but is limited to and from a few hub areas and costs money, similar to Morrowind.

Overall this is a sick RPG, especially for a measly $15.\t\t\t\t\t
