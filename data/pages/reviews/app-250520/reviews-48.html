\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNote: some spoilers below, I've done by best to tag them.

My sneaky-SMG-nader-crafter with 0 psi was a slow burner to start, but man did he take off around level 14 or so on Hard.

Before starting the DLC, I played up to the gates of the University. That meant clearing every quest in lower and upper underrail, with a few exceptions (Jet ski dungeon I couldn't be bothered to mess with, never figured out to rig the race in the Hopperdome, but those were the only things left undone). I was probably level 22 at the time I went into the DLC.

I'm of two minds of the expansion: on the positive front I liked the exploration and lore, the tension. On the other, I really disliked the condition for stopping native assaults was to genocide the natives. In the abstract that's fine, but in practice it was a * slog*. By far the lowest point of the entire expansion. In terms of a lever to motivate the player, Styg was effective with those native attacks. After my second one, for which my build is total garbage, I was determined to stop them so I could explore the rest of the Lemurian's facilities in relative peace. I'm a dirty completionist and used the wiki moderately so that I could eek out all the content, I got the torch and destroyed the liths, helped the one native shed his scales etc.


DC was interesting, I did zero prep before starting the fight and going down. Didn't stock up anything, just went into it. That's because I thought there would be a rift down there. After around in DC for about 5 hours, I realized I was wrong and *almost* loaded a save to stock up before I descended, but I'm glad that I didn't. Even though I had a stash of materiel that would have made Old Biocorp blush, I decide that being somewhat unprepared was the intended way to do it, especially given all the items available in the Warehouse district. So glad I didn't reload, because the zone itself was a cake walk as was defeating Tchort. I was level 28 at this point, and with tungsten carbide 7.62 and all geeked out on tribal nose candy I could shred multiple tentacles in a turn. Took 1.5 turns before I look at Tchort's HP. He died in a single turn, I think I took bio damage once.

It was a masterpiece, I don't even want to look at the number of hours this took, and I'm cautiously fabulously optimistic about Infusion. It's a good thing that it won't be out until we're closer to 2030 than 2020. Underrail was great, but I need a break.

My final build was https://underrail.info/build/?HAMICgMMAwfClmQAAB4AwpZfSyYZWGNMN1MAAAAATAA8Jjs5HWE3TxZJGUfChVMvTijioIoD4qKNA-KktgLip6oD4qicAeKxggHfvw



Compared to the build as I planned it, on Hard, I shifted some feats around and dropped a few as I became comfortable with the rhythm of my character. As I mentioned above, once I really started to get deadly (~16ish) MPs became limiting so I picked up Hit and Run a lot earlier than I anticipated - before blitz even. Another feat which sounded useful on paper, Escape Artist, I really didn't need until late in the DLC. You spend so much time sneaking and killing, roots weren't a large problem. Sure, rarely I would get hit by them, by they just added flavor to the ebb and flow of combat. Stuns on the other hand were usually lights out for my character, so I kept Bullheads handy.

In the same vein of MPs being limited, I only put a single spec point into Blindsiding because I wasn't damage constrained after spec'ing earlier feets, but I was MP constrained. Dropping those extra 3 or 4 pts into Hit and Run really maximized my per-turn-kills (I can't remember if I actually put a spec point into Escape Artist or not, if not then HaR got 4).

Weapons: I know some SMG users are all about the 5 mm, and maybe that would have been even more efficient, but with 9 DEX (no food), I like the 7.62 crafted jaguar. My end game gun was with rapidly reloader and muzzle break, combined with Commando and Spec Ops, I was like shelf-whacking pinata with AP falling out. My 7.62's item description was 13 - 39, 8 ap cost. I did use a refurbished 5 mm Mini-izu as my silenced weapon, which I used a lot, because I just couldn't craft anything that touched it.

I didn't like the food mechanic very much, I dawdle too much to keep eel burgers, so I went with 9 DEX and pumped the rest into PER. As others said, you need it because PER will contribute directly to your GUNS score, and you want every bullet of that burst to make contact. Late DLC and DC enemies I regularly had ~70-80% chance to hit with burst, with no LS or forward grip and no light penalties. 1 point of PER in the end game is worth about ~10 gun skill points, you can see it yourself by examining the gun skill before/after equipping +1 googles.

Last point: grenades were a lot of fun. A lot of fun. I was frustrated early on that I couldn't throw them that often, but pick the right feats and you'll grow into them. This was the perfect build for me, I love slow burners and there was a huge amount of satisfaction to be had once the character got into it's prime. Too much perhaps, as I'm a little sad I didn't play on DOM. But who can say, maybe the start would have been too annoying and I wouldn't have finished?

Anyway, 10/10. Pure slavic cave wizardry. I'm just a dirty railrat, and I love it.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA very good isometric RPG that expects you to already understand how to play RPGs. I'll try to review this game with as few spoilers as possible while also addressing some specific criticisms I have with it.

Note: I have only one "complete" playthrough, albeit with a good amount of reloading things to see alternate outcomes. The review is focused around the base game.

Pros:
  • Choices matter (even accidental ones). While there are many quests that you might forget about once they're completed, there are also many quests which are seemingly irrelevant that will have unintended side effects or may greatly affect major story points further on in the game. You can often see meticulous detail being put into the game to keep things interconnected.
  • The setting is very interesting and story-rich. I didn't expect much from it going in, but I did find myself delving into many areas just looking for bits of lore to explain how/why details of Underrail's setting. Most of the setting is explained, if you look around enough or try different outcomes.
  • Almost all NPCs can be killed. This may seem like a weird thing to put into the "Pros" section, however I find that unkillable NPCs can really break immersion. The previous points above also play into this as well, so expect that killing a major NPC to have massive influence later on. That said, there is at least one NPC who cannot be killed.
  • Goals and quests usually have many ways to solve them. The option of violence is basically always there, and is often encouraged or even forced upon you, depending on the situation. Diplomacy, exploration, stealth, trading, hacking, etc are so often available that if you wonder if you can do a certain method, you probably can.
  • Good crafting system. There is an incredible amount of crafting options available for basic items like healing items, trinkets, tools... However if you want the *best* equipment in the game, you must use crafting and dedicate a large amount of skill points into it.
  • Combat is very good. NPCs and enemies can often be killed fairly easily, but so can you. Preparation and positioning are necessary to ensure you don't get killed. You don't have a party, only yourself, so making the effort to keep any fight under your own control is necessary and satisfying.
Cons:
  • Walking. Lots... and lots... of walking. Expect to very slowly backtrack while carrying useful things to a stash for later use. The game includes a feature which allows you to speed it up, but being over-encumbered is seemingly completely unaffected by this, so you can't even speed that up. The game clearly intends that you should not be a pack rat and to often just leave loot where it lay.
  • Loading screens are very common and frequent. Every area has a level transition which will give you a brief black loading screen. In addition, some regions in the game just feel extremely small where there doesn't seem to be a reason for it. Combine this with the criticism above, you will have walking... then a loading screen... then more walking. And this is assuming you aren't over-encumbered. There is some degree of "Fast Travel", but you have to pay for it and it is only available for a few areas.
  • Occasional bugs or oversights. These are fairly rare that I can't think of more than a few, but I recommend saving frequently with several backups just in case you manage to softlock yourself. I have seen at least one NPC conversation that I had to reload because the dialogue to "Tell me more about X" gave no options except to change the subject.
  • Unique weapons are almost worthless. I have seen this discussed in several places, but overall, unique items are almost always a very brief means to an end. I don't mind some unique items being unusable for a specific character build, however this game is challenging and expects you to do at least a little bit of min-maxing. The only time I have ever kept a unique weapon is if it has some secondary utility like increasing an attribute or skill. Otherwise, it's shiny trash that you either sell or stash away. I would greatly prefer that unique weapons could be dissembled for a unique component or something similar that at least lets you use some of them.
  • Expect to save scum a bit and potentially start a new game. This is sort of tied in with the difficulty of the game, but I don't find the challenge to be a bad thing. The problem here is that the game expects you to know how to build a character fairly well and for your first or even second playthrough, you aren't going to know much of the game to be able to do this. You will be given a task in a certain region called Junkyard where your character is truly put to the test and if you find yourself having difficulty that requires reloading often, you should probably just take what you've learned and start a new character. Annoyingly, this is the part of the game where it really opens up to possibilities and it takes roughly a few hours to reach this point.

Extra Thoughts:
The game is definitely similar to Fallout 1 or 2, and if you like them, you'll probably like Underrail.

Perhaps I play a bit slowly, but I would estimate an Underrail playthrough with a large amount of exploration to take 200~ish hours. The normal (I recommended it) difficulty is challenging and rewarding as your character will generally need to specialize a bit to actually be able to survive. Characters cannot try every possible solution or explore every interesting place/detail, so you'll have to replay it to see certain things you missed in a previous playthrough.

The game is definitely focused on combat and building toward a strong combat-capable character. Don't go into this game expecting to be able to sweet-talk, sneak, or hack past every encounter. Those definitely help, but not always.

The game has a point of no return called Deep Caverns which is interesting in itself, but I don't like being stuck in a location for too long with no way to prep or use anything I stashed for a rainy day.

TL;DR:
I'd rate the game a 4/5 star RPG for people who don't expect to have their hands held. Definitely worth the asking price and a replay.\t\t\t\t\t
