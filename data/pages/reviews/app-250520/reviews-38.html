\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLook at the amount of time I have playing this game. Look at my other game reviews. I am not easy to impress. This game will filter you. It will filter you hard enough to make you limp home and cry for your lost innocence and money. It almost filtered me.

Do you know what finally convinced me this game was amazing?

At the end of the first act, a character asks you to give a report about the security condition of a military base you've just been to. He asks, how many guards? Are there turrets? Dogs? Are you allowed to roam freely? It was towards the end of my play session and, thinking little of the conversation at the time, I went to sleep. In my dreams I realized the devs were trying to tell me something. 'You are having a hard time because you don't ask these questions. You don't scout enemy positions. You don't equip weapons fit for your enemy. You didn't optimize your character build. You don't research your options. You. Are. Stupid.'

I awoke shocked. My unconscious mind had realized what my conscious one had not. I remade my character and focused on a theme: Angry, wandering samurai. I noticed that swords give enemies a debuff when you hit them that made them more likely to be critically hit. I focused on crits above all other considerations. For my defense, it was shadows and surprise. I stopped playing fair. Why should I use mere swords when I can place booby traps across narrow areas and around corners so enemies cant see them? When my enemies found my bear traps I emerged and chopped them while they were helpless. Why should I use a mere molotov cocktails when I can create a mark 3 plasma grenade and one shot whole groups of my foes at the same time? Why should I fight man-to-man when I can taser a foe and then chop his head off before he can even move? I noticed the really badass gear can't be bought, it can only be crafted. Remember, never neglect to craft. NEVER.

I won't spoil anything, but I just beat the game. I noticed that less than 2% of the players that bought the game actually beat the final boss. Less than 2 percent, people. Let that hit you.

If you love zoning out and gaming to chill, this ain't the game for you. If you love a story that tells itself and just kind of cliches you through the motions, this ain't the game for you. If you love playing the hero and saving the world and getting the praise and glory, this really ain't the game for you. It's a role playing game, you play the role and you play the game.

You are constantly thinking. Every new room is a new challenge. Every new enemy has a way to beat it. No character respecs, don't mess it up. Every level counts. Don't be a coward, pick oddity experience. Don't be a loser, never use the wiki. If you get filtered, come up with whatever excuse you want for your failure and get on with your life. Sorry you didn't appreciate this little gem of a game.

Remember: ANY problem in the game has a solution. Sometimes its hard and sometimes its easy. Sometimes you will laugh at how you couldn't have noticed the easy af solution before. Fight hard, take cover, gank the psionic first and always have a taser ready. When the cave walls close in around you, you're out of shield and the crawlers are lined up at the door, just shoot up that adrenaline and realize: You may be having more fun playing an rpg than you ever have in your life. Plus, your gas grenades are off cooldown. That's how you know it's time to party.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really enjoyed Underrail because it doesn't furnish you with easy answers. The plot only starts to reveal itself about halfway in, and even then the world is so oblique and mysterious that you're never entirely sure what you're seeing, hearing, and reading is even accurate. It gives one the impression of a place that, as a whole, is just barely clinging to life; the hows and the whys of Underrail's existence lost to time, pushed aside because these people need to survive. The best parts of Underrail, to me, were traversing the caves and tunnels completely alone; listening to Josh Culler's gloomy, melancholic score; salvaging equipment from rubble piles to sell; listening to the noise in the background in case of a surprise attack, weapon always at the ready. What was my character thinking of? Was he thinking about the weight of the information he's obtained in his exploration of forgotten places? Was he afraid? Was he thinking about his place in everything he's seen? Was he worried he'd be another skeleton rotting away in some dank corner?

Underrail is likewise stingy when it comes to information about how its gameplay works. Whether or not you believe this to be a detriment will be based on perspective. For my money, I appreciated this because it places the danger of the world as paramount to the experience of traversing the Underrail. Combat is difficult in the Underrail because survival in general is difficult. In the hands of less-talented developers, where the production design and world-building is not as cogent or cohesive as Underrail's is, a lack of explanation of gameplay mechanics just means you're stuck with a lackluster world. Much bellyaching is made about save-scumming in other reviews, but I see it as just another example of Underrail's many puzzles (some of which are ridiculously complicated, necessitating paper and pen). Come prepared for the fight, it says. If you're not prepared, leave and come back. Write a note on your map to come back later (I did this many, many times). Try out the traps. Use grenades. Switch up your weapons. Kite the enemies. Use positioning. Use stealth. When I leveled up enough to stand up to those son-of-a-b*tchin' Death Crawlers, I felt great. There's much variety to be had in builds, too: if all else fails, start over and pay attention this time (I did this maybe three or four times before it clicked).

I had annoyances with the game, sure. Mostly mechanical things (some area transitions are hidden behind the environment, making navigation difficult; or certain loot, based on where it's positioned on the screen becoming difficult to pick up via clicking), but these are so minor and trivial that I can scarcely hold them as true detriments. It's all the less important when you realize this game has a credit list of only 11 people, five of whom are credited as actual developers. Ridiculously impressive scope and depth for such a small team.\t\t\t\t\t
