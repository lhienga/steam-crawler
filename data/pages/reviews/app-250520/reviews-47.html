\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTook two attempts to playthrough to an ending on this one, no fault of the game, just my own issues.

It's simultaneously one of the best and most disappointing indie CRPGs I've played.

Combat is fun, builds and build diversity is fun, but at the same time sometimes long combats can be a slog, 'friendly' units on the map actively make your job 100x harder because one tiny sliver of friendly fire will turn their entire faction against you globally, and there are some locations where if you do not have a specific talent/build, you're in for a long long LONG slog.

And do not get me started on the final region and its infinitely respawning enemies. After an entire game of 'clear an area, then explore and loot' becomes 'clear an area, explore a few steps, loot a few things, and now you're in combat again' it was anti-fun to an extreme that I just said screw it, loaded up a CheatEngine table and saved myself hours of tedium so I could just walk through zones without being accosted by enemies. By this point I was max level, had a great build, combat lacked challenge, but the tedium of trying to get through this area and finish the damn game was not enjoyable and I just wanted to get it over with, hence the CE use.

Back to good things...The setting and its lore are fascinating to delve into, especially if you're playing an intelligent/high will character with psionics and the writing for the 'weird' events that occur just give that perfect vibe of unknown horrors beyond ones understanding. One of the standout parts for me was the long philosophical discussions with The Ferryman and piecing together the addled things said by Dude and relating them to the 'reality' of the setting. Just great stuff, but note that my fav bits of writing came from the Expedition expansion content. They seemingly upped their game a lot by that point.

While this is a positive thing for me, it may be negative for others...much of the 'background' story and history is not spoonfed to you. You need to go digging for it, you need to remember names, places, years, and try to piece together the puzzles on your own with the information you've found and remembered over the course of the game. I love this sort of thing, I love having to engage with a setting and its history in that way, rather than be given a list of people and events and told exactly what happened. Some people hate that.

But on the downside...a lot of questlines just seem to end very abruptly, especially any that are cantered around the various factions in the game. You do 3-4 missions for them, and...that's it. Nothing further until the ending slides. There's so much room seemingly for them to be added on, fleshed out, interact more, but almost every time you get to the last quest and are like "...wait, that's all? More please?"

I suppose for an analogy, the game is a really good meal with some rare and amazing flavors, but afterwards you still find yourself kind of hungry.

I really hope they take their time with content for the next Underrail game. Visually and mechanically it's seeming as though it'll be a big step up, but I want quests, lore, story all to drown me in their abundance.\t\t\t\t\t

Engages my mind rather my reflexes
Auto-saving of current sessions
Consistent rules for solving puzzles

Limited space for circuit specification
Strict syntax for circuit specfication
Puzzles have to solved in order
