\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI would pass on this one. The leveling system is very strange. You gain experience by finding things that are scattered around the world. This is interesting in that it provides an incentive to explore, but it fails in execution because your character moves so slowly. Traveling even short distances in the game is very tedious because of how slow your character moves.

You also never have enough money to buy anything because no merchants buy anything. You might as well just stuff all your loot in a container in your character's apartment because even when you finally do manage to find the one merchant who purchases the stuff you want to sell they only buy a couple and that's it.

As if those two things weren't enough of an issue, there are so few experience items in the world that you will always be under-leveled for the area you need to complete in order to progress the story. You fall into this catch 22 predicament where you need to level up in order to have high enough skills to complete your quests, but you can't explore to find more experience items because you are too low of a level to survive fighting the monsters and enemies roaming around. There is an option to change to a more traditional experience/leveling system where you gain levels by killing enemies, but the story and gameplay are just not compelling enough play the game on easy mode. This game is nothing that has not been seen before countless times.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDo not buy this game if you don't like spending a significant amount of time in an EXTERNAL character planer (google it. Use it.) or the idea of using someone elses build.
Underrail is extremly punishing when it comes to "messing up your build" since you will only truely identify the issues 10 to 20 hours into your run at which point it is very difficult to fix them, for the most part imossible.

That said: The world of underrail is nicely crafted and interconnected in several layers of verticality.
The NPC interactions are passable to good, the quests are good enough to get you interested in diving through which ever subsection of the cave and tunnelsystem you are in, but the real gem of this game is the combat. It is simplistic on the surface but the build veriaty opens up different playstyles which approach encounters quite differently from each other. In addition you fight enemies which, for the most part, possess unique mechanics, like more damage and critical chance when they surround you, unlocking more dangerous attacks when they stack on each other, an endless spawn of new enemies, proper ambush tactics which will have you fear empty dark corners of the tunnels. Human enemies are in essence player characters at a set level with set abilities and equipment, as such they can be EXTREMLY dangerous. All in all the different mechanics and t hreat level of the enemies you face as well as the map designe make you play this more like a messed up version of X-Com than like an old Fallout game.
Another outstanding mechanic is the crafting. You can craft consumables, grenades, ammunition, special ammunition (stun, fire damage, poison .. ), traps, weapons, armor and various utility items with components you either buy of vendors with your limited cash supply or find on hostile creatures or disensamble from the gear of your enemies.
There also is a Psi-System which is basically your avarage magic, coming with a drawback.
Those abilities can be used together with regular firearms, melee or what ever you want, or can be the very core of your build.

Overall it is a very good, but difficult game. I can not stress enough that you will not get an "easy" experience your first time through, no matter what guide you consult. Part of the difficulty is knowing when to use what and how not to burn through your limited ressources. Guides can not provide you with that knowledge and skill. That beeing said: Since even the easy difficulty is quite punishing, I highly recommend people who want a more casual experience to stay away, or treat this as an experiment, to see if they might enjoy something that requires a good bit of work to be enjoyed.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you love the first and second Fallout games, and or Love the Metro Novels/games, I would highly recommend you give this excellent CRPG a shot. At the time of writing this review, I managed to somehow put in 22.9 hours of this game in the last 3 days. If you too wish to lose a part of yourself, then look no further. Couple things/tips to note that I've learned so far:

- Your build truly matters, you cannot spec into everything and be left with a viable character. I still recommend going in as blind as possible, but if you're worried about getting stuck/needing to start over, there are lots of build guides out there.

- There are no ♥♥♥♥♥♥ way points. You have to navigate based on your own perception and vague directions (as it should be).

- You're gonna die alot, Combat is quick and brutal. You need to use every trick you can think of to survive.

- For the love of god, save your game, and I'm not just talking about quick saving. I was trapped in Hathor Mine (and early questing area) for nearly 3 hours because I had no antidotes, almost no health hypos and didn't have a save prior to me entering the mines. I managed to get away with it after struggling, but it could have easily ended my playthrough. Save frequently!

- If you enter combat and run into another zone (The game map is broken into sections) you will have a massive initiative debuff if you re-enter within 90 minutes, and will almost certainly go last.

- Failing quests is part of the game, don't be too worried about ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ up.

- Craft, Craft, Craft!!! You can save a lot of money.

- The economy of this game isn't as straight forward as other RPGs. Functionally its similar to Fallout 1/2's bartering system, however not all merchants are willing to buy your junk, and even those that are aren't willing to buy it all.\t\t\t\t\t
