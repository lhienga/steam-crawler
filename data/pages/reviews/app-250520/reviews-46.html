\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm really enjoying this game, most of the way through it now. As others have stated, it's pretty difficult and it does not hold you hand much at all. If it helps new players, here's a few things I would suggest. Follow the main storyline missions until you level up a few times, don't do the random missions (follow the wiki to know which ones are which), because those are simply unrealistic for brand new characters but it will let you do them and even tell you to, right away. Skip those until you're ready. The story line missions are progressed appropriately.

This build is working well for me: Probably the single most important feat was immunity from caltrops/acid puddles, then the sprint ability, and the one that adds movement points based on connected melee attacks. My character uses all manner of poisoned caltrops. Also, I use a lot of immunity from fire armor pieces, which can be very light, and have low armor penalty. So for tough fights, get close but don't trigger combat yet, and scatter caltrops everywhere. This won't start combat even if you saturate the battlefield with them. Then start combat by throwing molotovs, this often creates a fear status effect which panics them and they run all around the caltrops. Then basically give them a couple turns to run around, damage themselves, heal themselves (so they run out of meds), damage themselves some more, throw more molotovs, and stay in a defensive position. By the time they catch up to you, many of them will be down to 1% health (caltrops will never kill them, although the poison might. they just damage down to almost dead). And then it's easy, just run around and shiv/melee everyone. At first it can be hard to find enough metal plates and burrower poison to stay stocked up on poisoned caltrops, "Fixer" in junkyard sells poison, and Junkyard is also a good place to stock up on metal plate.

I really don't have anything bad to say about this game. It's fun. The crafting and exploration are great, the combat remains challenging and enjoyable, and the writing is really immersive. When I get some cash to spend I'm getting Expedition add-on and trying that out.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you liked Fallout 1 and 2, you'll like this game. It's pretty hard, minimal hand holding. The character build system is pretty complex and not super well explained. The UI is an improvement over the OG fallout games, but it still leaves a lot to be desired.

The world and writing are great. Combat is unforgiving, and whoever shoots first tends to win. This goes both ways. Walk into an ambush without a bullet proof vest and you'll have a bad time. Take the time to stealth around the battlefield and shape it to your liking with mines and caltrops and the enemy won't stand a chance.

8/10, highly recommend to all classic RPG fans.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI tried to love this game, I really did. But for all the comparisons to the classic Fallout games, this one falls off substantially. Here's why:
1. Humanoid NPCs and the player operate under entirely different rulesets. While in Fallout 1 or 2, you could run into enemies with 10 AP and ludicrously high HP/AC, they couldn't have more than 10. If firing a gun took the player 6AP, it took the NPC 6AP. Furthermore, because NPCs generally couldn't have more than 10 AP, combat was much more balanced in that an NPC committing to an attack and failing would give the player the upper hand in most scenarios, and if you weathered their attacks you could be certain to be able to retaliate in kind.
In Underrail, NPCs can move, open doors, and use high-AP attacks multiple times per turn. Usually, these NPCs are in groups, and with my limited playtime their resources do not seem to diminish. This means that the early game is entirely a joyless slog of getting repeatedly killed by bandits because for whatever reason they can sprint the breadth of a room and still crack your skull open repeatedly with a hammer in one turn.
2. NPC density is not conducive to the aforementioned gameplay. If the game featured one or two of these ridiculous NPCs at a time, it would be fine. However, routinely the player is thrown against groups of 6-10 NPCs. Therefore, it feels less like an RPG experience you'd enjoy and more like Little Timmy is DMing your DnD campaign and he wants to "win."
3. Death screens. Get ready for a lot of them, and they are by no means subtle. I don't have anything against chiding the player for messing up; Fallout famously has Ron Perlman roast you every time you died. But the death screen in this game in particular tells you that "death comes quickly in Underrail" as though it's some badge of honor. Yes, game, I am PAINFULLY aware I have died because I was "unprepared", but since you're the one in charge of preparing me, it kinda just sounds like gloating.
4. Focus on resource mechanics. Perhaps it's just me and my misguided delusions, but I like my games to be more about playing the game and less about digging through dumpsters looking for the one item that MAY help me work through this particular situation. Your character is incapable of utilizing skills without tools to do so; the tools themselves, however, are consumable. So even the most adept lockpicking god has a pass-fail binary check that always consumes a lockpick. This, coupled with the (admittedly quite realistic) pickiness with bartering means you spend most of your time either looking for supplies or finding people to trade those supplies to. This would be fine, but the aforementioned issues with combat encounters compound this issue dramatically. If I barely scrape by, using every tool at my disposal, painstakingly drag myself towards the prize and then get shot by someone standing around a corner, I can't help but feel like the effort and resources were a waste.
5. Binary skill checks. Skills in this game work on a pass/fail check system. This works in a game like New Vegas because there aren't objectives that require a certain skill, there are *approaches* which do so. In this game, main progression is locked behind leveling lockpicking and hacking, making for an excellent skill point sponge to dry up my enjoyment of character building. There are, actually, other approaches to these tasks. I was mistaken.

In conclusion, there is no one glaring, critical flaw with Underrail that demands all attention; it has genuine creativity and artistry behind it. The small annoyances and inconsistencies, however, stack up to a point where I found myself asking if I was having fun or trying to *convince* myself I was having fun.

Edit: The volume of flaming comments does not change my opinion. It does the opposite.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPersonally, despite enjoying both fallout 1 & 2, I did not enjoy this game.

The images left the impression that there was large amount of build variety in this game. It is true you can take you characters in a lot of directions, but it felt the options were not very even or well-balanced. Additionally, the oddity exp system kind of forces you to go into lockpicking/pickpocket/hacking or take an exp penalty. There is a classic exp mode, but the exp values are very inflated and you will level up much more often than you really should be.

It also feels like certain aspects of the game aren't finished. With a stealth skill and armor penalties I thought I could play an exciting sneaky character. Thus far, there is no way to 'sneak attack', you can't make noise to attract or distract enemies, you can't even set off traps by shooting them or something.

I do find the story and dialogue events pretty interesting, but the fact I dislike how combat plays and there is a deluge of combat in this game, I think I'm finished with it.

It is only 15$, and 15$ for 4ish hours of enjoyment certainly ain't the worst deal I've ever made\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tVery similar to the first two Fallout games, in the best possible way.

I loved the environments, the communities/settlements/cities. The sprawling underground railway, the smog-filled Foundry, and everything else to be found. The main story kept me going, the side stories got me wishing for more. The factions were great, I especially enjoyed that for once we don't get to join every faction. In one area we get to choose between three different ones, in another two different ones. Very nice indeed.

Overall I have mostly good things to say about the game, but I will mention that the difficulty is a bit all over the place. For one of the many factions you can join the first mission is kind of hard, lots of enemies to take down and most of them with good gear. The second mission for the same faction has you fighting essentially the first enemies you fight in the game. Feels like those two either should have been in a different order or with different enemies in the second one.

The other thing I will make the tiniest complaint about is that some of the faction quest lines kind of just end. You do one mission, you learn a big thing aaaaand no payoff. Everyone just acts like normal, no new dialogue, just... nothing more to do there. That's something I hope they change for the sequel, at least give the faction quest lines proper endings. Have the leaders go "You've done everything you can for us, thank you." and at least we know it's done.

I realize it sounds like I disliked the game with these tangential complaints, but this is definitely a must play in my book. A solid 8/10 game for sure.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI originally played this years ago, dropping it after a few hours. This turned out to be a horrible decision, because Underrail is a fantastic game.


TL;DR: A great and very challenging CRPG game with rich worldbuilding, varied characters, quests and choices, and gameplay systems with surprising depth.


TL;DR 2: Someone loved the Fallout 3 metro areas so much they created their own game within them and actually pulled it off well.


Gameplay i guess
You play as a blank slate character, with a skills and perk system very similar to other games in the genre such as Fallout 1 and 2, but a varied amount of "builds" to try seen in more modern games. Want to play as a gunslinger who clears an entire group before your turn ends? Or maybe as a psionic who freezes someone in time and psychic punches another from across the room? Or maybe a stealth character who lays traps in a single doorway and baits out enemies one by one? (me)
The game leverages this with two separate levelling systems that you choose at the start, the typical XP system and the "Oddity" system, where you gain XP through finding Oddity items in the world and dropped by enemies. The Oddity system is great as it encourages exploration, and it allows you to actually play into builds that skip combat, you don't lose anything except what enemies would drop, and they add extra worldbuilding value, they are all relevant to where you find them.
Underrail adds onto this with a detailed crafting system, which seemed confusing or downright useless at first, but can become very useful (and profitable) depending on your playstyle. Almost every craftable item has "extra slots" of which to mix and match different components to suit your needs by bringing different effects, with varying levels of quality determining how good those effects will be.


Combat
The combat, which again follows the typical "action points" formula, is split into movement and "action", being attacking, extra movement, using items etc. All enemies have stats and skills of their own, with human enemies having the capability of having the same skills as you. You are given a lot of tools to get the job done, a varied amount of weapons, ammo types, grenades, traps, drugs, food etc.
I originally dropped the game for its combat, I felt underpowered even against "beginner enemies", but that's because Underrail isn't a handholding game. It wants you to learn all the varying armour types and their weaknesses, ammo types and when and where you should use them, which enemies you should prioritise, and when to actually run away. A lot of this can be gleaned from common sense, "this molotov probably won't do much against this sentry bot, but these armour piercing bullets will", but this doesn't mean that the combat is flawless, there are many times where it was incredibly frustrating, having 20 enemies barrel down on you and barrage you with stuns and grenades and you end up throwing a grenade at your feet and walking into your own trap are not fun, but nevertheless expected in this genre.


Bad stinky parts imo
Where things fall short is with the stealth mechanics, which feel underdeveloped. Stealth is fairly simple: enemies have a vision cone in front of them that extends in a short radius around them, which is affected by certain conditions, such as lighting and your own skills and items. You can't SEE this, only an coloured icon indicator showing how alert they are. This works fine for high stealth builds; you can easily walk straight past people and be fine. Where it falls apart is with security cameras, which have wallhacks and aimbots and can spot you immediately and tell every enemy to sprint to your last seen location. You can't destroy these either (even with silenced weapons!!!) without also alerting enemies.


Epic Lore and Story and other things I suppose
Underrail is set hundreds of years into the future after some global catastrophe has taken place, leading humanity to reside inside a huge subterranean network of metro tunnels, caves and bunkers, known as the Underrail.
You play as a new member of South Gate Station, a relatively new settlement on the "frontier" of the Underrail. After completing the introductory stuff, the game opens up a surprising amount, the only barriers really being getting lost (VERY easy), the main questline, or running into high level enemies that one shot you.


Can't be an RPG without meaningful choices
In true CRPG fashion, many choices are given to you, where to go, shall I kill this one guy who's annoying, shall I steal from this shop? Shall I rat out this one guy who asked me to murder his wife over one argument? Should I plant TNT in the station cafeteria because why the hell not? Some quests range from absolutely mundane, such as catching food for somebody, to depressing, such as searching for this guys girlfriend who stumbled into a nest of dangerous animals, to badass, such as fighting in a televised gladiatorial bloodsport in the largest city in Underrail.
Almost all quests have multiple ways to complete them, with varying degrees of moral outcome, with some even having further consequences. The game does a good job of riding the line of the typical "you are the chosen one" and "you are a nobody", you are effectively both. You're just a guy who's good at living in this harsh world, and people want you for your expertise, and at the same time, you're the same as everyone else.


Eyeballs and Ears Reaction
The aesthetics are obviously Fallout inspired (in places) but aren't a one-to-one copy, of course it's a post-apocalypse game so there will be places where the houses are made out of junk, but then there are areas that just look like normal places (although really gross, they should hire more cleaners).
The music is usually unsettling and creepy, as it should be seeing as the game takes place mostly in dark caves and tunnels. There's no 'combat only' music, but in this case it doesn't need it, it would probably make it worse.
Unfortunately the setting does do it a disservice, it's kinda hard to make variations on "cave" and "metro tunnel", although each settlement and major location does look suitably unique with their aesthetic and music (shoutout Utility Tower for being amazingly tense and creepy).


Bad smelly parts
There is a notable lack of a "reputation" system, with the replacement being a disappointingly Skyrim-esque "if we know you did it we are all hostile forever even if we never actually saw you", when performing nefarious activities in designated places, which can also be ambiguous with certain quest outcomes. This also unfortunately occurs when you accidentally scrape a friendly character with a grenade, hitting them for one damage, turning all of them hostile (and the entire faction).


The DLC
Won't go into too much detail because it's really good, essentially just a three way brawl between a heavily armed school trip, snake worshipping albino tribals (who you can't even understand) and aussie pirates on a smaller version of Sunless Sea's Unterzee. It's fantastic, basically adds an extra game into the game, looks amazing and has incredible amounts of worldbuilding, rewards and just cool af stuff tbh. Also it has Jetskis.\t\t\t\t\t
