\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really wanted to like this one. I'm a huge fan of RPGs, turn based combat, and post apocalyptic settings.

Honestly, after only 6 hours, I'm ready to give up. The way quests are laid out in this game feels like the developer(s) are trying to catch you out. Oh, you didn't write down the completely unspecific instructions unremarkable NPC #592 gave you two hours ago? You actually presumed the game would act as if it was made after 1996 and record important quest information for you to look at later? Well... I guess you just have no idea where you're going now, because the NPC doesn't remember the directions either and can't repeat them!

I'm writing this review after alt-f4ing a side quest regarding a robot and some batteries. I have the batteries, I've spoken to both NPCs related in the quest (even though the game didn't tell me where they are), but now the only information I have is a vague instruction to recharge this robot. To quote the wiki, the NPC is "...just outside the SGS station proper, in the metro tunnel underpass. He is monitoring the north underpass exit to the Under-passages beneath Lower Underrail." Do me a favor and say that sentence out loud. If it sounds like your 40 IQ uncle giving directions, that's because it gives you no idea whatsoever where these people are. "SGS station proper" is not referenced ANYWHERE in the game.

Furthermore, where is the robot? Where was it sent? How do I use the batteries on it if it isn't a part of this temporal plane? None of these questions are answered in game, and worse yet, not on the first 20 entries on google either.

To quote the wiki "You're sent off to recharge it (any battery size will do.) Once the explorer bot is moving, escort it through the dark passages until you reach the northwest corner. From there is a small transition to a cave with a trapped group of smugglers, lead by Zack. You don't have to deal with their problems, just find out what's going on there. Go back to Harland for your reward."

It's written as if the robot is sitting next to the quest NPC, or in an adjacent location, but after nearly half an hour of searching I can confirm that this is not the case. I got to this part in the quest without ever seeing this robot. It is, I can only presume after reading some 40+ google entries, in some small dark cave somewhere on the periphery of the zone that I am in. However, I am unable to find any instructions whatsoever in game, or on the wiki, or on the first 5 pages of google where in the holy ♥♥♥♥ that cave might be.

I can only presume that this is an intended feature. The developer must feel that every NPC having selective amnesia improves world-building or something. It's definitely interesting, and is one way to stretch a 40-hour storyline into a 500 hour experience by forcing players to constantly back-track, write down every single bit of dialogue NPCs say on notepad, and scour the internet for some 5-year-old unanswered post about someone else with the same problem for every single quest, but it's not for me.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA friend of mine asked me, rightfully so, why I haven’t reviewed this game, yet, even after constantly talking about how much I love absolutely everything about it.

Well, today I finished my third playthrough and let me just quickly sum up what makes this cRPG so special compared to any other RPG out there, especially in a world that uses the term “RPG” ]VERY lightly.

  • The replayability is amazing, there is so much stuff to do, so much mutually exclusive content. One wrong dialogue choice, one second too fast on your trigger or one step too far and you’ve locked yourself out of the other way. This is a good thing. It makes your actions count. You know how in Elder scrolls you become the head of every guild in one playthrough? How rewarding did that feel? You can literally kill anyone and anything in the entire game and keep going. The fact that only one person initially made this entire game is mind-boggling.
  • The combat is breath-takingly well done. I can only speak for normal difficulty AI, which is not exactly good, but the variety: oh my. There are so many damage types, just to name a few: electric, mechanical, bio, acid, plasma, heat, cold and a few more I think. Every armor has resistances, damage threshold and so many stats that it makes Diablo II’s gear compare look like a child’s game. So many widely different enemies, so much utility to use against them. Traps, poisons, psi abilities, gas/smoke/incendiary/frag/emp/HE/plasma grenades of different types each, kicking people in the nuts, yelling at them. Every fight is different, tactics and preparation are everything. You’re not the chosen one in this game, you’re a regular human going against all odds.
  • Leveling is sooooo satisfying. There are so many combat and non-combat skills, feats (think perks) and abilities. UnderRail relies heavily on min-maxing your character, investing in what you’re good at instead of doing all at once. Also what I love are the feats. I hate it when a “perk” gives you 2% more chance to X. This game has perks like that, but always with a twist, but there are also lots of unique abilities like freeing yourself from immobilization effects or the ability to throw traps during combat.
  • This game is looter’s paradise. The only game I’ve played with more item variety is RuneScape, which, as an MMO, doesn’t really count. So many unique items to find and there are 100s of crafting components, almost all of them have a certain quality, so many recipes to find and it’s still not shoved down your throat. You don’t NEED drugs (at least on normal difficulty), but they are useful. What you need, though, is armor and most likely weapons of some kind, and the best ones are gotten by making them yourself. There is also lots of variety attachment-wise. If you’re into finding interesting stuff and rummaging through every container you find in any game you’re in for a treat.
  • Speaking of which: rummaging through stuff is heavily rewarded, this game has two different XP systems to pick from. The traditional “kill stuff, get XP” system for the casual short-attention-span kiddie-type player and the far superior “Oddity-System”, which gives you experience for finding stuff. Stealth through a camp of mutants instead of fighting them, find a random barrel with a mutated cockroach in it? XP for you! It rewards alternate play-styles and I cannot recommend it enough, especially if you’re OCD about opening every container you see.
  • There is so much love in this game, such a distinct feeling to every location, character, dialogue. So many small details. You’ve talked to someone more than 40 real life hours ago and he has told you a joke that the person you’re currently conversing with might find funny? Tell it. You found information on a computer in an abandoned facility in the middle of nowhere that you THINK relates to an NPC you’ve met 20 hours ago? Chances are this unlocks new dialogue with him.
  • Quest design has a wide variety, but rarely “Go to X, kill Y”, at least not without a twist. I won’t go into this too much, but every quest is different and there is so much funny and interesting stuff to see. Just take my word for it.
  • Last but not least: this game is not for the casual player. You have to read, pay attention to what people tell you, find your own way across the (HUGE!!!) worldmap and get vague directions for quest whereabouts and what to do. Almost everything can be solved in multiple ways, find your own solution. There are no quest markers, explore and talk to people. Normal difficulty is what other games consider hard. Hard is what other games consider expert. Dominating is what other games are afraid of doing.
I could go on for hours as I’m seriously blown away by this game with every new playthrough. The current one took me 100 hours to solve every quest including the expansion (which I also highly recommend). Bear in mind I already knew where to go for almost all of them. This is the gold-standard for what an RPG is and should be. The only thing I’m afraid of is that no game will ever come close to this. Luckily the devs are working on a second installment. I thought about checking the box "I received this product for free" simply because at this point I feel like I have cheated the devs out of their money for the amount of work they've put into this.

So if you enjoy:
  • Interesting dialogue with 100s of wacky characters
  • Looting and hoarding like a dragon
  • Great combat variety and utility
  • Replayability
  • Great quest design with multiple ways to solve them
  • A huge open world without quest markers but rather directions
  • Higher than normal difficulty with a rewarding feeling after every big fight
  • Lots of secrets, nooks and crannies

I have good news for you: Hand-holding is over. Fasten your seatbelts, Dorethy. Kansas is going bye-bye.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\ttl;dr at the bottom

This game is one of the best RPGs I've ever played up until the ending segment past the point of no return, where it becomes solidly one of the worst experiences I have ever had with a game.

I understand some of the authorial intent behind it in terms of setting a mood and mechanically it isn't *horrible* but some of the design decisions are absolutely horrid, tedious, and just downright not fun. It completely ruins the pacing of the game going from a climactic event that you just KNOW is leading to the final confrontation, to a long slog through infinitely respawning and annoying to fight enemies, technically limited supplies that there are few ways to (in limited capacity) replenish, and a completely un-guided scavenger hunt and an (optional, but if your build isn't super great probably nearly mandatory) puzzle that I absolutely cannot imagine doing without a guide. Most of these things would really be fine if it wasn't just all of it combined in to one very long thing at a point where you're probably the most pumped to get to the final boss, completely throwing off your pace. Also getting the "parts" for the puzzle requires you to first FIND all of them, with barely any clues where to look and a timer ticking down to very hard enemies spawning in certain areas, and then finding a machine that if you put in a piece, use a console, then remove the piece and put in the next one (there are 16 in total) tells you about the parts of the puzzle (but doesn't add it to their descriptions so you have to write it down because it would be nearly impossible to remember all of the info *and* solve the puzzle in your head).

It caps off with a very sequel-hooky "no questions answered but more questions asked" wind down from the final confrontation, that completely does not feel like it was worth going through all of it. I still have yet to do the DLC, but most of what I hear of it sounds fantastic, so hopefully it makes up for the final part of the main game.

tl;dr:
Really dang good RPG, lots of variety in character builds and you WILL get locked out of options / items / areas, you're pretty confined by what you can do for the most part but in a good way that encourages replayability. Definitely at least look at tips for building a character if you aren't familiar with these kinds of games (you don't need a prebuilt character guide on Normal/Easy, just advice on how to focus your skill points to be strong enough to complete the game).

Play the expansion, before the point of no return because:
The ending segment is absolutely horrible, try it yourself and if you get sick of it (which the majority of people seem to, especially if you don't make the "right" decisions in the game and make it exponentially harder and slower) just look up a let's play or something because it's at least worth seeing, and the sequelbait hooks are interesting. You will, probably, like me if you even finish the main game up until you can freely roam the world again, probably not want to play the expansion right after because the final segment will have just absolutely beaten you down.\t\t\t\t\t
