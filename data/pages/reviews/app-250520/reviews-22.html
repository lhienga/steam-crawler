\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is overall pretty good, but it has some flaws. The art and atmosphere is great, the story is engaging, the theme is original and well-done, and the game is super in-depth with lots of different ways to play the game. The primary issue that I have with this game is with its mechanics for two different reasons: 1. it's always the optimal play to travel back to base to heal at the doctor and 2. combat is highly random. Combined, these create a VERY slow gameplay experience.

Since it's always optimal to go back to the doctor to heal, you'll spend a long time walking back and forth between wherever you just finished a combat and the doctor. And because of the randomness of combat, you're almost required to savescum many different combat encounters throughout the game. If the game only had one of these, it would be fine, but having both of these issues in the game just makes it drag on and on.

There is one other issue that's less important - you're basically required to level up a wide variety of skills to get through major areas, but the game rewards specializing into one particular skill set. Some players could see this as a nice challenge to try to balance your combat skills with the non-combat requirements, but for me, it just felt like the game was forcing me to play in a particular way.

TL;DR: Good game, but has some serious flaws. Pick it up on sale.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDespite a steep learning curve, this game is absolutely worth the time and effort you invest if you enjoy tactical combat, meaningful RPG decision making and an imposing, grim atmosphere.

The steep learning curve mainly stems from a lack of respec and on higher difficulties some builds simply not cutting it. However, this can be remedied by reading some guides and doing some theory crafting. Once you learn the fundamentals, you can craft some really cool builds which can vary massively between subsequent playthroughs.

I personally find the combat encounters to play out as tactical puzzles, where you're trying to determine the correct sequence of actions and movements to kill a group of enemies before they kill you. For example, my current character has stealth, traps, shotguns and high mobility which allows him to start combat in an advantageous position, get a strong opening shot, and then move into cover which forces enemies to follow without being able to unleash their weapons.

My only slight criticism is that some attributes and skills feel either compulsory or just extremely useful and I'd struggle to play without them. For example, perception in combat is tied to accuracy with guns, so useless for a pure melee or psi (magic) character. But outside of combat it allows you to find secrets, detect traps and find hidden enemies. All of which are extremely useful for exploration. Lockpicking and hacking provide a ton of extra loot, while pickpocketing can really shift the economy in your favour in the early game.

One final thing I love is the unique (and optional) xp system. You can just choose 'Classic' which awards xp like any other rpg for killing enemies, finishing quests etc. But the 'oddity' system awards xp points for finding specific items out in the world, be they looted off enemies, found in containers or awarded for completing quests. This incentivises exploration and eliminates any grind or pressure to clear every room.

In conclusion, if you enjoy tactical RPGs, I highly recommend Underrail.
