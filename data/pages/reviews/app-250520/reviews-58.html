Could you look me in the eye, and tell me that this truly dominating experience doesn't make some sick gamer nerve in your spine shiver in anticipation?

Underrail is a brutally fair game. Trapped in a series of caves, caverns, abandoned industrial facilities and railway stations you will meet, greet and beat the remaining dregs of humanity to a pulp or get dominated trying. Combat is turn based, and like a more algebraic version of rock paper scissors than most CRPG's you may have played before. There is no "best" weapon, there is only "best tool for this situation" and you can be expected to be brutalised for a lack of flexibility, foresight and necessary skills.

On that topic, if you don't like restarting the game several times, this is a game where premade builds are not going to be optional. Most people will not have enough of an understanding of the mechanics or what they need to be building towards, and will hit a very hard wall at a place called Depot A - if your build is not viable, this area will be borderline impossible. Mid-game is where Underrail really shines, with a range of factions to support, some completely hidden without following several questlines in very specific ways. Go become the best gladiator in Underrail and use your Invictus title to talk your way through the door of other places. Track down a ratman who wants a wife, and bring him peace (or a wife) with the nearby hunters.

Where most people will get stuck on this game is the Deep Caverns. Entry to this area is one way until you beat a particular enemy critical for finishing the game. I got the achievement for beating this enemy today (and finished it, woo!) and was shocked to see that less than 0.9% of all players have unlocked it. This means it's entirely possible that you could sample a random group of 100 people who have played Underrail and not find anyone who beat the game. If you can accept that mid-game is really where this game shines, or are one of the few degenerates among us who enjoy the suffering enough to slog through to the end, this game is for you and I absolutely recommend it.

On the other hand, if you have a short fuse, don't do well with complicated combat activity, or prefer lore and plot to be straightforward, this game is not for you. Diplomacy minded players will find that while persuasion is viable for solving some quests and situations, combat is not optional and many quests including several main quests have no non-combat solution.
Players who prefer direction will hate when the main quest line seems to "end" and you are expected to explore and eventually get bored enough to go try out something completely unrelated to get it moving again. The Deep Caverns again, are brutal and incredibly unforgiving, expecting you to hold out for as long as it takes you to blunder your way through without running out of medicine, ammo and repair kits.

Great game, but it's not for everyone.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've been eyeing this game for a long time after finishing fallout 2. I want to make a really long review because I feel a few words wouldn't do it justice.

Character builds. That's the main allure of the game (for me) tbh. The game has some of the best balanced and fun character builds of any rpg. It took the formula from fallout 2 and refined it and gave it more layers. Enemies are balanced and varied to challenge different character builds so some have more strengths than others but are weaker to certain enemy types. Robots are most likely going to kick your ass regardless though and outright counter some builds *cough* *Thought control* *cough*. The quests in this game branch out a lot, your choices have impact on them and sometimes, influence the outcome entirely. Underrail is hands down, one of the best games when it comes to quest and character writing.

The crafting system is good but I feel gets more credit than it deserves. It's pivotal for most builds and mandatory for some. What's annoying is how it's sort of tied to the mercantile skill because if you don't have mercantile and don't pass the skill check for some merchants, you're not getting high end components which you might need for the late game. My opinion is that the player shouldn't be mandated to invest points into mercantile as well if they want to do really high lvl crafting. I say that the crafting system is a bit overpraised because of how crafting components and shops' inventory work in this game. It's unpredictable whether a merchant sells you a necessary component you want to craft something before you progress. And the quantities they sell items are never enough either which means you wait around a lot until inventories are restocked and you get to walk back and forth across the map from merchant to merchant (RIP SMG builds). This promotes hoarding, backtracking, tedium and other things that really break the pace of the gameplay. There's a lot more negative things to say about merchants in this game (limited currency) but to for brevity, I'll just stop here. Other than that, crafting is a really well done and balanced system.

Combat is seriously satisfying if you make the right build and you see it persevere because of the feats (perks) you chose. It's the bread and butter of the game and the main thing you'll want to do. My main caveat with the game is that there's not enough combat scenarios in this game where you're tested to your limit. Some builds are also inviable and just can't work. This might be your fault because you specced into bad skills and feats, or because the build is just weak by design (throwing knives, machete, spear etc.). I once tried a sword build. The way it works is that I do flurry attack. If I miss at any point, I'm dead. It sounded good in concept but when I tried it, I got so pissed because I kept missing so often. And best case scenario, even when I never missed, I never could do as much damage or crowd control like other builds could.
Leaving yourself at the hands of RNG is never fun, trust me. Psi abilities are overpowered and super fun to try out. You can mix and match so many things that it provides so much replayability for the game. You'll want to try out different builds and playstyles to see different quest outcomes and to try out combat encounters differently.

Underrail has a really weak start. It takes a really long time for the game to actually become fun, the early quests are really lackluster and generic like other crpg quests (Omega outposts, hopper roundup, psi beetles etc.), starting areas are ugly and you're not introduced to the fun mechanics or gear still. Moreover, the quests at the start are linear and railroady so you've got no free will or player choice, you just have to do what you're told or deviate slightly for little or no impact at all. The majority of character builds struggle at the start because they're lacking the necessary equipment that's meant to make them function. So you've got to slog through the start waiting till you learn this one cool psi ability you're looking for or to craft this gun or to find this belt to really make your build shine... which means latent gratification from a game design perspective. You're WAITING for the fun to start, The problem with this is that the game has a lot of replayabiility value but it's such a pain to go through the start for each run because the start is long and boring. You just wanna start a new run and have fun but it takes so much investment before you enjoy yourself. This is exaggerated even more for veteran players who know the game like the back of their hand and the starting area is soooo repetitve. When I first got Underrail, I was seriously considering refunding it because the start was so ♥♥♥♥ and so was the art, the writing and the lack of early game content. But the thing is... the game is back loaded (meaning that most of the content is found later in the game).

This game does a reverse Bethesda. You know how Bethesda designs the start of their games so that most of the early game and start areas look good?... kind of like a mcdonalds ad. Underrail has a weak start but after that poin, the game gets really good. You're then introduced to so many new quests, many of which have really good writing, combat starts to get really interesting past lvl 12 and the story starts to finally pick up. All of a sudden, characters have hidden motivations and layers to them, they've got charm. The quests branch out, the equipment you'll be using gets more fun to use and your build finally starts to leave its stage of infancy. Dude's quest, The institue of Tchort's quests, The oligarchs' quests, Foundry's quests. They're exponentially better than the ♥♥♥♥ you have to go through in the beginning. My point being here is that it takes a lot of effort to reach the stage where the game is actually good and that's a problem.

There's a giant DLC for the game loooooads of quests and hidden places to explore, so much content to go through in just 1 playthrough. Once you finish the game probably after 200+ hours like me, you're gonna want to go back and do it again to try out different quest choices and builds. If you can get through the torture that is the poorly written and badly paced start, you will sink in a lot of hours into it. I only recommend this game to people who love CRPGs, can endure savescumming for half the game, can invest some time to pass the early game (and maybe Depot-A) and love: character building, crafting, deep lore, good writing, branching quest design and replayability.\t\t\t\t\t
