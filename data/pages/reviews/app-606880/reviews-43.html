\r\n\t\t\t\t\tYou should buy this game. Especially now, while its on sale.

This is only the second game I have ever bothered to review on steam, the other being a game i have thousands of hours in. That's because this game is interesting. It makes me think. Its design exhibits a great deal of care and intention, albeit with some limitations which arise, I assume, due to a limited dev budget.

It puts me in mind of Dragon Age: Inquisition, because it seeks the same goal, to strike an engaging balance between kicking ass and managing the responsibilities of running a nation. In my opinion, Greedfall succeeds at a similar level to DA:I, which is high praise. That success can be attributed first off to the unique placement of the protagonist in the plot and in the social structures of the world.

You do not play as a wandering warrior, a sole survivor, a humble farmer blessed with a mysterious gift, or any of the other tired fantasy rpg tropes. No, you play as a diplomat, a mid-tier noble. During an age of colonisation and exploration, you have been assigned to assist your cousin as he is charged with the governorship of a settlement on a newly-discovered island, on which the powers that be hope to find a cure for the mysterious plague ravaging the old world. This is complicated by the fact that the nation you represent is allied with two other ideologically fierce nations who are at war, and all three powers are present on the island, along with the powerful and mysterious native population. The player must navigate this quadrifecta of delicate relationships while defending themselves and their loved ones from all manner of skulduggery and seeking to bring light to a web of ancient mysteries.

The choice to make the player a diplomat makes a huge impact in terms of the weight of the player's actions. Each stealthy incursion, each tense negotiation brings with it a constant awareness of the potential effect on your web of relationships. During the opening act, you are going to need to pull off a couple bouts of sneaky in a port sector controlled by the faction whose boat you are about to get on for a multiple month journey, and whose pretty-boy captain I desperately wanted to impress. I navigated that section with a real fear of getting caught, not because I wanted to avoid a tedious fight or a loading bar as i pulled up a clean save, but because i wanted to avoid a very difficult conversation in which I would have to explain to a man who is supposed to be my ally why I was smuggling goods into his hold and busting prisoners out of his brig. On the other hand, if I didn't go through with it, I knew that I would have to explain to my allies' emissaries why, despite my failures, my country's commitment to them remained steadfast. I knew that if I made enemies now, it would cause me problems later.

I can't overstate the weight that this game puts on your actions and how rewarding it makes everything you do. When I failed a persuasion check with an overzealous inquisitor and had to kill him in order to keep him from slaughtering native allies of mine in an incredibly misguided attempt to convert them, the characters took time after the battle to discuss how they would hide the bodies and ensure that word of this diplomatic snafu wouldn't get back to the Mother Cardinal, who I had to go meet with immediately after. And, wouldn't you know, throughout that whole meeting, as I selected dialogue options laced with thick honeyed words, I felt the weight of trying to maintain a working relationship with a woman whose men I had been carving up in the woods not ten minutes prior.

The gameplay itself is nothing groundbreaking, its a lot of "go to a place, talk to a dude, pick up a thing, talk to another dude, unlock some stuff, report back with what you found." The combat is engaging enough, difficult to pick up at first and rewarding when you do, but not with the sort of depth that hardcore folks will really sink their teeth into. Its vaguely Souls-ish with a soupon of BioWare layered on top: skills on a quickbar and a focus on dodging and parrying with high consequences for getting knocked down or getting your armor broken. Its generally real-time and fast-paced, but with a tactical pause feature which can give you a break to slam a quick potion or otherwise strategise yourself out of a scrape. There are some noticeably reused assets, especially the interiors of houses and the faces of the less-story-critical npcs, but I honestly couldn't care less about the shortcomings cause the game just intrigues me so dang much.

It's not even that the story carries it. It is good, don't get me wrong, but its more like the story and the game fit so well together that the game makes me care about itself despite the fact that it is thinly spread in some places. I have played games with huge budgets and far slicker mechanics that did not make me feel a single thing, games whose crowning achievement was being an exceptional time killer, a top-shelf dopamine machine. This is not that. I feel like I have barely dipped my toe into this game, but already it has a hold on me. I was truly revolted to discover the respective atrocities that both my allied nations were committing on the native people, to whom I was in the midst of attempting to extend an olive branch. I felt real concern when faced with the reality that my cousin is probably not mature enough for the responsibility placed on him, and resignation to the fact that at some point he is going to up hugely and I am going to have to clean up his mess, again. I felt real frustration and righteous anger when the town guard had been intimidated into silence by corruption in their ranks, making it even more difficult for me to assist the quartermaster in rooting out the dirty cops who had beaten a young recruit to death. I felt truly betrayed when a foreign leader who I had placed trust in used a peace meeting that i had spent hours and traveled miles setting up as an opportunity to launch a sneak attack on his enemy. I felt truly nauseous when forced to threaten a good man, to stop a bad man from doing worse to other good people, because the first good man was only following protocol and trying to do his job.


Beyond all the praise I've heaped on it so far, this is also a game with something to say. The setting is that of an Age of Exploration, post-renaissance world of flintlocks, tricorne hats and tall sailing ships, with a bit of magic and nature-flavored eldritch horror subtly mixed in for flavor. This setting and the diplomatic framing device of Greedfall open it up to a lot of heavy discussions of colonialism, and the game eagerly challenges you to balance curtailing the despicable ethics of your allies without offending them or putting your own nation at risk. It is a welcome dose of respect and awareness in a genre overstuffed with slaughter-happy power fantasies.

In a lot of RPGs, it doesn't really matter in the HOW you stop the big bad. The choice between a delicate touch or a "kill em all, let god sort em out" approach is only informed by how you want to play, what experience you want to have. Either way, you still get your loot at the end. In Greedfall, you are choosing what sort of world you want to live in, what sort of world you want to create, and how far you are willing to go in order to create it. Yea, you might get your cool new armor, but how sweet will that feel if all your allies hate your guts and refuse to tell you how dope you look?

Upshot: Buy it. I don't think it has much replay value, but I don't care. In terms of impact and resonance, for me it's up there with Dishonored or any of the BioWare classics, despite the difference in polish level. I can confidently say that I've already got more than my money's worth with less than ten hours in it so far. In my opinion, Greedfall is a remarkable achievement, a credit to the name of everyone who helped create it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a great RPG. What more can I say? The story is fantastic. You start on the streets of Serene where you can feel the desperation from raging plague (the atmosphere reminded me of Dishonored somewhat). The city is dark and depressing, but beautiful in it's own way. Then you go to Teer Fradee, with it's amazing vistas, lands and creatures.
The companions aren't cardboard cutouts, they all have character, quests and things to say about your adventures.
The only flaw I could name is that it takes some running around instead of fast travelling immediately. To fast travel you need to reach a campsite, your personal residence or a point of travel.
And the music. The music is so good that it literally creates the atmospere much more than the landscape around.
Also, The De Vespe Conspiracy DLC was fun and lighthearted compared to all other events (I played through it close to the endgame). The music in it is absolute perfection as well.

Small note of advise: do party members quests as soon as you get them, and do quests for Theleme and Bridge Alliance before progressing far in the main quest, otherwise you will be locked out from some quests after certain point Coup DEtat and wont be able to complete the "setting the campfires" mission.


TL;DR: Would definitely recommend this game to anyone who likes RPG games. A real gem.\t\t\t\t\t
