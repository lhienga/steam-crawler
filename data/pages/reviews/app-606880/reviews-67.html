\r\n\t\t\t\t\t“My name is De Sardet and I’m the legate of Merchant Congregation”
Rather hard game for me to finish. I spent like two years to do so, because it’s just not easy to get into. I dropped it a dozen times, but still picked it up later. It’s 100% certified euro-junk, so be warned. Either you like this kind of games, or you don’t.
I really like the idea behind it. Action RPG about warring diplomat in new land is basically dream game for me. You know, something like Star Trek NG Pickard, but in fantasy setting. In this case the implementation leaves a lot to be desired.
Lore and art direction are pretty good. My only complaint here is rather weird fraction system. We got commerce – Merchant Congregation, religion – Theleme, science – Bridge Alliance and military – Coin Guard. It makes no sense that competing factions on new land employs the same military company (Coin Guard) to protect their interests against each other.
The story and overall writing is also somewhat okay. Characters are passable. Voice acting is even good in some parts.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFirstly I'd like to say that I played as a magic guy, I played on normal, and I played with a controller.

This game is pretty amazing for what it is. I've paid more for less from bigger names with more resources to use that had more bugs and less polish, and these bugs persist in those games years after they were made. However, this isn't about those AAA games that offer less and cost more. This is about GreedFall and Spiders, a game made with a smaller budget, smaller team, but made with passion and genuine love for this type of game and the storytelling these types of games provide.

Now, I could go on about the minor problems that people point out in other reviews, which are quite frankly blown out of proportion in the negative reviews. Rather, I want to talk about the things that I enjoyed about the game.

The setting is nice. It drew me in, I wanted to know more about it. It felt like a taste of a bigger world that can be explored and expanded upon. I look forward to exploring the world more in the upcoming sequel. The characters were great, I felt they were real people with their own agenda and motivations, plus I adored that the natives have South African or Dutch sounding accents. It's absolutely a breath of fresh air to hear accents that aren't American in a fantasy game. American accents are game breaking, they're jarring, they just don't "fit". The story was compelling and seemed to constantly evolve and blossom the more it unfolded, it was captivating to the end. The combat was great once I got the hang of it. As I mentioned at the start, I played magic guy and with a controller. On my next play through (which won't be any time soon), I will try a gun lady. Overall this game is one I would have bought full price instead of on sale if I knew how much I'd have enjoyed it.

Last things I want to talk about are the repetition of certain buildings, faces of npcs, and other things like that. I don't mind, though some might. This isn't a AAA game, they had less resources (including time) to polish the game. To me, this doesn't detract from it. However, the random "black" native islanders is just stupid. I don't care that the continent nations have mixed and diverse demographics. But it was jarringly out of place to see "black" natives randomly thrown in to the mix. Eh, guess these are the times we live in where lunacy like this is considered a selling point...\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR: Buy this game on a good sale if you're really curious, otherwise just play a Bioware or Witcher game instead.

This game is one of those "Almost but not quite" experiences. The world is a bit interesting in that it has both guns and magic and a very vague Steampunk + Plymouth colony sort of aesthetic, supplemented by a "Hey, religious missionaries are a pest to indigenous people at best, a cancer on the land at worst" theme, which I suppose I appreciate, but I wish there was just...more in-between the gaps of the story beats in terms of the world's depth. The maps are quite empty, there are tons of NPCs that just info dump, and the quests are literally just "Go to Point A. Go to Point B. Go to Point A." or "Talk to this Person. Run downstairs and pick up a quest item. Run back upstairs to talk to them about it." with small tweaks to these formulae.

Honestly the whole time I was reminded of Dragon Age: Inquisition, and while a small part of me wishes there had been as much interaction and in-depth zealous religious talk between the different groups in that installment of the franchise as this game presents, DA:I is arguably the better game for the characters at the very least. This game feels like DA:I, too, except none of the characters are interesting or memorable and the romances feel a bit abrupt and empty.

The game does sort of grow on you once you start interacting more at length with the indigenous people, and the historian in me did very much enjoy the perspective of how these tribes are trying to cope with this sudden influx of very pushy and aggressive sanctimonious foreigners, as you witness this veritable clash of cultures, values, languages, and faiths, but I'm someone who deeply values the quality of the companions and environments a game provides you to play with, because it is the characters and environments that motivate me to come back to replay a game again and again. This game falls a bit short on both fronts.
