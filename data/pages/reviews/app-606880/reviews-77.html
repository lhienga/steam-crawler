\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe main thing that's impressive about this game is the fact that it manages to actually pull off the whole "meaningful choices with meaningful (and logical) consequences" thing better than Bioware. Also some pretty spectacular views. The setting doesn't *quite* have an elaborate backstory full of lore like, say, Dragon Age, but there are some history mysteries to uncover.

Essentially, it's early modern era but with magic, and Teer Fradee is a sort of a cross between the New World and British Isles if they never became British. The nations are fictional but vaguely resemble real world enough that you shouldn't need a whole ton of exposition to understand what's going on. Magic seems to have "divine" connotations (i.e. is typically used by clergy) but in practice it isn't tied to any sort of morality or alignment system like D&D divine magic (there are former priests turned bandits who still use it), and isn't so powerful as to cause setting-defining drama over what to do with mages. You have the ability to learn it, but you also have the ability to just learn to shoot a gun really well.

Combat system has timing-based mechanics like dodges, blocks, and interrupts, but it also has tactical pause, as well as "easy" and "story" difficulties if you don't really care about it. It's a bit similar to a basic version of Dark Souls or Fallen Order but there isn't a huge variety of weapons, skills, or enemy types, and overall game isn't really based around combat being the primary feature, so much as just a way to resolve situations where diplomacy has failed or isn't an option. There's also some rudimentary stealth mechanics: crouching makes enemies not see you until you're super close, and not actually "notice" you unless they see you for several seconds, haze potion makes you even less noticeable, breaking line of sight and hiding in bushes is a thing, companions are (thankfully) ignored altogether when sneaking, etc. There are a few missions where choosing to sneak past enemies leads to a different outcome. But overall it's not really a sneaking simulator a la Dishonored either. The main feature (in my opinion, anyway) is still talking to people. If you don't like to do that you're going to have a bad time. You are playing as a diplomat, after all.

A few things you should probably know when you start that the game doesn't really explain very well:

The "mark" on your face is not a symptom of malichor. What it actually is isn't something your character (or most people) would really know at the beginning of the game, but as far as you (and people around you) know, it's just something you've always had, it's not a disease, and it's not contagious.

Some chestpieces have insignia of different factions and allow you to "disguise" yourself. However, this only works for avoiding getting attacked by the patrolling guards in specific locations belonging to a faction you're disguising yourself as. It doesn't seem to work for actually pretending to be a member of a faction when talking to people (or if it doesn't it's not consistent). So generally speaking there's not much point trying to collect clothes of various factions in case you might need them. The game also does not seem to care what your companions wear at all (even if they're not disguised) although this might be different on higher difficulties.

Some gear gives you +1 to certain talents. A cape gives +1 to charisma, for example. Many chestpieces have an upgrade slot that allows you to put +1 to science, vigor, or lockpicking on that chestpiece (although you probably shouldn't count on that in the very beginning of the game). The Adventurer Gear DLC gives you a unique hat with +1 to insight (as far as I can tell, no other headpiece in game comes with a bonus or an upgrade slot for one). It also gives you gloves with +1 to craftsmanship, but you can come across those later even without the DLC. Having +1 to insight and craftsmanship plus +1 to a talent you choose at character creation is a really useful boost for early game.

Talent points can be easy to confuse with attribute points at first, but the general rule is attributes (and some skills) determine what you can equip and use, whereas talent points determine what you can do. For conversation options you'll generally want to have insight and charisma. Charisma is chance-based, so even if you have just one point you'll have the option available (though your chance of succeeding might be very low). Insight will work 100% of the time if you have enough for that option, but if you don't you might not even be aware that you had an additional option. Some extra conversation options can also be unlocked with the science talent, though it doesn't happen often and there's usually a workaround if you don't have a high enough score. Often times (though not always) you can also bribe people, but a bribe typically costs around 100 gold, which is a fairly significant sum, at least in early game.

Companions can give +1 to their talents if you have them with you, but only after you get their relationship rating to Friendly (so generally mid-late game). They have to be selected as actually accompanying you though (i.e. if they're all present at a camp you still only get the bonuses from the two that are currently "following" you).

Generally speaking, most of the "relationship points" come from personal missions they eventually ask you to do. You do occasionally get opportunities to gain (or lose) extra points from conversations, but it doesn't happen terribly often, and each point is fairly significant (you don't just get +1 every time you say something a companion agrees with, generally speaking it happens when you take a stance that has consequences). Above Suspicious you have Nice (at about +2 or +3) and Friendly (at around +6); it doesn't seem to go above Friendly and the game does not show you the actual score as such, but you should know that does not "max out" or "lock in" at that point, so earning additional points when you're already Friendly can still potentially count if something causes you to take a hit later.

Personal missions (and some other missions) seem to be connected by your progress in the main storyline, so they might not unlock until a certain point in the story. They can apparently expire if you wait "too long" to do them, but as far as I can tell there is no actual hidden timer in terms of how many days you have to get them done; however, you do risk getting locked out of being able to do them (and other side quests) if you progress the main story without finishing them. Some missions also do have an actual timer, but in those cases it's actually shown in the mission tracker. In most cases it actually makes you wait a certain amount of time, but there are cases where you have to do something before the timer runs out (though there are only a couple cases of that and the timer is pretty reasonable).

Once you agree to do someone's personal mission they're locked into your party until the mission is finished. You can still do other side quests or travel or do whatever, but you will not be able to accept someone else's personal mission until you resolve the one you're locked into.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDo you like walking simulators? Prefer a dramatic entrance/exit absolutely each time you encounter a door? Love some good ol’ colonialism? Then Greedfall must be your thing.

I wanted to like this game, I love playing RPG’s and a small studio made this game. I really tried, I played it until the end, but it’s just mediocre at best. I can’t recommend it. Here’s why:

The good:
  • \tThe scenery is pretty gorgeous.
  • \tSome of the voice actors did a pretty good job.
  • \tThe architecture is nicely done, each city has a distinct style, at first glance.
  • \tStory feels sort of original, as are the characters.
  • \tThe intro of the game is well done.
  • \tThe companions are a nice mix from various backgrounds.
  • \tIt’s nice that upgrades to your armour or weapon changes the look of it slightly.
  • \tThe main story isn’t predictable, at first.
  • \tThe language of the natives is well done.
  • \tNo useless feminine armour, male and female armour design is the same.
  • \tSome nice cutscenes.
  • \tPirates.

The bad:
  • \tThe world feels kind of empty, meaningless, just hollow. The cities feel lifeless. A lot of buildings aren’t accessible, and if they are they are just empty, there is nothing to find. I like to search every nook and cranny when playing a game, but there is just no point to it in this game. There is also no banter to be made with random NPC’s, no interesting conversations to eavesdrop on, your companions don’t say anything about places you encounter unless it’s part of a quest, and even then it is minimal.
  • \tSpeaking of, when playing an RPG I expect conversations with your companions, but they are very, very minimal. You spent literal months at sea with several companions, but apparently you haven’t spoken to them at all. You just pick up right where you left of when you boarded the ship. You have to camp every time you travel somewhere, but there are no conversations at all, even though all companions are there. You can talk to them, but they just say the same line they always say, there is no point. You have to walk miles and miles in this game, but there is no party banter at all. There are companion-quests, but outside of those there is just no real interaction.
  • \tThis game is very black and white morally speaking. Do you want to be support cruel mad scientists? Deluded religious fanatics? Or just let the tree-hugging natives, I don’t know, live? Most characters are just hilariously simple, there is no depth to the characters, most are just stereotypically good or bad.
  • \tThe writers seem to think that the player is literally or mentally 8 years old. Things are overexplained when they really don’t have to be. Very childish conversations, they make things ridiculously obvious when it would have been better to just slowly build up to something.
  • \tAlthough the architecture is nice, the designs and layouts are very repetitive. There are three big cities on the isle and though they’ve each got their own style, each palace has the exact same layout. Other buildings also are exact copies on the inside. These are buildings you visit often, in different locations, but the layout is just the exact same each time. Copy, paste.
  • \tBad lip syncing. Bad facial animations. Also, it regularly occurs that all characters in a cutscene adopt the same pose. Not a horrible thing, but it looks silly.
  • \tPaths to romance makes no sense. You have to make very specific non-obvious dialogue choices or you just mess it up permanently. If you do succeed, the romance is almost non-existent, there is very little interaction and doesn’t make much of a difference in conversations.
  • \tHidden treasure chests are not actually worth finding, they just contain low level junk instead of something useful.
  • \tCombat is pretty boring, very repetitive.
  • \tThough some of the voice acting is great, a lot of it really is not. Especially the natives, they have been blessed with an accent that would not be out of place in a bad b-movie in a random foreign country. They all speak English but with a cringey and overdone accent. Also, they stubbornly refer to illustrated coins on a flag as ‘eyes’ since they don’t have money, but they do accept yours. Also they do know the word ‘lion’ , even though that creature does not exist on the island, thus they have never seen this creature. Does that make sense? It doesn’t, right?
  • \tIt’s understandable that being a diplomat means you have to be a of the convincing-type, but the way most conversations progress are pretty unrealistic and childish. It’s as if every NPC can’t think of possible consequences, only the MC can, and once you mention them they just do a complete 180. Are they all that stupid? I feel like this could have been done more creatively.
  • \tUgly outfits. Unless you stick to the same set things just clash horribly. Also, in order to be able to wear most items you need to invest points, which are very hard to come by. There aren’t many designs available either.
  • \tBoring, repetitive quests. Go talk to her, go talk to him, go fetch that, go fix that table. There actually are literal messengers in this game, but for some reason they are only used once and after that you, the legate, the diplomat, seem to have been demoted to messenger-boy/girl.
  • \tOnly a good and a bad ending really. Some grey areas would have been nice.
  • \tThis has already come up in several points but I feel like even though there are many faults the writing is just the biggest problem. This story had real potential. The setting is interesting, there are many factions and some characters that could have been so loveable. But instead, the story becomes predictable, characters lack depth and conversations are shallow, unrealistic and childish. It just really lacks depth.

This game is not the worst. I did enjoy parts, but there are so many better RPG’s to spend your time on, this game is just not what it should have been.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI did play the game on story mode and I honestly think this may be the best way of playing it to really appreciate the story being told and the importance of the RPG element with the different companions and factions. Where other games minimize side quests to nothing more than running a errand for a reward, It was refreshing to see that the side quests in greedfall were given just as much importance as the main quests and contributed significantly to the overarching story being told; it really enhanced my enjoyment of the game.

My main critiques:
1. Map - perhaps I have been spoiled by other open world games but it was unfortunate you cannot personalize icons on the map to mark places of interests. As a result I missed out on some personal missions because it's difficult to keep track of the areas other than what is already marked.
2. Music - again we've gotten some great soundtracks in some recent games but other than the main menu it seems like music was an after thought in this game, which is such a shame.

I played the dlc during my first playthrough of the game and I would say if you enjoy the first 10 hours of the game and want to play more greedfall, definitely buy the dlc.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is one of those half a thumb up, or "buy at discount" recommendations. It's a decent game with an interesting setting, but one that routinely fails to live up to its full potential.

Gameplay is rather vanilla. It doesn't have enough jank to be frustrating, but neither has it anything you haven't seen a thousand times already. Character development has you mostly increasing numbers and getting able to use better equipment. There's also the usual crafting, dialogue boosts and such. New abilities are quite rare.

The setting is where the game shines, sort of. It's a fantasy take on the European colonization of Americas, which actually works. I was fully prepared to cringe at either an Angelic Indigenous vs. Colonialist Baa-ahd conflict, or a great savior wandering around the island, solving problems of grateful primitives. Well, you do in fact solve a lot of problems for them, but mostly as a diplomat resolving conflicts between very different peoples who at best don't understand each other, and sometimes don't care to. The protagonist's status as an emissary of a powerful nation gives him a plausible reason to explore the lands and intervene in different situations, something lacking in less carefully crafted RPG stories. There's also no high-powered quest giver who for some reason gives the important jobs to some random peasant instead of taking care of things himself.

Unfortunately, the writing, while by no means abysmal, isn't good enough to do justice to the interesting themes Greedfall's stories explore, and frequent typos don't exactly help. There are interesting choices to make, but no real branching results from them. Level design is OK (if occasionally linear), but settlements have a tendency to blend together, which is made worse by the way some buildings are just copies of each other. The endgame also falls somewhat flat, as it's built around a character that never really became more than an useless, slightly whiny burden. There are some solid, if rather obvious plot twists that might have hit hard had I cared at all, but this was never the case.

The game grasps with admirable greed at greatness, but falls short due to insufficient budget and talent. If these shortcomings are rectified, the developers may one day make a classic.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLet's get one thing straight. This game is an average RPG. Nothing more, nothing less.

Fighting is terribly boring. Same moves, same mobs, same boring outcome. Even at higher difficulties it's a drag. Graphics are beautiful at ultra, but it's not optimized well enough so the FPS tanks if you try to run around with the max settings - even for me with a great computer.

Buy it if you got it on sale. Not worth otherwise.
