\r\n\t\t\t\t\tImagine for a second, that you are a particularly important diplomat for your country in a fantasy world mimicking how europeans colonized the americas. So... you... a diplomat do exactly what you expect a diplomat to do. That is, you talk to literally everyone. Shaping your nation's relationships with the other nations.

But of course, this is a fantasy roleplay game. So you also happen to be a really good fighter and... you're the main character obviously. Full of... destiny and all that.

I saw some reviews about how it's a walking simulator before I got the game.
I'm about 20 hours in. And... oh yeah. You're gonna do lots of walking. And lots of roleplaying as a diplomat.
Which means LOTS of talking with people. Solving their problems and negotiating.

Luckily it's all voice acted so even my lazy brain could just sit and listen.
Game has been super pretty so far. Few weird lighting transitions sometimes.
I like the characters and the lore of the different factions so far.

If I run into any issues or anything major as I continue this. I'll change this review.
So far however, I enjoy this game.

This game is hard to recommend and not to recommend at the same time.
GF is one of those instances, in which the black and white system of steam can't do justice to the matter at hand. In Sum: this is a game from a very small developer, who had had a good and grand story in mind. But committed to good writing and only a small team at hand, cuts had to be made on a technical level. That way Greedfall succeeds for most part in music, characters and writing, but is only solid in terms of game play, graphical design and variety of settings. Oddly enough Greedfall does this on a very high level: It cuts corners in the right (?!?) places to achieve, what was important to them and the players who are willing to look underneath the surface. For most people it might still be too much ask for and understandably so. But it deserves a deeper look. So let's talk:

Greedfall is very much a game about diplomacy in a fictional setting thematically close to the 18th century in Europe. As such the uprising of science, the changes in religion, the ongoing colonization and the changing political implications are at the center of the game. The game took me 75h hours to complete only leaving out the side missions, which were solely about collectibles. The main characters are well written and believable in their motivations. Which is important, since you have one main character you control and two out of five you build a team with at all times. Every relationship felt different and meaningful, both in the personal realm and the big picture, the game achieves to create. Even the side characters you interact with are interesting enough, to not feel generic for a long time. Side missions in no small part even reveal a complexity, length and engagement, that they easily could have been main mission - and good ones at that. Only the last parts of the main campaign, while not necessarily being bad, felt tagged on. It was only then, that I felt the game should have been 10h shorter. All that said the game is not flashy, but very down to earth and I liked that a lot in a medium, in which hyper realism hero fantasies are always around the next corner. In regard of writing and structure I could only compare GF to The Witcher 3 or the Mass Effect Trilogy. Greedfall certainly falls short in comparison. But I think when those are the comparison you have to come up with, in order to find one, that's already a job well done. That said, GF never felt like it copied from those games. It learned from them and then did its own thing. In that regard it succeeds on a very high level and it is easily the strong suit of the game. There is a lot in this game I will remember for a long time.

What hurts the game the most though, is how corners were cut to make the scale of the game possible, but it is also interesting and Spiders will be a developer to look out for in the future for that reason. The Graphics are aiming high. They may not be AAA standard, but they are definetely close and everything you find within the game is done beautifully for the most part. So what's wrong? Well... everything has been used so many times, that once you get to the third city, you have seen everything five times already. There are certainly variations, but every house you enter has the same layout across factions, when it is of the same kind: every living house, every tavern, every palace looks the same with very little changes to them. And while the main characters are very distinguishable from everyone else, there are only a few side character models (including the ones, you have conversations with), which have been used time and time again. Same goes for the landscapes once you leave the cities. While the layout of the maps is different, it is always the same flora and fauna with very little variation. And of course this also applies to the enemies. Certain types you see again and again. In addition to this the game always has this brownish or orange filter, which brings down the color pallet quite a bit and takes away from its beauty. Everything feels so brown, yellow or red in this game, that I was actually surprised, when I found some sprinkles of real gray mixed into it. Rarely felt a game so samy to me, that every sprinkle of variation became a an orange firework to my eyes in the end. I am not even kidding: In the end I was more aware of the little differences and appreciated them, for some odd reason.

To put all this in perspective: I had my suspicions already, but after seeing the credits roll you can pretty much tell what happened here. The whole graphical department was way smaller then the crew of voice actors alone (who did a great job for the most part btw.). And it seems Spiders decided to do a few things really well and then reuse them a lot. With the small team they had, aiming for the quality they wanted in the scale they were aiming for, that seems what they have chosen to do, given the small team and the lag of resources they had at their disposal. And I can't tell if it was the right decision, BUT I think they did the best with the path they took and followed through with it. It hurts the game in the best way possible given the circumstances. It would have been worse, if they tried to achieve sth. They couldn't accomplish. I am very split between my admiration for the decision they made, to reach their goal and the fact how much this decision hurts the game. It feels like cutting off a leg: depending on the situation, it might be the most competent decision to make, in order to survive. But even with a job well done, a part of the body will always be missed.

What wasn't missing though is the variety of customization, when it comes to the outfit of the characters. There were some really nice options and it really made thinking, what clothes to apply to what character depending on their backstory.

However the reason behind the decision for the graphical design can certainly also be applied to the polish of the game. The game is not aiming for a flawless experience. There are invisible walls, bugs, glitches, mistakes in the subtitles etc. but the game is polished enough, to not stand in your way too much and hurt your experience. It is not bad, just very noticable when it happens (somewhat frequently) and I never found something game breaking. It is sth. I found acceptable for the most part and tolerable at minimum level, given the quality of the narrative and once I had my suspicions, which were verified in the credits, as described.


As you can guess by now, I felt the same way about the combat system: it is nothing to write home about, but it provides a competent solid base, with enough options for some variety. And as such it works. It felt very close to the Witcher, but not as compelling or interesting. It provided some variety to the game play in between leg work and dialog options, whenever I needed it. And since you can pause combat and apply commands, the problems occuring to overview in real time combat are easily avoidable and make the game very accessible even on the hardest difficulty. It feels again like a competent and easy solution, for a problem, they couldn't fully polish, given the resources. And it works. Other than that, I missed some umph behind it, to interest me in the long run.

There you have it: In the end this is a very good game, when it comes to the story. It is one I will remember. But I will also remember this weird place of ambiguity, when it comes to the technical decisions the dev made, in order to create this game. You feel the care for this game and the pain of design decisions the dev team had to go through. But it is through those flaws that you can tell how much they cared and that GF is a actually a really competent game, which is why I can't give it a bad rating. It is an interesting and memorable spot for everyone who is interested in a good story and/or game design in general. But it might be too much as a payoff for everyone else.\t\t\t\t\t

Welcome to Wine Reviews with Kirsche!

Pros:
  • The characters are well written for the story. And I don't say this because I genuinely find all romance options incredibly appealing. Each character is categorized with very unique views, outlooks, and feelings on matters. This is golden age of bioware quality characters. All party members are confident yet unique in their confidence. They all have very different emotional weakness. Each of their stories stand well and to some extant all feel valid interwoven into the story.
  • The story itself is stronger than I expected. Some mild hiccups at points, and you being this involved as an ambassador is fun but unlikely, but still lives to be entertaining. The over arching lore is fantastic and well incorporated as well as confronting different faction views and how you influence the world.
  • The variable play options. You have stealth, charisma, interesting classes that play into your social interactions.
  • Artistic design! The music! The enemies! The environments outside of cities! Its all gorgeous, but this will come up later.
Cons:
  • Heartbreaking as it is, city design. In some ways, surprises me, in other ways the reuse of assets is clear. Also every main "town hall" has the same layout/design and feels so empty. Just fixing the main town halls would have skyrocketed this to decent designed towns and probably wouldn't warrant a mention.
  • Too many cookies in the combat jar, but they are all sugar cookies.... There is so many combat types and it feels only two matter, both are melee. Magic feels nice but ultimately too simple. Its all purely supportive, and guns are completely unnecessary. If they played into a form of combo and they made the combat for combo driven with reward, the guns and magic would be more impact. The system needs an overhaul to reach modern standards for games.
  • "Stealth" has no challenge or rhyme or reason. In principle it seems fun to incorporate, but you hunch-backing around and very easily not being seen is.... something else entirely. Stealth should force your party members away from you and punish you for being caught alone. City designs don't support it despite it clearly being a mechanic for the city.
  • How long the game takes.... I don't recall Greedfall being 35$ on release for me. A game should be worth 1 hour per dollar you pay +1-5 (Really good games should be +10 hours). I did a LOT of side-quests and restarted my game twice and still have less than 30 hours at completion of my first play-through. And my second one is started too.
  • Questionable side quests.... space/time/location, impact.... there was a lot of lazy filler.

However, despite all of this, I still recommend it. A story like this is, as a mentioned, strangely well written for its premise. If this was a higher budget studio willing to take a story this complex, the combat system would be fixed and unnecessary quests might be improved. The romance with your main love interest feels a tad bit more organic if not for the overall issue with side-quests, and while some surprises occur, non are so jarring that it can't make sense. Ideally the next GreedFall will make a much more exciting combat system, do away with stealth or make it complex, and diversify its assets in city environments (fix the hubs!).\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt really wants to be Dragon Age: Origins. It has the clunky aspect of it down, less polish, and quests that force you to walk around the big map and see everything.
Seriously. All I can think about while doing missions are that the developers really wanted to make sure you got to see the map they made. Fast travel dropping you not inside the towns, but outside.

Also enemy encounters take place in this invisible zone, and if you happen to step out of it whether by accident or on purpose, the enemies get all their health back and reset. So if you're running around in a fight(Keep in mind this isn't like Dragon Age combat, you have freedom in combat), you can step outside of an invisible boundary and heal all the enemies to full while you keep the damage.

I found the party members pretty unlikable too honestly. Its nice to see a game trying to be a spiritual successor to DA:O, but it missed the mark a bit.\t\t\t\t\t
