\r\n\t\t\t\t\t It is quite an enjoyable game. Here is my analysis, brace yourself it is a long one :) :
This is one of the best game play designs I have ever seen. It feels like that game designers learned the "not so fun parts" from other games in the genre and alleviated that problems. Here is the basic design decisions which made the game pretty good in my opinion:
- Inventory management : Ever since Diablo series I hated that I had to do a lot of hauling for the inventory items. Over the time inventory management came with different problems. In some games it was the size and in some others it was the weight. In this one, even though the weight counts as inventory limitation, you can easily manage it by different means.
- Hero class : In other games again, once you make a decision you are "mostly" stuck with your characters class. It is even harder to have hybrid classes in some games. This one allows you to invest in different classes without any penalty. You can reassign the points as well, if you want to.
- Diversity of weapons can be used at once: You can use weapons or rings and pistols all in same fight with ease. I liked that feature a lot. When you are having a sword fight you can draw your pistol and fire and keep on the sword fighting immediately after. Made the fights quite pleasant.
- Effect of the abilities : These are like lockpicking, crafting etc kind of abilities. Essentially you dont have to have them. These are making the games features simpler in most cases, giving you different access mechanism to some places for example. Even if you dont have access you are not loosing a lot so you keep on playing the game.
- Enemies & Fights : They spawn occasionally, not every time you enter an area etc. So it is quite pleasurable to have the fights cause they are not irritatingly frequent. Even with the hard enemies you can find a way to beat them.
- Companions : These are quite strong companies you have. Hence they are effectively helping you. Making fights more manageable.
- Maps : Maps are not unpleasantly long. Again from other games when I start a map it was a like a nightmare after sometime. Once you start a map it was like a commitment for 5-10 hours etc. Greedfall again quite nicely handled map sizes.
- Navigation in the map : Unlike single "portal" like structure, each map has several camping areas where you can change your companions, unload your inventory or use as portal for fast travel etc. This is also one of the reasons that made inventory management simpler.
- After sometime some missions are like going one place after another just to get confirmation etc. Again due to the different portals you can easily go to somewhere close to your missions hence it is not much of a burden.
Overall it was one of the best games I have ever played in its genre.
Highly recommended.

Way too much walking back and forth between quests. This for sure needs to be shorten or addition of mount/sprint option.. to speed things up a bit.
Overall I loved this game, and completed all quests in 30 hours. 8/10 would recommend if you enjoy RPG

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTo truly appreciate this game, one has to understand what it is. It is not, as many think, a group-based RPG with action combat, but rather a very expansive walking simulator. This is shown in most aspects of the game, once you have a closer look.

Traversal
As expected of a walking simulator, walking is the main means of travel. This allows for clever map layout to maximize the experience. Without a jump functionality and a seemingly infinite amount of invisible walls, even the smallest maps can be turned into mazes, that are hard to navigate. Can you walk down that slope? Can you step over that pebble? Walking was never more of a thrill.
There are, of course, shortcuts, but they are locked behind talent requirements. To prevent players from using shortcuts to spoil the walking simulator fun, talents increase painfully slow. Even with the help of crafted gear and companion buffs, players have to focus on one or two talents to actually make use of shortcuts. Yet those talents are actually the worst choice for efficiency. One might get tricked into picking useless stuff like lockpickung or vigor, which saves only a small detour, but will then be rewarded in turn by failing dialogue checks and having the pleasure of so much more walking.
Unfortunately walking will sometimes be interrupted by dialogue or even pesky little combat encounters, but thanks to the innovative backtracking mechanic of quests, players get to enjoy the views they might have missed during combat on their peaceful way back.

Fast travel is implemented, yet the points are masterfully placed to never even warrant a visit during quests, as walking to a fast travel point, fast travelling and then walking to the next quest step takes more time in most cases than just walking directly. Thus the walking simulator aficionado immediately notices: fast travel exists to allow for a quick change of scenery between quests, not to actually take away from the walking experience.

Visuals
The world is mostly enjoyable, yet best viewed while walking. There are not enough details to warrant a closer investigation, but that is not what walking simulators are about in the first place.
Character creation presents the player with a choice of mostly hideous preset faces with very little customization options other than colors. Facial animations are even worse. It is clear from the beginning that this game's characters were designed to be perceived silently and from behind, as it should be in a walking simulator.
Customization of the character by means of equipment on the other hand is immense and can even be further customized. While there are apparent gameplay consequences to the visual choices, they can be neglected, as combat is a rather dull and uneventful part of the game. To the developers it was clearly an afterthought, as they were developing a walking simulator, but we will get into more detail further along.
Buildings are mostly copy&paste, which is a plus. This allows players to immediately recognize the structure and quickly do their business inside before they can resume the walking simulator pleasures of the outside world.

Quests
Quests are the driving force behind all the walks in this game. Usually they only require you to have dialogues with people in different places, thus the walking, yet the occasional collection of objects is also included. The story is just interesting enough to motivate your next stroll in the world.

Systems
The amount of systems is exceptional for a mere walking simulator. There is pointless gathering and crafting for menial upgrades to equipment. There are reputations with different factions and even companions which - other than the odd buff to a single attribute - are properly pointless. Armor even belongs to factions and can be used to trick people into thinking you are one of them. This at least is great, as it allows to skip the occasional combat. After all, we just want to walk in peace without having to worry about... well, gameplay.
Then there are three trees. One for skills, one for attributes and one for talents. In a way they are all the same and with the exception of the skill tree unlock so slow and in irrelevant intervals that they might as well not be there - as they should in a walking simulator.

Combat
It is abundantly clear that combat was the very least of the developers priorities. Greedfall is one of the most unbalanced games in that regard, that I have ever encountered. While there is only very little in the way of fighting - meele, guns, spells and traps - none of these are even remotely comparable to each other. While meele is a rather painful experience, it is mandatory, as everything else relies on limited resources: mana, ammo, traps. Yet gunplay is vastly superior to swordplay and magic makes the combat basically pointless as soon as the player has access to stasis and a point of vigor or two - which can be right at character creation.

In general there are only two combat encounters:
The first one is a swarm of enemies that will make dodging or parrying a horrible mess. Along with the clunky attack animations of non-human foes, those encounters are best approached by spamming stasis on all but one enemy.
The second is a single enemy with a massive health bar and area attacks that the companion AI is too stupid to handle. In the end, you will always have to fight them alone. Unfortunately those enemies also have incredible amounts of armor, that stasis or gunshots can slowly chip away at. While the first boss in the tutorial can be gun-shot to death in a minute, the later bosses will take a lot longer. Now you might wonder how entertaining it is to watch a boss's armor slowly disappear while keeping him locked in near constant stasis. This is, once again, proof that Greedfall is a walking simulator. When bossfights are the most boring part of the game, you better run.

There are also supposed to be red skulls marking high level enemies. While they do exist, they appear to be random. Enemies without them are sometimes still able to kill you in two hits, which makes the swarm encounters with tougher foes neither fun nor interesting, but a constant dodge fest that only waits to punish you with a death screen.

Controls
Controls are mostly clunky, especially when interacting, looting or just turning around. They are even worse in combat. Only the absolute basics of hit, parry and dodge are bound to keys. Spells, guns, potions, etc. require the player to go into the tactical pause menu to chose the action. This makes for the most tedious, boring and actionless action combat the developers could have designed. And that, of course, is a good thing. Why would a walking simulator encourage players to engage in combat, when there is often a longer detour available to avoid it?

Conclusion
Greedfall is the best walking simulator that ever tried to masquerade as a party-based RPG with action combat. Sooo much walking!

No, seriously. This game is incredibly mediocre. The writing is surprisingly okay for the most part, but the dreadful walking and backtracking, the attrocious facial animations and the boring, unbalanced, yet partially incredibly punishing combat turn the game into a chore. I might be able to finish it, but I can only tackle this in small sessions. It's just too tedious to actually immerse myself and lose track of time.\t\t\t\t\t
