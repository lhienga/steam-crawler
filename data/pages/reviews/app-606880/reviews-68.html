\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFinished the game just out of curiosity and my overall opinion is totally negative.
First of all, just like everyone in the reviews have complained, the fast-travelling system is utterly ridiculous. Since most of the quests requires you to constantly go to the point A then return point B then go A again, it becomes annoying at some point. You don’t have anything to ride, you cannot jump, no mini-map, disgusting map designs, no interesting places to discover, not enough fast travel points… Another thing is that the game constrains you so much about the attributes and talents. I mean it is okey for the games which you want to play for the second time, but it is not that kind of game. At least lockpicking, climbing or crafting should not be a big issue for this game. (Ofc, I presume that you will give all your skill points to charisma and intuition to be more satisfied on the storyline.) Also, no need to mention about the bugged quests. The bugged ones I remember were: “Queens farewell”, “finding the survivors of the battle of red spears” and “treason quest”.
Finally, the biggest problem of the game is that it completely embraces the whole idiocy of holding hands and singing together. I was expecting some difficult choices to make such as turning a blind eye to the monstrosities of Bridge Alliance so that they can find a cure or something. But it is not the case. It was way too easy to make good relationships with everyone. You are not sacrificing a relationship with a faction to gain a relationship with another. But to be honest only the choices concerning Thélème were difficult to make, peculiarly the discoveries about San Matheus. It really made me think about the outcomes for solid 10 minutes. And there is also one thing that made me laugh so hard at the end of the game. HUGE SPOILER ALERT: Our pretty angel De Sardet was all about goodness and morality through the whole game. Especially about the Dr. Asili quest. But somehow at the end of the game it gives you a choice to support Constantin and become the very monster he was supposed to destroy. It just doesn’t make any sense after hearing his opinions on the various quests about morality and pacifism.

Excellent skills and talents upgrades

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t20-30 years ago, this game would have been brilliant and inspiring. Today, it merely lacks innovation or enough stimulative gameplay to keep me interested. The plot is absolutely solid-- the overall message is inspiring. It would make a great book, or perhaps a mini-series. But the way this unfolds as a game is utterly boring, repetitive, bland, etc. Many times, the game mechanics & tropes are predictably shallow. Many times, the choices you make are unpredictably consequential. It's a grand effort at something, but I regret it did not meet its mark.

With such a great story to tell, it may have been as simple as removing the busy-work & chores. I could forgive the meaningless talents, beyond the essential intuition & charisma. I would have been less annoyed if there were more fast-travel options, like directly to governors & other quest-related NPCs; and the ability to fast travel without having to reach a camp or travel landmark. The lack of mini-map made city navigation absolutely awful. So much time was spent horribly navigating from point A to B to C back to B then A again. Combat was a bland diversion, at best. Loot was absurdly meaningless.

I suppose in the end, the game was a gigantic & ornate stick, but lacked any carrot to entice.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tUltimately, this game has a good basis upon which to build a nice RPG. Multiple paths to complete certain quests, some element of choice, excellent detail to clothing and gear items. Nearly all of that is overshadowed by the shallow nature of the game, lack of coherent worldbuilding that the player can engage in, and a lifeless feeling world.

People don't seem like people, they seem like caricatures, and while there are multiple paths to complete quests most of them revolve around "sneak in or burst in." I tried to give the game time to develop but it just failed to be interesting after an investment of nearly 3 hours and that's much too long to wait for a story-based game to pick up.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you'd asked me whether I'd recommend this game when I was ten hours in, I would have said "no." I might have laughed brokenly. In at least one message to a friend while I was playing it, I said "Greedfall certainly is a game that exists."

The game world was beautiful, one of the most gorgeous I'd seen, the historical ambience was top notch (and I loved the choice of the 17th century, since it's not often used as a fantasy setting and, as a medievalist, it's sometimes nice to not be tearing my hair out....though as a Celticist, certain worldbuilding decisions gave me new things to tear my hair out over), as was the blend between history and fantasy, I enjoyed aspects of the lore and the characters, but something seemed to be missing, something crucial to enjoying the game. The combat was excruciating, I found that the exploration of serious topics felt hollow, and, even though the world was gorgeous, I couldn't say I was immersed in it. It felt a bit like going to a theme park where everything had been designed to give you the feeling of being in an area, but you still know that you're in a theme park. Directions were poorly given, meaning that it could be very easy to skip opportunities, especially with romances, which are notoriously easy to lock yourself out of. I had my doubts about whether I would even bother to finish it and, at about the ten hour mark, I bought about 4 new games from the summer sale just to give me some hope in video games as a general medium. I wouldn't say that Greedfall depressed me, but I would certainly say that it didn't fill me with high hopes.

Then, sometime around the 15-20 hour mark, I began to hit my stride. Combat went from excruciating to almost too easy (reaching a balance somewhere in the last hour or so of gameplay), characters revealed layers of depth, even though their storylines still didn't get the same development from, say, a Bioware game. Even though there were several continuity errors (in one case, a character who'd been sentenced to be exiled showed up to be introduced in the next quest as if we'd never met!) unimportant sidequests revealed new nuances to the characters and the world, sometimes with consequences if you don't complete them in the right order (me stalling on completing one quest led to a minor character being killed.) It was no longer a static world, but one where tiny decisions, if you weren't careful (or didn't look up a guide beforehand) could have repercussions down the line. If the exploration of serious topics still never went as deep as I'd have liked, the variety of topics brought in still left me with room to think. I warmed increasingly to De Sardet, a quick-witted, golden tongued diplomat with a strong sense of compassion (if you achieve the golden ending), who undergoes betrayal and loss and still does what they (in my playthrough, I played as a woman) can for their family and their people. Though I wish we could have developed them more as a character, since certain revelations were begging for additional sidequests and material, and though I wish we couid have had more interactions with their companions outside of their designated missions (though there is some interesting dialogue you can get depending on which party members you have at any given time), I ultimately ended the game with a little bit of mist in my eye, saying goodbye to the intrepid diplomat and her band of fire-forged friends, at least until I miss her enough to load up the save again. New game plus would have lightened the mood a little, but there is still a high amount of replayability in the game, not the least experimenting with other character builds and taking different routes in the various quests.

Overall, I'm not sure if it's worth full cost, but it's certainly worth a look, if you're willing to take it for what it is and if you're willing to be patient with it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFeels very much like a Proper Bioware Game (from, yknow, before they became... what they are now), which is pretty high praise from me. Good story, points for the novelty of approach (actually being an ambassador as well as the period). Excellent voice acting.

I was initially a bit disappointed with the character customisation options, until I realised this was actually in a region closer to Witcher than to Mass Effect (for example) and that was fine.

There was a little bit of jank, though at this point after release, I really only saw a few typos in the subtitles and the occasional place where they referred to Lady De Sardet as "he." The worst bug I encountered was getting stuck in a rock, and I was literally less than two minutes from the last save, so ti was neither here nor there. (No CTDs, which shouldn;t be remarkable, but...) I've had FAR worse experiences with games from bigger companies, so...

Combat was fine, though I am not really an action-RPG person. (I managed Witcher 1-3 and Jade Empire, but that's about my limit.) I did not like the sprint and dodge button being mapped to the same key, personally (partly because I simply CANNOT do parry/dodge stuff in games). I did feel at points though that the characters were rather unresponsive to my actions, apparently because it takes them so long to do their animations to perform the actions and for me, with a laughable about of hand-eye-co-ordinatiom, it was frustrating. This made the first boss really tedious (in a sadly-typical inverted difficulty curve).

Greedfall, then, it what I would, I think, consider a great example of a Perfectly Good Enough Game. Could there be parts that could be improved on? Sure, but show me my top three favourite games of all time and I'll point out the flaws. But it doesn't NEED to be better, certainly in terms of production values - the ones it has are plenty sufficient to do the job they need to. (Frankly, graphics hit the Good Enough stage a long time ago, and I think we have not only passed the point of gilding the lily, but the obsession with better graphics is actively detrimental to the experience. Needless to say, I don't play so-called "triple-AAAAAAAAA" games and haven't for a long time, Witcher 3 being the last arguable exception.) At one point, I paused to watch the Spiffing Brit do a video on it and he referred to is as an "AA game" and I think he's quite right. This, frankly, is the space I'm interested in, and I'd take a half-dozen of these games (sequel, Focus?) over any amount of Cyberpunk 2077s.

A very solid job, overall then, and the devs should pat themselves on the back.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGreedfall is the first RPG that's kept me engaged long enough to finish it in a while. This is helped by the fact that it has around 50 hours of gameplay if you do everything, which is a breath of fresh air in a sea of games I haven't been able to finish because I don't have the time. Some stuff that stood out to me:

First, because it's most likely you'll just read this part, a warning to potential players: colonization is a major context and theme of this game and the colonizers are often casually and unrelentingly racist against the indigenous people of Teer Fradee. This became exhausting for me as a white person to experience, so I imagine it might be worse for an indigenous player. The game itself definitely tries to depict the indigenous factions sympathetically and tries to lambast colonial practices as harsh and cruel, but I'm not sure it succeeds. While I believe the dev team tried to be compassionate toward indigenous people and that it shows, there are definitely times when they missed the mark. Like whatever is going on with their accents.

Apart from a set of notice board quests and two exploration quests which you can complete through finishing the main story, almost every side quest in this game feels relevant to the main plot. A particularly satisfying example is when a blink-and-you-miss it side quest in the prologue/tutorial chapter can end up foreshadowing and adding depth to one of the main faction quests several story arcs later.

Speaking of depth, I've yet to meet a game that better encourages and rewards you to engage with all of your companions. With RPGs I tend to pick out my favorite party members early on and use them for everything, because it doesn't really make a difference mechanically. Greedfall makes your companions' presence count and that relevance helps them grow on you. I instantly disliked one of my party members when meeting him, but then it turned out he was really good at keeping the Spanish Inquisition of one faction from bothering me. Since a major story point involves that faction, I kept him with me for a few solid hours of gameplay-and by the end of it, because he helped me out, I liked him and brought him along to other situations that might be diplomatically dicey. Bioware, take notes.

More reasons for developers to take notes: Greedfall allows for essentially two different play styles: diplomacy and combat (with shadows of stealth). They intermingle because it's an RPG so there will be combat and you play a diplomat so there will be diplomacy, but each is fleshed out to the point where it's still satisfying. A lot of games offer "diplomacy" as flavor text...that leads to prescripted combat scenes. Because of that, I took the opportunity to see what I could talk my way out of with a high charisma stat and companion intervention...and it turned out to be quite a lot.

One gratifying experience was when a rival noble tried to blackmail me to a faction leader. She rattled off a long list of technically true marks against my character...and because I'd taken the time to build a relationship with his faction, he told her that he didn't know her and she should get out of his throne room. I loaded an earlier save to watch it twice because it felt so satisfying. If you like combat, you can fight your way through Greedfall--but if you're a fan of diplomacy or stealth, you might make it five hours in before ever having to solve a situation with violence, save random encounters with bandits.

...that being said, there are animals and monsters and bandits on this island and they will attack you and it can get repetitive, especially since not having a mount means a lot of time is spent running through the bushes and woods. The fast-travel-by-camp helps, but being exclusively on foot can start to drag. This was especially true at the end, where the narrative tension made me feel like I should be progressing quickly right now, but the mechanics wanted me to go to remote areas of the woods to weaken the BBEG.

Romances were very thin, but what was there did make me "awwww" and I grew attached enough to the characters outside of their romantic plotlines that it helped with wanting more lovey-dovey stuff. I'm hoping there will be more in-depth romance in the sequel. You'd think a French studio would know how to write love...🤔

And last but not least: it's been three years since this game came out and there are so many spelling errors in the subtitles, as well as some audio bugs when people referred to my character with male pronouns while I was playing a woman De Sardet. I know it's a French studio and I would understand some localization bugs in the first few months--hell, even the first year of release. But the game was released in 2019. Patch it. Hell, send me your captions files and I'll do it.\t\t\t\t\t
