\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGreedfall is a diplomacy heavy RPG with combat, magic, crafting, companion relationships and meaningful quests.
Unlike most other RPGs where you start as a nobody and work your way up, in GreedFall you start as a Noble and a Diplomat, giving you a reason to be able to walk into the halls of the leaders from the other factions.
While running around and talking with people, you explore the island's history and discover the different diplomatic ties of the various factions, as well as fight creatures and join a war or attack an outpost from time to time.

Cons:
Npc faces gets reused a lot, which is noticeable, especially this one older guy in a lot of the starter quests.
Not able to jump (except for down from climbing platforms).

Pros:
Battle tactics matter; hard fights gets doable when figuring out the right tactic, and there is a huge difference between taking on a group at once, compared to taking them one one at a time by freezing the rest with stun potions or spells.
Choices matter; most of the quests so far, you are given different ways to complete quest objectives, depending on your choices and on who is in your party.
The guest have a meaning; I have yet to find a "kill 10 rats" quest, and so far every quest have been in relation to the main story or to that of my companions.
Beautiful graphics, especially the landscapes.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAbove all else, the biggest selling point for Greedfall for me has got to be it's story and the way in which it is told. Nearly every quest i went on, especially so with main story quests, I truly did not know what turn it would take. instead of it being straight and fairly clear cut, quests are more often than not winding and long, and often quite cinematic. The end destination of the quest is commonly completely obscure and tucked in the dark until you the player ventures the precarious trail to get there. Once there, i always thought in my head "Holy sh*t i wasn't expecting that" and further engrossed me in the story being told as i played.

Game play is ok, some parts better than others. Combat is pretty simple and the enemy AI is also quite simple, and you'll often wander upon the same group of enemies in the same area you passed before, grouped together as if just casually placed there. Not the most engaging thing in the world but even so you are so pressed to unravel the story that you keep trudging on, and this kept me from getting bored where another game may have. I should add, the larger boss type enemies were really really awesome to see and were a fantastic part of the story. Both visually interesting and intimidating to see (often very unexpectedly). Additionally, your companions are actually pretty capable and do help in a fight. they aren't OP and they also aren't brain dead and constantly getting killed. While your are experiencing all this, you have some pretty solid graphics to see as well. The environments aren't anything crazy but they are nice and complementary to the overall theme.

Overall, its by no means a perfect game, but it is a game that does indeed have some powerful merits and accomplishes having a truly compelling and motivating story that is full of dark mysteries and engulfed in adventure. As you play, you get more and more drawn in, in little time you'll be sucked in completely and you are rewarded by a story that keeps getting deeper and deeper the more you interact, play, and experience this hidden gem.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGreedFall is a curious case of many mediocre elements coming into one comprehensive, above-average picture. Game that I shouldn’t enjoy, but I did. Game having a lot of flaws, still managing to cover them up with sheer charm of its unique world and story.

Spiders Studio is getting steadily better at making games. Their every new game is better than the last one… but overall jankyness, visible budget constraints - I think it’s becoming a kind of Spiders trademark. Greedfall is no exception, balancing on the edge between indie and triple-A gaming. Sometimes failing… but mostly succeeding in being a memorable new IP.

So, what’s Greedfall? A semi-open RPG with a rich, unique world. Wrapped in kinda bland mechanics and annoyingly tedious “errand boy” sidequesting. Theres like dozen of half baked ideas here in terms of mechanics, but Greedfall has that CHARM. After few hours hours you will feel and see every crack and wasted opportunity in this game… but the overall charm prevails to the last minute of gameplay. Delving in that world is really, really enjoyable. Greedfall may be a perfectly mediocre game… but maybe sometimes, we just need that kind of game. Not flashy, over-ambitious or complicated. Just simple, cozy and safe. With a dash of good old jank ;)

Finishing Greedfall will take you around 30-40h. I recommend buying the gold edition, with De Vespe DLC. It’s a small additional story, but fits perfectly with the main questline.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've been playing Spiders games for the last two years, because I was always curious about their products - and they made a fan out of me.

For the uninitiated, each of the Spiders game (that they've woven in Silk Engine) is a solid upgrade from the one before. Greedfall is no exception, as it is a considerably massive upgrade from The Technomancer. While I'd hoped that their breakthrough to the mainstream would be with The Technomancer, Spiders earned their much deserved appreciation with this Greedfall. Though the gameplay loop may be a little weird to new players, it is something they've developed throughout the years with each new title. There's nothing much to say here, as I've praised their gameplay loop in previous titles.

One thing that Spiders absolutely amaze me every single time is that of their skill at world-building. The Technomancer was a shining example of this, and Greedfall doesn't lack in this point of view. They throw you into this massive background with sneaky little details here and there, and expect you to understand what's at stake and what's cooking behind the scenes. The extraordinary feature in this title was the whole Yecht Fradi language. As a linguist, I've never understood the mesmerising process of creating a language for a particular storytelling, untill I've heard the phonetics of Tir Fradi. It really piqued my interest, and I started to write certain words down to speculate about their morphology.

I was unfortunately late to this party, however Spiders haven't let me down on this journey. My next stop will be Steelrising.

On a side note, I was really expecting a continuation to Mars: War Logs and The Technomancer universe, however I'm quite interested in a sequel to Greedfall as well.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSometimes I wish steam would have a % rating of recommendation instead either 0 or 100, because this game falls quite in the middle. In the end though, bitterness about some things win over.

Positives:
Visuals (world design, beasts), interesting characters AND the story.
Mage class being powerful for once.
Female voice acting (altho the "I am de Sardet legate of... does start to ptsd after a while).
It really is a marriage between Dragon Age and dishonoured.

Negatives:
The game is rough around the edges, characters moving/jumping/hanging in the air or bugs is not uncommon occurrence.
The quests and travelling (see: running) get boring fast - the basic idea of "go fetch" and occasionally "go fetch and maybe fight a big boss".
You get locked out of romance with any character if you answer just one of their (very easily missable) questions wrong and there is NO chance to remedy that.
Fighting also gets boring after a while (someone mentioned just spamming 2 spells - I felt that too)
For ending only one (final) choice truly matters and there is little to no gradient - either it is doom and gloom or rainbows and unicorns.
Also teeth. What the hell is wrong with teeth???

In end - it is not worth full price, but solid 30+ hour sink with decent story for entertainment, if you don't mind the repetitive parts and very easy miss romance options.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tConsidering the independancy and budget constraints of the studio, I will say they did a good job. Congratulations.

That said, the game is not anything as advertised.

1 - Open World: not at all. Maps are divided into small section with travel in between. Each section is just a dungeon with open sky. You can't walk around freely as the paths you can choose are all bordered by either map limits or stone canyons. To top, you can't jump. Yes, there's no jump button. You have to walk around every small rock and root.

2 - Choices matter: Unless you want to play a ramrod who has been assigned as a diplomat to a new world by who-knows-what moment of dumbness, it is too easy to make everyone happy. You are bound to loose a little favor points with som factions along the way, but usually whatever you do, you can get friendly with everyone and puff! happy ending.

3 - Role-playing: You don't have the ability to choose between two actions for 95% of the quests. Just go, grab something, get out. Or kill everyone. And go report to one NPC. For a game with 5 competing factions, you should have had a choice on how to use your gained info or items.

4 - Legendary items: They are useless. Most of the regular items are stronger than them or can be stronger with blacksmithing. Also, the most powerful legendary weapons are only usable by main character, and only if you chose to play as a warrior. If you are a rogue or mage-type, good luck. The best legendary weapons are not usable by any of your companions.

5 - Diverse setting: This is where the game fails miserably. Cities of settlers are all same, villages of natives are all same. Can you imagine having 3 immensely different cultures (merchants, theleme and bridge alliance) having the exactly same palaces for governers, or build you the exact same house in each of their cities?

I can write a lot more, but for such a promising setting and background story, the game is a dissappointment. Play it if you have a lot of free time in your hands but don't expect a true open world role playing chance.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really wanted to like this game; the lore and premise are solid but the execution is ultimately just too lackluster to come together into a coherent whole.

The companions are written well enough and the voiceacting is good, but outside of a very small dialogue tree that you quickly exhaust and three companion quests each, you don't really interact with them so I never felt a particular connection to any of them.
De Sardet's dialogue is, again, well written and the voiceactor did a good job, but you rarely get to decide how to react something, so I had a hard time connecting to them.

The prologue allows you to make decisions about how to handle the introductory quests, but in the rest of the game, your options feel very limited when it comes to the outcomes except for a handful.
The first half of the game allows you a lot of freedom in the order you do quests, but the writing doesn't reflect it - I apparently played them very out of the intended order, as after a while, I suddenly was "discovering" things that I had already come across a lot earlier.

The environments are pretty, but travelling across maps loses its appeal as there's the same enemies everywhere; the allowed paths are very limited (you can jump down cliffs but you cannot climb over a log, apparently) and you have to do a ridiculous amount of backtracking. Sometimes the game offers to teleport you to finish a quest, but often not. Sometimes it takes you back without asking.
When you fast travel between maps, you are dumped into a small area with a camp and a merchant (obviously intended to reduce annoyance to run to merchants to dump your equipment and to hide loading screens), but when a quest needs you to travel to multiple maps in quick succession for nothing more than a dialogue check, it gets very old very fast.

While I managed to finish it, after about the halfway mark, all these annoyances (some minor, some major) just built up to sap any goodwill for the story.\t\t\t\t\t
