\r\n\t\t\t\t\tExcellent RPG! A bit like Dragon Age for style and how companions work, except set in a 1600's like colonial environment. I was misled by reviews saying this is a 45hr game -- that's only if you skip half the content. It took me ~65hrs, which was doing all the side quests except for one (DO do the side quests, as they substantially change the story and your relationships with people, as well as how moving the end is). I really enjoyed that you play a diplomat here, and even characters/allies I didn't like at the beginning I ended up liking or respecting at the end because of how we had worked together. You also have nice customization options to play a woman, no one is sexist to you, and your character's voice acting is amazing. If you don't like character or dialogue-driven games this probably isn't for you, but I recommend this for fans of fantasy RPGs like Dragon Age, Skyrim, etc who like engaging with the other characters in the story. There are multiple endings, but if you try to play this game morally, then it's beautiful and poignant and rewards hard work.

Issues/complaints:
- Tech wise, there was a very common bug where when you drop off a ledge you keep falling through the ground and had to wait till you hit the water way below the game before you bounced back up. Most of the time this was inconsequential, but once I had to take an alternate route because it stopped me, and on the final boss, when I was struck downward by a blow, the same thing happened with me falling through the ground but then the boss' health refreshed when I bounced back up, which really sucked.
- There is a rather troubling colonialist decision you need to make at one point, where the developers I think aren't as aware of anti-racism and decolonizing ideas as they should be, because you are forced to do something that's actually kind of racist (Decide the leader of the indigenous people despite that you are not really one of them and it's completely inappropriate for you to weigh in, and that when you do, choosing Deirdre won't get you a good ending when it should
- you can only romance one companion, you can't break up with them once you start, and you can only romance them if you make a few particular dialogue choices which, even if you're trying, you are unlikely to make without looking up the answers online. One of the romance options is also inappropriate.
- Some of the travel, and especially the wild beast attacks get really repetitive toward the end, so I just ran away from them all because it wasn't worth my time and the only loot you get are a couple ordinary alchemical ingredients...
- ...because the alchemy here was also really lackluster. I used health and magic potions and that was basically it; there were so many more options but most of them were just entirely unnecessary.
- And lastly...why does everyone talk while baring their teeth?!?\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA walking sim, they say? I disagree.

Played in 2023, Greedfall successfully incorporates many of the great aspects of modern RPGs. Branching skill trees, deep story with multiple (mini) endings, romances and even factions relationships, excellent graphics (the lighting effects are especially intriguing). great controls (Dragon Age Inquisition, anyone??), varied combat style options, etc.

There is plenty of fast-travel available, which pretty much does the job. The way the designers made the relationships work between different factions, party members, and results of your quest/decisions is perfectly done. You in-game decisions don't detrimentally ruin the ending, so no fear there, but your ending will definitely reflect how you played most of the game, which is great.

Bit of strong advice: ALWAYS save 1 talent point for until you need it (lock picking, vigor, etc). Science might be the most important to put one point in right away, then vigor, then lock picking. Lock picking doesn't actually doesn't get you much, to my surprise. DON"T BOTHER with charisma! Petrus can give you a boost, your armor can give you another, and just make sure to save right before you know any major dialogue is coming up. If your persuasion happens to fail, just reload right before the conversation and do it again. Piece of cake. For each of the armor sets that you'll end up holding on, upgrade each one with a talent upgrade on the chest as well. I found magic to, by far, be the strongest skill route, but make sure you have plenty of willpower.

I very highly recommend this game to all RPG fans. Doing maybe 75% of the side quests, I clocked 35hrs.

...oh, and don't look too closely at the grass!!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGreedfall is a great game in my opinion. But that is not to say it's a game for everyone. Not because it is too hard, or it's got a ton of confusing lore that only Harvard educated historians can decipher. It's because of the fact it tries really hard to sell you a unique and interesting story, while falling flat on its face in some of the ways it tells that story. To explain what I mean, let's see what the main story is about.

You play as De Sardet, a noble of The Congregation of Merchants, a nation that prioritizes free trade with other nations over anything else, meaning that no matter your political beliefs or your religion, as long as you respect the Congregation's autonomy, you will be allowed to trade with them. De Sardet (you) is tasked with taking a venture to the newly colonized island of Teer Fradee to establish a foothold for the Congregation by engaging in political theatrics with two other nations who have established themselves there, and the Natives who have lived on the island for centuries. You serve under the governor of the new Congregation city there, who also happens to be your cousin. (Nepotism is awesome) When you arrive on the island, you immediately are embroiled in a tale of intrigue, political deception, occultism, and lies with a constant cloud of tension broiling above you, threatening to lead to all out war at all times.

Now just that quick summary alone sounds awesome right? And it is! The writing is intricate, well thought out, and paced very very well. But the issue stems from HOW the game tells you that story. From the lip syncing ranging from sort of strange to downright puppet like with how exaggerated and out of sync it is sometimes, which is a real distraction at points when you're supposed to be in an intense and emotional scene only for the character's lips to move like they're a malfunctioning android. Or to the fact that many areas where big story events take place (interiors for buildings) look the exact same as every other building you have been in, with the only real distinguishing features being what colors the walls and rugs are, and what kind of furniture (if any) is placed.

It's small intricate details like these that can turn the average gamer, who is used to AAA games that tote their top of the line level design and graphical tech off from this game. Now there are plenty of redeeming factors besides the story that make this game worth playing. While the interiors are all copy paste setpieces, the outside areas of the game are varied and stylized like crazy. Many times I was thoroughly impressed with how much scale they were able to convey with what they had to work with. The forest areas and humble tribal villages especially are a wonderful, mystical sight especially when you contrast them with the big cities which from the outside are vast and extremely impressive to behold. The combat, while very easy according to some, is very fun and engaging, and has plenty of abilities and skills you can upgrade to unlock more slick moves and weapons to toy with to find what fits your playstyle. The party members are also quite useful in combat and your party loadout can make a big difference in how engagements go.

All in all, there is plenty to appreciate in Greedfall, and while it isn't for everyone, you can really respect the things it has done with such a small team of 40 developers behind it. And with Spiders hard at work on the second installment, I can only hope they can bring the world of Greedfall to a wider audience that is able to appreciate what they have made by implementing what they have learned from this first installment. If you like games like Gothic, Morrowind, Risen, or any other famously "hard-to-get-into cult classics" then you will like this game.\t\t\t\t\t
