\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGreedfall is an RPG that clearly is inspired by Dragon Age: Origins, The Witcher 3: Wild Hunt, and The Elder Scrolls V: Skyrim. It borrows elements from each of these classic RPGs, adding a story to the mix which uses a Golden Age of colonization as background. The player takes up the role of De Sardet, the cousin of a newly appointed governor of an island. De Sardet represents a Merchant Organization, and has to deal with three colonizing factions, a faction of seafarers, and the island's natives. A plague called "the malichor" has invaded the island, infecting many of the colonists, which seems to be tied to the extraordinary rituals performed by the natives. De Sardet has to find a cure for the malichor.

Greedfall is a mixed bag. A few elements work well, while most are mediocre at best. The world created in the game is interesting, and the environments look great. The cities feel real, and the island's nature is impressive. But everything else in the game does not manage to live up to this excellent background.

Character development, a core element of RPGs, is very limited and does not give the opportunity to create a character that is interesting to play. For instance, you can invest in Vigor or Intuition; the consequence is that if you invested in Vigor you can climb steep walls and balance over fallen trees, while if you invested in Intuition you can sneak through small passages. You may find that to reach a particular spot, you have to have a certain level of Vigor -- however, if you invested in Intuition instead, there will still be a way to reach that spot by sneaking through a small passage. And there will also be a path for which neither Vigor nor Intuition is needed. Therefore overall, it does not matter much what you invest in.

Combat is simple and boring -- I started the game on "normal" combat, but it was so uninspired that I turned difficulty down to "easy" to get the combat over quicker (also, the controls reacted slow, which is probably because I used MKB). There are only a dozen or so different enemies in the game, and all of them can be defeated by simple bashing.

The quests get tedious very quickly -- almost all quests have you run all over the map (often using fast-travel), running from person to person to exchange a few words with them, repeating this a considerable number of times, until you have finished the quest. It comes over as the creators wanting to tell a particular story, and to provide some gameplay they have the player travel from spot to spot to experience parts of that story.

What I considered a big mistake in the game is that your choices in the game are supposed to matter -- and they evidently do -- but you make those choices long before their effect becomes apparent to you. For instance, you can choose to antagonize a clan of natives or befriend them, which will have some impact on the game, but you do not know that it will have any impact at the time you make the choice. Only much later something may happen which may make you think "if I had befriended that clan three hours ago, this might have gone differently." So the choice you made is effectively a non-choice, as you had no way of knowing or even suspecting what the impact would be.

The first half of the game I found reasonably engaging, as I was exploring new areas and new cities, and I just enjoyed the travel. But after the first 20 hours there was nothing new to discover, and I just had to fast travel everywhere to do those damn quests. After 25 hours I was hoping that the game would be over soon, and I seemed to be heading towards the finish, but the story is actually quite intricate and took new twists and turns. New twists and turns which were not uninteresting, but I was more or less done with the game and just wanted it to end.

So what it ultimately comes down to is that this game simply does not have enough good playable content to warrant its length. 45 hours for this game is far too long. If it would tell a similar story, leaving out many of the boring sidetracks, in half the length, I would rate it "good." Now I have to rate it as "a failed attempt."\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI don't know if I've ever reviewed a game on here, but I just finished Greedfall last night and can't stop thinking about it. I had a blast and loved it, but it won't be everyone's cup of tea. I recommend buying it on sale.

The things I really liked:
The party members are excellent. Their voice acting is great and they're all interesting characters. They each have (sort of) optional story lines that are not only great character development, but awesome world-building too. As you're out wandering and questing, they talk with each other or give De Sardet their two cents about the current quest which I really enjoy.
The side quests are engaging.
Choices mostly matter.
I like the romance, I think it's well paced. I made my brother draw me Vasco fan art for Christmas.
I like the skill tree, it actually matters what you put points in to.
The combat is repetitive, but it can be fun if you crank it up to hard.

Really though, I loved playing Greedfall so much because it sometimes falls into the "so bad it's good" category (these are just a few examples):
The voice acting is great but the dialogue can be really cheesy, abrupt, and unexpected. I found myself laughing after a lot of cutscenes.
The story beats can be really funny when characters and factions seemingly randomly have changes of heart about you.
I got some hilarious graphical glitches. Every time I talked to Manfred during the first Coin Guard quest the camera would clip inside a background characters head, so the cutscene would be framed with the back of their eyeballs and teeth.
Guns. If you put your stats in to guns matter the difficulty level, every battle can be finished in like 30 seconds or less with a gun and a handful of grenades. Even the battles that are played up to have challenge and narrative weight, it's delightfully funny.
A couple of times quests "overlapped" with each other and took me to locations where other quests take place later in the game. So sometimes the game would spoil itself and dialogue that made no sense to the situation at hand would trigger.

The bad:
The biggest thing I hated was the way the story handles colonialism and depicts the Natives ranges from "ew" to "oh big yikes." However, it's a French developer so they probably aren't too keyed in to the political climate in the states/Canada. It's a silly video game so you kinda have to take it at face value. I was planning to give it another playthrough, but after completing the game I don't think I will because of this.
The armor/weapon upgrade system isn't anything good. The way I played the it was really poorly paced out when I got weapons and armor as rewards for quests, so they were always weak compared to mine.
Once again, quest rewards generally sucked which often disincentivized me doing them unless I was interested in their stories.
It gets really fetch-questy, which is a slog sometimes.
The beginning was slow, I almost put the game down 2 hours in.
The ending left a lot to be desired, I feel like they had a lot left to expand on. The Bridge Alliance chose not to help me and I doubt the ending would be different if they came to help. I did love the cut scene where the Alliance governor told me to f*** off after sending assassins to kill me, admitted to his men poisoning Constantin, and guilted me for killing his genocide loving scientist. \t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've replayed this a few times since I got it and it runs well, I usually end up playing a rogue like character with a gun and bombs and poisons and have romanced all the characters as played both male and female DeVespers and the world is beautifully designed. It has its flaws but they aren't game breaking flaws just design choices and mislabelling of my character if playing as a female, oh and the two nauts you can speak to regarding the disappearance of Jonas in Serene, once on the island they are mislabelled when speaking to Lauro - the guy - you get an 'ask her how things have been' and for Flavia - the girl - 'ask him how things have been' which always makes me chuckle and I always feel the ending just happens, feels very abrupt and anticlimactic. I will explain more below.

Now onto pros and cons. I shall start with the pros...

The pros
The world is very pretty, the town of serene where the game starts I really liked the look of that can just imagine it in its prime before the malichor ravaged the town, the isle of Teer Fradee is gorgeous and varied. The new towns built on the island vary a lot, first time approaching Hikmet my jaw hit my desk and this town risen from the dusty sands and has that look of an old town in the desert that is bustling with life, having been to Egypt I visited some towns that reminded me of the style of this and wow, inside the town the roads are a maze and gotten lost wandering around. The town of San Matheus is a stark contrast of stone architecture, gothic towers and paved roads, you get quite the shocking introduction when approaching the town square which I won't spoil. The regions and camps of the natives vary a bit from region to region but all are clearly native camps.

The npcs, while there are a lot of generic filler npcs that can't be spoken to the ones we can speak to are interesting and some we encounter in quests are on the whole interesting and for some there is no clean cut good / bad answers on what to do in this scene, such as with a scientist in Hikmet we can get executed, a brilliant mind, but how you feel on what this guy did but comes down to the players moral code on what is considered too far but he will die regardless.
The weapon and armour customisations, I really liked this aspect and as I mostly use the guns in this and the changes you can make to the looks I loved, same with the armours, for my companions they'd be given armours I had customised to fit their roles aesthetically.

The quests are interesting and there are so some fetch quests but every single rpg has that element it seems we cannot get away from those but they are not that frequent, least not after playing Elder Scroll games, I REALLY wish I could punch the Governor of Hikmet, I really can imagine DeVesper getting so angry at him that after the verbal bashing punches him, I really wanted to punch that scientist who goes after Mev when you are trying to find her to ask for help, either when hes trying to bribe me to let him go and have him take Mev to be experimented on or tell him to get out of there, Mev will react whatever you do and if he survives, you meet him again later in game and he makes a derogatory comment about being a fan of the natives and oooh wish I could have give him either a verbal bashing or a punch to his smug face shows the writing was good to bring out these emotions. Yes I usually end up siding fully with the natives as screw those coming to their island, I'm not the most diplomatic DeVesper thats for sure.

The DLC, it doesn't work with mods which took me a while to figure out but simply removing mods from the folder while you do the quest and put them back in when its done. The Conspiracy is a fun quest of deception and intrigue and I was tense the whole time playing it expecting an attack and ending to that feel mostly satisfying, I just wish I could of seen the trial and execution if that happened.

Could be improved
Weapons. Honestly the weapons could be more varied, so many extreme Bloodborne inspired style ridiculous looking weapons, there was so many for 2 handed heavy hitters but hardly many simple one handed swords / rapiers.

The copy and paste castles and homes, this feels very let down compared to the story, I would of like to see the castles reflect the cities they are in and also the homes could of been good for variation, I suppose we don't spend a lot of time there so they didn't think it was worth it but it is a shame everything looks the same, seeing as we don't go into that many buildings I feel more could of been done to personalise these interiors.

I really wish the guardians recognised you, I hate having to kill them I really do and lack of rewards when you do kill them feels like a complete waste of time, if nothing on the guardian a simple chest containing rewards nearby would of been good.

The romance could of been improved, it's like the most basic romance you can have I think the one in Skyrim is worse off then this, so many areas for a romantic scene could of taken place with your chosen, all we get is a couple of flirt options and a fade to black one time sleep with them and then a little scene on the mountain near the end, feels like a big gap is missed with the flirt options and then that. I am just nitpicking that but yeah, lazy romances are always a let down.

The end, I have always done the good ending but either one thats the end and thats it, doesn't feel finished in that sense The use of cards to tell either ending could of been done different, if we got the good ending could of had Courcillon in a smart detailed office talking in person telling the events of what happened, if the bad ending a rough looking Courcillon in a ruined building despondent telling the bad ending would of been an improvement and images of the growth of friendships or the destruction from our choices.

But do I recommend it? Yes I do, the game is good and its these minor things mentioned that let it down but they don't break game play its more of a wish of how it was.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a janky re-interpretation of the classical Bioware RPG formula. After every RPG developer collectively decided to make their games open-world time sinks of OCD item collection traps, having a game where the world is split between smaller levels and several hubs is a welcome change. The world presented is also fairly unique and does a decent job to avoid being labeled as "generic". If you're a fan of French 17th century architecture in a steampunk-ish + magic = love setting, this is the game for you. The writing is a bit cliché, but the gameplay is good enough to keep you going regardless.
