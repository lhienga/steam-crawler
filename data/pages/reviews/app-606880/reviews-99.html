\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA very good and solid single player RPG. It reminds me of both Gothic 3 and Risen.
I played as a mage and spent all my skill points their but complimented with a good pistol and one melee weapon.

+ Excellent Graphics
+ Perfectly good sound effects
+ Good RPG build with tough choices
+ Lots of equipment and ingredients
+ Lots of cutscenes
+ Good story with a lot of side-quests
+ Easy to travel around
+ Very nice scenary
+ Perfect balance of progression
+ Very addictive game
+ Nice time period

- Bad and naive voice acting of your main character
- Too much black and white regarding to morale. You always knows what is the right decision.
- Too limited in your ability to move in the wilderness. You are restricted in many places and you cannot jump.
- Few caves and all of them looks the same.
- Very few if any dungeons
- Towns and cities are very empty. Nothing to explore or find anywhere.
- No rewarding for explorations. Chests and Loot stops being interesting after level 15.
- Very few special items to be found
- Not very special music. No catchy theme to remember.
- Lock pick skills almost useless. Items you found not worth investing in the skill.
- After level 25 it seems very hard to get better equipment for mages i.e rings, amulettes etc.
- Slow and clumsy movements in combat
- No way to set default "Run" so you have to press shift all the time
- A lot of running back and forth on the same maps especially in cities where there are few fast travel options.
- After level 25 nothing interesting to buy in shops. At least not for mages.

In total a very good game. But the gameworld feels empty. You cannot do anything anywhere except if you have a mission. Nothing to interact with. No secret doors or special treasures. You run around several times back to caves and places you have already explored. Missions put items in places which were not there the first time you visited the place etc. Lacks soul and content.

Still, a very addictive game which I highly recommend. Rating 4 out of 5. One of the best games in its genre the last few years. Despite all my minuses this game is a must buy.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOne of the better plot-centric games I've experienced in a while. The design and art style of this game are very charming, and they sometimes remind me of exploring the gorgeous worlds in Dragon Age: Inquisition.

It feels a little... strange to play as a colonialist for the first parts of the game, and I found myself pretty regularly wishing that there were better writings and nuances with how they're handling this theme (in the first 20+ hours, give or take...), but the story and characters did start to take an interesting and engaging (albeit somewhat expected) turn by last 1/3, and I eventually found myself conflicted and struggling in face of certain decisions towards the end--which is always a good sign imo. I'm always happy (if that's the right word) when a game challenges me with moral/ethical/emotional dilemmas.

All in all, although this game did take some time to grow on me, I eventually came to enjoy the story :) Just have to be a little patient with it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe good: 1. Captivating story and unique setting with serious themes, like exploitation of the native population, religion vs science etc. 2. Mostly great visuals - the island feels lush, the towns - gritty, and the details on the armor are exceptional 3. Engaging and rewarding combat system. On one hand it makes you fell that with enough skill all enemies are beatable, and on the other it is unforgiving enough to keep you on your toes throughout the whole game.

The bad: 1. Most of the quests are generic and tedious and require multiple transfers between already explored locations to talk to a bunch of npcs, with some kind of fight in the end. 2. Exploration could be much much more rewarding. Most of the stuff that you find during exploration is only good for dismantling for crafting components. 3. Party members feel underdeveloped, and do not evoke any emotional attachment unlike in similar games from Bioware. 4. While there are choices in the game, the consequences of the alternatives become obvious during the first playthrough, therefore the replayability of the game is doubtful.

The ugly: 1. Facial animation looks like it belongs in early 2010s. 2. During playthrough the game crashed at least 4 or 5 times.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGood setting, story, and characters. Companions both have their own agenda / story AND a relationship with MC. Central plot has twists and turns that aren't just Shamalan-style revelations. Good job of gradually revealing setting info and foreshadowing.

Gameplay is fun. Interesting leveling system where you will basically maximize one aspect of your character part way through and then progress by broadening out. However, a lot of the wilderness encounters get very tedious, as you may just have the exact same fight five times in the same playthrough.

Which brings me to the biggest issue - retreading the same paths. Like most RPGs, if you want to do all the content you have to spend a lot of time running here and there. But it's even more annoying in Greedfall. There's basically no open spaces in the whole game map - everything uses twisting paths, and there's just a compass (no way to move with the map up). Fast travel points are often ~ 30-60 seconds away from the NPCs you repeatedly visit, so you need to either run down a lot of dead-end-alleys, pause to open the map a lot, or memorize certain routes (e.g. San Mateus house -> Mother Cardinal). But even worse, the quest design generally doesn't allow you to do what you do in other RPGs, where you can optimize your errands list by just doing a ton of things in one area, only move on once all of the objectives in that zone are done, then move to the next and tackle them all. The sidequests are generally a big web all over the map, where if you start two in the same zone, each might take you through five different zone transitions, none of them in common (e.g. Quest 1 takes you to zones ABCBC, quest 2 takes you to DEBAF). Also there's several times you will go somewhere, only to be told you need to backtrack to a bed to wait for a specific time. What's especially annoying is that the sidequests in the prologue show that the devs know how to avoid this, because they all include little pop-ups saying "travel immediately to the next location?" or "wait until relevant time?", but they only afford you these conveniences sporadically.

If you like RPGs, the experience is well worth that annoyance, but it is an annoyance.\t\t\t\t\t
