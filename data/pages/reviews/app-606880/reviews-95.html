\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe good:
Very good gameplay, art style, and story.
Very good RPG systems, e.g. leveling, battle, equipment, crafting. The "loot" hierarchy is a bit minimalistic but the crafting "materia"-like system helps a bit and is visually appealing.

The less good:
While you can note the devs effort to add a voice over to every NPC dialogs, which is mostly a cool thing, the voicing is a bit goofy at times, as the dynamic dialogues sometimes mix a tense situation between characters followed by an friendly exchange.

The map level design is overall good but a few bits that sometimes a bit subpar in the sense that you may enter part of the map you're not supposed to if you follow the story and it leaves you sometimes in situation where you can be confused by why a map element is there without explanation.

The worst:
The game is actually hurt a lot by the traveling system, which most of the time forces you to run back to the nearest exit or camp before being able to teleport to the real near location of the next story point.
And the quests like to make you run a lot for no reason at time. This will drive you crazy if you don't cut your playthrough in several smaller attempts.
The simple addition of a "Travel to nearest camp or exit" option would actually save this game for me, and I'm not buying the DLC for the sole reason it is not there.


All That said, if you like the victorian style of this game mixed with the unknown island mystery and magic, you'll have a very good time.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI needed a fix for my Dragon Age crave, and this looked perfect.
Well, it wasn't, as so often when you really want to like a game but then it doesn't live up to your hopes.
Don't get me wrong, Greedfall is beautiful, and the gameplay indeed steps in the Bioware tradition. But it is tedious. After 15h of gameplay I was through with all the giddy new game excitement, I had unlocked most of the maps and characters, some good, some less so, and I found myself just... clicking through dialogues, not caring about anything, racing through main quests hoping to get to the good stuff: when can I make significant choices? When do I get to romance and bang one of these NPCs? Will this tired colonialism story actually surprise me in the end?

I will never know, because after ten more hours of mindlessly walking to questsgivers and back, grinding through fetchquests and running through mobs to avoid the bore of hearing the same NPC catchphrases again and again, and getting another ton of loot I didn't care for, I gave up. Greedfall, I really tried. I'm sorry.\t\t\t\t\t

There's a deeply intricate and elaborately woven tapestry of quests in this game and depending on the order in which you do them, or the manner in which you get them done, the outcomes of later missions can be radically different. Building and maintaining alliances is a key function of this game, so be ready to work with some rather unscrupulous folk in the name of the greater good. It does offer incentives for multiple playthroughs however with multiple endings and 3 classes that play out somewhat differently from each other. So if you're looking for something fun and new, Greedfall might be for you. I hope there's a sequel on the horizon.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'd purely recommend trying this game out, as i may just have been spoiled by the likes of skyrim / fallout, or simply may not have progressed far enough (im only past the first act) to have gotten into the real depths of the game. however, there are many flaws that i'd probably have seen in games from like 10 years ago.... i may be being too critical, but whilst the graphics are nice, it just feels like an early access game.

firstly, the first act isnt really what I'd class as open world. yes you have some freedom moving between the city, however, there are so many glass walls, areas that cant be accessed as theres a barrel, or a skimpy chain in the way.

all the internal houses are copied and pasted, plus you cant jump or swim. all these really lead to breaking the immersion. that and sometimes the characters mouths dont move when theyre just talking in the general game. every door and entry has a "loading animation" to hide the need for a loading screen. but this is...EVERY...DARN....DOORWAY. it may seem overly critical, but even inside of houses when you go between rooms it does this! even something simple, like taking a pile of coins off a table! you take the coins and all that happens is that they stop glowing and remain on the table!!!

progression is extremely controlled. for example, in skyrim youre just left to the world, so can technically level up before facing the main quest. this caps your progress before you can progress (again i'm only past the first act, so this may change) the people look like bad ventriloquist acts when they talk, and when theres the general commentary, their mouths dont even move!! with a third person game, this is something thats really...just creepy!

without giving too much away, at the end of the first act theres the expected boss fight...however...within a big city with guards (i'd assume) you're left alone in the middle of the streets to deal with the boss. to make things worse, after this happens EVERYONE CARRIES ON LIKE THIS IS A DAILY OCCOURENCE!!

I wont go into all the other bugs / immersion breakers, but it just feels like diluted potential. Anyways, so far i wouldnt recommend this game, but i could change my mind as I progress, maybe itll get better, or maybe itll just end up being half played.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis was a very well made rpg. Different factions competing against each other, and a world in which the player explored. It was a very well defined setting and characters, that didn't seem as bland, boring and cliched as the Dragon Age games for instance. There were almost no bugs, and those there were, were minor.

I do think games like this lose sight of the fact that at some point when you've collected 5 out of 5 widgets to do some task that needs to be done before you have to wait for nightfall to see some character and follow them, before you can gain the favour of some character feel like a sequence of scripted events. So it stops being interacting with a world and becomes a series of scripted events that were perhaps checked off a list by a juniour game developer.

Minor problems:
- When you run into something it activates, and when the characters stand around you the difference between them getting out of your way and you triggering their tiresome dialogue again is small. It adds tedium to the game,
- There's some travel pause camp between locations, it gets super tiresome to have to skip this every single time and double load the movement between locations.
- I could never work out what time it was or how to sleep right. So I often ended up standing around waiting for things to happen. Nearer the end of the game missions often opted to let you wait as part of the scripted trigger and this was an improvement.

Apart from that I would recommend this as a high quality rpg.\t\t\t\t\t
