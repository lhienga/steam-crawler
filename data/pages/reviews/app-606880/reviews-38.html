\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA good dragon age 2-style game with a unique setting

The game has a charm about it that is leaving a nostalgic impression on me.
I compare it with DA2 because it shares many of the mechanics, including party, exploration quest/main story exposition etc. It's not as good, but it's solid all around. The first half of the game so far is a slow burn where you gradually uncover more info and mysteries about the new world. The environments are nice, and while companions do not stand out like mass effect/Dragon age, they still interesting enough to learn more about.

The quest designs are a strong point, they weave into each other and the discovery of the world very well and are well-designed and full of surprises. Combat is fun but best done on hard difficulty. Upgrades really have an effect, unlike the latest assassins creed games where everything is in tiny increments that have no effect that lasts more than 20 minutes. The towns are large and beautifully realised hybrid 16th/18th-century style.

Definitely recommend on a sale and if you are a fan of the older dragon age games, particularly DA2!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOverall Score: 9/10

While you can tell this came from a smaller (indie?) game studio, the scope of this game is much bigger than I expected. Excellent writing and fun and challenging gameplay. I noticed a few reused assets in places (like the government buildings all being the same building inside just reskined), but I /really/ appreciated the detail to giving each location its own personality including the geography. Each village and city has a distinct character to it by using decoration, architecture, and geography/foliage. Tir Fradee is a very hilly island (makes sense since it's volcanic) and the game makes great use of that. Not to mention the game covers some pretty heavy topics (colonialism) that I think they handled pretty darn well.

I've got two main gripes. There's a lot of running around and at times it got a little tedious, but there was enough in the environment that it was only when I had to backtrack that it became an issue. My other main complaint was that I wish the main team of followers and some of the major NPCs had a bit more to say. I'd have loved more dialogue options to learn more about them and the world they come from.

Overall I really enjoyed the game. It's got some flaws, but the story, characters, and combat made for a really engaging game with some twists I didn't expect. Definitely recommend!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLet me start by noting that I haven't yet finished the game. However, I doubt my opinion will change much.

I have been enjoying the experience enough to keep playing, but it's not quite living up to expectations I had. The story has carried it sufficiently, as have the characters, but the game itself suffers from I'd call "Dragon Age II Syndrome." My critiques of GreedFall are almost exclusively gameplay related.

That is to say, there are very few enemies collectively. They're just different colored versions of the same enemy for the most part. Or some might be slightly larger and textured a little differently. I've identified five or six enemies with a few reskins, not counting bosses.

The major buildings are all identical structures also. The houses of the tribal elders or major tribal leaders in the setting are all identical with a couple tables moved around. The governors' mansions are all identically laid out with even the same furniture in most places. The individual buildings in the cities are all identically laid out with mostly the same furniture as well. There are a few exceptions, but not many at all.

Beyond that, enemies all drop the same few things with one difference being scaled enemies drop scales. They all drop "beast essence", leather, bezoars, fangs, and the occasional intact heart. Most of these items are crafting materials but the crafting materials have no variance, so killing fifty of one creature is the same as killing fifty of another.

The quest design is also rather rudimentary for most things, and while I'm doing most of the side quests because they increase my relationships with various factions, I rarely feel like those quests are having much real impact. The quests are also extremely short, for the most part, and focused around a single action: go here, do this. It doesn't feel like, short of faction affection, my choices have much of an impact on the story.

Now, I don't necessarily expect a full BioWare experience where every choice I make has an effect on the endgame. And I know not every game is designed that way and was created by a far smaller development team. However, it did promise me a "core roleplaying experience" which doesn't honestly stand up to other titles of comparable merit. I also want to note that the sheer number of misspellings and volume of poor punctuation in the subtitles is, frankly, disappointing. One quest had the word "phantom" spelled as "fantom" for a good third of it before it corrected. And comma splices are everywhere.

Normally I don't nitpick grammar and punctuation in my games, but this one was bad enough that it really did catch my attention and drew me out of the immersion pretty hard.

Finally, the exploration element to the game is a bit weak. There's very little reason to explore the map much. There are the same collectables (hawthorne, nightshade, pine sap, hoof fungus...) everywhere, and other than finding the occasional skill point shrine, there's very little reason to comb through the map to explore it. Which is a pity because the level design is really pretty. The number of lovely vistas and interesting elements (like caravans ransacked by beasts) would make it interesting going if there were any incentive to actually explore the thing. Sadly, there is very little in the world to attract a gamer to leave the main paths unless you're a completionist who wants to get all of the achievements.

I'm going to finish playing the game and follow the plot through to the end. I like the characters well enough that I want to keep exploring their story, but the gameplay itself has proven to be pretty lackluster overall in terms of the bones of the design. I'm not sure I'd play it a second time after this. I don't regret buying it -- I've certainly paid money for worse games. But overall, I'd give this one a solid "meh." You might enjoy the plot enough to play it, like I did, but don't go in expecting something revolutionary.\t\t\t\t\t

Greedfall is a visually stunning RPG that takes you on an adventure through a beautiful and diverse world. While the game's graphics and design are undoubtedly its strongest points, the experience as a whole falls short in several areas.

The game is set in a fantasy world where magic and technology coexist, and the player takes on the role of De Sardet, a diplomat sent to explore a newly discovered island. As the story unfolds, players must navigate a world torn between warring factions, forge alliances, and uncover the secrets of the island.

Pros:
The game's world and art design are beautiful and immersive. The developers have done an excellent job in creating a unique and interesting setting that sets it apart from other RPGs. The island is diverse, with a mix of vibrant landscapes ranging from lush forests to arid deserts, each with its own wildlife and challenges. The music is well composed and sets the mood for each area. Combat is fun and engaging, and there is a decent amount of character customization available. Additionally, the dialogue system is well done, with choices that have meaningful consequences that can alter the course of the story.

Cons:
Unfortunately, Greedfall lacks depth in several key areas. The story, while unique, is ultimately forgettable and fails to fully engage the player. It lacks the emotional investment that many players look for in RPGs. Players may find themselves caring less about the characters and the story's outcome as they progress through the game. The lack of investment in the player character or allies is a major letdown. The relationships built with allies feel superficial, and the companions' storylines are underdeveloped, making it hard to get attached to them.

Another problem with the game is its lack of innovation in the RPG genre. The game follows a standard formula, with few new mechanics or features that differentiate it from other RPGs. There is little in the game that hasn't been seen before, and it feels like a missed opportunity to bring something fresh to the table.

The game's world and art design may be beautiful, but they also come at a price. The game's graphics are demanding, and even with top-of-the-line hardware, the game's performance is subpar. Players may experience frame rate drops, and the loading times are long and frequent, which can break immersion.

The game's quests and side activities are also lacking in depth, with many feeling like tedious fetch quests. The game's economy is also unbalanced, with a significant emphasis on collecting and selling resources. Players will spend a significant amount of time on resource gathering and management, which can detract from the overall enjoyment of the game.

In conclusion, Greedfall is a decent RPG that is visually impressive and engaging, but it falls short in several key areas. The lack of investment in the player character or allies is a significant flaw, and the game's story and quest design are underwhelming. While the game has its merits, it is hard to recommend it over other RPGs in the market. A score of 6/10 is a fair rating for this game, with significant room for improvement.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tParty-based, hack-n-slash rpg that owes more to Dragon Age than Witcher. Well-written characters, good stories, multiple endings, decent combat and crafting systems, and great graphics. On the down-side, there is a LOT of running in Greedfall (even with all the fast-travel points available), interiors and buildings are constantly re-used (not surprising), facial animations are below par, the graphics are demanding, and the game feels about 6 hours too long overall.

A lot of ups and downs with Greedfall as you can see, but it succeeded in taking me to another place, telling great stories about that place, and making me feel a part of that place. That's everything I ask for from a role-playing game. Loved it. Looking forward to Greedfall 2.
8/10

DXVK_ASYNC=1 DXVK_FRAME_RATE=60 gamemoderun %command% -high -USEALLAVAILABLECORES

Exclusive Fullscreen
3440x1440
vsync disabled (can cause stutter)

Texture Quality - High
Anisotropy - Normal x4
Dynamic resolution - 100%
FOV - default

Greedfall ran flawlessly with these settings - no hiccups and maybe half-a dozen frame-drops over 35 hours of gameplay.\t\t\t\t\t

Is the game still worth buying in 2023?

Absolutely, take this review with a grain of salt since I dont normally play RPG/Adventure games.

What is Greedfall?

Greedfall is a role-playing game developed by the AA studio Spiders and published by Focus Home Interactive. The game takes place in a fantastical world filled with different types of magic and holds many mysteries, and players take on the role of a diplomat sent to the New World to establish diplomatic relations with the native tribes and find a cure for a deadly plague that has spread across the so called Old World.
One of the strengths of Greedfall is its world-building.

The game has a rich and detailed setting, with a deep history and lore that players can uncover as they progress through the game. The graphics are also top-notch, with beautifully rendered environments, character models, and cutscenes that bring the world to life. I truly enjoyed the setting and the visuals are absolutely stunning if your can max out the settings.

Another highlight of Greedfall is its combat system, which is fast-paced and strategic. You can choose to fight using swords, firearms, or magic, and you can also recruit companions to help you in battle. The game also offers a range of choices and consequences, allowing players to make decisions that impact the outcome of the story. It's not for everyone but the majority of people who played loved it.

Character customization is deep enough for an RPG game and I have no issues with it. Firearms are pretty overpowered, however a bit expensive to maintain until you figure out the game economics. You can block nearly everything. Yes, even that attack. And that one...

However, the game is not without its flaws. The character animations can be stiff and clunky at times, and the game's performance can be uneven, with occasional frame rate drops and bugs. Additionally, the game's writing and voice acting can be hit or miss, with some characters being well-developed and others being flat and uninteresting. Furthermore;

-\tThere is too much walking! Again take this from a player that doesnt normally play RPG games, there is an aspect of fast traveling and in order to do so, you need to discover different campfires and villages. Some quests have you run back and fourth several times and its generally repetitive.

-\tThe main story is somewhat boring and falls slightly short of the side quests in my opinion. I found myself doing and enjoying the side quest more than the main story, only really did the main quests to unlock more side missions.

-\tNow there are some bugs and some quest wont fully show up on the maps but if you paid attention to the conversations then you shouldnt have any issues with them.

Overall, Greedfall is a solid role-playing game that offers a rich and immersive world to explore, exciting combat, and plenty of player choice. While it may not be perfect, it is definitely worth playing for fans of the genre.

My biggest tip for you when you first play this game is to dont look up best weapons or best armor. Sit back and enjoy the game for you should only experience the game by yourself. The replayability is absolutely there. If you are replaying it for a second time, just grind levels in the Coin Arena using the 5/2 (you will know what this means when you play the game) if you lack good weapons or if you cant do the 5/3 in less time than 5/2.\t\t\t\t\t
