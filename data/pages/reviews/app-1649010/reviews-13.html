\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI came to Miasma Chronicles after finishing and loving Mutant Year Zero.

While Miasma (and Mutant Year Zero) is a Turn-Based Tactical game, it's significantly more approachable than Xcom games. This is incredibly story focused, where you work through the game in real-time, with snippets of Turn-Based activities. Both games are incredibly focused on stealth, with the game being almost impossible unless you move around and take out some enemies before actually beginning the battle with the remaining enemies. Battles are a natural part of the environment, and you aren't "preparing your loadout" before each battle.

I've waited to play Miasma Chronicles until a few patches came out, and had very few bugs (except a weird one crashing the final battle when using my REWASD configured PS5 controller). I also played at the higher difficulty level, which I enjoyed a lot mainly because I came from Mutant Year Zero.

Battles are really more like puzzles. Each enemy is specifically positioned within the environment, and you need to look into different distractions, high/low shielding, and getting up higher. Through the final battle I really felt like I had to think about the limited resource I had, the abilities assigned, and where my characters were before starting the battle.

Similar to their other games, resources are really limited almost to the point of making the game incredibly difficult in the beginning. It remains difficult, and can definitely put people off if you're used to normal turn-based games.

One of the hardest things to get past is that you get very, very little experience from battling enemies. Experience comes from completing quests and finding the hidden items. It took me a while to realize that after each battle it made no different in my experience. Then I started clearing out the side quests and suddenly rocketed my levels up.

Pros
- When a character dies, they come back to life when the battle ends, or can be healed mid-battle (good since there are limited characters)
- Great, linear story with orchestrated battles that feel like puzzles
- Really unique characters with distinct build options
- Enemies don't respawn, making it impossible to "farm" resources

Cons
- Really difficult almost from the start
- Resources are pretty scarce\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t(Spoiler free) Miasma Chronicles is not only a good story and game play, but a worthy addition to the growing "Xcom" style games. It brings some thematic qualities, refinement, and improvement to what mutant year zero created.

The Story strikes a very interesting balance thematically. Its gritty and dark while also preserving some of the best qualities that make us human while still acknowledging the needs that we feel that sometimes conflict with our altruistic nature. Each of the main characters are deeper then they might seem initially and with time open up as bonds are forged and secrets revealed. All this makes the story very relatable for the player in my opinion.

Game play, if you like xcom or mutant year zero, you'll like this enough said. If you haven't here's what i'll tell you. Its a turn based grid strategy game where some of the most important parts of combat are how you prepare for it. It may challenge you, leaving you frustrated, but it always feels rewarding when you get the hang of it and have your "A Team" moment when the plan comes together.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWatching U-tube videos of this game I found it was extremely interesting and entertaining, which led to the purchase of this game.
Unfortunately, the actual game dynamics are wholly eccentric and do not play well with other aspects of the game. The combat mechanisms are nothing like the XCOM features aside from being turn based and cover oriented, which limits damage. The control configuration on the PC keyboard are exceptionally difficult to understand and memorize, not to mention that the tutorials are essentially garbage and do not give actual instructions on how to manipulate the players actions, like which button to press to go into ambush mode, etc, which has previously led me to refund games.
After purchasing Miasma Chronicles which I regretted for obvious reasons. For one the game lacks a lot of the attractiveness and game-play ability of both XCOM and Dishonored, two of my favorite games. Miasma lacks continuity and is exceptionally difficult to progress beyond the level of beginner for obvious reasons, We all like a challenge but miasma is much more than a challenge, it is a monstrosity of epic proportions.
There is no way that I, of humble video game nature, will ever be able to play miasma chronicles despite my efforts. It is cool story, with engaging characters and a proper goal, etc. Unfortunately, unless you have a 195 I.Q. and a photographic memory, Miasma Chronicles is probably not a good game for you. Let us not forget, that we are talking about games, not life solutions here,
I am revising my Review after playing for 15 more hours, I am still quite inexperienced at this game and I still have some misgivings about the mechanics of this game. Nevertheless, I am progressing and I have discovered much by essentially trial and error. I feel like some of the voice acting is crude and uninspiring, saying nothing about the typecast stereotypes, which is a little condescending and annoying. The story-line is a bit strange but at least it's not another zombie hack and slash rehash. Thank God!!
The combat mechanics are somewhat bizarre, and nothing like XCOM and the genius simplicity of that Rock-God of a title. It is nowhere near that good, however, there are some cool nuances in this game that are attractive and enticing, I love the fact that a player can snipe enemies on the down low and thin the herd prior to engaging in all out existential combat. The tricks of the trade are brilliant.
What I fear at the end will most assuredly be too short of a game which I struggled through for the first 12 hours only to find myself struggling through the entire game. However, I will have to leave the rest for a future rendition.
After finishing the game, I realized it was far too short for the money I spent. I still haven't completely grasped the nuances of the controls and they oftentimes seem to lock up or lag in execution. I also spent a good deal of time trying to mend my accidental movement mistakes for pressing 'E' instead of 'W' on the keyboard. Furthermore, I do believe that supplying more game revenue, in this case it is "plastic". and revising the cost of certain weapons and other items would have made this game a bit more enjoyable. All in all it was decent, but for the hours played, it was hardly worth it. I have over 1,000 hrs on XCOM, and despite how old it is getting, I still occasionally play. I will probably play this again maybe a couple more times, but I fear it will be then forgotten like many other games.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI give this game a 7/10. I would probably rank it higher if I wasn't comparing it to the previous game from the same developer, Mutant: Year Zero. Overall, I feel like this game was built to protect the player from up too badly, which unfortunately takes away a lot of the freedom that made the previous game enjoyable. I think this game had a ton of potential, but it suffered from uneven pacing and a predictable plot. Still, it is a pretty tightly designed game that does what it wants to do well, and doesn't overstay it's welcome.

One other thing that bothered me on a meta level is that this game relies on the same basic joke that M:YZ relied on, which is that characters in a post-apocalyptic setting misinterpret artifacts from pre-apocalypse times. It's basically Ariel from the Little Mermaid thinking a fork is a hair comb, and then that same joke over and over for an entire game. Sometimes it's really funny, like the line about the founding fathers fighting dinosaurs, but it gets old after a while. Normally this wouldn't be a big deal, but a narrative game like this sinks or swims based on the humor and storytelling, so I think it's relevant. If you never played M:YZ, I can also see this humor staying fresh longer.

Pros:
-The character writing is solid and funny. The characters have unique personalities and good dialogue, and the voice acting is quite believable. I wish the Mayor got more screentime; the main character is kind of bland.

-The art and environments are great, although I think there could have been more variety in locations.

-I like the stealth gameplay and exploration. However, engaging with this mechanic forces you into certain builds. Enemy AI is very basic and enemies have very predictable patrols. Would have liked to see more distraction options, stealth powers, ways to pit enemies against each other, etc.

-The turn-based combat is solid with some unique interpretations of familiar firearms that incentivize you to reposition enemies.

Cons:
-I didn't like the exploration as much as M:YZ. In that game, most of the areas in the game weren't locked. You could progress through a bunch of sections, stealthing around enemies with much higher levels than you, and gather powerful resources that would help you a lot. It felt like you weren't "supposed" to do this, which made it feel like you were getting away with something, which was a lot of fun and made the stealth mechanic seem worthwhile. Abusing Dux's flight ability to traverse the map was also a ton of fun in that game.
The exploration in this game is more parceled out by progressing the story. Areas are locked until you reach the appropriate story milestone. Strong enemies that are higher levels than you hang out in their little corners of the map and you don't have to engage with them until you're ready. There are never really enemies that are so powerful that you have to go our of your way to avoid them (except for one memorable encounter in the swamp - that was great!). There are a few strong enemies at the very end of the game, but they are just reskins of enemies you've seen already, with too much HP. Not really scary in the same way the big-ass, fast robots were in M:YZ

-Less party variety. The main character Elvis and his robot brother Diggs are always in the party. Eventually, you can put one more person in the party. For me, I literally played through the entire game with the sniper character as my third party member because she had the best stealth ambush abilities, which are basically required if you want to use the stealth mechanic at all and pick off enemies to make fights more manageable. M:YZ let you have any combination of 3 characters in your party - I think there were 5 or 6 in total, which allowed for more experimentation and varied playstyles.

-The actual plot structure. I think the characters were written well, but the plot itself is so generic. They could have taken this in a lot of different directions, and it's just another "you are the chosen one and you have to save humanity." A little third-act twist doesn't change the basic structure. The main character abruptly does something stupid, and then changes his mind for no real reason a short while later. M:YZ had such a cool plot! You were a weird duck or pig monster, and you were trying to figure out why. That was an engaging mystery.

-The pacing - It feels like you spend half of the game in the tutorial area fighting frogs. By the time the world opens up and you start getting some lore, the game is almost over.

-The magic system. Spells cost too much MP (electricity) to execute. For most of the game, you can only use maybe one or two spells before you have to use a consumable, or skip a bunch of combat turns to recharge your batteries. This led me to hoard battery consumables in case I needed them later. The spells are also just weird. You can pick up an enemy and throw it at another enemy, but this doesn't do any damage unless you equip a modification chip that inflicts a status effect, but makes the spell cost more to cast. You get a spell that allows you to mind-control an enemy, but by the time you get it, all of the enemies in the game are immune to the spell? You get a teleport spell, that is like the pick up an enemy spell, but cheaper. The only spells I used for the entire game were the ones that let you reposition enemies, which I used in conjunction with the spell to summon an explosive barrel. Summon barrel, move enemy next to barrel, shoot enemy and barrel with a shotgun. Worked in every fight in the entire game.

- The last fight of the game - you have to crowd control a powerful enemy while fighting a bunch of other enemies. If you do not, this enemy will stunlock 1 or 2 of your three characters every turn. Don't have any of the 3 abilities that let you crowd control this enemy? I guess it's time to reload and respec your characters (which is free, thankfully). The powerful enemy cannot be killed, but the game doesn't tell you this. The enemy just returns to full HP after every time you attack it. I honestly could not tell if this was a bug or not the first time it happened. Just annoying and dumb.

-Bha Madi - She was just insufferable. Shut up, Bha Madi,\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tONE OF THE MOST UNFAIR REVIEWS I'VE EVER READ

I am a turn-based strategy RPG buff. I've played dozens of X-COM likes, and I believe I can tell if a game is good or not. Miasma Chronicles is a good game. I would give it a 4/5. Solid gameplay, beautiful graphics, great setting, interesting characters and even a compelling story (which is something very rare in the genre - probably the best one I have seen so far, if we don't count The Banner Saga). Sure, it lacks some polish, and the main guy is not the most memorable, but I had a blast playing through it.

So I was very surprised when I saw that Eurogamer gave Miasma Chronicles a baffling 1/5 rating. That's the rating you give to a completely broken game, with horrible gameplay and forgettable story - which Miasma Chronicles objectively is not.

As I went through the review, it did not take me long to realise that the reviewer was clearly blinded by some kind of extremist "D&I" ideology ("wokeness", some would call it).

The reviewer was particularly bothered by two things:

1 - She considered the sidekick character to be a racist trope. That's because he is a tanky sassy/brash robot, voiced by a black actor, who is very loyal to his white friend (to the point that they consider themselves brothers), but does not get much credit for their feats. First of all, just because a robot uses black vernacular, it doesn't make that character black - it's a damn robot! Second, how is having a black sidekick racist? Sidekicks are omnipresent in fiction; it's not a crime if one happens to be black, as long as not all black characters are sidekicks. Plus, the supposedly "bigoted stereotype" robot sidekick at hand is easily the coolest character in the game. The reviewer also mentions that this robot descends from a slave race, but this parallel does not hold. Robots (obviously) used to serve humans in the early days, when they were not fully sentient. Some of these non-fully sentient robots can be spoken with in the game, but they don't have "black" accents or anything like that. Sure, the game's story revolves around political tensions between humans and robots, but so what? That is a very common trope in sci-fi, and actually works as social commentary. Plus, the game has plenty of human NPCs of colour (literally more than half of the protagonist's home town). Some of them also have very noble roles, such as the protagonist's mother, or the town militia leader. As for robots, they are voiced by a great number of different actors, presumably from all ethnicities.

2 - Apparently there's a "throwaway transphobic line in a diary entry about the author not caring if drugs cause them to grow boobs". I haven't even read that line, as diaries are very secondary collectibles in a game that took me almost 40 hours to finish, with thousands of perfectly fine voice lines. codes entries and collectible diaries. Clearly, a single diary by a random NPC doesn't make the entire game or its message transphobic.

That's it. Those are the reasons. The reviewer does go through gameplay, which she curiously considers much worse than the previous game by the same developer (which she liked). That's curious, because the two games are in fact very similar in terms of gameplay, as noted in practically every other review out there.

It's so sad to see a game made with so much love fall victim to a reviewer (who describes herself as a "fat, disabled trans woman") fueled by some kind of rampaging self-righteous obsession with "inclusivity" that really had little to no role to play here.\t\t\t\t\t
