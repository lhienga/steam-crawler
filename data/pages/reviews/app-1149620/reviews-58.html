\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTalk about a game that's way better than it should be. I picked up GSS assuming it would be a delightful jank-fest I could play for a half dozen hours and move on. What I got was a fairly janky but surprisingly competent mini-game-based, story-driven management game. The game is mostly just a series of small tasks, ring up goods, pump gas, etc... played through simple mini-games. What the game does shockingly well is pacing and escalation: while some mini-games remain fairly static, adding new parts and facilities adds complexity over time to the point that hiring your first employee hits that perfect sweet spot: you check off *one* thing you don't want to do, or can even have *something* running while you do non-business related tasks. It shows an excellent grasp of why this loop is so engaging and provides something devoutly desired: freedom from a task, while also adding it's own extra layer of management.

So yeah, I was genuinely surprised. It's not just a fun little janky time waster, it's actually a pretty well-designed game in its own right and manages the rare feat of escaping the "simulator" brand of "core gameplay loop ... we didn't know how to expand from there." If you like these styles of games, this is definitely one of the best I've played, ranking well above Hardspace, PCBS and Power Wash Simulator for it's much stronger story and gameplay.\t\t\t\t\t
