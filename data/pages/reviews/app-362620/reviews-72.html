\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a very well made game that has everything going for it as a tycoon game but I didn't really find it fun. It has so many deep levels to it, from the different levels of software production, to contracting, to office space management, to managing teams of programmers, lawyers, the air conditioning vents. And while that level of depth is what makes it different to other software dev type games - it's what ultimately detracts from its playability.

You see the issue with this game is the fact that it's too realistic. Tracey is complaining about the noise from her colleauges computer, this person doesn't get on with Tracey, this project is behind schedule. This person is stressed because they have nothing to do. There isn't a vending machine. There isn't enough pot plants. The floor is dirty! This computer broke down. The headaches and levels of issues are too problematic.

If you do enjoy and relate to endless micromanagement and being an anal kind of boss then you probably will enjoy this game. But the general vibe I got from this was how to whip several people who don't want to be there, into enough shape to release some software that the public complains about and doesn't like. All the while managing, lots of microscopic issues and the heating system not working.

The learning curve is too steep and complex and depth is too much to be fun. It got on my nerves. Well done on the developers for making a game with masterfully artistic levels of realism. But that is ultimately it's downfall and why I don't want to play it any more.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAcross the board, better than any of it's competitors.

You start as the single employee of your own software company, and you can choose to make a wide variety of software products. You can do it the simple way (just choose a balance of features that you think will appeal) or the complicated way.

  • Design it.
  • Develop it (Alpha).
  • Fix the bugs (Beta), or make sure you have a decent support team to deal with the complaints and fix/patch them afterwards.
  • Market it and release it.
  • Order physical copies (or print your own)

The more products you release in a category, the more followers/reputation/recognition you get, and the higher your future sales for those products goes.

Later on, you hire more people and split out the tasks - but there's no point in hiring 50 programmers for one product, they'll just get in each other's way. You have to manage your teams carefully at first, but if you hire the right leader they can hire, train, and even take over the whole development cycle for new products, including marketing & support.

Other things you can do:
  • Research tech levels (by year) to develop cutting-edge products, and/or patent innovations to earn royalties
  • Choose which OS to release products for, which editors to use to make them
  • Develop your own OS and 2D/3D/Audio editors to corner the market, or keep them in-house to keep the cutting-edge tools to yourself
  • Set up your own software (physical) printing
  • Hardware manufacturing/production lines for smartphones or consoles
  • Build your own buildings, or an entire campus to house your employees

It goes on. The depth to this game is just stunning and it puts other similar games to shame. 9.9/10 - the only teeny tiny gripe I have is that marketing feels a little wonky, but zero bugs or other issues which is amazing for a game of this price, made by one person, and not even finished yet.

Love it.\t\t\t\t\t

Early game is super stressful, trying to stay afloat and keep from going bankrupt with your minimalist team, rushing release after release while pumping out contract work to fill in the financial gaps. Always at the whims of the publisher in terms of deadlines. Every failure is a catastrophe, and on the run up to a launch, you're on the edge of your seat. Every success is glorious and you jump for joy and then sigh in relief.

Mid game is very technical, trying to min-max every way you can, trying to keep competitive with the AI. Form a solid strategy as early as you can and be very careful about how many of each role you have on your development teams. Keep inflating your support and marketing teams as needed to keep up with your releases. Maybe start manufacturing, start a server farm, research and patent with a legal team. Anyway, make a strategy (there are quite a lot of options in how you want to build your company) and carefully implement it.

Late game is all about executing your strategies well and doing everything you can to corner your markets and outpace the AI in profits and development. End your founders life with the best ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ company you can and die a software and/or hardware god.

That's how I have been playing the game anyway. It's absolutely AMAZING!!! Heartily recommend. Just fantastic. Can't wait to see what the game is like once the super-dev-man finishes his major content updates and balances the game to his vision.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game suffers from too much complexity in it's UI. It's not that it's difficult to play, it's just tedious. It has too many menus and sub menus that you have to navigate to do even the most basic things. This is a problem with many modern management games. The developers over-engineer the interfaces so even doing really basic stuff ends up being a task of remembering where everything is while you left click to open one menu, then navigate to a tab and click to open another screen that you have to right click on to open the context menu... It isn't fun to feel like your playing a multi-page spreadsheet.

The makers of new management games really should go back and play some of the old classics like Sim Earth, Sim City or Civilization to learn how to make a good user interface that isn't tedious and doesn't require the user to remember where stuff is among pages and pages of extraneous information.

I'm sure it's a good game, but I lost interest during the tutorial because of the rubbish UI. And for this I cant recommend it.\t\t\t\t\t
