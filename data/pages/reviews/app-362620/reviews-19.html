\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI like playing this game, but it also irritates me a bit. The game is called Software Inc., but I feel like I spend more time building than anything else. Everything takes several steps. There is a high degree of customization, but it comes at the cost of tedium.

Once you make a couple of decent pieces of software the game loses all sense of difficulty as money is no longer an object (within reason). In 2002, I'm turning a profit of $1M a month with 100+ employees and can just build and hire whatever. This was playing on medium difficulty with default settings and 1 founder with 75% creativity.

There are a lot of systems in this game, but when I started playing this game it was confusing and the tutorials do an okay job at explaining things.

My biggest complaint with the game is the lack of challenge. You're developing software competing against other AI companies, but the software design process is lacking. The default Simple mode will more or less set you up for failure as you don't know what the market trends are or have a choice in what operating systems you want to support. Early on, more operating systems increase Alpha stage time and can ruin your publishing deals. In Advanced mode you get more control over the OS that you support and what the current market trends are for that software you are developing.

This is where I take issue with the design process. Over time the market will become over saturated in 1 or 2 categories for each type of software. So the reasonable thing to do is develop a software targeting the under saturated category(ies). The problem with this is that you may be developing a sequel product (i.e., My Office v2) but because Presentations are popular now you have to lower the focus on presentations on your sequel (the tooltip for the graphs specially says lower is better on the trend analysis when designing new software). The only way to do this is strip out features that were part of your previous software and/or framework.

Why I think this is a problem is that for all that it tries to do to emulate a software firm, it waters down the core part of the game into balancing 3 software-dependent categories to make a great selling product. I would like it better if the software design, for sequels, required you to carry over features from the previous iteration. What I think should also be added is that over time certain features should be expected or else it will be a poor selling product. As the game currently is, the only challenge is having enough staff to pump out the software and support it.\t\t\t\t\t
