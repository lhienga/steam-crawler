\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a surprisingly deep, complex and fun software business simulation game that's worth a shot if you're the type of person who likes the idea of earning a profit and growing a business.

There's a lot to do in this game. Despite playing it for 30 hours, I still feel like a beginner. There are interesting and varied paths to success, there's a great building editor, and automation to reduce micromanagement. There's a surprising amount of joy to be had when you get all your ducks in a row, a major release goes well, and you see sales numbers you never thought possible before. If you enjoy economic games, you'll probably like this. Making good use of a visionary designer, or simply achieving the milestone of being able to buy out your competitors, also feels great. There are lots of great gameplay experiences here that you have as you figure things out.

The main downside is that this game has a tough learning curve, and more tutorials than a swarm of bees. Don't try to tackle all tutorials all at once. Just do the tutorial for a menu when you need to actually start using that menu. Do start your first game on Easy difficulty. There's a lot to learn, and its easy to lose track of how much employees cost while trying to release your first product. If in doubt, start the game with as many founders as you can, since they don't use facilities and you don't have to pay them. Make liberal use of the pause button, and be prepared to re-design your building regularly as your needs change.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tVery fun game.
I like how much variety there is. You can't just start a software company, but also a game company, a hardware factory, a call centre, and much more. You can create tons of different types of software too, and work from a pre-built office or build your own. You can also acquire competitors, invest in rare metals and use off-shore accounts to dodge taxes, trade in the stock market and more.

I even tried to start a power plant after unlocking solar panels, which went great, until a fire broke out and I didn't have the money to repair them, causing my company to go bankrupt.

Just like when I misjudged my manufacturing capabilities and took a way too big order, leading to a 20 million dollar fine (which lead to me being bankrupt again).

I am now wondering if it would be possible to start a financial company. Like, if you just take out a huge loan at the start, and then instantly invest it in a stock that's going to go up, and only hire accountants and lawyers to keep taxes low. I might try that one next.\t\t\t\t\t
