\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAfter Assassin's Creed: Origins I honestly didn't think any of the 'new generation' of Assassin's Creed games could get any worse than that.
I was wrong, of course.

Assassin's Creed: Valhalla .. despite the beautiful world creation, the great character story and general historical attention to detail .. fails to hit the amazing high standard of Assassin's Creed: Odyssey in several ways.

Mostly small things, but also important things, such as the inability to craft arrows, but also the poor combat mechanics, some movement mechanics and the stupidly hard fights with ridiculously powered enemies at low level.
Included in the extended list of poor ideas in this game are .. the lack of any personal storage for old gear and armour, or any ability to deconstruct old items for materials, and somebody had the incredibly stupid idea of creating a set of gear for the collectors edition that has a fire effect .. in a world where nearly everything is made out of wood and straw and then making fire damage one of the worst kinds of damage you can sustain over time, so setting fire to yourself and everything around you becomes normal.

The Ration/Health system has to be the single worst and most stupid health system I've ever encountered in any game, ever.
When a game sets out to seriously limit health regeneration items found in situations, like Raids, when you most need them, especially to heal members of your own crew, so you can't actually heal yourself and the crew, this kind of deliberate stacking the odds so high against the player means there is a 'Fail' mechanic built into the very base of the game, which is both an insult and stupid game design.
A challenge is one thing, but making it so you cannot do the things you need to succeed by having NONE of the things that can create that scenario, where you have to run around looking for healing items for yourself and your crew and finding NONE, means the game design is made by morons.

The so-called 'Stealth' system in this game is utter sh*t.

The so-called melee 'Combat' system in this game is utter sh*t.

I got the Complete Edition in a sale, for £30.00 .. and I thought I got it at a bargain, but considering the things I've said and my ongoing experience (if I buy a game, I like to play and finish it, especially if the story is good enough) .. I have to say this game isn't worth £3.00.
£2.50 for the great story and world design .. and £0.49p for everything else, including the unresolved crashes.

This is not a AAA game, at best it's a B+ .. and that's for the lovely world and historical details, nothing else.

There is only one Assassin's Creed game worth the money and worth playing .. Assassin's Creed: Odyssey.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPlayed and loved both Origins and Odyssey for hundreds of hours. This doesn't match up. It's a step backwards in design, and a step downwards in quality.

The facial animations and character designs are less high-quality than anything I've seen in a mainline AC game frankly since Unity. None of the emotiveness or personality is visible from the last three games. Free-running and climbing is somehow less intuitive, leading to me falling for no reason and getting stuck mid-run on random scenery - an occurrence I thought had been left behind in modern AC.
Keybindings have been moved around for no reason (mounting and dismounting a horse is now on separate buttons. Why?) while the inventory and gear system has been condensed for fewer pieces, yet leaves you more confused because it displays even less helpful information.

The talent tree is god-awful, and since you start the game with it half-hidden, it's basically stumbling around in the dark and clicking on things to hope that you eventually reach an ability. How are you meant to design a playstyle for your character when every choice is a random lottery of potential next skill-ups?

I actually don't have any comments on the story, which is a shame, because I'd been fascinated by the story of the last two. There's some emo Viking who is sad because their dad died, and has no interesting character traits beyond that. There's nothing that matches the level of the brooding and intense love of Bayek, or the cocky self-assuredness of Kassandra/Alexios.

The fighting - the whole draw of the Assassin's Creed premise, post-Origins - is lacklustre and unintuitive. It's impossible to learn how to dodge or parry at the right time, because the visual cues given by enemies don't actually match the timing of their attacks hitting. Odyssey started you off with a cool ability to make your character feel impactful - Eivor has none of this, and instead clunkily throws an axe around, hoping to hit. But again, there's almost no indication of whether an enemy is blocking. Sometimes I chain four-hits. Sometimes the same enemy knocks me over and kills me before I can respond.

Valhalla tries to bring back some features of older games, which frankly I thought we'd consigned to the dungheap for good reason. Like chasing after a page fluttering in the wind, or playing badly-implemented minigames against overtuned AI. Frankly, Ubisoft should apologise for any of these making it past the drawing board, never mind being in the final game. The dire absence of naval combat, which Odyssey had polished to perfection, makes this downgrade in non-main story activities even more humiliating.

I made it 6 and a half hours through this slog before giving up and uninstalling, and still didn't manage to leave Norway. Apparently there's an England bit later?

It's an immense shame to see such a fumble from what was one of the highest-quality ARPG's going, one that with Origins redefined itself and the genre. Much like the real-world story after Desmond's death, Assassin's Creed seems to be losing direction.\t\t\t\t\t
