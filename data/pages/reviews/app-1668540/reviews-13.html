\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo, this game takes place after the events of Trails of Cold Steel 4. Crossbell is about to finally declare it‚Äôs independence, but a new threat is rising and it‚Äôs up to our heroes to save the day again!

If you are familiar with the series, there is not a whole lot new here. The game takes place in and around the Crossbell area. Unlike the other games there are 3 distinct paths that you must play, and you must swap between the 3 different paths to progress. 1 path is Rean Schwarzer and mostly Thors students and alums, another path is based around Lloyd Bannings and the SSS, and the last is made up of some old and new faces but focuses on the character named ‚ÄúC‚Äù (it‚Äôs a surprise!).

There are 3 new playable characters that all appear in the 3rd path mentioned above. Swin and Nadia who are some types of assassins and a lovable new character called Lapis. I would rank Lapis as SSS+ tier, Nadia as A tier and Swin as F tier, I just sort of find him Forgettable.

Overall, this game has an insane number of playable characters. I didn‚Äôt count but it‚Äôs probably around 40 or so. Almost every playable character from every game is here, minus a few from Trails in the Sky series. This is both a blessing and a curse. It‚Äôs fun to run some old comps and mix them up but honestly it also just feels daunting. Luckily the game makes it relatively easy to swap out characters by granting you potions you can use to give any character XP. When the end game rolls around you can really field anyone you want. The number of XP potions is not enough to upgrade everyone in the roster, but you should be able to put together a sizable team with plenty of options.

The game is also not really that grindy as far as JRPGs go. There is a place called the Reverie Corridor that opens partway through the game where you can run dungeons. It‚Äôs like the old schoolhouse in Trails of Cold Steel 1. You also find gems that unlock other things like side stories, minigames, and gear. It feels rather rewarding overall. You only must go through the first 4 stratums to complete the main story and you can grind the rest after completing the main story if you want.

The main story has good pacing, and the story moves along in typical Legend of Heros fashion. The game feels really streamlined. It would probably take you around 60-65 hours to play through the main story, and I never felt bored.

After the main story there is still a good 20ish hours of game play, if you want it. You can grind the rest of the Reverie Corridor and unlock more characters, side stories, and mini games. This is not really required; there is a mini story baked into the rest of Reverie Corridor but it‚Äôs nothing special and probably will not have any real impact on future games.

Considering the number of characters and locations in the game you should be warned that this is not a great first entry into the series. It would be like picking up a long running show like Stargate SG-1 in season 6. You can totally play the game without knowing all the backstory and characters. However, to really appreciate the game it is required to play ALL THE PREVIOUS GAMES. That is a lot to ask. There are 9 previous games that stretch 60-100 hours EACH. Trails of Cold Steel 1-4 feel basically required. Following that up the Crossbell arc games, Trails from Zero and Trails to Azure, are also highly recommended. The Trails in the Sky 1-3 are the least important, but also there are many characters from those games in this game so very much recommended.

If I have any real disappointment, there are some old favorites like Scherazard and Princess Kloe that hail back from Trials in the Sky. They make minor appearances but are not playable. I guess I just find it odd considering they have models and voice acting but not playable.

This game was fantastic, from the characters to the story to the pacing. The port is well done and I didn‚Äôt have any issues. I would rank it as one of the best games in the entire series. In short it gets a very high recommendation!\t\t\t\t\t

I played all Trails series. The Crossbell arc is the best follow by ColdSteel 1. After that, these series just keep falling of the cliff

Story
Not sure why we need this series. This episode try to bring together all the cast in CS1-4 and Azure/Zero plus some from Liberl and 3 new chars. Yes, it is one bloated party and there are 40 playable story char with 10 more locked only for leveling hub.

This make every screens filled with 4-20 chars, plenty of weird greeting in the mist of serious conversation.
It is just too crowded. There is also less development on these chars because they just tag along to your party, trying to solve an immediate crisis compare to small party of 6 going for long journey.

The negative point i have in CS2-4 is still appear here. No one died despite having a war and bullet is flying left and right. It actually surprise me when one of the character can be injured by a bullet but of course, no one die in this game, she get saved in the end. All these plot armor just remove any tension of the crisis.

I miss the darker tone we have in Trail in theSky 1-3. I still remember how dark Renne backstory in Sky 3- gate15.
All those stuff is gone now, it all happy ending and friendship overcome everything.

In the end, entire crisis resolved and nothing changed to original plot.


Gameplay
Even the gameplay is suffered because you have to rotate between several set chars due to story. As you get more Tier 3 Quartz, you will notice how hard it is to unlock the slots for all character. There is also S-link, which you usually stuck at lv3-5 if you keep swapping chars.

Because the story focus on 3 different character at the same time, there is no room to level up like the old titles. So they added a leveling hub where you can get everyone together just to level up. Of course everyone memories mysteriously sealed off because they not allow to talk about plots and story development in this hub, they not suppose to meet in real world.

Overall, i still enjoy this part a bit. I like playing different char and customizing their quartz. But i'm sure this is not for everyone.



Conclusion
I guess the goal of this game is just fan-service, trying to pull every character together for make up story so you can enjoy using them a bit more.
If you are thirst for more Trails games, you should give it a try. You won't miss much of the story if you skip this. Buy it on discount if you can, it really not worth the full price.

Updated:
After finish post content: The side story (DayDream) similar like gate in CS3 is pretty good. All the character get story development/conclusion there. But my opinion on the bloated character in main story still remain. I hope they go back to smaller cast in next series. Just let previous cast move on with their life.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe first thing to know about this game is that it is the 10th game in a series which started with Trails in the Sky, First Chapter, and that each game in the series builds upon its predecessors. As such, this is not a game to start with, and this review will assume the reader has played all the preceding games and will freely spoil those games.

Trails into Reverie is the epilogue to Cold Steel IV and Trails to Azure, set six months after the end of Cold Steel IV, and showing the aftermath of the Great Twilight and Crossbell‚Äôs struggle for independence, which is once again stymied by forces unforeseen, resulting in a new, but all too familiar situation that quickly escalates into (yet) another calamity which could very well have catastrophic consequences for Zemuria, and of course it is up to our Heroes to save the day. In itself, this is a good anime/JRPG story, full of dramatic reveals and epic moments and such. Where it falters is as a part of the larger Trails narrative.

Unlike the previous calamity, the Great Twilight of Cold Steel IV, there is next to no build-up to this one. The Great Twilight was the culmination of events that, for the player, started no later than Trails to Azure, or even Trails in the Sky the 3rd, and was the result of the deliberate machinations of multiple powerful individuals and groups. The situation in this game, on the other hand, is the result of what amounts to an unfortunate happenstance, without ultimate agency, reason or meaning. What‚Äôs more, the entire story (minus the prologue), from peace and stability to the impending apocalypse and back to peace and stability once more, takes place in little over a week, giving the event a strangely random air, as if such things ‚Äòjust happen‚Äô in this world. In addition, while the magitechnobabble that is supposed to explain things is vaguely plausible given what we already know from earlier games, certain crucial parts of it have flimsy rationales at best, creating the effect that ‚Äòanything goes‚Äô, and weakening the coherence of the worldbuilding that Trails is known for.

Structurally, this game is rather disjointed. Instead of following one group of characters centered around a single main PC, this game has you following 3 different groups, with three different main characters, Rean, Lloyd, and the mysterious ‚ÄòC‚Äô. These three groups each have their own storylines as part of the larger narrative, coming together only in the finale, though they do intertwine here and there when they meet or otherwise interact with each other. You can switch between the stories, but only to an extent; when 2 groups are going to interact, you will have to wait with progressing one storyline until you‚Äôve advanced far enough in the other one.

The different storylines themselves are of various quality. Lloyd‚Äôs story is the weakest, being little more than a repetition of the Finale of Trails to Azure. On the positive side, the story deconstructs the concept of Heroes, which is refreshing. On the negative side, the only response the characters seem to have is to constantly affirm their belief in The Power of Friendship instead. This has, of course, always been a part of Trails, but never to even remotely such a degree as here, and it seriously weakens the characters and turns them into caricatures of what they were before.

Rean‚Äôs story is much better, addressing various consequences of the fallout of Erebonia‚Äôs attempt at world domination in Cold Steel IV. The worst thing that can be said about it is that a large part of its plot feels like a secondary plot to the main storyline, but even so, it is worth it for giving closure to the Erebonian side of events. More importantly, Rean‚Äôs involvement in the events of the larger story allow him to face, and overcome, some significant personal demons, showing his development as a person and establishing him as a well-adjusted and emotionally mature young adult.

By far the best storyline, however, is that of ‚ÄòC‚Äô. In order to avoid spoilers, not much can be said about its plot, characters, dialogue and such, but all of these are unquestionably up there with the very best Trails has offered in the last ten games. It is heartwarming and tearjerking, yet also serious and philosophical, not to mention darkly humorous and mischievously twisted. One thing to note is that two of the characters in this group are the protagonists from the Three and Nine books that could be found in Cold Steel IV, and that it is advisory to read, or reread, those books before playing.

But that is not all there is to the story of this game. In addition to the main story and the three storylines that embody it, there is also a postgame story, as well as a large number of side stories. The postgame story can‚Äôt really be discussed because of spoilers, suffice to say that it is in itself a short, but emotionally powerful tale. The side stories, for their part, are too many and too varied to be discussed in detail either. What can be said about them is that the general quality of these stories is quite good, being some combination of entertaining, interesting, endearing and/or moving.

The game mechanics & combat of Trails into Reverie are essentially the same as those from Cold Steel IV. There are a few new types of Quartz and Master Quartz, but otherwise the only new thing during fights is United Fronts. These are powerful group attacks, magical attacks or heals, which are fueled by the Assault Gauge and which also give the characters stat bonuses, cure ailments and replenish EP, CP and/or BP. As for the rest of the game mechanics, the main thing they have done is just to increase the numbers to everything: Character levels go up to 250 (and its actually feasible to reach that in the postgame), Master Quartz levels go up to 15, Black Zemurian Ore weapons can be enhanced twice more, Quartz and Accessories have an additional level to them, and so on.

One thing that does differentiate this game from the previous ones is the True Reverie Corridor (TRC), which is an alternate dimension where the characters from all 3 storylines end up at various points, though the characters can leave for the real world and return to the TRC at almost any time (except during postgame, which takes place entirely within the TRC), and which has both a hub with shops and healing points and such and a randomized dungeon which can be reset and challenged as often as desired for the sake of gaining levels and items, as well as finding the various minigames, side stories and bonus characters (which can only be used in the TRC). Plotwise, there is a reason for the TRC‚Äôs existence and the characters presence within it, but that reason won‚Äôt be discovered until the postgame and is very much a ‚Äòmagitechnobabble means anything goes‚Äô kind of explanation.

A more practical issue is that, by bringing together all characters from all 3 groups, plus the bonus characters which can be found in the TRC, the available pool quickly swells to 40+, and eventually to a whopping 51 playable characters. Having that many characters inevitably means a lot of them just won‚Äôt stand out or be particularly worthwhile to use, unlike in a smaller group where their presence might actually have a meaningful impact. There are probably a lot of players who enjoy playing with this large cast, but for those who care more about the narrative than the gameplay it just means lots of grinding/farming for levels & gear, even if the game has mitigating measures available.

In the end, I did enjoy playing this game, and I do recommend it to those who have played the previous games in the series. However, the unevenness of the games narrative quality makes it far less good than it could have been. As for the gameplay, it really depends on how one views the TRC and the gameplay it brings. For some, it might be the best thing ever in Trails, for others, it doesn‚Äôt add anything and creates a lot of meaningless chores to make it to the end.\t\t\t\t\t
