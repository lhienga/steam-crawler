Extremely disjointed story (by design) and launched unfinished (2/3 of characters having 7 link-levels of only "Link Attack" which enhanced nothing level over level (this may now be fixed?)). Shorter than all other entries because with the introduction of a pure grinding zone, many historical or interesting plot items were put behind weird and restrictive flashback sequences you have to unlock and play with stock characters. It's also somehow more linear than all other entries.

This entry removed nearly all ability to customize master quartz - you can unlock scant few and requires unlocking some extraneous characters in the grinding zone to be able to swap around as you might like. With the grinding zone, you'll get the opportunity to just run around in circles in identical environments to farm quartz, sepith, and gear to enhance your characters, but not actually swap master quartz for your builds until the game is nearly over.

It really, really feels that these chapters should have ended with CS4. This one felt extremely phoned in.\t\t\t\t\t

The Reverie Corridor is for me a highlight as well, especially the daydreams. Game runs perfectly fine on the Steam Deck.


If you have played the other games, and still wonder if you should get Reverie, the answer is YES!

If you're a newcomer to the franchise, I encourage you to put it on your wishlist until you've at least played the Cold Steel games, and preferably the Crossbell duology as well. The more games in the franchise you've experienced the more you'll get out of this entry.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game should be thought of as a prestige campaign, much to the likes of a DnD prestige campaign. The original campaign was CS 1 to 4; this is the coda campaign. Being a prestige campaign, that means the player can pull out all the stops in designing the most broken character build and fight against the "back of the codex" monsters that were intimidating in earlier games. Additionally, the scope of the campaign can be the most ambitious. Rean and friends have already had their standard 'hero's journey'; this game features the phase of storytelling after the hero's journey. That means the story stakes are even larger than the previous game (which itself was continental in size) and the archvillain has to be even more overwhelming than the previous archvillain. The archvillain and the scope of the conflict is much more metaphysical than in the previous games, which can be a detraction because the previous games were more grounded relative to this one. And the grounded quality gave gravitas. But again, all this ambitious stuff comes with the territory.

The main thrust of this game is surprisingly not the story but instead the gameplay. Indeed, this game features the very best gameplay in the entire CS series. And the addition of a "speedup" option makes battles lightning fast. Because I know every single characters' stats, crafts, and arts like the back of my hand having played the previous 4 CS games, I very much can lull myself into an auto mode and let my fingers fly around the keyboard and execute umpteen battles within moments. Battles very much play like a slot machine, which is designed to reward you constantly with visual and audible stimuli (and not coins) in order to keep you hooked perpetually. That is what this gameplay is like.
The inclusion of more than 50 playable characters would seem bloated to the uninitiated; however, since I've played all 10 games prior to this, I believe it or not know quite well each and every playable character and character mentioned in dialogues. Only once in my entire playthrough did I stop to wonder who this or that person was. And maybe I jogged my memory; I don't recall; he was such a tertiary character from whichever entry. Thus, it is surprisingly easy to keep track of all 50 playable characters because the previous 10 games were my warmup to this one. And leveling each character is a breeze, even when some are 20 levels behind: simply tag the undeveloped characters with the powerful ones, and within 5 battles the undeveloped characters will gain about 99,000 experience and jump several levels in one go.

There is a convincing air of mystery to the story by the beginning. We don't even glimpse the main villain until about 30 hours into the storyline, let alone duel with the main villain even further down. The bloated cast mainly backfires during the main storyline; there are simply too many cooks in the kitchen. The best characters remain the central protagonists Rean, Lloyd, C, and Lapis and friends, and C and Lapis have a the best dynamic. Thus, their scenes and interactions are always the best.
But Rean's pals whom I've gotten to know over 500 previous hours of gameplay are relegated to side comments. In order to include them all in group dialogue scenes, fan favorite characters like Fie, Laura, and Alisa simply have generic comments which anyone else could have said. For example, Emma Milstein is stuck always talking about mana, the higher elements being present in some area, or identifying some magic spell being used. Wow; how predictable. Yes, it makes sense to assign that dialogue to her; but, does she not have anything else or personal to say? And in order to keep Rean's and Lloyd's posse open for all Trails Shipping fans, each lady has generic compliments to them, and nothing is personalized or deeper than surface level. This is disappointing because I for example shipped Rean and Laura over the four previous games (with Fie as a very close second), and all Laura says to Rean is "we should train more!" Gee... real dense stuff there, Laura.

The game's answer to the bloated cast number is the inclusion of 'daydreams', much like the star doors in the Sky the Third game. Sky the Third's cast was much tighter (16 versus 55), and the star door scenes were brilliant writing sequences; many of those scenes I remember vividly how many years afterwards. In the daydreams, two characters typically are featured at a time interacting, which means the scope is much more personal. And the game's writing retains its brilliance in these moments. Scenes where the story zooms in on two are by far the best: C and Lapis, Swin and Nadia, Rean and Lloyd together. Estelle anywhere makes a good scene great; after 10 games, her character retains the exact charm she had from FC. That's staying power!

There is a lot of narrative convenience in this game; and once again, given the bloated cast and the 'prestige campaign' quality of this game, I think much of this comes with the territory. Mostly, lampshading the plot conveniences simply jars my attention out of the game; that is the main detraction. For example, Renne very early on on C's chapter joins Nadia and Swin. And to fans, this makes absolute sense: the three are childhood assassins, and it is natural Renne would join them. However, she herself never describes her past to the Nadia and Swin, which makes it unclear to the two of them why she tagged along. Indeed, Swin asks at one point, "why is she with us?". Hmm... that plot convenience could easily have been ironed out had the writers included a 5 minute scene with Renne describing who she once was and why she identifies with the Miserable Sinners. A missed opportunity. There was a needless disconnect between what the player knows about Renne's past and what the characters themselves knew. It simply distracted me.

In all, the plot itself gets an A minus. It is still very coherent, tightly paced, and organized. The three routes was a novel approach to linear storytelling. When one storyline got too slow paced, I could change perspectives on the fly. And the main campaign was simply too short. It was a great concept, but by the time I had gotten deep into the weeds, well, here's the final boss battle. This contrasts previous entries, which often completed upwards of 120 hours. The 50 hour mark was the real start to the story. And by this 50 hour mark, I was in the final dungeon. Since I have marathon-long expectations of this series, I simply wasn't expecting a 50-hour campaign length. That's not a detraction looking back; it was short and sweet. Compared to other storylines that have A average or A plus writing, this story gets an A minus. Still better than most any other game story I've played, so I'm quite happy with it.\t\t\t\t\t
