\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI highly recommend every game in the Trails series. The greatest strengths in these games for me is the huge cast of characters where each gets a great moment. They do not all get equal treatment, but for me, generally they give the most concentration to those characters that resonate broadly.

In addition, they do a fantastic job populating the world with interesting details and conversations to help provide insight into how NPCs live. In so many ways, despite not having sprawling open world maps, this series manages to feel more lived in than so many open worlds. You can see in the small conversations between people over the course of the many games how their lives are transformed by the events of the series, and how they experience those events differently without knowledge of all that your characters know.

I also find the spirit of the small kingdoms and their desire to be free from oppressive large states a great element from an old-school American perspective.\t\t\t\t\t

Closing West Zemuria arc

Just to give a little background on this, I have already played all games up to Kuro no Kiseki II.
I play these games in Japanese on console at launch and then replay them on PC when the English version releases, usually 3 years later.
I think if anyone has reached this far into the franchise, there's no need for a review talking about why you should play the series or how the game works. It's core mechanics is pretty much the same as CS4.

Trails into Reverie is a game that could only be possible due to everything the series has been building up to now. 10 games on the line with interconnected stories across almost 20 years. Trails into Reverie brings everyone, since the first game, marking a turning point for the series. A final chapter, a departure from Western Zemuria before we go to Calvard and begin a new arc and move to a new era for the series.

This game requires the player to have played all games up to it and all the knowledge and attachment to the characters in order to fully enjoy Reverie. It brings a closure to Rean and Lloyd stories while also adding a new story line of a character named C.
This game actually acts more like a last chance to revisit Erebonia and Crossbell and play with your favorite characters rather than actually setting the ground into Calvard territory (we don't go to Calvard in this game). And everyone is in the game, more than 50 characters to play.

The story in this game is on the shorter side (takes around 50 hours to beat) but it was well paced without padding. It feels more like a spin-off story in Crossbell.
Reverie is actually a new story that feels contained within itself, it begins and ends within itself. Trails into Reverie did not bring any answers of the various questions and mysteries raised in the end of CS4 and did not bring us into Calvard territory but it does give 3 episodes that explain a little bit about what's next for Calvard regarding some of the characters and 1 episode that teases a little bit about Ouroboros Final Plan and Grandmaster.
Reverie is really a "last chance" game to reuse all assets of the previous games before diving into a new era of the series. At least for now when finishing the game, maybe it will pay off in the future Kuro games when the consequences of what happened here starts to appear in the future (I already know the answer, just teasing and keeping spoiler free for readers lol)

Despite not bringing much answers about the previous game or foreshadowing the next one, it was really great to play with the 3 protagonists and change between them and the story was interesting.
As for the Reverie corridor, it is pretty much an enhanced version of what we got in CS2 and where the padding content is, with a lot of enemies, trials, minigames, etc... It has a arcade feel to it, where you can choose anyone you want and defeat a bunch of enemies to reach higher floors and unlock stuff. I will be honest that I'm not a big fan of these corridors in Trails games because they feel very repetitive without adding much significant progress to the lore. Thankfully the distribution between when each floor is unlocked and the main story progression is good.
The insane amount of characters available to play can be hard to manage. It's good that everyone is in the game and you can play with your favorites, but managing equipment and quartz for everyone is hell and XP is not shared between them, so I recommend just picking up a few characters including the main ones from the main story and invest in them.

Zero issues with the PC Port. It ran perfectly at 1440p on a RTX 2060 Super. Thanks to the legendary Durante for the PC port.
Soundtrack in this game is good but still quite weak and forgettable overall compared to what Falcom used to deliver in the past. A few tracks are excellent, but the soundtrack as whole is average.

Trails into Reverie is a good game, by reaching this far into the franchise I think playing it is an obvious choice.
However it is not one of my favorites of the franchise since it not bring a lot of answers raised in CS4 and barely showed anything about Calvard or Ouroboros. Also not a big fan of the Corridor structure and character bloat.
The story is like a spin-off standalone (for now), but it is interesting with good pacing and the gameplay is very fun and polished.
I'd put Reverie right into the middle of the series rank (for now). Not one of the top games but definitely not on the bottom. This game, like Sky the 3rd, may get better over time, as we learn more about the series lore and discover that many things that appear in this game actually may have connections to the events in future games.\t\t\t\t\t

Pros:

1) A good story to conclude the "Erebonian" and "Crossbell" arcs (although the overall story for this particular game felt like "spin-off" story that was designed just so to give closure for multiple main characters from previous games more than anything else).

2) This game has SO MANY boss battles (even surpasses "Cold Steel 4" when counting all monster/human boss battles in the game) with plenty of good boss themes, which is what I look forward to the most in these turn-based JRPGs.

3) Music in general in this game is very good, although I would say the normal/boss battle music didn't stand out as much as previous games in the series (the bar was already very high from previous games, so it's no surprise that attempting to top those off would be a difficult task in general).

4) Newly added characters who are making their debut in this game turned out to be quite interesting and made me wish that they had their own dedicated game that explores their past, leading up to the events in this game.

Cons:

1) The volume of character voices in this game seems to be all over the place. Some characters sound very loud, while others sound very quiet. It was very annoying for me to keep changing the volume over and over throughout this game depending how loud different characters were during conversations in or outside battles (the in-game settings didn't help very much). The developers could have definitely made a better job in "voice optimization/normalization" for all character voiceovers for a more pleasant game experience overall.

2) Too many clipping issues where clothes/accessories/weapons clip directly through the characters' physical bodies (which can be "immersion-breaking" at times), which is something I haven't really seen/noticed in prior games, so it is kinda disappointing to see the developers put in less effort in polishing/addressing these things before releasing the game.

3) As stated above, the overall story for this game felt like a "spin-off" story (it's NOT, but it did feel that way at times) as the "main threat/villain" for this game felt "disconnected" to previous games and more like a "last minute fabricated villain" just for the sole purpose of "giving closure" to some characters and setting the stage for what's next in the "Trails" franchise (this story, or more so "the villain" of this game, doesn't feel "as integral" to the plot of the overall series and so this story feels that it can be "skipped/ignored" when evaluating its significance compared to previous games, similar to "Trails in the Sky the 3rd" which had a "self-contained/side" story plot that can be overall skipped, in my opinion).

4) While there were many "mini games" in this game, none of them stood out to me, in fact, I found some of them to be quite annoying to deal with and while I could've skipped them for the most part, the rewards that you gain from completing them the first time are SO worth it that makes them feel "mandatory" to complete at least once before moving on from them (wish the "Blade II" card game was back in this game, but sadly it wasn't).

All in all, this was another good great "Trails" game and I have thoroughly enjoyed my time with it and I simply can't wait for what Nihon Falcom has in store for us next in the "Trails" franchise! :D

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAnother excellent entry into the Trails franchise, if personally not at the absolute top of my list. An A- entry into an A+ series is still nothing to scoff at though.

However, as every other review you've seen of this game says, don't start with this one - it's the tenth game in a continuous running story that assumes you've played and finished every game that came before. More over, it acts as an epilogue to most of the major plot threads from all of those games. Beginning with Reverie is like starting a TV show halfway through the final season. Getting into Trails is absolutely worth the time, but this is unquestionably the worst starting point. Go pick up Trails in the Sky, Trails of Cold Steel, or the upcoming Trails through Daybreak for much better entry points.

Story acts as a solid capstone and sendoff to the previous arcs, even if some of it feels like a bit of an emotional and narrative retread of parts previous games (especially Cold Steel IV). Even so, I frequently found myself grinning from ear to ear at just how much I love these characters and this world. Some people understandably dislike having such a huge roster of characters (over fifty that are playable, and dozens more named NPCs of various importance with backstories that you'll be familiar with from all of the previous games), but I never found myself any more frustrated with the "everyone chimes in with a line or two in every cutscene" storytelling style that has become synonymous with Trails than I was in previous entries.

Gameplay is definitely the most fun so far, with tons of post-game team building content that just begs you to optimize the hours away looking for that perfect setup. Reminds me of what Persona 5 attempted to do with Mementos, except dramatically quicker and more engaging. Master and Sub Quartz are a really excellent RPG system that actualize Final Fantasy VII's Materia system into modern sensibilities, and Reverie's implementation of the system is head and shoulders above previous entries. Brave Orders are still generally entertaining and serve as a great way to add functional gameplay elements to characterization of the cast, and the new United Front system feels suitably "power of friendship" for a game that at its heart is about how much the player appreciates a cast of characters developed over a decade and a half.

Ultimately, if you're at the point in this series where it begins to make any sense to pick up Reverie, then you don't need a review. Buy it and continue onward into what has probably become one of your favorite franchises.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHoly Aidios!

(Please play the other Trails first)

TL;DR:

- Gameplay: The formula started way back in Sky FC refined to perfection. If you know what you are doing, this game is a breeze. If not, a true challenge. Especially in Nightmare.
- Graphics: They do the job. Very well, too. And that's the point.
- Music: Falcom Sound Team. They need no introduction. OST is perfect, ok?
- Replayability: In spades. And you'll want to replay it again, both for the challenge and to just bask in the...
- STORY: The capstone to the big tales started in Sky FC, Zero and Cold Steel I. All comes together here. So DO NOT play this game first, go play the others and work from there. The impact of this conclusion and new beginning is all the better that way.

Well worth the many, many hours invested. Quality gaming indeed.

Le Rant:

What an epilogue! Because this beast of a game is the actual epilogue of the first half of the Zemurian saga. I kid you not, play the others first. They are all here at Steam.
And you have to play them. I've been following the series for 13 years, playing each and every game as fast as I could get them. I've taken my time with them, true, but seriously:

You won't find another JRPG series like this one (or the Ys series). From the smooth gameplay that can make the game a breeze if you mastered its quirks or a challenge if you didn't, to its gorgeous, far-reaching story that even to this day has me loving every GD minute of it. Now on my third run.
All 10 games. Back. To. Back.
All those known faces, all those stories in West Zemuria that finally find closure here. And the endings, both the main story and the after story... I can't stress it enough, but if you've played all the other Trails before this one, you'll love to this final, wild encore.
And with Falcom's JDK music to boot! Which, as anyone knows, it's beautiful and fits this epilogue like a glove.

If you are a fan, you don't need this review. If you are starting: please, please play the others first!
Support Falcom so they can continue making these amazing stories that keep growing and growing, wilder and wilder. You won't be disappointed.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFantastic game. Really a capstone game to the Crossbell Arc and the Trails of Cold Steel games. Definitely play those prior to this one else you won't understand the setting or characters. The game itself is classic Legend of Heroes, great writing, great characters, fun combat, and good music.

The implementation of the Reverie Corridor in this game is vastly superior to the one in Trails of Cold Steel 2. Basically it takes the best elements of Phantasma in Trails in the Sky 3, and drops many of the bad elements. My major complaint on this iteration is that the last part of the Corridor gets very tedious. Fortunately it was easy to speed run through, skipping all but the bosses. Unlike Trails of Cold Steel 2, the last boss is a satisfying fight that doesn't feel frustrating or pointless or usurping the end of the game. Instead there is enough of a transition from the end of the main game to the end of the Corridor that both felt satisfying. By far the best part of the Corridor though are the flashbacks, which like the ones in Phantasma are some of the best writing in the series with some great character moments. I definitely recommend getting them all and playing to the end.

I have six main complaints about the game, but they are all minor in the grand scheme. Still because I am a big fan of this epic series I will list them.

1. Destruction of Juno: I call shenanigans on no one dying in this event. Even if a token force, you don't leave a fortress like that vacant. Thus somebody should have died when this happened.
2. Olivert's Wedding: I know it was the "ending" of Trails of Cold Steel 4, but all we really got was a clip show. It would have been fantastic to have one of the flashbacks be the actual wedding and reception. That would have been a nice pay off for long time fans who know all the different characters. Having the tournament though was a nice touch.
3. Beach-side Vacay: It would have been nice to pull in not just Class VII but also the SSS, Bracers and other major side characters too. Give Lloyd a chance to have dates with his main love interests, and maybe a nice moment with Estelle and Joshua.
4. Romance Options/Events: It would have been nice to have had Claire as a valid option, this though is a long time complaint from me. It would have been also nice to get Lloyd in on the action with his main three girls (Rixia really came into her own in this one and I really wanted a chance to see more of that). In reality having a flashback event for each of the three main protagonists (Estelle, Lloyd, and Rean) with their main girls would have been great. Bascially one with Estelle and Joshua alone, one with Lloyd and either Elie, Rixia, or Noel, and the one with Rean and his bevy of babes. I understand it would have been a PITA to put that all together but it would have been a nice event that deepened the bonds. As it stood the vacay date event was nice, with Musse and Towa being the best.
5. Corridor Length: As noted above I lost interest in the Corridor in the coda. The only thing that kept me going was the additional flashbacks and the fact I could speed run and bypass all the fodder. I don't generally play these games for the grind but rather for the storylines, music, and fun thoughtful combat. It got boring when it was super repetitive. The Corridor should have been 5 or at most 6 levels.
6. Tyrfing Game: This should have been modeled after the Divine Knight Battles. it would have given the player time to refresh their memory on those ahead of the encounter in the ending fight. Plus I actually like the mechanic for the Divine Knight Battles as it requires some strategy and tactics.

tl;dr: Falcom keeps knocking it out of the park. Can't wait for the Calvard arc to come to Steam.\t\t\t\t\t
