\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLike many PC gamers who grew up in the 1990s, I have fond memories of that golden age of real-time strategy games. Command and Conquer, Red Alert, Warcraft, Age of Empires, Homeworld… plenty of deserved classics belong to the RTS genre of that era. Yet the RTS’ fortunes have declined of late, and I can see why. With some notable exceptions like Supreme Commander, the core gameplay loop has evolved little over the years, while obtuse interfaces and the need to micro-manage often dim-witted squads threatening to render these games enervating clickfests; APM triumphing over strategic nous.

First published in 2004 by Homeworld veterans Relic, Dawn of War was released toward the end of the RTS’ high water mark, and is still held up as a classic of the genre. But how does it play now, in 2021? To be honest, I found it to exemplify many of the genre’s stereotypical qualities, for better and worse. APM reigns supreme. Where there are innovations, it’s a case of two steps forward, and one back. Organising infantry units into squads, for instance, is a great idea, instantly undone by the need to click a button every. Single. Time the player wishes to replace a fallen soldier and their heavy weapon. The graphics are woefully dated, of course, but the chunky unit designs and brutal combat animations give the skirmishes a pleasing feeling of weight and consequence. Even small squads can stand their ground for some time, giving the player a chance to fall back or reinforce. Larger engagements, however, can devolve into chaotic slugfests. Base-building is both clunky and perfunctory. Crucial information is buried in tiny text in obscure corners of the UI. Dawn of War feels as though it’s forever tripping over its own feet.

A word on the setting and story of the campaign. Dawn of War is often held up as a fine use of the Warhammer 40K setting, Games Workshop’s maximalist, grimdark sci fi. Without being familiar with the tabletop game, this is a fictional world I’ve found in previous brushes with the setting as good in theory, but let down in practice by puerile writing and plotting hamstrung by the requirement to fling alien armies at one another in unending battle. So it proves here again. Dawn of War’s plot consists of gruff men bellowing at one another, hordes of aliens showing up for some reason, and everything exploding. The dialogue is overlong and poorly-written, the in-engine cutscenes are abysmal. Combined with a braindead AI and predictable campaign mission design (build a base, amass a pile of units and hurl it at the objective marker on the opposite side of the map), these are significant problems for what must now be an entirely single-player experience.

Overall, then, Dawn of War has aged poorly. With it and its many expansions reasonably priced, however, it might be recommended to completionist RTS fans. For those looking for superior sci fi RTS, the original Supreme Commander (published only a few years later) and its expansion, Forged Alliance, are vastly better-designed and more modern-feeling, and offer both an active multiplayer scene and a compelling, varied campaign. For those looking to revisit the chunky low-poly RTS of the early 2000s, Warcraft III and its expansion offer a superior single player campaign.\t\t\t\t\t
