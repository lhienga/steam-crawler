\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm a bit torn about this game, as someone who's trying to get into 40k but struggling because of the stupidly huge amount of lore this franchise has by this point. So, as a newcomer I'll try to be as fair and neutral as possible on this review. As I usually do, I'll start with the negatives and work up from there. I should also mention, I haven't played the multiplayer, nor I have much of an interest about it. I'll focus mainly on the singleplayer content.

First and foremost... Man, by the time this game came out, Warcraft 3 had been out for 4 years. The campaign is VERY hit or miss. It only has 11 missions on it, but they feel VERY dragged out depending on how aggressive or defensive you are - which in some of the missions, the sheer spam of units will force the tempo of the mission to grind to a halt. Took me around 9 hours to get through the story itself on hard, but it wasn't really exciting as every mission ended up being the same: capture points, build defenses, get some buildings going, amass an army, blow everything (or the boss) to pieces. What breaks it the most however, you have FOUR different factions on this game, and the campaign only lets you play as the Space Marines. With Warcraft 3 as an example, you can probably see why that's dumb. The Eldar barely even make an appearance and feel like they were added last minute.

The pathing, f#ck me in the ass dude the pathing on this game is a nightmare to deal with. Never seen a game that offers control groups but actively makes the game worse if you use them. As it is, moving units in control groups makes them spread really far from one another, and due to the sheer difference in sizes between one unit to the other, they'll often collide with everything, get stuck or have to take stupid everywhere. Send them somewhere, and they'd spread all over the place, you can fit multiple turrets in the space in between them.

This one affects me more due to not being as familiar with the setting, but I can see it becoming an issue for many: The game was made exclusively for big 40k fans if the lore is anything to go by. The story doesn't really bother to explain sh#t, and if you don't understand something then tough luck. Worst of it all, I can't even be certain Relic Entertainment is to blame given this is a trend: Only a couple 40k games even bother with explaining the lore a bit, the other ones either try to make it matter as little as possible or just don't explain it at all. Which means, most 40k games are unwelcoming for new people. What makes these orks different from the usual fantasy orcs or the Warhammer fantasy ones? The f#ck are the eldar? What is a librarian? What about inquisitors? Who are these guardsmen and why do they die by the hundreds every ten seconds? Why are chaos such edgelords? If you have to go by this game, who knows. The answers were probably lost to time in the codex or something.

Lastly, a minor hiccup: Given it's a 2006 game, I won't be too harsh about it, but the game does not focus on your main screen, and if you have multiple ones, prepare for your mouse to get off the game and end up lost in any other screens you may have. Back then barely anyone had multiple screens, but nowadays is common.

Now, that looked harsh. Does the game DO anything good? Well...

First of all, most people rarely speak of sound design, but let me tell ya Relic Entertainment clearly loves it. Hearing troops engage in banter while in combat is one of my favorite tropes in strategy games, a trope that I don't see as much as of late. And as cheesy as the dialogue can get, hearing space marines scream FOR THE EMPEROR at the top of their lungs mid-combat is both cool and absolutely accidental comedy, since EVERY TIME they do it, I look back and see them kicking the ♥♥♥♥ out of some green goblin dumbass like a beach ball.

While I might not be a big fan of how this game caters to the defensive playstyle on these games, I do like how resources work. The requisition system is something I haven't seen much, and I do believe this game was one of the first ones to popularize it. Big fan as well of how you can upgrade your troops in different ways, but at least on the Space Marine faction, these upgrades only seem to expand on the Space Marines themselves. Other troops and vehicles barely get anything, which is... Weird?

Lastly, I'll say that for its time, the graphics aren't absolutely horrible. Plenty of explosions and effects for a strategy game, but when the game does zoom in, they look like toys. Special mention to buildings, I like how every faction has different animations that REALLY work well with each faction, gives them a more proper identity (which IS something they successfully took from Warcraft 3).

In the end, do I recommend this game? For people that aren't into 40k, it is a hard sell, honestly. For full price? No, don't even think about it. But I got it in the Winter sale for 75% off and for that price, I find it acceptable. 40k fans will likely get more of a kick out of this one, as I've mentioned before the game isn't very welcoming of new people wanting to learn more. Just don't burn me in the stake and call me a heretic, did my best to remain neutral and fair here. And before anybody asks: Yes, I know the second game is better. I bought that one to play next along with the expansions. Yes, I know the third one is absolute f#cking heresy, I refuse to play it for that reason.

TL:DR Edition: For its time, the game is good - but the campaign is very average if you're still learning about 40k or know very little about it. Game seems to actively avoid explaining any lore while things just sorta happen. On a big sale, it's decent enough to award purchase.\t\t\t\t\t
