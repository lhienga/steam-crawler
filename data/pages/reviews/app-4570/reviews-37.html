\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's a fun game. The campaign can be a trudge at times, there are some annoying pathfinding issues, and unnecessary micromanaging and ordering of units once you have dozens of them, but it's still a remarkably solid and enjoyable RTS. The biggest selling point by far is the theming and story, they're incredible and worth playing for that alone if you enjoy the lore or aesthetic of Warhammer 40k even casually. The models, animations, and voicelines are all lovingly crafted and very fun, never get tired of seeing drop pods crash down from orbit for every unit you make. The models and weapons matching up with the real life models and having parallels to the tabletop rules is great as well. This game's story and characters more than earned their place in the 40k canon and are still referenced in to this day in current 9th Edition materials.

Works on Steam Deck for the first 7 levels of the campaign, but becomes unplayably laggy on Mission 8 even on lowest settings due to the volume of units and bullets. I suspect Steam OS underallocates hardware resources to the game in the same way it does with certain emulators. Holding out hope that the hardware underusage will be fixed soon, because this is a perfect game for Steam Deck and plays wonderfully on the go with all-mouse controls.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is my favorite RTS, It's also my favorite Warhammer 40k game. You need to buy the master collection, it's a good deal, especially when it's on sale. It's on sale very often too. The game runs on a toaster, but visually it still holds up. The base game and winter assault each have a campaign that's worth going through. Dark Crusade has a risk style map in single player, where you take territories by winning matches against the ai. Then you get bonuses based on the territories you've taken. This is the same in Soulstorm, except it's a planetary map with territory on each planet. You also get a hero unit in the campaign mode of Dark Crusade and Soulstorm, and you get armor pieces, as well as weapons for the character as you progress.

I mostly play Soulstorm and Dark Crusade campaigns because of the replayability, as opposed to the more linear nature of the base game and Winter Assault's campaigns focused on story. I play Dark Crusade when I want to try different factions. My favorite faction is the Sisters of Battle, and I'm so mentally ill I don't like fighting them. But you can't fight them if they don't exist, so you play the Dark Crusade. Each expansion adds new factions as well of course.

The gameplay is great, it's focused on taking and controlling strategic points and relics on the map, rather than harvesting resources. You also build generators for your secondary resource, which can give you extra with certain points on the map. Except Necrons, they get speed bonuses from Capturing points, and build off of energy. The way this works encourages you to go out and take points on the map, to scout, to be aggressive, to hold defensive points further into the map instead of turtling in your base.

You also have squads for infantry, and you can spend resources reinforcing the squads, raising the total amount of soldiers in each squad, equipping them with different weapons, and you can attach commanders and such to each squad, to give them special benefits.

There are some spellcaster units you can use with active effects, but for most factions I don't think the game is very micro heavy. If I had to say anything bad, it's that flying units were added later, but the game wasn't built with them in mind, so sometimes the get caught on high terrain. As well in Dark Crusade's campaign, after taking a territory, you kept everything you built on your side of the map. So if you were attacked there, you had an advantage. In Soulstorm they got rid of that, though you can find a mod to let you keep what you've built. The mod I found lets you keep everything though, so if you choose to build too much it's a bit unfair for the ai. There are also a few bugs with numbers on the Sisters of Battle you might want to fix with the sisters of battle, but it's playable without it. On my old account I played them tons with no mods.

There are also some great mods people have made that adds tons of different factions, even titans and whatever else you could think of. These mods would require a somewhat better pc compared to the base game though. As of now none of them apply to the campaign, just skirmishes against the ai and multiplayer. I'd recommend playing through each campaign before installing massive mods, because the game is good as it is. They made a second and third game, but they weren't the same, especially the third game. That's why people keep modding this game instead. On top of that, after hundreds of hours of playing this game on other accounts, I don't think I've ever had issues with crashing. From windows vista to windows 10, from old toasters to new computers I've put together myself, it runs fine.

This is also the game that got me into Warhammer 40k, I first saw it in some computer lab and didn't even know what it was. Unfortunately I didn't have much time to play it there. When I saw it on steam I recognized it and bought the master collection on sale. I've been playing it on and off again ever since. And again, this isn't the only account I've had it on, I've probably got a couple hundred hours on this game. It's one of my go to games for listening to long videos on youtube as well, especially warhammer 40k lore videos.

My hours on this game and on Winter Assault were mostly just replaying the campaign. Most of my hours are spent on Soulstorm and Dark Crusade, and more on an old account.

TLDR Buy the master collection, play every campaign, then maybe try some mods.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPlayed this game when I was a kid off steam for years, shown to me by my father. He was really into Warhammer Fantasy stuff more than 40K, but he enjoyed this game, and the two of us played it a lot (along with similar games). He also got me into Warhammer Fantasy tabletop around the same time.

Fast forward nearly 20 years, and this game still brings a smile to my face every time I boot it up - and yes, the game is ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ brilliant in every way, but there's a bigger reason.

The past better part of a decade my father has gone through serious ups and downs with his mental health (which in reality had been happening all along, he just managed to hide it), and he's been on a upward trend of recovery for the past 4-5 years - and it's only really been in the past couple years where I feel like I'm finally reconnecting to him in a meaningful way, and it's because of this money laund- I mean great franchise of Warhammer. Earlier this year I got really into 40K tabletop (love of the lore for years and many co-workers/friends played it) playing as the Imperial Guard, and a month or so ago my father picked up some Orks to play against me (after a bit of coaxing from me), and ever since seeing his face light up with delight (or anger from me blowing his ♥♥♥♥ up) from that first game last month, well.

I felt like I was back as a kid, playing pretend army on our kitchen table with my dad - and it feels good guys.

No he's not dead or anything and yes I'm crying at this point and no I did not plan to share so much, but it all kinda just came out. So I suppose if you are on the fence about buying this game, it gets a solid pass from me. Give it a go with some friends, and make some memories.

Love you dad.\t\t\t\t\t
