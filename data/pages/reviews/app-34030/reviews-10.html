\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTHIS IS A RECOMMENDATION WITH CAVEATS

Notwithstanding a couple of forays into other genres, Creative Assembly have essentially been remaking the same turn-based overworld / RTS one-two punch for which they were rightly lauded more than two decades ago. You might imagine they’d have the fundamentals of the Total War series down to a fine art in that time, but 2010’s Napoleon is an uneven iteration of the franchise. If you’re new to Total War, don’t start here. But if you like the series and enjoy this period of history, there’s plenty of good times to be had if you can overlook Napoleon’s misfires.

Hot on the heels of the ambitious sprawl of Empire, Napoleon tightens the focus to Europe during the twenty-year span of Bonaparte’s rise and fall, while retaining Empire’s muskets, cannon and colourful uniforms. It streamlines or ditches some of Empire’s systems while still offering a sizeable strategy sandbox in which to lead a major power to dominance. You’ll develop your nation, unlock elite units and research technologies, but the centuries-spanning arc of imperial ascent isn’t present. The focus is very much on Europe circa 1790 - 1815, and the main campaign is gated behind a couple of shorter, linear, tutorial-like endeavours that will still take around twenty hours to complete. These more circumscribed affairs aren’t unwelcome, but it seems an odd decision to lock the main campaign behind their completion. Likewise the historical battles, which add welcome variety and scripted AI to the lobotomised and somewhat repetitive scraps of the main campaign, but with the next only unlocked only upon completion of the last. I’d be tempted to call the Napoleon base game stingy, but all available DLC is now rightly bundled into this “Definitive Edition.” This offers mountains more content, including – thankfully – the option to play as nations other than France. Even with this as standard, be aware that the scope is more limited than other Total War games.

Elsewhere, Total War’s usual problems raise their head. The AI is distractingly awful at both the campaign and the battle level. In the campaign, it never really recovered from the transition away from Risk-style provinces to Rome’s smooth strategic overworld; rarely mounting sustained aggression, and shuffling around vulnerable single units haphazardly. Scenarios which rely on your allies acting capably can be frustrating. The AI also struggles in the battles, especially with deploying cavalry effectively, while your own units will quite contently stand and get mowed down if they’re facing the wrong way, and cannon batteries will fire salvo after salvo into the side of a gentle incline, or the back of your own troops’ ranks. Playing on harder difficulties grants the AI advantages that just about shakes out to an interesting challenge. A mediocre UI adds to a fiddly, sticky feeling in the battles. Supreme Commander, released prior to Napoleon, offers a masterclass in RTS design – including a superlative UI, and units that respond sanely to battlefield conditions without relinquishing player control of their overall objective – that Creative Assembly wholly ignored. The graphics have dated poorly, with a heavy reliance on 2D sprites that pop in noticeably, while the 3D animations are stilted and weird, especially during melee and when 40 cavalry reload their guns in perfect synchrony. It’s frustrating that a common bug around graphics settings requires a manual fix for a game this mature. The naval battles remain tiresome, hamstrung by finnicky unit positioning, hopeless AI and a glacial pace.

For all that Napoleon feels like something of a missed opportunity, the core premise remains hugely compelling, and Creative Assembly’s evocation of atmosphere – especially through some stellar audio work – remains hard to beat. Grumbles aside, Napoleon is, fundamentally, an accessible strategy sandbox in which to explore a fascinating and under-utilised period of history. One for Total War completionists with an interest in the era. If you’re new to Total War, where to start is a matter of debate, but the Warhammer games are more modern and polished, albeit in a fantasy setting, whereas Shogun II is arguably the best of the historical entries. I also recently enjoyed Battlefleet Gothic: Armada II, which transplants the broadsides and boarding actions that cameo here to the grimdark 40K setting, in a much slicker and more spectacular fashion than does Napoleon.\t\t\t\t\t
