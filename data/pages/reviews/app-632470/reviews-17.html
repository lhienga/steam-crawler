\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAt the outset: don't buy this unless you enjoy reading. This game is closer to a visual novel than it is a typical videogame.

My experience with Disco Elysium is shockingly similar to my experience with Planescape: Torment. Both games are CRPGs that focus on their stories and characters instead of traditional videogame mechanics. Where Planescape has a clunky D+D combat system, Disco Elysium instead has political theory. And, most importantly, both games have overly-adoring fans who insist the story is so incredibly deep and cerebral that if you don't think its a crowning achievement for the RPG genre, then you just don't understand it.

Like with Planescape, the devotion shown to this game by its acolytes hurts it. I came in expecting incredibly philosophical discussions that would challenge my political beliefs, but that didn't really happen. Instead, what I got was a strong narrative, well-developed characters, some humorous moments, and a great climax shaped by the decisions I had made along the way. And if this sounds great to you...it was! This game is excellent. But it isn't what the most vocal fans say it is. There are some moments that don't land, some obvious conversation paths you wish you could take but just can't, a few times when doing something out of order causes another conversation to ignore actions you've already taken, and a surprising number of audio bugs and clunky movement.

On the topic of the game's implementation of political theory, something often cited about this game, I found it interesting, often insightful, and surprisingly witty. The authors did a good job making all the political choices feel like the wrong choice, showing how all these systems look awful when presented in an unflattering light. However, there wasn't as much of it in the game as I was led to believe. Early in my playthrough, I decided I wanted to do a second run as a meathead communist, but abandoned the idea once I realized how much of the game would remain unchanged by that choice. Still, what the game does have is legitimately compelling.

My biggest disappointment was the canyon between the quality of the art pieces used for the game (character portraits, skill icons, landscape photos, etc.) and the quality of the character models. Character models look poor, and often don't mimic what the player is doing. My text box will say I am clapping, while my model remains motionless. It really messes with the immersion, and is a point against the game.

The characters and music are both great. The game has well-written characters that range from one-dimensional caricatures of a concept or theme (actually great design when used properly in my opinion) all the way up to characters that I will never know if they were "good" or "bad" (and that's a good thing!) The music, for its part, consistently set the right tone.

Overall, this game is an easy recommendation. If you don't go in expecting "greatest of all time," you'll have a very rewarding experience. That said, look into the developers recent conduct before deciding to buy.

If you enjoyed this game, or think you'd like it but are put off by the developer's conduct, I have 2 recommendations: Planescape: Torment (the overlap between the two games is immense), and The Talos Principle (philosophical masterclass).\t\t\t\t\t
