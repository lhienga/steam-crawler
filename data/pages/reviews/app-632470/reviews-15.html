\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDisco Elysium: The Final Cut presents a complex and intricate world ripe for exploration, but it's not without its drawbacks. Developed by Robert Kurvitz and ZA/UM, the game offers a unique blend of detective noir and existential introspection, set against a gritty, politically charged city.

One of the game's standout features is its richly detailed narrative, which unfolds through a series of branching conversations and choices. From unravelling mysteries to navigating moral dilemmas, players are given a remarkable degree of agency in shaping the story and its outcome. The writing is sharp and witty, with dialogue that crackles with personality and depth.

Visually, Disco Elysium: The Final Cut is a treat for the senses. The hand-painted artwork captures the gritty atmosphere of the city of Revachol, immersing players in its seedy underbelly and faded glamour. Each location is meticulously crafted, with a sense of history and decay that adds to the game's immersive world-building.

However, the game has its challenges. Despite its engaging narrative and atmospheric setting, Disco Elysium: The Final Cut suffers from pacing issues and repetitive gameplay mechanics. Some sections of the game feel overly drawn-out, with long stretches of dialogue that can become tedious. Additionally, certain puzzles and tasks can feel arbitrary and frustrating, detracting from the overall experience.

Furthermore, the controversy surrounding developer Robert Kurvitz and ZA/UM cannot be ignored. Allegations of workplace misconduct and toxic behaviour have cast a shadow over the game's release, prompting some players to reconsider their support for the project. While it's essential to separate the art from the artist, these issues have undoubtedly impacted the perception of Disco Elysium: The Final Cut within the gaming community.

In conclusion, Disco Elysium: The Final Cut is a mixed bag. While its narrative depth and atmospheric world-building are undeniably impressive, the game needs to be revised because of pacing issues, repetitive gameplay, and controversy surrounding its development. For players willing to overlook these flaws, there's much to enjoy in the richly layered experience that Disco Elysium offers. However, those seeking a flawless gaming experience may find themselves disappointed.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAn excellent point and click adventure with RPG elements.

I didn't know what to expect going into this game. I didn't watch any lets plays, read ny articles, anything. I only knew that it was good. Turns out, it's a point and click adventure with RPG elements that factor into dialog choices and background rolls that determine what dialogue you get to see. Superficially it's what games like Skyrim, Fallout 3, etc. do, but as this is a narrative driven game, it's done properly here.

It gave me Planescape Torment vibes. The writing is very good, the world is familiar but also alien, and the voice acting does so much to set the mood and develop the characters that you interact with. The characters feel... real, I guess. As real as a fictional character can feel. They have their own motives, history, reasons for being who and how they are. There are no obvious good or
bad people. They have some depth to them. I feel like without the voice acting this would be a somewhat different game. Still good, but not as striking.

The entire start of the game is one big mystery. It's a bit cliche, many stories can be reduced to a common set of themes. It's the events, writing, and characters that make all the difference. This game does it very well. Be warned, there is a *lot* of dialogue. This is a reading game, but along with that comes in depth world building and many dialogue paths. While most of the time you can go though all of the choices to explore all options, some choices do lead down a path of no return. Having said that, I'd suggest picking a role and going with it. This is a role playing game as much as it is an adventure game.

I'd say, if you're into point and click adventures with a lot of mystery and dialog, you'll probably like this.\t\t\t\t\t
