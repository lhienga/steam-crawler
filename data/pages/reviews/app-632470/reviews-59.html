\r\n\t\t\t\t\tKim Kitsuragi is a legend

I have tried to write my thoughts about this game several times since I finished it more than a week ago, and I kept failing to properly express my feelings. Let’s try one more time. I still don’t really know what to say about Disco Elysium.

OK, let us take a look at the Disco Elysium subreddit, and let’s sort to show the most upvoted post of all time.

Reddit Link

Yes. That sums it up. This game has a LOT of reading. Like, really a lot. It is an interactive novel more than what you might traditionally think of as a game.

Over the past few years, I have actively been trying to play a wider variety of games; different styles, different genres, different studios. Disco Elysium was first recommended to me by a writer on the Assassin’s Creed dev team, and it is a game unlike any I have played before.

For the first few hours I found the game really quite strange, and I was really struggling to “get it”. However, after perhaps 3 or 4 hours I started to get into the groove of the game, and the more I played, the more I became completely obsessed.

It was on the second day when the first major point “clicked” with me. I realised the game’s design supports the player fantasy: I often had absolutely no idea what to do next, so I consulted the notebook, thought about what to do next, and re-interviewed characters until a new thought or clue appeared – exactly as a detective would do.

It was on the third day that I realised the genius of the skill tree. As more skills are developed, the more complex and entertaining the internal monologue becomes. The use of the skills, and the various “checks” that are influenced by previous decisions and your skills, offer immense variety and freedom of play.

The writing is superb. The person who recommended the game to me described Disco Elysium’s style as “crunchy”, and I did not understand this until I played the game. It has a unique style that all gamers should experience – it is different to anything other game I played. The depth is excellent. The world-building is fantastic. There are elements of supernatural that add the mystery and create a sense of other-worldliness. The “shivers” skill gives intense, visceral, and sometimes disturbing insights into the stories taking place across the city of Revachol.

Disco Elysium is a game that would reward multiple play throughs. Would you reach the conclusion? Would you lock yourself out? Can you die without solving the crime? I believe these are all possible. What are the story endings for Kim, Cuno, and our protagonist? I don’t know. I might play it again and make very different choices.

Disco Elysium stands out for me as a unique gaming experience. It wouldn’t work for everyone, but for anyone interested in classic detective games or role-playing/choose-your-own-adventure games, Disco Elysium is a must.

But more than anything, play Disco Elysium for Kim Kitsuragi. He’s one of the most-wonderfully written companion characters in gaming.\t\t\t\t\t
