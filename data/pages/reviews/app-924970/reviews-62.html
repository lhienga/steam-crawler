\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOf course this was basically DoA for whatever reason that certainly must have more to do with bad management -- including them infringing on certain aspects of design -- than it being a bad game per se.

It seems to be a quality product. Brings some arguably needed freshness to the genre if you ask me. I like the rpg aspects. At the same time, if you've played L4D/2 ;) you'll encounter many familiar tropes and even some locations.

The gunplay is very CoD. I don't like the huge hit indicator (which I think you can turn off or tweak, I just haven't yet) but commons die in one shot from almost anything If I've observed correctly. The engine feels fast. And it's gory.

Overall UX...

I want to play it in the campaign order before getting all "wild" with drop-in games and losing the "plot", such as it may be. So, trying to "host" (Create Lobby), i frequently find myself waiting about 30s (or maybe a bit more) "searching" for a server and then I presume it drops back to letting me host. I don't love the waiting.

Have played a couple games w/ actual ppl but mostly bots so far (by choice). But my point is there are actualy humans playing. The game may be dead-ish -- though I might honestly be just too new to even know, because I'm not playing preferred settings, etc., -- but it's definitely not "dead". Worth noting if you think you will cruise through with bots as your backup, though, they are predictably terrible. Actually at times good and at times horrid. Kinda like ppl. YMMV in terms of completionism for achievements etc. if you don't(/can't) play w/ ppl. Personally I waited on this game for however many years, and I was bored so I finally did decide to roll the dice, but I did still have those reservations. You will of course not discover the nuanced answers to all these issues within the 2-hr. refund window. :P

Can any of these problems be solved by some kind of tweaks? I honestly haven't looked that far into it. Game is notoriously unmoddable which led to lots of the hate. Is there not so much as a config file? That would be lame. And barely conceivable.

I don't know of any additional maps but some ppl on nexus are certainly doing mods.

Anyway, I went ultimate edition for the sale price of $20. I would not buy unless at a discount, just on principle.

And overall I am going "recommended", it's a decent take on the genre, and although most people would have wished for the game to have a longer lifecycle, ultimate ed. seems to have a decent amt. of content.

Subject to update when the honeymoon is over. ;P\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFor a game who's creators established much hype for up until Back 4 Blood's release based solely on Turtle Rock Studios' previous involvement in Valve's Left 4 Dead franchise, there are certainly key differences that B4B has compared to the L4D franchise as a whole, with many of which highlighting many of the advantages that contribute much more to the latter's entertainment capabilities. B4B was extremely flawed and bugged at launch unfortunately as with many recent major game releases, and so analyzing and comparing it to the L4D franchise after the many months of updates, improvements, and gameplay additions truly enables a much more favorable outlook for Back 4 Blood. Some of these stark contrasts listed below:

Characters
The 8 main characters of the L4D franchise easily outshine all 12 of B4B's playable characters, with the difference in depth being so far as noticeable with the contrast in NPCs between the two franchises, where namely Virgil in L4D2 has a much more in-depth interaction and rapport with L4D2's main crew compared to say Phillip's with any and all of B4B's cleaners, which is ironic considering Virgil's lack of a physical presence in his interactions. The voice acting, interactions, and dialogue between the major and minor characters of the L4D franchise illustrate just how immensely lacking B4B's characters are, as it is glaring the minor to nonexistent character development in B4B's campaigns as they progress compared to L4D's enormous contrast in this regard. Ultimately, especially when using a vocalizer in the L4D franchise, it is abundantly clear that the characters of Back 4 Blood should not be championed as one of its major selling points.

Modding
Back 4 Blood lacks incredibly in mod support and thus the game is unfortunately severely limited in this aspect, whereas the L4D franchise, with L4D2 in particular, holds a delightful abundance of modification capabilities. Likely, the modding community of the L4D franchise has enabled a lot of continued growth and activity for the franchise which hasn't experienced a new release within a decade. This contrast highlights yet another unfortunate drawback of B4B, where the L4D franchise champions modding capabilities whilst B4B makes those capabilities nearly nonexistent.

Weapons & Items
There is a definite uniqueness in the weapons and items of Back 4 Blood whether it be the glorious Embezzler, a Desert Eagle that provides copper (the game's in-campaign currency) on kill, or just a simple AK47, Molotov, or Medkit. This variety in rarity of item and weapon types can enhance the progression of each Campaign, especially for the game's longest, Act I which can take upwards of 4 hours to complete. Complimented by the Card System in this regard, an established Melee card build can benefit from a higher rarity Iron Claw weapon, or even the Tenderizer, which helps to keep an hours long campaign entertaining and captivating. Compared to the L4D franchise which may have a very distinct few weapons that are optimal in nearly all cases, Back 4 Blood shines with this feature through in through in every campaign.

Card System
The current Card System of Back 4 Blood can be regarded as one of the most entertaining and intuitive aspects of the game, enabling a multitude of different approaches in play to create a truly unique experience each and every time. With card builds of one's choosing enabling a build that could say benefit usage of a certain weapon, or no weapon like it's Black Ops 2 with the Combat Knife card centered deck. Touching more on the previous character section, one benefit of the card system is the parity that can be applied with a deck on a certain character, say for instance a Healing Efficiency build with Doc, or a Melee build with Holly. Ultimately, this system offers an enormous variety in game-play and coordination with other players even further accentuates how awesome and integral this element of Back 4 Blood is.

Zombies
"The Ridden" of B4B have a truly laughable name and the Mutations of said Ridden show much of their influence from the L4D franchise, such as with the Hag's resemblance to the Witch, or the Breaker to the Tank (minus the incredible music), the Stinger variants to the Hunter, the Reeker to the Boomer, the Tallboy to the Charger, etc. Even the cut Screamer from the original L4D is extremely similar to the Snitcher of B4B, the common and Special Infected of the L4D franchise easily outclass the Ridden and Ridden Mutations of the Back 4 Blood franchise, especially with their fantastic and unique musical cues.

Multiplayer
In a co-op campaign, Multiplayer can be incredibly enjoyable, especially with a pre-made group as one can coordinate who should run what build and prioritize who gets what. Making any one of the 4 cleaners per run incredibly unique on their own within a specific match, although in a normal public match much of this can be thrown out the window as exists a clear lack of coordination in this case. The L4D franchise's Versus mode is also leagues above B4B's Swarm mode, although the Versus community of L4D itself might bring the franchise to shame on its own for the many who lack proper human interaction. All in all, a great experience with friends on Back 4 Blood.

Progression
Another major highlight of Back 4 Blood is account progression, whether it be earning supply points by beating levels with varying amounts based on difficulty, or earning Skull Totems by acquiring them at the end of Ridden Hives. These factor in nicely early on to make a sense of wanting to achieve and play more as you unlock more cards and thus are enable to expand your horizon and potential play-style at the onset of an Act. Once you unlock all the cards, it is typically just random outfits and skin customization options for weapons or your B4B account that are nice, but don't really do much outside of their aesthetic. For as far as the depth of the game is concerned, it is an amazing touch that sets it apart from the L4D franchise that it attempts to pay homage to.

Conclusion
Despite much of the inspiration and similarities B4B and the L4D franchise hold, B4B should not be considered Left 4 Dead 3 by any means given the reasons above, but on its own stands a solid zombie-killing experience. This, above all else, like L4D, becomes a better experience for each additional friend you have to join you in your journey of becoming a one-man cheeseburger apocalypse. If B4B had proper mod support as mentioned previously, then it could truly be one of the games of all time as you start the Midnight Riders finale pulling off Nick's greatest con while listening to Chocolate Helicopter as you proceed to decimate the undead with legendary weapons and your card deck filled with every Copper card.

TL;DR: Buy the Deluxe Edition of Back 4 Blood when it is on sale if you like killing zombies with friends.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's hard to give the game the review that truly encapsulates everything that needs to be said. For starters, it truly is a shame the dev team gave up on this so soon in its history given the lasting success of L4D2 and the community that kept it alive and well with truly creative modding. Having said that, I am happy with the vanilla game and where it stands considering that the last update it received will truly be its last update in its lifetime. Others may disagree with me on this but I enjoy the campaign from start to finish and the extra additions. I think its very cool that each one of my friends has a specific character that truly accentuates the build they want to make via the card system, myself included. And that the fine tuning difficulty of Trial of the Worm lets us have just the right amount of challenge that keeps us playing. Overall, I have fun while playing this game but only if there a team of three other people willing to run it with me, which I almost always have, fortunately. I also greatly enjoy the ridden hives and the rare double hive encounters for extra totems. There's just something about deep diving into the rotten hellscape and walking out with legendary guns that feels fun every time.

If you have several friends who enjoy zombie shooters and need to kill a few hours this is a great game and I believe it's worth at least the sale price of 12 bucks. If you're a solo player or struggle to consistently get your friends away from whatever lame-skin royale they're playing, this is not worth it.

For the numerous and depressing downsides, I'll start with the fact they couldn't even be bothered to have holiday themes on autopilot. For one year they had festive themes and music changes to the campsite, even double skull totem weekends. Not anymore. And while I do enjoy the numerous characters to choose from and the buffs they provide, only after playing for two years did I hear a SINGLE realistic dialogue that remotely resembled an actual conversation between humans. And that was literally yesterday. The one liners are bad, the dialogue is bad, but not unbearable and is easy to tune out except for Tala. If nothing else I wish they would remove every one of her voice lines from the game including her campaign arc.

TL;DR
It's not the best game in the world and it's not the worst game in the world. If you've got several friends who want to kill an afternoon this is a great way to do that. But only when it's on sale.\t\t\t\t\t
