After the release of critical successes Populous and Black & White, Molyneux set his sights on an ambitious RPG title for the original Xbox - Fable. It was, in his words with aplomb, “gonna be the best game ever”. When it released in 2004, it was met with warm reception but some were quick to point out that marketed promises were not met. Surely, the sales pitch of player pregnancy would sell copies to fetishists in the name of “immersion” and realistic tree life-cycles would corner the treasure trove of potential sales known as gaming arborists, but one has to ask the question: “why?”

Despite all the nonsensical embellishment prior to release, Fable is an enjoyable title. Its morality system lacks a single drop of depth and combat is about as complex as primary school math but it’s a fun system to break. Tools such as Slow Time and Multi Strike trivialize every combat encounter but the novelty of completing quest challenges with weapon-spell combinations never wears off. It’s a brief glimpse of clever design that rewards curious players. The developers even had the foresight to reward those who applied this approach to Fable’s mini-games as well.

Fable’s greatest strength is its believable world. While the idea has been done before, Fable conveys the commercialization of heroics in a candid and hilarious manner. It’s offensively British, with a hodgepodge of ideas that could create a tonal clash so great it instantly kills the suspension of disbelief, but it doesn’t. It lacks in strength compared to the golden age of Bioware games released around its time, but what other game lets you stuff yourself full of baby chickens, put up a severed head in a real estate listing, get more evil points for divorcing than murder, and perform the YMCA?\t\t\t\t\t
