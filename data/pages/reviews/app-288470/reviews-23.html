\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAh, Fable, the game that whisked me away to a world of whimsy and adventure, just like that time I stood in line for its midnight release, way back when. It's like finding an old pair of jeans in the attic – a little faded, maybe a tad snug, but oh so comforting.

You see, Fable Anniversary is a time machine that transports me to the early days of Xbox, when graphics were a bit blocky, and NPCs had voices that seemed to be coming from another dimension entirely. But hey, we didn't care! We were too busy being enchanted by a world where chickens could be kicked, and we could choose whether to be a dashing hero or the naughtiest rogue.

The nostalgia hits you like a punch from a Hobbe (those mischievous little critters from the game). It's like reuniting with an old friend – one that you shared all your dirty secrets with, like that time you tried to seduce every villager in Bowerstone, or when you spent hours fishing in pursuit of that elusive golden fish.

And let's not forget the humor! Fable Anniversary has humor in spades, from the absurdity of the combat expressions (you'll never tire of flapping your arms like a chicken) to the hilarious commentary from the townsfolk, who seem to think you're the second coming of either a hero or a menace, depending on your choices. I mean, who knew that breaking wind could be such a powerful weapon?

But the best part is that Fable still holds up surprisingly well today. Sure, the graphics may not be as jaw-dropping as the latest titles, and the loading times might give you time to brew a cup of tea, but the charm is timeless. The core gameplay, the moral choices, and the sense of whimsy are as engaging as ever.

So, dust off your old Xbox or grab it on PC, and relive the magic of Fable . It's like catching up with an old friend over a pint of virtual ale at the local pub. It may not be perfect, but it's a classic that's worth revisiting from time to time, just to remind yourself of the simpler joys of gaming – and the fact that you can still laugh at fart jokes with the same enthusiasm you had in your teenage years. Here's to Fable, a game that's forever young at heart!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWell this was a nice surprise. I never played the OG Xbox version of this, (probably too busy with Halo and Morrowind at the time) and then I moved to PC as Half-life 2 came out a month or two after this.
It’s definitely ‘of it’s time’ and is one of those games that would look like shovelled ♥♥♥♥♥ on a desktop but it looks really charming on the Steamdeck.
It’s basically a third person RPG-lite with some minor life sim elements on the side. Your character can get scarred, old, married and become mayor, not necessarily in that order. Your actions affect the look of your character- act like a bastard and watch as you sprout horns and your skin goes red. My character was a blonde behemoth topped off with a glowing halo by the end, it’s gratifying to see your actions reflected back in the character’s appearance and it’s rare nowadays.
Gameplay is veeeery basic, sword fighting is basically bashing the same button until someone falls over and ranged attacks are the same, but slower. There is a large selection of spells, but by halfway through I had maxed out a particular spell that would just destroy large groups of enemies, including bosses, and by 3/4 through I had the most powerful sword in the game which also restored my health and mana, so this is not a hard game by any standards.
However, this is clearly a warm and lovingly made game with a very British sense of humour (and surprisingly dark in places) and I enjoyed it to the end. A mention must go to the music, which is outstanding. The main theme is by Danny Elfman, a Harry Potter/LotR strings mashup that had me looking for the album online and the rest of the game by Russell Shaw is equally as good. Best game soundtrack I’ve heard in years.\t\t\t\t\t
