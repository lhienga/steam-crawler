\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI normally avoid leaving negative reviews on games that didn't work for me. The way I see it, my personal dislike of particular features or plot points doesn't necessarily add up to a bad game for someone else. Unfortunately, in the case of Cyber Manhunt, I feel justified in leaving this review because I think the game fails at what it sets out to do: making the player feel like a hacker solving a compelling mystery.

The first, and perhaps greatest challenge to immersion I encountered here is the writing. To clarify, my primary issue is not with the English translation. I do believe there are many odd choices that add up to very stilted and unnatural-sounding conversations, but I would have been able to see past this if the characters and stories involved were more thoughtfully written to begin with.

Most of the characters you investigate in Cyber Manhunt embody one-dimensional stereotypes. This makes the stories you uncover about them highly predictable. The social issues the game claims to focus on (according to the blurb: cyber violence and privacy breaches) are explored in what I personally believe to be an equally shallow, one-dimensional way. There are also additional social issues, such as sexual violence and human trafficking, which I believe the writers were simply not equipped to handle at all—and I say that as someone who has had to deal with these issues in my past work. The behaviour of both the victims and the perpetrators is painfully unrealistic, to the point that I suspect the writers took more inspiration from internet creepypastas than real life cases.

Because the puzzles rely on narrative complexity to be interesting, I believe the game falls flat on this front as well. You scour social media accounts, emails and webpages for names and other personally identifiable information. Databases and password cracking tools help you access more data to scour. There are occasionally phishing sequences where you select dialogue choices. All of these mechanics have the potential to be a fun, highly simplified social hacking simulation, and I can see Cyber Manhunt is trying its best to do just that. I even believe it might have worked—if there was more thought put into how the characters communicate and what their motivations might be as full-fledged human beings. A predictable story with one-dimensional characters who speak and write in a stilted, unnatural sounding way just doesn't make for a very engaging data-scouring experience.

All in all, I am disappointed with the wasted potential in Cyber Manhunt. I hope that if the devs go on to make more narrative games, they will make quality writing a higher priority of the overall experience.\t\t\t\t\t
