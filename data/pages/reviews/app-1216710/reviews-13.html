\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLong story short - this is a really good game that lacks a lot of polish and has plenty of bugs. If you can look past that and appreciate the gameplay and the surprisingly accurate portrayals of hacking tactics, then you will find PLENTY to enjoy in this game.

First, I want to praise the game for its portrayal of hacking and the steps that make it possible. It's simple to grasp and communicates the very real threat of phishing and social engineering while keeping things entertaining. The opening title cards of the game say that this was one of their biggest reasons for making the game. In that respect, they 100% succeeded. If you are looking for a game that accurately and easily explains hacking, this is the best example I can think of.

As for the game loop, there are many similarities to the Ace Attorney/Phoenix Wright games.

- you are a given a small handful of clues
- you enter an investigation segment where you search for more clues/information by clicking on everything you see (just like Ace Attorney)
- you take your findings and confront someone, similar to cross-examinations in Ace Attorney
- you go through a "Revisualization Sequence" and gather all the information you have to label somebody as killer/witness/hacker/etc
- repeat

This game loop works well and definitely encourages exploration, as any failures or mistakes made along the way will only send you back a few minutes at most, and usually only a few seconds. It also helps that the game focuses on depth rather than a wide variety of mechanics. There are many different minigames along the way, but they are usually one-offs or done a few times back to back. The core game mechanics are a small set and persist throughout the game. These mechanics are fairly open-ended, so the flexibility you have to work with his rather high. And what's more - the game often chains mechanics together to really test your mental flexibility. Sometimes, it can feel cheap - as in brute force would be a more efficient method than logic, but it rarely ever gets that bad.

That said, the game does have issues with polish. It's actually very similar to Ace Attorney where you have to select the exact piece of evidence that the game wants, regardless of how close or relevant the one you chose actually is. Other times, the game has race conditions that occur that don't exactly softlock the game, but certainly glitch it out and make it hard to progress. While I have never been softlocked in this game, I have run into glitches that force me to fail time sensitive tasks. And regardless, several people have reported softlocks exist in this game, so it's something to beware of.

Also, the UI is a bit finnicky. The primary UI you will be working with is meant to look like a computer desktop, which is really cool and makes a great backdrop for the rest of the game, but there are definitely some cut corners. For example, you cannot use your scroll wheel to scroll through content - you must click and drag the page or the scroll bar. That got annoying fast, but I got over it. Also, similar to Ace Attorney, investigation segments will involve you hovering over everything and anything you see until your cursor (or in this case, the background) changes to signify that a clue is here. This makes the process of finding clues a bit repetitive, even though the game throws a few new mechanics in for each chapter. And I could be wrong, but I am fairly certain that the color-change-on-hover mechanic has a weird hover detection, leading to false negatives. I am not sure, and even if it is the case, it is rare - maybe once or twice a chapter.

The story is surprisingly engaging. For starters, it is a fairly mature game, as it delves into some pretty painful and disgusting subject matter. Examples include permanent bodily injury and human trafficking. The game uses these subjects to explain the threat and danger of an insecure internet and how people exploit it for their benefit. We see recurring characters who struggle with these threats and dangers and feel the pull to use the elements for their own purposes. Seeing the struggle really makes the story feel impactful, real, and threatening, while not running into any form of power creep. The conflicts are unique, but self-contained. The characters grow and develop as the story progresses.

It's a bit difficult to say whether or not characters have depth. Technically, they have a lot of depth, but most of their depth is literally given to the player as bullet points on a board highlighting what the character went through. As more bullets are added, their motivations get further explained and then their justifications become revealed. In that light, I'd say almost every character we come across has depth and purpose for being in the story. But that said, it's still just bullet points on a page - there's a chance that the characters will feel cheap since their traits are rarely revealed through dialogue or conversation.

And on that note, it's important to say that the English version of the game is full of typos, bad grammar, run-on sentences, and otherwise broken english. And I don't mean once every few minutes. I mean multiple times per paragraph - sometimes, multiple times per sentence. It's clear that this game was translated into English, but not very well. The text is perfectly understandable, so I am not bothered at all by it, but I imagine others might.

As for the voice acting, most reviews seem to dislike the voices or find them passable, but I thought all of these voice actors did a phenomenal job. I was actually shocked at how good some of these portrayals were. The voices didn't just add another dimension, they actually communicated character traits that the text and story alone could not. For example, Van's voice actor really highlighted just how unhinged the character was - something that the story alone doesn't clearly portray. Another example is Coral's voice actor communicating shame and embarrassment on top of the fear that the text displayed. Every single voice was great, but special praise to the voice actors for Ashley, Van, and Quiller.

The music is surprisingly well done, but there aren't a wide variety of songs. The art has 2 styles - the computer desktop/applications and the people/locations. The desktop/applications are sleek and beautiful. The locales/characters are emotive and detailed. Character animations are a bit static/stiff, but I feel it adds to the charm.

All in all, I had a great time with the game. I finished it at 40 hours, but that was with me taking the long way around and digging through everything. This is more like a 5-15 hour game, depending on how much you guess right the first time. I enjoyed the accurate portrayal of hacking, the story, music, art, and mechanics. I got annoyed with glitches, but they were usually easy to resolve or easy to recover from.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCyber Manhunt is a fun, engaging, online detective game that easily drags you into the world of online secrets. Fitting together little bits of information of persons of interest, by scouring the pseudo-alternatives of real world social media platforms, the game continuously drags you down yet another rabbit hole.

I finished the base game without DLC in roughly 10 hours.

Beware though that the game is published by a Chinese studio and it shows. The script has been translated from Chinese to English and it is sometimes painfully obvious. It's far from perfect English, which can ruin the immersion from time to time. Also, the voice acting leaves a lot to be desired. Both on the inconsistent, unnatural intonation, but also voice actors saying different lines than their script or subtitles.

But if you can look past the bad translation and voice acting, and you're a fan of these types of games (similarly to 'Orwell: Keeping an eye on you'), definitely give Cyber Manhunt a shot!\t\t\t\t\t
