If you don't get invested in the story, then I think you are very likely to bounce off of the terrible difficulty curve and give up on the game. + Story-rich
+ Relaxing

-- The absolutely ridiculous difficulty curve that's more like a 90 degree angle in the last chapter, I had to use a walkthrough to maintain my sanity and complete the game\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tReally disappointed about this game. I was hoping for some good sleuthing, but as I've read in other reviews the game soon becomes hovering your mouse over text to find predefined clues, reading content optional. All other game mechanics reach like 10% of their potential:
- "googling" for clues: you can really only search for people's names, everything else returns "not found"
- hacking passwords: copy/paste info from another window and click the button
- phishing: you have to trial and error until you find the random sequence they imagined
Aside from that, getting into emails and other accounts, all gets repetitive really fast.
About translations: I read about other reviewers complaining about the English. When I purchased the game they had just released improved translations, but there is still much work to do...
Last point: what I would have liked to see, is a much more open gameplay, where one has to read everything to find clues, take their own notes, and has chances to make mistakes so that there is at least an illusion that the path to success isn't linear.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI was 80% of the way to giving this a thumbs because hey, it's ten bucks and it will kill a few hours of your life, the puzzles are somewhat challenging and clicking around playing pretend-hacker is always diverting.

It doesn't quite get over the finish line for a few reasons:

As much there are ominous warnings about sensitive subject matter and deep thoughts concerning your privacy, the game is REALLY rough from a narrative standpoint. The game is translated from Chinese, and while their English is a great deal better than my non-existent Mandarin, it's still not good, with really clunky dialogue that kills any emotional heft the story may be going for and some variable (but mostly bad) voice acting. And when you're tackling serious topics like sexual assault and bullying and online harassment and such, you shouldn't be giggling at deadpan "he attacked me like an animal" deliveries. In other words, don't expect to lose yourself in the story here.

But whatever, it's a budget game, they couldn't shell out for a localization team, that's forgivable. You can still have a good time playing pretend-hacker and solving puzzles, right?

Well... you could have, maybe, if the game weren't so full of bugs. Some are merely annoying, like someone asking you over the phone the name they used when they e-mailed you and you put in that name and they say it's wrong, so you double-check and look for one of the two other options there, but you don't see it, so you throw your hands up, look at a guide, see the answer, look again at the e-mail wondering where they ever used the name and then just put it in because you've already wasted ten minutes and just want to get on with it. Someone quizzes you on what part of their body was injured, and that information was never made available anywhere. You can try these puzzles repeatedly, but you can't back out if you fail and go double-check stuff again, you have to go all the way out to the main menu and then re-continue the game.

None of this was enough to turn me off the game until I hit Chapter 5. Without going into details, Chapter 5 was so buggy, it forced me to quit and re-load the game three times, and finally reached a point where I really couldn't progress because a specific tool I needed to finish a puzzle wouldn't load. By that point, I was done.

What inevitably earns this game the thumbs down is just that there are much better hacking games and "surveillance is bad" games out there. TIS-100, Hacknet, Grey Hack, Orwell. Even if this game is cheaper, go spend the extra money elsewhere. It will be worth it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSolid procedural social engineering simulator that is marred by what the esteemed screenwriter Orson Borchardt Welles calls "corny dialogue that would make the Pope weep". I would not ordinarily harp on translation , but this game is almost entirely narrative-driven , so it really has to matter in this case. The gameplay loop consists of finding hidden associations between people and events by parsing news stories, hacked emails and IMs, and call transcripts to extract a handful of data points that can then be used to expose more hidden associations, etc. The point is, with few exceptions, everything you investigate is some sort of document, and they all read like a phishing email, which may be terribly ironic here, but not in a clever or fun way. I'd recommend Orwell instead.\t\t\t\t\t
