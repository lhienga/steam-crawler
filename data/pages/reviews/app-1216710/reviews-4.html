8/10

Although Cyber Manhunt may appear to be a hacking simulation game, I believe it is more appropriate to call it a social engineering simulation game. Overall, the strengths and weaknesses of Cyber Manhunt are quite evident: it has an outstanding and thought-provoking storyline that is both contemporary and ironic, with significant real-world implications. However, its gameplay design may not satisfy all players.

If you do not know social engineering, this game can give you a glimpse into the popular social science of gathering information about others through various means. By unraveling the secrets and events behind each character through information gathered from various social media platforms, websites, emails, phone calls, and more, players can engage in activities such as hacking, deception, and disguises to gather intelligence. For players who enjoy puzzle-solving and have patience, Cyber Manhunt can provide an excellent gaming experience. Additionally, the game's storyline is quite deep, depicting various cybercrimes, school bullying, exchange of interests, and blackmail. The game connects the characters through numerous fragmented yet interconnected clues, presenting players with a captivating narrative filled with criticism, irony, reflection, and depth. It is definitely worth a try for any narrative game enthusiast.

The shortcomings of the game primarily lie in its gameplay design. The game itself is relatively short, and the process of collecting clues through mouse clicks takes up a significant portion of the gameplay time. Many puzzle mini-games added to enrich the game's content may not be particularly interesting. Although they diversify the gameplay, some players may find them off-putting. Players familiar with social engineering may also feel that the game's gameplay design and certain plot developments are somewhat contrived. Furthermore, the game's depiction of hacking techniques is rushed and lacks rigor.

Overall, Cyber Manhunt surprised me beyond expectations. I recommend it to those interested in social engineering but with limited knowledge, fans of realism, and enthusiasts of text-based adventure games. If you can accept its shortcomings, you will find that Cyber Manhunt offers great value. On the contrary, if you are not interested in its gameplay and storyline, there is no need to give it a try. It is worth noting that this game draws heavily on Osmotic Studios' Orwell series released in 2016, with the main difference being that this game focuses more on narrative description and characterization, while Orwell offers a stronger interactive gaming experience.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOverall I enjoyed this game and was drawn to it because of the hacking theme. The story lines are interesting, and also on the mature side. The game play is fun, and I enjoyed the way the game prepared you to use the hacking tools and let you explore on your own. There were some parts of the game that I found a bit unbearable, like the long phone call audios and the parts where you called from anonymous number. Those sequences mainly called for using your best guess to choose the right voice prompt, and often I found it intuitive and just wanted to get it over with. Otherwise, I enjoyed the hacking game play style of this game and the attention to detail in many small parts of the game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe English is challenged. The creators do not understand how the internet works. If I type-in Defoss, for example, I should still get search results. In a news article, the company's name is Defoss then Devos. These are not the full name of the company, and without the full name, you get no results when you search.

When you do a new search, sometimes it opens a new tab, other times, it closes a tab you had open. It's unspeakably annoying! MY browser doesn't behave that way; never did, not even NetScape.

There is a part of the game where you have to guess what approaches are going to work best with insane or mentally-unstable people. You can choose between "appease", "intimidate", or "ask for the truth", for example. Now, none of these yield results the first time. There is some secret combination of these that you have to figure-out. You have three tries. You do not know enough about this person to understand how they will react, because they ARE mentally-unstable.

What do I dislike the most? Once you are into any screen like the inference screen, a phone call, hacking, etc., you cannot back out and have no access to your notes; you also cannot escape. I was wanting to double-check someone's interests, but was not allowed to close it or escape it. I wanted to double-check data I had collected while making a phone call. Not being a mind-reader, I didn't know ahead of time what I would be asked. IRL I would be able to retrieve the information upon being asked, like re-visiting a website.

That is not fun. The typo of the company name is highly annoying in a game, where you are searching for clues, as is the inability to search the company name and be shown results. This needs an overhaul.\t\t\t\t\t
