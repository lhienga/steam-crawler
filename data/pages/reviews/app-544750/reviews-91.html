\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI was first introduced to the Soul series, back in the Playstation (PS1) days, beginning with Soul Edge and had also played Soul Calibur on Dreamcast. Sadly, the series never appealed to me in terms of gameplay due to the Tekken-styled (or rather, 3D fighter styled) inputs and lack of flashiness (shallow, I know) - so I never bothered with the series thereafter. In fact, I remembered so little of the series that the only 2 things that come to mind are: The Edge of Soul intro on the PS1 copy and the character, Ivy. So, I decided to get Soul Calibur 6 to sample it as the Deluxe Edition was heavily discounted (at 84%) during the Lunar Sale. And I'm glad that I came back to checking the series out again, and also that I've only purchased it at a heavily discounted price.

At its core, Soul Calibur 6 (SC6) offers a good weapon-based fighter experience which has pretty balanced mechanics (as far as I can tell) and an acceptable level of complexity & execution for a casual player. While there are combos, they are fairly short and isn't something that one must master in order to succeed. Unlike most fighters, each fight in the game is based on 3 rounds rather than 2 (which you find in most titles). There's also a ring out mechanic - a staple of the series - where you can win a round by hitting an opponent out of the ring (which I personally didn't really like).

For single player content, the game has 2 story modes that plays differently:
1) Chronicle of Souls where you play each SC6's character storylines (after finishing the initial main arc).
2) Libra of Soul where you create and play as a custom character with RPG-like elements and an overworld map where you can pick locations to travel to.

The storytelling in both story modes is executed in a Visual Novel fashion (i.e. static pictures and text) and I didn't enjoy it at all. While I do like the static artwork that the story mode uses, the execution felt cheap and it makes it hard to care about the story even though I was initially interested - I eventually resorted to Google instead.

Apart from the above story modes, the other fighting modes that are available are the standard Arcade, Versus and Training modes - yes, just these 3. To anyone looking for the interactive tutorial, it's there but for some reason, is obfuscated in the Libra of Soul after clearing the first mission (referred to as the "Dojo").

One of the more notable feature in the game is the Creation mode (or usually referred to as create-a-soul, CAS) where you can create a custom character for the creative folks out there. If you happen to be the creative sort, be prepared to spend majority of your time here because there's either too much you can fiddle around with, or that it takes ages to get something to look the way you want. The CAS is a rather capable tool, but it has limited options (as in the parts and what you can tune) and can be rather frustrating to use at times, especially when it comes to colors.

For non-gameplay related stuff, there's a museum where you can look at story entries (text), unlocked cutscenes, concept art and listen to the soundtrack. In certain DLC packs, soundtracks from older Souls titles are included and they can be played here as well. One feature I found in the game settings that I rather like but didn't really use much of is the ability to configure what stages use which music - and you can pick any music that is available in the museum. So, say you bought Season Pass 1 & 2, you will have access to all the music tracks in the Soul series and you can use them in the configuration. Sadly, I've never found the series music to my taste and the only song that I really liked, The Edge of Soul, wasn't included. Nevertheless, I still find it to be a feature that I appreciate.

In terms of performance, the game runs well on 1080p with my GTX 1060 6GB at max settings and I've not experienced any major slowdowns or crashes to date. As for presentation, the game looks good but not great by modern standards. The 2D portraits used throughout the game, however, is top-notch.

As for the entire package, I think SC6 is somewhat bare bones. The most crucial gameplay-related stuff (that is fighting-related) is pretty much the 3 standard modes that it offers. I didn't find the story modes engaging to play and its Visual Novel styled execution doesn't seem to go along well with a 3D fighter - it only gives me the impression that they went for it because it was cheaper to make. The static artwork used in the story modes is amazing but the 3D character models looked pretty average by comparison (and visibly different). This is pretty obvious in instances like the character selection screen where it initially displays a 2D portrait then switching to the 3D model - for example, Sophitia looks nothing close to her portrait.

Overall, I think SC6 feels too lacking as a package to recommend buying at full price - that is, unless you are a series fan, the museum might make it worthwhile. On the other hand, I also think the game deserves a strong recommendation at its discounted price as it's a pretty solid weapon-based fighter on its own. If you're deciding to purchase, the Deluxe Edition is no doubt the better package compared to the base game - assuming both are at the same discounted rate.\t\t\t\t\t
