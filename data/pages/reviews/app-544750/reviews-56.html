\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSoul Calibur 6 is a fighting game which carries the tradition of Soul Calibur games in providing 3d arena battles prioritizing melee weaponry as the means of combat. It also carries on the long tradition of giving and taking features, as opposed to linearly improving the experience, making each Soul Calibur game different enough to stand on its own merits, but similar enough to make every trade feel like a profound loss.

On its own terms, Soul Calibur 6 provides numerous characters with varied fighting styles to choose from, an arcade mode which feels like an obligation and an afterthought in its implementation, an ambitious create-a-character driven story mode with RPG and virtual novel elements, and an audacious lore driven story mode which places the main characters' stories all on a coherent timeline wherein events interact to tell a more complete tale. I call this last mode audacious, because some characters' storylines consist almost entirely of lore dumps with barely any battles between them- looking up comprehensive videos I found that all of the lore content of the two story modes comes out to over 6 hours long. All of these modes are interesting diversions and pad SC6 with content and methods for feeling as though you are accomplishing something, making progress in the game.

The real meat of any fighting game, however, is in its combat. Soul Calibur 6 does little to distinguish itself from its predecessors in terms of combat. For those uninitiated, Soul Calibur's main combat consists of slappy horizontal strikes, powerful vertical strikes, kicks for spiciness, directional modifiers to each attack type, and a myriad of satisfying combos to achieve by mixing them all up. You can also grab an opponent, which interrupts blocking and stylishly deals massive damage. There is also a soul gauge which, when filled, allows for special attacks or buffs.

New to SC6 from the previous games is the Reversal Edge mechanic, wherein players can enter into a slow-motion exchange that is basically rock-paper-scissors. It looks neat but I don't care for it much and feel like its main intent is to break up combos and slow the fight down. Oftentimes it feels like a method for a desperate player to force a match to be decided by chance, and when I succeed at any Reversal Edge I never felt any satisfaction for it. It's not a terrible addition and I can understand why somebody would like it, I just could do without personally.

Another new addition, Lethal Hit, was complicated enough for me to figure out that I just never did and it never hindered me much in gameplay to lack that knowledge. Take that for what you will. I'm a fairly casual, noncompetitive player of fighting games, and feel that the game lets me get away with that. At higher levels of online play I couldn't possibly compete for a myriad of reasons, my lack of knowledge about the Lethal Hit being the very least of them, but I never feel like there isn't a place for me to have fun engaging in combat.

A major aspect of the Soul Calibur franchise since the third game has been its character customization. As a huge fan of character customization, let me just say that Soul Calibur's is some of the most fun, streamlined, and surprisingly powerful character customization available. If you're creative enough, there's very few limits to what kind of humanoid you want to fight for you. Unfortunately, with the character removals from the previous games, there are fewer styles and weapons which can be given to the characters. There's also an unfortunate lack of some fighting styles which were previous in previous games but are sorely missed here- such as an unarmed style or hand-axe styles. Regardless, there's a plethora of swords, a double-edged sword, some zweihanders, some polearms, more katanas than an otaku convention, Zasalamel's scythe, and the rest. DLC is definitely worth it for the expansions to available movesets in character creation, especially Hilde, Cassandra, and Hwang.

Speaking of the DLC, each DLC character comes with their own little story mode addition to the timeline story mode. Out of them, I really enjoyed Setsuka's and found the rest pretty forgettable. I did enjoy some of the character interactions from Hilde's story though. I'd recommend all of them, however, because I think the game is at its best when it is its most full.

As of writing this, I have 100 hours in the game. I don't think I'm great at it, and I've spent a lot of that time in the character creator, but I've been playing Soul Calibur games since SC2 on the Gamecube. I've found each one to be delightful and disappointing in their own ways since, and Soul Calibur 6 is no exception. It gives me a great deal of hope for the future of Soul Calibur in a way that other franchises don't, because it always leaves room to improve while never being so disappointing as to warrant being called anything less than a "good game." I really cherish my time with Soul Calibur 6, for all of its ups and downs, and wouldn't hesitate to recommend it. I have never regretted purchasing a Soul Calibur game, even at full price.\t\t\t\t\t
