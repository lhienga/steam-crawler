\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTime to have a real talk about Destiny 2 and figure out if it is worth the time for a new player thinking about picking it up.

F2P:
Reading that the game is Free To Play is misleading. It is more of a free trial where you get some of the most basic modes.
If you go in and say, "Wow look at all these planets that I can't wait to explore" then I am sorry to be the one to tell you. Planets are effectively meaningless unless a quest tells you to go there. Previous season content that gave life and purpose to these places has passed leaving an empty husk of repeatable chores. You get access to the first couple of missions for each expansions but not enough to get you anything beside generic loot you can get anywhere else.
You won't be able to play any of the higher tiers of difficulty for the different activities so you will settle for just casual matches.

Deciding what to buy:
As they continue to add new collections to the store that bundle some DLC, its important to note that majority of content is paywalled. It is one of those situations where you either buy most/the biggest bundle or none. Because it will amaze you how much you miss out on- or won't be able to keep up with others for not having the meta content from a specific activity or bundle.
With the next and final season being delayed I would recommend NOT buying until its shown to be good or not. I promise the preorders aren't that worth it as you can just get that content later anyway.

Is this product for me?:
Destiny 2 can really be a fun game but it is a very different experience when going solo vs a group.
As a new player reading reviews and deciding if this is for you, then you'll likely not be able to solo too much of the game as its designed to be completed with either 3 or 6 players depending on the activity. That being said, the 3 player activities can absolutely be solo'd and they have fun rewards for it but with the clock winding down and the mass layoffs the studio did I don't see the passion going into rewards or great new activities ever coming for d2's future.
If you have either a group of 3 or 6 and want some pretty fun shooting and power mechanics i'd say this would be a fun game for you, but only if you all are willing to sink the time and possibly money into it.

Is this something I recommend?
See I don't know. I've put a lot of time and got my enjoyment out of it- but I don't think I could recommend it for someone new. I got a couple of friends to pick it up earlier in this season, around August 2023. It is entirely competent but doesn't ever teach you how to really enjoy the game now. They are racing for a finish line and accepting all the damage that comes with it. People have been begging for so much for years and now as the game begins to sunset they let what life is left burn away from bungie.

Its sad really. I have had great times while playing, but that doesn't feel like it was Destiny 2 anymore. What this game is, feels like a husk being pulled along by corporate strings, forced to do a little dance as we watch in horror for its the husk of something we once loved.\t\t\t\t\t

A Disappointing Goodbye

I used to play Destiny 2 a lot back then, from its launch, but it's been a year since I quit, and honestly, I don't regret it. The game I once enjoyed has lost its charm, and recent events have only made things worse.

Bungie's decision to fire employees and blame players for the game's troubles felt like a slap in the face. It's clear the game's direction has taken a wrong turn. Destiny 2 used to be a big part of my life, but now it feels like a cash grab.

I'm happy that I moved on, but I can't help but feel like I've lost something important. With my hours played on Battle.net, I've probably clocked in around 3000 hours, and those hours meant something. I hope to find a game that will bring back the same feeling as I had the first time playing Destiny, finishing my first Raid, getting my first real build, and experiencing those unforgettable moments. Destiny 2 used to mean a lot to me. It's sad to see recently how many games are going down the same path, prioritizing profit over player experience.

Here's a list of problems that accumulated over the years:

- New content often felt like old content revisited for various events, leaving a sense of repetitiveness.
- The constant introduction of new, overpowered gear made it almost impossible to play with anything else, forcing you into buying the latest DLC. Or you had gear that never went out of fame because of how op it was. Just balance issues overall
- Some seasons seemed to offer nothing genuinely enjoyable. Overly difficult community challenges had to be changed multiple times, and grinding the same content repeatedly for that one perfect roll was just straight up bad.
- There was no real system in place to modify or farm armor with the desired stats, leaving everything to RNG. (also applied to weapons, but the smithing helped)
- Removing old content seemed unjustified and took away a significant part of what defined Destiny, all for the sake of saving a bit of disk space. In my opinion the worst thing that happened to Destiny
- uninteresting antagonists in my opinion. Who let mega mind into the game man...
- The aggressive microtransaction strategy surrounding skins for new exos and new weapons
- The absence of thrilling 20-minute challenges for secret exotic weapons, which used to hype the community and add depth to the story, was sorely missed. Instead, it became repetitive farming.
- The frustrating inconsistency in Sparrow speed, why not make them all equally fast, rather than having one Sparrow that outperforms the rest.
- Cheaters ruined PVP\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tMy journey with Bungie started, as it did for many, with Halo. I was immediately captivated by the gameplay, the way they had designed the world, the lore, and most importantly, the music. Bungie captured something special with the soundtrack that Martin and Michael brought to Halo, and later to Destiny. The soundtracks would go on to be some of the stuff I listened to the most as the years turned into decades, I think the Halo 2 OST soundtrack "Delta Halo Suite" may have even been the first purchase I made when loading up my brand new iPod Nano back when that was the hot new thing, and if it wasnt the first it was certainly among the first. The game became something I poured a lot of time into growing up, always looking for more secrets and more ways to break out of maps, moving through levels in ways that Bungie certainly did not intend. Halo 2, 3 and Reach all fell into that similar focus. Halo 2 specifically has always been my favorite. I have spent decades now, consuming Bungie content, and being the voice in support of their choices. Some small hope dwindled in the waning days of their ownership of the Halo IP, and the games seemed to really lose a lot of life after 343i took over development.

Then Bungie announced Destiny. I immediately knew from the beginning that this game was going to be something special. The beta test saw myself and a couple close friends running the House of Devils strike over and over and over again for hours, days even. When it finally came to release day, I poured everything into the game, every spare minute was spent grinding for loot, raiding, goofing off with my friends, and enjoying the admittedly very low effort campaign. The story itself was disappointing. Not in the writing, but the content. I have seen what Bungie can do as far as world building and lore goes, and Destiny was certainly not their best effort. Regardless, I stayed optimistic and for a time, was rewarded with expansions like House of Wolves and The Taken King (I rather liked HoW, though that may be an unpopular opinion). Through it all, there were signs and writing on the tower walls that something was changing. The partnership with Activision made a lot of people, including myself, nervous for the future of Bungie as a company, knowing Activision's propensity for shutting down studios. In my mind, Bungie had become to big to fail, they were beloved by a whole generation of FPS gamers, and were solidly among the best studios of the time.

Over time, it became harder and harder to justify the way they went about managing their game, and to a larger extent, their community. Bungie has always been great about engaging with the community, though in recent years it's become pretty clear that they do much more talking than listening, which became more apparent with the release of Eververse and an ingame currency and microtransactions. Microtransactions and preodering were, and and will continue to be a cancer. This move by Bungie had done little to settle the nerve of longtime fans, and many had already stepped away from the games and the company's content. I remained. Through the trials and tribulations of the original game and the controversy within the community, some remained hopeful that there would be a radical shift in the way the game was managed once Bungie and Activision parted ways, and for a brief instant, it seemed that the game would be saved.

Destiny 2 arrived, and I eagerly jump aboard and started my journey anew. The game looked incredible, felt incredible, but also lacked personality. It felt like a graphic update, some shiny new guns and armor, but at the core of everything, it felt empty. I played a while at the beginning, and quickly grew disinterested and even felt a pang of disappointment with the way it had been handled. I took a long break and came back right as Season 1 was starting, and played the out of the game for months afterward, the seasonal activities were loads of fun, balancing and other changes made the game feel fresh, and I was once again on board with Destiny 2. Eventually the honeymoon phase ended, and it once again left me wanting something more tangible. I returned for some seasons, ran some raids with friends, and somewhat jumped back on the loot grind, but at the end of the day the game had become dull, and boring. Repeating the same content over and over again, while other content was slowly vaulted and became unavailable. Once vaulting and sunsetting weapons became the norm, I knew the game wouldn't be something I spent a great deal of time on anymore. I, and many others, grew disenchanted with the loss of content, while Eververse continued to get more and more. It's astounding the amount of stuff they have in their now. Astounding, and sickening.

The company that had created some of my favorite games, had allowed themselves to value the microtransactions more than the players, which has become increasingly more apparent as the last few expansions have come and gone. The game feels hollow now. Hollow, and lifeless. A shell of itself, and a specter of what could have been. This could not be made more apparent than by the recent communications between Bungie and it's remaining playerbase, after the blame of the layoffs were placed not at Bungie's feet for the lackluster effort they've made to repairing and building the game and community back up again, and instead placed it on the shoulders of the players. I have been, admittedly, flaky when it comes to Destiny 2, but this was the straw that broke the spider walker's back. I have since uninstalled the game, and will not be returning.

I met some of my longest lasting friends playing Halo, and Destiny. Those memories I will always cherish and hold in a special place. My love for what was, is only matched by the disappointment of what now is. I will always be grateful for the memories, but this is a stark reminded that you either die a hero, or live long enough to become the villain.

From the bottom of my heart, Bungie, thanks for the memories, and you.\t\t\t\t\t
