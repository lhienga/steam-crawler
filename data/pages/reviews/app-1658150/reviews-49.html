\r\n\t\t\t\t\tReally a quite wonderful and charming game with a lot to be said about it. Mostly positive things to say from here. It's marrying of Farming Sim and Creature Collecting was executed in a way such that you could focus on either aspect at your leisure, without the sense of urgency the former usually comes with and the grinding of the latter.

For a detailed rundown--

Likes:

- The art. It's very simple, readable, and polished. Reminiscent of GBA pokemon games or LoZ Minish Cap. Nothing feels cluttered. It could have maybe benefited from having a bit more variety in the environment, but what is there delivers.

- The designs of the spirits. The majority of the spirits are wonderfully designed. With the exception of a couple "everyday objects with humanoid features slapped on them" designs that make me sigh, they all have a very unique feel to them with their own charm to offer. There's enough variety to their designs and enough similarity between their gameplay (we'll get to that later) that I'm confident in saying there's a few for pretty much anyone to love.

- The balance of the spirits. This game forced me a bit out of my comfort zone that pokemon instilled in me of just grinding my starter and using it to plow through everything. That distinctly will not work in this game. Going into midgame the wild spirits will always show up in groups of three, and there's no real forced targeting system to keep them from attacking the one they're strongest against. And they -will- attack the one they're strongest against, potentially wiping out a type disadvantage party member in the first turn.

Holding onto your starter all the time is impractical at best, and you will be punished for it. Having one favorite of each type and swapping them out depending on where you plan to explore is greatly rewarded.

- "Go your own way". Something I especially appreciated about this game was that the only really necessary point of focus was battling with the spirits. Everything available in the game benefits you to some degree: Farming gives you extra stamina to do more material collection and improve the stats of your spirits, mining can improve your tools, fishing can nab you some extra cash, relationships can give you extra goodies, etc. The greenhouse to grow things in any season is easy enough to unlock early on, removing the sense of urgency of getting crops done by a certain deadline.

Generally, it feels like there's a lot available for you with meaningful benefits, but fairly little forced on you if you just want to focus on exploring and training your spirits.

- Decoration Options: Exploration frequently awards you with crafting recipes for your decoration table, and boy is there a lot of them. Compared to the somewhat bare-bones feel of the decoration in the overworld, the decoration options you have for inside your house feel rather robust, including the ability to make custom wall and floor tiles.

Now, let's get to the games few less-than stellar qualities--

Dislikes:

- Lack of character customization: I don't think the game is in need of an elaborate character creation with dozens of options. But in a game that is clearly friendly towards the LGBT+ and recognizing of ninbinary individuals, it feels rather out of place to lock the player into a distinctly femme presenting player character. They feel very "up to viewer interpretation" most of the time, and are never referred to by gender, but when you go to bed and change into night outfit it feels pretty apparent what the MC's presentation is supposed to be.

Gender presentation aside, it'd just be nice to have a few outfit options (unlockable, maybe?) or the ability to dye your outfit so you can match with your favorite spirit. Beardini supremacy is all I'll say on that last matter.

- Bare bones overworld design. The world is procedurally generated, and I imagine part of how they managed being able to do this in such a simple game is having rather limited assets. Not counting the few objects (spirits, chests, two plants), basically every island has sprites for a big tree, a little tree, a big rock, a little rock, and grass. The sprites change depending on the element of the island you're on, but islands of the same element tend to feel very...same-y. The mines have three sprites, a rock, a copper rock, and a silver rock.

The temples are the most egregious, though. They're very designed-up and neat looking compared to the rest of the game, but ultimately all have the same format of "put object on pedestal, walk forward with x item, fight thing, rinse and repeat."

-Lack of resource restoration: There is no returning of resources in this game from what I've seen. If you clear the trees on an island, they're gone for good. There's no planting seeds for new ones, no having some come back next season or next year. My understanding is that, as it stands, if you clear all the resources off of every island, they're gone for good and you can no longer acquire wood, stone, ore, or fiber.

- Spirits of the same element are basically all reskins: Aside from having their own unique ability, spirits of the same element functionally all get access to the same skills and are subject to the same "you choose their stats" mechanic. This leads to feeling like there's not really much of a sense of discovery with getting to use a new spirit you've found. You level up that new fire spirit and the cards given to it look exactly like the cards of your previous fire spirit. There's a positive to this, in that you're never really gonna run into "well crap, this spirit I really like sucks compared to the others", but a negative in that there's little sense of individuality when it comes to these spirits in a combat context.

- Lack of information: There's really not a ton of hand-holding, for better or worse. There's no sort of tips menu to explain various mechanics of the game, and some things are just...false? For example, a letter you get in the beginning of the game explains mechanics around collecting the game's most precious resource: Moonstones. It says that every island only has one -per season-. But if you loot a moonstone and go back to the island once a new season has begun, there is none to be found. As far as I know, once you've collected a moonstone, the island will not make another.

They've made updates to fix some of these communication issues, but there is still broadly rather poor communication.

- Controls. The updates are showing they've been patching this and...honestly it's still pretty bad. Walking up to things and having it select something behind what you want to select is a frequent occurrence and sometimes it's so bad you just have to play the "get in the right pixel for positioning" game just to select something. Hopefully this one gets remedied with time.

---

Ultimately, none of those cons break the game for me. I've gotten a good chunk of hours out of it and will be going out it for more. There's a simple charm to it that's easy to appreciate and laid back. Perfect for winding down at night.\t\t\t\t\t

From the moment I saw my boyfriend send me this game, I immediately got into it. I had it on my wishlist until the day it came out and I bought the same day! The game is essentially about a young alchemist who has to leave home for a year for training (Kind of like the beginning of Kiki's delivery service). From there, the game becomes a mi of Stardew valley, Pokemon, meets card game combat! I honestly really loved it. It's very peaceful, there's lots of monsters to catch, and people to romanticize.

Even though it came out recently, I found myself playing quite a bit of it. Are there some elements that need polishing? Yes. I felt like going out with the villagers to the same 3 places for dates could be a bit repetitive.
Some villagers were hard to find.
Trees and rocks didn't respawn.
Getting into a relationship feels like it can be a bit difficult.
And a lot of the dungeons had samey feels to it.

But even with all of this, I still enjoy the game and I am looking forward to seeing updates on this game!

So if you like all the stuff I mentioned, make sure to check this out!

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOctober 7, 2023. 4.5 hours on record:
Play the game if you either liked Stardew Valley, loved Stardew but didn't really get into the farming, liked Pokemon-esque games, like MetaWare High School but wish it wasn't a psychological horror, or if you enjoyed the battle system in Paper Mario: Color Splash.

This game's like a dream. It has 18 dates (not Heart Events, actual DATES) per romanceable character, basically three genres jam-packed into one that (mostly) align together, and this is just a personal thing, but I really align with the creators' sense of humor. If you have 20 dollars to spare or you really like one of the games mentioned above, buy this game NOW. That's a threat. (Not really.)

January 23, 2024. 10.2 hours on record:
I'd like to think I've gotten better at making reviews since October. So I'll just be blunt.

I like Moonstone Island. Some parts of it are great! But it is VERY rough around the edges. I'll start with the farming:

I have literally never farmed in this game once. Frankly, I don't even know why I should. Granted, I haven't made it past the first month or two, but I don't really see what I'd need to buy.

Relationships aren't much better. I chose to romance... Ossana? Ossona? Ossano? I don't even remember her name. Wanna know why? Dialogue in this game is extremely limited. There are "talk" buttons, each with percentage chances of increasing your relationship level. And that's it. Aside from like three dialogue lines they say when you first start talking to them. Or, you could go on dates... which are also percentage based and thus, if you're lucky enough, you can get a date first try with a villager you've never talked to. The percentages scale with your level, but still.

If you really want romance, my advice would be to focus on "talking" to one person and brute forcing a date with another. You'll get somewhere eventually.

But romance in this game is... almost boring. And funny story, the villagers comment on how little YOU talk in some of the dates. Since, silent protagonist and all. I don't mind it, but I feel a lot of people... definitely will.

There's a reason I remember every Stardew Valley bachelor, bachelorette, and even villager to an extent. They're memorable. I remember in my frist playthrough almost clear as day, I wanted to go after Maru, then I thought about going after Penny, and after getting 10 hearts with EVERY bachelor and bachelorette, I married Haley, and Haley to this day is still my favorite bachelorette and character(?) in Stardew.

In Moonstone... The cyan haired one I chose first and the one I'm currently still going after is... Ossana? Then there was also the pink haired one (Terra?) and I also tried dating the goth girl (Uhh...) out of brute force just for funsies and it actually worked out.

Now to the Spirits! These are the saving grace of the game. Collecting them is fun, they feel balanced, and whenever I die in combat, I 100% feel it was on me for being too headstrong or encountering something way stronger than I was.

They're all so unique and wacky and charming and... gosh. I chose Capacibee as my first Spirit and they're still my favorite, the little rascals. I don't remember the other two on my team as clearly, but I haven't played since like October (or November or December, don't remember) so that's on me.

TL;DR: Moonstone Island excels at what it does new. What it shares with OTHER farming games (cough cough Stardew cough cough)... leaves a lot to be desired. 6/10, 6.5/10? It's definitely not for everyone, I can say that much.

I still really like the humor of the devs, though.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI have gotten so much fun and joy from this game and I am not even close to putting it down. At first glance and upon hearing all of the gameplay elements that go into the game: life/farm sim, creature collection/battler, and deckbuilder, it sounds like a lot and like it should not work. However I find the opposite to be true and that there is a lot to unpack and explore in this game, which keeps it exciting. There are plenty of choices on how you spend your time and although there is tension in battles and with hazards, the game is also fairly forgiving if you lose, which brings down the stress quite a bit. Pair it all up with soothing music, cute spirits, and the joy of flying from island to island, and I would definitely recommend this game.\t\t\t\t\t
