\r\n\t\t\t\t\ti want to like this game and was very excited to try it out but at the moment i can't recommend it. there is a huuge potential in this game though and i'm looking forward to see how it changes in the future.

there is stuff to do, technically, but all of it feels a bit pointless (there is a thin bigger plot and quests) or weirdly grindy. i'm at a point where i kind of have stuff to do and islands to explore but i can't go too far because my monsters levels aren't high enough and they level up slowly against monsters of their own level (also because the fighting can get slow because of rng). you don't have any way to level them without battling and i'm not looking forward to finding a new monster to add to my group since i'll have to grind them up to others level. usually in these sorts of game you can have monsters in reserve that get exp when you battle difficult monsters your higher levels can handle but in this game all three are on the field and i'm pretty sure your monster doesn't get exp if they faint mid-battle.

the pixel look is lovely but i dislike the character art and not being able to customize your character is a huge miss - you can't even talk, you have no personality, but yet you're stuck on this look. i don't particularly like the npcs either and there are no old people or kids in this town.

also, the beginning of this game feels.. awful? it's very rng - you need to destroy rocks and hope you find a cave under. then you have to travel to different islands to find moonstones - but wait! you can only do that when it's dark out. and the clock is ticking. and there is only one moonstone per island. which you won't know until your dad sends you a mail (or you don't realise it before your dad sends you a mail). and on top of that you can only have three monsters and if you want a new one you have to release one of them before you try to catch a new one.

and when you finally, finally get the barn you have to craft specific spots for each monster or they don't have a place to stay. then you have to put food for them (awkwardly, since you have to navigate between monsters and the controls are sometimes wonky). and then you realise it's pointless to even keep them because they don't seem to get levels in the barn so you can't use them in combat anyway.

overall - i hope one day i can change this review to positive but at the moment i don't see myself playing this game very much.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tMoonstone Island feels like a game that should be everything that I like in a game, but falls short on everything.

Spirit Collection system feels like there's a lot missing, the combat is fun but often times feels hindered - there are type advantages but I found that this never mattered as much since I had Spirits that could bash, one-shot, and buff each other. The type advantage of breaking 1 single armour felt weak as a consistent mechanic since I could just bash, stun, then one-shot the following turn. You are able to feed and buff your spirits, this also works on wild spirits, but again this mechanic is overshadowed by mid-game because you are able to build spirits such that you can combo them in a single turn.

In general the game deters players from collecting spirits, the mechanic of taming a creature is quick and uninteresting, and taming itself depends on the amount of resources you're willing to farm - and continue farming, as it costs a large amount of wood and vegetation to sustain the spirit collection aspect of the game. The lack of evolution's as well as card variety is disappointing.

Farming System just feels very shallow, by around 8 hours in I had already unlocked a majority of the tool upgrades, and felt that there was little to no reason to farm (or invest into the tools) as you could easily craft a sprinkler that renders the watering can redundant. There is a wide variety in plants, however, collecting most of them is halted behind very expensive Moonstone crafting recipes. The only reason I was able to collect Fire and Lightning based plants is because I had gotten lucky and found consumables that would allow me to briefly visit these places.

Romance/Social System is very underwhelming, NPC dialogue is basic and uninteresting, most NPC's lack any personality or character, and the RNG system of choosing Talk(75%), Joke(30%), and Flirt(15%) feels robotic at most. By the end of the first Season I had maximum affection with a majority (6/11) of the villagers. There is little to go off of with Gifting, and I found that the Gifting mechanic was completely unnecessary since you gain more affection exponentially from talking. Cutscenes give a little bit of personality and character but this is limited to a few lines of dialogue, and I don't enjoy having to ask every character to a date (always at 20:00) just to experience that. I haven't found any reason to enter any of the NPC's houses, the few that I have, had nothing to gain or explore as a player. Overall I just didn't care about the NPC's once I got max affection from them. The time system doesn't mesh well with this game as I found that I would have to cut my exploration short so I can watch a 10 second cutscene where a character talks about their one insecurity. The time system just often times feels so intrusive in a game that's trying to be wholesome.

Exploration System was fun to start, but eventually became very tedious. Once I had realised that there are several islands that just house trees, plants, or the occasional Shrine - the exploration aspect became less fun and more work. Spirits will constantly follow you if you don't have a perk (low levels only) and collection animations last so long that the spirits can easily just camp over you and force you into combat. This becomes a problem especially later on when you're on an island that has hazards. Island exploration eventually became a monotonous job of collecting plants or resources, and hoping I could find a precious Moonstone so I can move forward in the game. There is a lack of platforming and methods of scaling the Z axis, which makes exploration often times frustrating, especially when spirits can traverse the Z axis for free. And since most islands lack a variety of uniqueness to them, I would often just tell myself to save the time and just go to a new island.

Crafting System is completely redundant, I very rarely needed to craft anything other than bridges and sprinklers an hour into the game, eventually you will need to craft tables that have exclusive recipes, and EVERYTHING is locked behind the precious Moonstones, the game feels so tedious because everything is locked behind the acquisition of moonstones which you get very little of.

I think the game visually is beautiful, however my experience playing the game felt shallow, most of the mechanics and systems just lack synchronicity and Moonstone Island falls short because of this. As of right now I do not think it is worth playing and further, I do hope that these systems feed into each other moving forward.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhile the review itself is very much negative, I would recommend this game because I see the potential that the game has.

This game tries to be a fusion between Pokemon, Legend of Zelda, and Stardew valley. While this is a very interesting concept, this game doesn't fully utilise the mechanics of any of these games to provide an enjoying experience. It seems like it tries to be different by not adding what makes each of the aforementioned games good.

What I can describe this game to be is that It's a jack of all trades, but all of the abilities are basic.

For example:

Music:
There is a lack of variety in battle music. In a world filled with different biomes and environments, I expect myself to feel as though I am in that place. Terraria for example has excellent immersion when it comes to its soundtrack.

Battle:
While each biome presents its quirks, its spirits are not different from eachother other than their actual abilities. Their cards are limited to their type which means that all spirits that are of poison category, have the same poison cards. Not only do I want unique abilities that come with the spirit, I want there to be special cards that are only for that spirit.

Additionally, I did search up that evolutions will be a things in the future, so I hope this mechanic would change, but I still complain that evolution should've been a part of the game in the first place.

Animations:
During battle, spirits do not have an attack animation aside from their card moves. They would just walk up to their enemy and just stand there while an animation of their card plays. I do not mind the card animations, they are completely fine. It doesn't even have to be indicative of their type of move, they should all have an animation that shows that they are attacking.

For example: Ankylo, the dinosaur spirit should have a chomping animation.

The cave system:
With preference, I do not like how we find caverns by chance by breaking rocks in the surface. It could probably take multiple days before I could find a cavern to grab some materials to make new crafting stations and items. While the caverns do visually yield a lot of resources, it actually only takes a few days before those resources are gone, where I would have to find another cavern to mine in.

For a farm game like Stardew Valley, I feel as though the reason why Stardew's caves can be found in the town's mines is that the materials can be found easily that corresponds with the player's progress within the game. It's very tedious that I would have to find new caves to expand my farm and progress.

While this game involves more exploring than Stardew Valley, I suggest open caves that are like the dungeons where we can just see the entrances without the need to break open rocks to reveal them. For exploration, they can be implanted into some of the islands. As well as moonstone, instead of materials replenishing every season, caves can restore their resources every year.

Relationships and Dialogue:
While I do like how the player can increase the relationship status with the NPCs, I wish there would be more dialogue that is associated with them that shows their personality. I don't want to go on a date with them just so I could see more of what they are.

With the relationship status system that features conversational and gift options, a great idea would be that when choosing the talking option with a character, they would hint on what they would like as gift. Like successfully talking to Zed for example, he would say something like "Making stonefruit pie is one of my favourite things to do!", and so the player would give Zed a stonefruit and be grateful. A simple sentence like that can help the player involve themselves more into the NPCs' relationships and further their understanding with them without going on dates.\t\t\t\t\t
