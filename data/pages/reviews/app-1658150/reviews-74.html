\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm only 2 hours in and I can already see the many more hours I will be putting into this splendid game.

The art style is gorgeous, the music is lovely, so far the Spirit design is original and interesting, and in some cases, ABSOLUTELY ADORABLE.

I really like that there seems to be so much to do, and anyone who's played a card battler like Slay the Spire will find familiarity in the battle system. In game days feel long enough to me, and you have abundant energy right out of the gate, with easy ways to restore it if necessary.

The medallion Spirit management system is rather interesting, as is the nature of upgrading your Spirit decks.

I will say that there are definitely some QoL improvements to be made, especially in movement and using tools (it makes you wait for animations to complete before you can move, so things like using the scythe just don't feel fluid), and sometimes the game stutters or freezes for a blip, but it's negligible.

The homages/influences are very clear, and I love the Ghibli territory this treads (what originally drew me to the game!). The guardians in the shrines look rather influenced by the guardians out of Castle in the Sky,

The world is vibrant, exploration is rewarded, I can already tell it's going to be a little bit grindy, which I like. Some negative reviews say it tries to do too much and doesn't do anything well, but they're rather incorrect. Especially if you're not overly snobby about each genre Moonstone Island pulls from. It's so far just a lovely experience, and I can't wait to get to play more.

EDIT: They already fixed the scything gripe I had. So far, this small dev team has me beyond impressed.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLovely pixel-graphics. Fun characters. Chill vibes. Grow crops, craft stuff, make money, buy furniture, etc. Also collect monsters and make them fight for you. Sounds great, right?

Sure. If you neglect to ask the central question: What about the actual, you know, GAMEPLAY? And that, unfortunately, is where it stumbles. Card-based combat that leaves you entirely at the whims of the RNG, exacerbated by the 'armor' system. The idea is to use ONE kind of card to break down the enemy's armor, then use a DIFFERENT kind of card to deal damage after inflicting armor-break, which leaves the enemy vulnerable for a turn. Do you get to CHOOSE what kind of card you draw? Of course not! So here, have a bunch of damage-dealing cards that will do 0 damage because of the enemy's high armor. Oh, did you just manage to inflict armor-break at long last? Then here, have an entire hand full of now-useless armor-reduction cards and low-damage/utility cards.

I genuinely don't understand how these card-battle systems have become so frustratingly PERVASIVE in the indie-scene in recent years. Other than, perhaps, that they're just really easy to make?

Anyway, the rest isn't much better. The pacing of the early game is just painfully off. At the start, you can only own 3 monsters, and have no way to store extras. Reaching the point where you have a Monster Barn for that purpose takes WAY too long - you'll come across loads of interesting monsters along the way, but can ya catch 'em? NOPE! The game doesn't even offer you the option of replacing one of your current critters with a newly-caught one, it just goes "No room, byeee!" and that's it. The actual catching is also extremely simplistic, lacking any of the strategy and nuance you'd expect from a dedicated monster-collecting game.

There's also the way 'Mines' are absolutely key to your advancement, but - at least early on - the only way to find them is to break every large rock you can find, which is a slow, boring, and of course, RANDOM affair. Maybe you'll stumble on one pretty quick! Maybe you'll spend a whole day breaking rocks and have nothing to show for it 'cept several hundred useless pieces of stone. Do ya feel lucky, punk?

I could go on. The game's beautiful to look at, sure, and with so many AAA-games coming out as hopeless lumps of bugs and copy-pasted features, it's tempting to just shower any halfway decent indie-game with praise. This one, however, unfortunately doesn't deserve it. It isn't TERRIBLE, and it's certainly in a far better technical state than anything the AAA industry seems capable of producing, but there's just way too many frustrating aspects, and the way the RNG is just constantly up in your face, making it perfectly clear that your success or failure has nothing to do with skill or strategy and everything to do with luck, earns it a thumbs-down from me.\t\t\t\t\t
