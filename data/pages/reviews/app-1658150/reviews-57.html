This game blends a whole bunch of genres in a way that works really well. If you are a fan of these cozy farming/town games, card battlers, and creature collectors: you will find a lot to love here.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'd give this game a sideways thumb if I could. I enjoyed it but it did disappoint me in a several ways. The game is a lot shallower than it appears at first. Sure there's a hundred handcrafted Islands but they're all pretty much the same. if you thought this game might scratch an itch that you have for exploration, look elsewhere. (FYI, I did finish the game, but did not 100% it.)

GAMEPLAY SPOILERS AHEAD.

…The islands all have one moonstone, often a dungeon, and sometimes a teleporter thingy. Really there are just five types of islands corresponding to five different elements. Also, the islands are laid out on a giant, predictable, square grid, which looks pretty silly when looked at from above.

Once you've seen a few of each island type, you've pretty much seen them all. The same goes for the spirits. As far as their stats and moves go, I found very little difference between each of the fire spirits, each of the poison spirits, and so on. If you're comparing this to Pokémon, I'd say that Pokemon in general are about 10 times more complex (and interesting) than these spirits. I leveled one of my guys all the way up to 50 and got no new moves for the last 25 levels.

The optimal strategy that I found was to just catch Spirits at as high a level as possible, then use those to battle others to level up my ability to catch even higher level spirits, etc. This means that I had to abandon my few starting Spirits which I had grown quite attached to but they were simply too weak to continue on. Unfortunately, these high-level Spirits have tons and tons of junk cards in their deck which you have to spend a lot of cleanse talismans to remove, though it was worth doing.

However, I shouldn't have bothered. It turns out that you can grow plants in the greenhouse that provide experience to your spirits directly. And quite a lot of it, too. And they can be picked over and over again. (I'm talking about Willowisp. Spoilers!) So, there's no need to even battle anymore. Combine that with a tool that directly turns stamina into growth for plants, and you'll find there's no reason to even try to battle anyone anymore. Later, you got a staff that lets you teleport over enemies, even dungeon bosses, meaning you don't have to battle a single Spirit to complete dungeons anymore. (This might be a bug/oversight, since I didn’t get an achievement for beating all the dungeons, even though the game says I did)

The battle system is actually quite fun, and it's the best part of the game… well, until you discover overpowered cards like “Blind Blow +”, costing only 1 energy, with the description “Draw the top two cards from your deck and reduce their energy costs to zero.” …and it's easy to get, too. This might as well be called the “win the game card.”

That's the main problem with Moonstone Island. For most of the mechanics that it has to offer, it also offers a convenient way to skip right over them… leaving you with little game left in your game. You certainly could choose to play the game without “abusing” these mechanics, but it's not the player's job to balance a game like this. And frankly, the game has very little replay value. It's just shallow.

Oh, and there's a boss that works differently from everything else in the entire game. This boss has a power that will randomly increase the energy costs of the cards in your hand by 1, every 3 real-time seconds. And it never stops. This is absolutely insane in a game that is otherwise completely turn-based. I could hardly believe it. I ended up pausing the game every time I looked at a card just to give myself some time to think, as usual. I was able to beat it easily by doing this, so I think they made that boss easier to compensate for this bizarre power, but it just goes to show that I don't think the game designers knew what they were doing. I'm sure that many others will be complaining about this as well. I'm also sure that some people loved it. But it definitely has no place in a turn-based game like this, though I really can't properly articulate the exact reason why. It just felt so wrong.

There are other bizarre small issues and oversights, like the fact that trees never regrow, but you're never told this. So if you clear-cut an island thinking you can just plant a bunch of trees later, you're out of luck. Permanently. I didn't even know that talismans regenerate each season, so I started hoarding them until the next season came around and they all, mercifully, came back.

There are a lot of ideas here, and I don't think any of them are particularly fully thought out, other than the battling, which again is pretty good except for the few cards that break the game.

This game was clearly made with a lot of love and passion, but it has a lot of rough edges on it. Like, I couldn't even level up my spirits because the only islands/dungeons available were fire islands and the team I had selected was mostly weak against fire. On top of that, I couldn't find an island that was at the same level as me; it was just much higher or much lower than I could handle. That is, until I figured out that you can just grow crops to earn experience and then the game was a breeze from that point on.

Again, the game designers put in a lot of things that let you just skip over parts of the game, possibly due to a lack of confidence in the systems they have created.

Oh, and I absolutely hated the way that the game handles tools. They are all independent from your inventory and on a giant wheel with awful fiddly little arrow buttons that let you swap individual tools for something else, but you have to hold down a button to get to it. I'm on a PC so this wasn't necessary at all since I have plenty of keys to choose from, and I absolutely never got used to it. For a tool like the warp whistle it makes sense to have to hold it down because you want to make sure that the player actually wants to warp home. For the tool wheel, having to hold down a button was just frustrating. Also, using the rest of the inventory hot bar was clunky and annoying. It was just slow to scroll through. I admire them for trying something different from Stardew /Minecraft/etc, but it just fell short.

A lot of people say that this game is Stardew Valley + Pokemon, But frankly, those games are better than this one. It's not that it's impossible to combine those two ideas. They've done a decent job. But there is a clear lack of experience showing through here, and some deep structural problems with the game that I'm not sure are able to be solved. Will they just completely get rid of the crops that provide experience? Probably not. They seem to be a big part of the game. But they shouldn't be here at all. I felt like I had to use them because leveling up the traditional way was getting to be quite tedious.

Well, that’s all I can think of for now. Sideways thumb. Had some fun. But won’t be back.
