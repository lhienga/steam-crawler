\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEdited review: Terror From the Void is the best thing to happen to PP. It's an amazing improvement in story and gameplay. It captures the spirit of the game, extends the play by reducing the artificial urgency of the original while replacing it with a sense of creeping dread. It does the same thing for PP that Long War did for XCOM.

I rarely run mods, and then almost never before I've beat the game on vanilla. I've never beaten PP, but TFtV gives me hope that maybe, just maybe I will someday. Bravo modders, and props to Snapshot for embracing it.

Original review: At first a laggy buggy mess, PP:Y1 has greatly improved. The DLCs have fixed some strategic gameplay issues, and the patches and hotfixes have improved gameplay from "cross your fingers and hope it doesn't crash" to "about as stable as WotC".

A fitting successor to the original XCOM, depth of story + side quests + much better combat mechanics (who here wouldn't donate a body part for free aim in Those Other Games, eh?) = recommendable.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPhoenix Point is Lovecraft-inspired turn-based strategy made by some of the original creators of the original X-COM games (the ones before 2012 Enemy Unknown reboot).
This is one of those weird cases where i cant simply say that the game is good or bad.
Did i enjoy the game? Yes. Most of the time. Would i recommend it? Yes
But with some "buts".

First thing out of the way: the game is essentially an indie-project funded by a kickstarter. As a result there's a few places where the game feels... Unrefined, should i say?
"Cinematics" are just sets of mostly still images with voiceover, no actual animation. Clearly a shortcut, but an understandable one. Not that i can even see them, because they do not work for me because of some unity engine bug (got fixed with next update).

Maps feel... How can i put it... Soulless? Lacking in detail? Even XCOM:EU had maps that inspired certain atmosphere and felt like real places, minus couple of exceptions. PP's maps are kinda bland and liveless. I don't know of its a result of general design, visual style or something else, but... I don't like them.
There's also not quite enough of them. "Pandoran Nest" basically has only 2 map variants that look pretty much identical and by the time you will be assaulting your 20th nest you will know them by hand. The game tries to spice it up by giving you random spawn points, but at the end of the day it's still the same map.
(Map variety was improved with later updates, it seems. Not quite there still, but better than it was)

I'm currently in the process of writing big-ass review that i might potentially make into a video, and simply nobody would read it here otherwise, so i'll try to keep it short from here.

The single best thing about this game: lovecraftian lore, world-building and atmosphere.
Short summary: in 2022 global warming started melting glaciers, crabs started attacking people, creepy mist came from the sea, a good chunk the humanity just walked off into the sea and never came back, then literal crabmen started appearing attacking everything that wasn't one of their own.
2nd coming of the mist dealt even more damage, and 3rd coming is where you join the fray.
This time this is it. Either you find a way to destroy pandoran threat forever, or its game over for humanity.

Strategic Layer: Great, but bloated. During late game the sheer amount of micro-management is pretty overwhelming, but some people might find it pleasant.
XCOM, for example, arbitrary limited player to 1 plane that only could be sent on 1 operation, while aliens would try and do 3-4 simultaneus moves. This forced player to make choices and face their consequences.
Here, player can build any amount of planes and outfit them with any amount of soldiers, eventually having a full squad of experienced soldiers having every continent on lockdown. Of course, it costs crap ton of resources and won't happen soon, but its something that you can do, eventually.
Some UI shortcoming also became more clear with time, but nothing too critical.

Base building: simplistic, nothing particularly special. You can have many bases at once and each base can be outfited with individual facilities that have stackable effects giving it a specific purpose or role. Like having a base with 4 training facilities that grant any soldier in the base crap ton of EXP, or having a base with multiple living quarters and medical facilities, so soldiers could rest / heal there REAL quick.

There is a "diplomacy" system in this game, but its pretty... Flat. Doing bad things to factions (like stealing their stuff) or taking actions in the world that they don't approve of worsens your relationship with that faction, while potentially improving with another. Destroying pandorans' nests improves relationships with everyone. Apparently the optimal tactic is to just be a to everyone equally and eventually they will forgive you, because while you stole their expensive aircraft and killed 4 guys protecting it... You also sabotaged someone else's food supply and that makes it worth it.
Yes, it doesn't make much sense. Trading or helping to repair stuff in their havens have no effects on this either, or at least i couldn't see any.

Combat: Has its gimmicks, but ultimately somewhat stale. Soldiers desperately lack defensive abilities and items. Its all about dishing out as much damage as possible by spamming the out of soldiers' abilities. Yes, XCOM had that issue too but at least there WERE defensive options, like smoke grenades, flashbangs, yadi yada. PP has none of that.

Instead PP prouds itself on Physics Based Combat. Every projectile shot by a weapon is an actual projectile with individual spread that deals individual damage to the individual body part that it hits. For example, you can destroy a weapon or a weapon-holding limb of the enemy and that will forbid them from taking offensive actions using that weapon or limb.
THIS is PP's "defensive action". You just destroy opponent's limb that gives you most trouble and there we go. Defensive action, achieved through offensive application of firearms. Interesting mechanic for sure, but ultimately its all about shooting things, just shooting them in specific places.

Cover is not as important as it was in XCOM.
Because it operates on "realistic" physics cover turns out to be absolutely worthless most of the time. A slightly better angle on that corner and the enemy sniper will have absolutely no issues in hitting your soldier. Usually >45 degress is all it takes to counter any high cover.
This introduces an annoying aspect that you have to ACTUALLY understand how sightlines work in this game. Just because the game tells me that my soldier can shoot the enemy from this here particular position - it doesn't mean that he actually can. Sometimes it turns out that he see like 3 pixels of the enemy and there is no way he's going to hit that.
Soldiers are also and will, sometimes, just shoot their own cover point blank. Nothing like watching your soldier shooting a corner he's hiding behind.
And AI is cheating because he understands sightlines WAY better than you.
"Hey, that unit just moved out in the open, there is no way he is that stup~ oh, i literally cannot hit him in there because he's being blocked by some 4 pixels tree branch. Fascinating."

Vehicles are and are only good for scavenging operations or other operations where actually killing enemies is not a priority.
(kinda fixed with upgrades from Kaos Engines DLC)

Pandorans' evolution is random, from what i understand, and is not actually tied to players' tactics. It's also kinda underwhelming. Yes, couple of times pandoran units will get access to new abilities or tricks, but most of the time its just extra armor, extra health and a weapon with a bigger barrel.

All and all: a fascinating experience sometimes mixed with inconvinience, bugs, overwhelming micro-management and lack of polish.
If anything that i mentioned sounds interesting to you: go on and try it.

For reference: my first blind playthrough took me 130 hours of playtime to beat. Make of that what you will.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo if you can't tell Phoenix Point is a Xcomesque game with some large changes in gameplay. Phoenix point rarely introduces new enemies (although in the end there's still quite a few). Your enemy, like you, is constantly improving getting new mutations like armor, abilities, and weapons. Also you no longer have Xcom's bs chance to hit system. Instead you have a circle/cone that your round(s) will land in. This allows you to target limbs, but also makes guns operate more realistically where an enemy standing in the open is always going to get hit by at least a few assault rifle rounds, snipers are all or nothing, and shotguns will always hit up close, but see damage drop offs at range. Also unlike Xcom where you have 1 team of super soldiers do everything (unless you're running long war) you actually have multiple bases, vehicles, aircraft, and squads. So this means you can't rely on one team for everything and losses aren't catastrophic since they're more or less a localized problem. It's also worth stating that they have a really good leveling up system. So when you get skill points you can improve baseline stats or grab abilities. Every soldier gets 2 classes past level five and has some personal skills unique to that soldier you can unlock. Ultimately I found the system a lot more versatile than xcom and it made me actually care about a soldier's individual abilities. Personally I hope to see more squad based strategy games like Phoenix Point in the future.

Pros:
-Good Story (utilizes an actual virus, but gives it scifi capabilities)
-Multiple Endings based on choices
-Better Leveling up system than xcom
-Amazing Shooting System
-Actually feels like managing a global organization
-No xcom rng bs
-Lots of choices between equipment, vehicles, and planes

Cons
-Untraditional gameplay can cause poor choices early on (which can cost you the game later, I'm on try 3 and never lost xcom)
-Factions constantly warring despite being pushed to extinction is ridiculous, also constant starvation is annoying I would prefer pop loss be based on inability to save havens
-Some monsters are just stupid OP (looking at you umbra)
-Monsters are *always* improving while many equipment upgrades for player are just differentiation (damage vs accuracy, ext)
-Managing 6+ teams can get tedious\t\t\t\t\t
