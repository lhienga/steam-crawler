\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA very good strategy game from the original X-Com creator Julian Gollop with weaknesses towards the end.


What I really liked:
- Tactical combat is way better and more predictable compared to the later X-COMs with the aim and cover system used here. No more 95% hit chance misses with a mob standing 1m in front of you and only visible parts can be hit. Some cover may be penetrated though. The very diverse builds that are possible here and the huge weapons array at your disposal allow for interesting strategic options.

- The world map is huge and gives you opportunity to explore, re-establish bases, trade and enter missions. Resources are limited so you have to plan some what to build, which bases need certain facilities and what items you really want on your squads.

- The Factions have an interesting background and beliefs and offer very diverse tech options.

- And the story and the way it is told and how it unfolds the further you progress, that's very, very well done and atmospheric.

What I didn't like:
- My biggest critic point is, that after I passed the 40ish hour mark combat became tedious work. I've seen everything already a few times and combat environments don't offer enough diversity to have you try different approaches.

- Additionally some builds are comically overpowered; a melee build with Rapid Clearance (+2AP on kill), equipped Vengeance Torso (1AP melee attacks), speed ~ 15ish, obligatory Berserker as 2nd class and on top of it the Brawler perk (+50% melee dmg) let one man do the job of a whole squad, only faster and without giving the enemies the chance to even react. I cleared small to medium maps with such monsters in one turn, some big enemies included.

- At some point you explored most of the map and no more of the very atmospheric mini events which reveal much of the lore of the game are there to be found and investigated. You just rush from one base defense to the other and clear some infestation on the way.

As a conclusion I'd wished for a shorter, more compact game with less repetition.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPhoenix Point is an excellent successor to the original X-Com games (from the 1990s). While Firaxis' newer XCOM series is a bit better balanced, a bit more fun, and a lot more stylish, Phoenix Point is a little truer to the original X-Com formula.

Overall, Phoenix Point is a little more complicated than XCOM in ways that are mostly good. It incorporates a firing system that I've been dreaming about seeing in a turn-based combat game for years: where you can shoot anywhere you want and it simulates the bullets' trajectories to determine if you hit anything or not, and where cover is not magical for anybody. Cover doesn't simply reduce the hit chance by X percent. Instead, you just move to put something solid between the enemies and you - the taller and wider and stronger the better. Your guys will crouch down near low objects automatically, but any bits that are sticking out can potentially be shot. A stray bullet can miss one guy and hit another. Cover can be destroyed and strong shots can penetrate cover to hit the enemies behind (though the penetration values are a bit unrealistic).

The overwatch system requires you to draw out a cone where you determine the width and length of the cone. This makes it more finicky, but also gives you more flexibility. I also like the fractional action point system which allows you to mix moving, shooting, and other actions in any order and as much as you want, as long as you have action points left. Special abilities have no cooldown but are activated by spending will points - using an ability reduces your will - which seems kind of weird but I like it. I think the idea is that you strain yourself to pull off your special abilities, which puts you closer to panicking, suffering mental attacks, etc. You get will points back by killing enemies and a couple other ways.

Equipment is more flexible, too. There are separate head, torso, and leg slots where you can place different kinds of armor. Each slot also supports a modification that you can put on top of the armor, and later you can unlock mutations and augmentations too, letting you further customize your men. You can give a heavy a sniper rifle, or a sniper a rocket launcher, or take the jump jets off your heavy and give them to your sniper so he can fly up to rooftops. Accuracy will suffer if you use the "wrong" weapons, but each character can take two classes, so you can make a sniper/heavy hybrid, or whatever.

Speaking of classes, there are seven: the basic assault, heavy, and sniper that you have at the start plus another four that you can unlock through the factions: berserker (melee), technician (robotics & healing), priest (mental powers), and infiltrator (stealth assassin). There is also a variety of vehicles like tanks, hover-thingies, etc. which can scout, carry men around, and provide heavy weaponry and mobile cover.

On the down side, I mentioned that the game is less balanced than Firaxis' XCOM. It also gives you fewer hard choices to make when it comes to your squad. In Firaxis' XCOM, you can only choose one perk at each level-up, but in Phoenix Point you can eventually unlock all perks on all characters, so perks aren't how you distinguish characters. The customization mostly comes from your equipment and class combinations. Characters do get a few randomly selected, unique perks to differentiate them, though.

On the strategic layer, there are numerous points to explore and many human settlements belonging to the three factions (plus a few independent settlements). You have the whole world to fly around in, but your range is limited by how many refueling points you've explored. Almost the whole globe starts out in the fog of war, where aliensmutants are out there doing their missions and destroying human settlements. You lose if the mutants kill too many people. There are three opposing factions to align with, and if you get on friendly terms they will give you cool new technology. You can also attack them, steal their tech, steal aircraft and other vehicles, etc. if you want to be a bastard. Ultimately, you can only choose one faction's approach to the endgame but you can be friendly (or not) with all of them. You won't be able to save every city (and if you try, you'll stretch yourself too thin and probably lose the game), so the strategic map is where hard choices get made.

I haven't finished the game yet, but so far I've greatly enjoyed it. It's not quite as harrowing (or complex) as the original X-Com, and I haven't seen anything as scary as a terror mission yet, but it's a worthy successor and I fully recommend this game even at full price. Unfortunately, the DLC seems to be a bit weak, but there's at least one worth getting. The game also has some unofficial mods, for additional replayability.

Unfortunately, the game has some bugs and the developers don't seem very responsive in the forums, but they haven't ruined my fun yet. Just make sure to keep a couple save slots, and save every so often. Another complaint I have is that the game is a bit too long.
