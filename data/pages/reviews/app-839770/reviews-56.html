\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI finished a campaign, but never again. The game is still riddled with bugs that really hurt in missions. The ones I've listed below have been reported but are still not fixed:

* Overwatch bug: often on a load of a in-mission save file, soldiers will not be able to Overwatch because the game claims they can't do it "again". Somehow it grabs the state from the previous turn.
* Quick Aim Action: occasionally, triggering Quick Aim does not decrease the number of action points for the next shot.
* (not sure if this one was reported) Shot sometimes miss even if the entire aiming area is over an enemy.

While I can appreciate that damage and shooting is more realistic than similar games, some things still don't make sense. For example, a shotgun can completely miss if there's even a sliver of your aiming area that doesn't cover an enemy, but that's not how a shotgun works! It shoots all bullets in a spread, so if 95% of your aiming area covers an enemy, _at least_ 1 of 10 bullets should hit even if it does no damage.

The whole system of shooting and damage calculation is too highly variable in a lot of situations for my tastes. Some randomness is supposed to be there, but especially on an easier difficulty, that shouldn't go from the enemy killing multiple civilians and soldiers to barely any damage if you reload the same enemy turn.

Difficulty scaling is also an issue. Very quickly you will encounter enemies that your regular (or even upgraded) guns do almost no damage. The weapons you receive and research aren't always better than your base weapons either, so you're wasting more resources and time.

The game also lacks a sense of real progress. I have no real idea what I'm working towards and if I'm getting there, especially for helping factions. I also have no idea how what the factions are doing affects me.

Legacy of the Ancients DLC was not thought through unless the goal was to drag the game on. The idea behind it is cool, and introducing new enemies and resources is great. However, there are only a handful of new weapons and you waste so much time and resources in order to build them. It's just really not worth the effort. I recommend reading a Phoenix Point wiki page or thread that explains how it works.

In general, a lot of things are unintuitive and in-game explanations are lacking. Even being familiar with similar games, I had to look up a lot of things and write a lot of things down. (My desk has a ton of sticky notes now, which is not something I want in a game.)

I'll replay XCOM any day over trying to get through Phoenix Point a second time.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf I was considering just the base game, I'd say the 'mostly positive' rating for the game is more or less fair. The game seems to have come a long way since launch, and I'd give it a tentative recommendation to fans of the genre.

Even then, some of the core issues remain -- the manual aiming system still being the big one. This system just has fundamental problems baked into it, and considering how important it is to the game, some of the decisions made when implementing it were just bizarre. Why do enemies still rotate through their idle animations during and after manual aiming, for instance? Why do so many windows or glass panes block line of sight, both in manual aiming and overwatch? Speaking of which, why does overwatch seem to use a different, much less sensible line of sight system than regular aiming?

Despite that, I'd still say the base game is just about a mostly positive experience if you treat it for what it is; an interesting experiment on the genre, albeit a janky one. It has far higher potential as a sandbox than XCOM, and far more variety in available playstyles.

The reason I can't recommend the game as a whole is the DLC. I've never seen a game where each and every DLC not only adds little of value to the player, but actively makes the experience worse. Festering Skies is the worst offender, to the point that I wouldn't even enable it personally, which has been discussed in many other reviews, but even Corrupted Horizons feels like a slap in the face.

If you're not specifically going to use Mutoids, it's better to turn Corrupted Horizons off; not only is the corruption system just inherently tedious from an interface/healing perspective, the Acheron enemies can often spawn multiple additional enemies in larger Haven defences before you've even found the thing. Combine that with Chirons spawning their own worms and it can double the amount of time you'd be taking to complete the mission compared to vanilla. You're paying money to make the game considerably more of a slog, to eventually spend a ton of resources on Mutoids that are more often than not worse than vanilla mutated units, for the same if not more total resources.

Blood and Titanium is a similar experience; if you're not interested in bionics that playthrough, then you're adding in the Forsaken and Pure, highly armoured enemies that take much longer to kill than vanilla enemies in the early game. B&T isn't the worst DLC, and it comes as a free extra, but it still feels like I'm just adding in tankier, more time-consuming bullet sponges that take up more time for the same end result.

My first two playthroughs on vanilla were by far the most enjoyable, even though I was just starting out and knew the least about the game. Since enabling even single DLCs for playthroughs, my already somewhat mixed opinion on the game has soured considerably. For now I'd only recommend buying the main game, on sale, if you're already a big fan of the genre.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI have to say I was disappointed with this game from before it came out. The creators of this game already lost all credibility for me when they screwed over the kickstarter backers in exchange for an exclusivity deal with epic despite the fact that they weren't exactly starved for money.

A year after the game came out and very few people played it, we got it on steam with very little fanfare. I played the original X-Com games, as well as the newer ones and I have to say this game somehow manages to disappoint me on so many levels.

Firstly, there's a very obvious lack of polish to the game. The "bullet based" shooting is terrible. It's a great concept, don't get me wrong, but it's implemented terribly. The RNG somehow manages to be more horrible than that of the newer X-com games, with shots 50% of the time shooting OUTSIDE OF THE OUTER CIRCLE IN DIRECT CONTRAST TO THE IN GAME TEXT. There is an RNG system at play that has NOTHING TO DO with the circles you shoot with. The damage is also completely RNG based but with absolutely no in game information displaying how. How do I know this? Because you do get information about how much damage a shot allegedly makes, and how much health the enemy has and whether or not it should kill them. So when it doesn't, and there's no apparent reason for it, it makes you scratch your head. A myrmidon taking 3 direct shots at point blank range from one of the hardest hitting heavy cannons in the game not dying makes sense to anyone? No, it doesn't.

In contrast, the enemies absolutely cheat. I've seen cases of enemies exceeding their action points, shooting through walls, and even causing bleeding on a cybernetic part of one of my units. This is lack of polish, or the devs just mess around.

The DLCs are nothing to speak of and not worth getting. They add very little to the game. In fact, there *is* very little to this game. This "X-Com" killer fails to deliver on a narrative level, a gameplay level and a satisfaction level. I don't feel like I'm saving the world, I don't get attached to my soldiers and the missions are so repetitive I got tired of this game (in my favorite videogame genre mind you) within 10 hours of playing it.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI have been a XCOM fan since the first came out, almost 20 years ago, and this game is very similar to the last couple of XCOMs (enemy within and enemy unknown).
The key difference, in my opinion, is the fact that the planet is split into 3 factions and you need to manage diplomacy between all (you can also steal tech and vehicles from these factions).
During gameplay, you are presented once in a while with a sort of RPG choice that will affect your resources and your standing with other factions.
As for units and weapons, it's the same recipe as XCOM: You start with standard and research and upgrade your way into some cool weapons and you can recruit from factions faction exclusive units, and you'll be able to upgrade, enhance or mutate your soldiers.
It's fun to play, although and lengthy and the opponent's AI keeps trying to adapt. The true ballistic physics is great and you can take charge of the aiming. (that's also new!)
The main drawback I find is that the interface needs a bit more work while working with inventory and manufacture, but nothing too serious.
My review for this game is a solid 85%
