\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOverall, Phoenix point is a grand scale take on tactical turn-based strategy that offers you a massive hill to climb and high difficulty curves even on the lowest difficulty level. It's a race against time as the threat you face is constantly evolving into bigger, badder, and more capable enemies. There are factions, resources, tonnes of character customization for your squad - but ultimately it misses the punchiness of the X-com franchise (both modern and 90s). There is so much going on here, that I can understand why reviews are fairly mixed. On one hand, this game can feel like a bit of work, excel spreadsheet kind of work. On the other hand, it can be really satisfying once you start to master the game and things start to fall into place. This game feels like you're really waging a war on the virus on a global scale, and it really feels like you're not winning for about 90% of the game.

My main criticisms of the game are length/pacing - even when I climbed the hill, it still felt like the game overstayed its welcome. Apart from character abilities I felt like there weren't exactly clear technological winners in weaponry. Some weapons from certain factions were generally better than others and it didn't really grow substantially from there, until the very end of the game.

I enjoy tactical games a lot, I can't get enough of them - this game is not for everyone. If you're time starved I'd give it a miss. For a simpler, more punchy experience i'd go straight for any game in the X-com franchise. The question of recommendation here is who to. I'd recommend this game if you really love the genre, have a lot of time on your hands, and are generally extremely persistent (a completionist).\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI’ll be keeping my original review below, but I’m changing this to a thumbs down due to a gamebreaking bug where loading a save causes the enemies to do nothing on their turn. It’s easy to reproduce and has been in the game since launch apparently. Shame on the developers for not fixing something this egregious for LITERAR YEARS. Also, the lategame balancing is horrendous. You either run a squad of mostly Sniper Infiltrators with Thief, Sniperist, or Farsighted (with maybe the token Heavy Technician or two for warcry, healing, turrets, and electric reinforcment support) or give an Assault/Berserker with the Strongman perk a heavy machine gun and watch him wipe the entire ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ map in one turn, maybe with a Priest to soften them up with mindcrushes. The game is a complete slog if you don’t at least employ one of these two strategies, and preferably you actually employ both at the same time. The game stopped producing flow state after the early game. It was much too hard in the midgame, and way way WAY too easy in the lategame with max level multiclassed soldiers. Additionally, because the perks you get with max level soldiers are what make the lategame so easy, this doesn’t make them difficult to replace, it makes them IRREPLACABLE. This is similar to Colonels in Firaxis XCOMs but in a game where new recruits and gear do not cost a small amount of resources. They cost a lot. Which is very strange consider this game is from the creator of the original X-COM, a game where losing soldiers was manageable so long as you had good logistics in place to replace them. So long as you had the resources to replace the soldiers you lost you could keep going after soldier deaths. The soldier leveling and perks being the factor that helps you scale with the aliens is honestly a dealbreaker. I don’t want a hero squad romp. I want a gritty, realistic campaign where my soldiers die but I push forward knowing I can replace them with someone who, while less capable, is still competent enough to face the alien threat so long as I give them the best gear I have available. I don’t want a game where I know that if I don’t reload/restart when one of my soldiers dies, the new soldier I replace them with will be very useless for a long time, and will probably never catch up to the same power level as the rest of my squad due to the incremental SP per mission system and the way SP converts into raw stats and unlocks abilities. Soldiers don’t max out when they reach max level, they max out after 50-150 missions depending on the difficulty. A soldier death should not be a permanent setback you can never recover from. It should be a temporary setback until you get a rookie through a dozen missions or so. I’m 100% ok with needing veteran soldiers to go toe to toe with the aliens. What I’m not ok with is how long it takes to train a rookie up to replace one of those veterans. Sure you can throw rookies into a base with loads of training centers to somewhat quickly get them to level 7, but because of the per mission SP system, those soliders will inevitably be too weak in terms of stats and/or number of unlocked abilities. Even in XCOM 2 WOTC, soldiers do not earn additional ability points when they are at max level! You only get extra for the shared pool, WHICH MAKES SO MUCH SENSE. Having a hard individual soldier cap means the enemies which also scale up to a hard cap can tailored precisely to the max power level of your soldiers to create an appropriate challenge. This doesn’t happen in Phoenix Point, because your soldiers individually scale in strength continuously over the entire campaign, which means it’s impossible to create enemies that will scale appropriately with any squad that loses members, because the replacements will ALWAYS be weaker than the rest of the squad, creating a permanent power reduction setback.

My original review is below, but keep in mind it only really applies to the early game which, while fun, doesn’t last long:

This game is cursed Firaxis XCOM. If you can look past the relatively low production values and unforgiving difficulty you'll find a worthy successor the original X-COM: UFO Defense that approaches the modern turn-based tactics genre in a way that doesn't devolve into killing everything before the enemy gets a turn. I would say this game is the geoscape of XCOM 2: War of the Chosen, but with much less linearity. The map is open for you to explore rather than simply making resistance contacts, completing routine missions, and choosing when to raid alien facilities. Instead of a single base you can restore a network of bases. There is not one set story mission progression, there are multiple objective paths with various outcomes. The tactical battles are much closer to XCOM: Enemy Within. No turn timers returns things to the slower paced long range combat that inevitably became much less common in XCOM 2: War of the Chosen. Overall the game is good, but don't expect the flair of Firaxis XCOM games, it's not here. This game is much more grim, cold, and apocalyptic, with factions that actually go to war with each other, and a narrative that is devoid of lightheartedness. In Phoenix Point, the only guarantees are death or save scumming. Lost soldiers are much more difficult to replace, and tech will not be enough in the absence of powerful veterans. Though if all else fails, you can bring an armored car with a missile launcher. Just keep in mind it has limited ammunition.\t\t\t\t\t
