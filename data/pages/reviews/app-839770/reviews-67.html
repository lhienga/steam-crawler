\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI want to focus on tactical gameplay in a tactical game with some campaign level management, not micromanage resources on a campaign level that mostly dictates your tactical options and abilities. If I wanted to micromanage resources, I'd be playing a city builder or survival game. Including more features just for the sake of doing so or to give the illusion of challenge does not equate to better gameplay. Scripting gameplay toward resource grabs on a doomsday clock in a tactical game because everything disproportionately depends on doing so is bad design.

For example, there are scavenge missions early on but some of these have heavily armored enemies that constantly spawn anywhere around you while it's still too early to research better weapons to practically counter them. Don't get me wrong. I'm an xcom veteran and have beaten such maps before. But just because I can do it doesn't mean I enjoy it because of such unbalanced design.

Now you could say "but wait, you don't have to attempt that mission immediately and can wait for better weapons before doing so." Nope, if you want to put the player on a doomsday clock, do NOT design a disproportionately balanced scavenge site so early in the game that wastes the player's time!

There were even times when my overwatch clearly had line of sight but it still failed to activate for some unknown reason. And some early enemies have stealth abilities that you cannot counter apart from wasting grenades to avoid giving them a free shot and wasting yet more precious resources to replenish the grenades! And all these on normal difficulty! Don't get me wrong. I also like challenges, having played games like Oxygen Not Included, Frostpunk, This War of Mine, Total War and Stellaris. I have no doubt this game is not to challenge but out to troll the player. Don't waste your money on it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game gets a lot of bad reviews for not being XC2. I loved XC2 and this is not it's spiritual successor; it is its autistic cousin. I use this word because it is accurate; there is a lot you will not understand without having patience and enjoying being constantly surprised.

Compared to Vanilla XCOM 2 + DLCs, Phoenix Point is a better game. On the tactical and strategic level—especially the strategic level—the game has far more complexity than XCOM 2. Notice that I say "complexity" and not depth. While there is more depth than XCOM 2, it does not scale with complexity, which is a bad thing.

First thing you'll notice is that the strategic game is far more difficult than XCOM 2 for the right reasons. It does take a lot more time to figure out than XCOM, and there are some UI issues (ironically, not enough information) that can be fixed with Nexus mods.

Yes, there isn't a Steam Workshop, but it does have mods on Nexus. Okay, so mods are where XCOM 2 maintains its overall lead. Even a "fully" modded PP cannot compare to the modded XC2 experience, and this isn't just because there isn't a Steam Workshop; the game is harder to mod. XCOM 2 (and most firaxis games) are among the easiest games to mod by a fair margin. Using mods in PP requires a mod manager, which you can find on Nexus. The mods you'll see there, as of writing this review (Summer 2021), are mechanic and UO overhauls, or making Vanilla assets accessible, but you will not find "50 Female Haircuts" or "Tali Zorah Head."

This far, I haven't found any bugs or crashes, which I appreciate.

You will notice that the game is very, very hard. The difficult is not quite at the level of From the Depths or Dark Souls, but it shares things in common with both. For example, the AI will constantly beat you over the head and as soon as you figure out a good tactic, the AI will overhaul its own units and change its strategy, and this is what makes it a better STRATEGY game than XCOM; XCOM is a TACTICS game with STRATEGIC elements. This is more of a strategic game and unless you constantly pay attention to every number you're presented with on the Geoscape, you will lose the campaign. As for how it compares to From the Depths' difficult; it explains very little to you and you will need to individually study every me hanic before you can make something with any tactical utility. The learning curve drops you off in the deep end and expects you to sink or swim. 12 hours into the campaign, I've decided to restart the campaign bc there was A LOT I wish I knew. There is a lot of stuff you just have to learn about on the fly, for better or worse. For better, this means that the tactical combat is a huge step up from XC2 is complexity and depth.

Overall, the game is good, and if not for how difficult this game was to mod, it could have the potential to overtake XC2.\t\t\t\t\t

Review in Brief:

Niche game, but a solid 98% masterpiece if you’re in that niche. Like, 65%-75% if you’re not into it.

Pros:
  • Unparalleled rich tactical gameplay on many levels that all work together.
  • All aspects of the game feel well-considered and skillfully made, from the mechanics to the writing.

Cons:
  • Steep learning curve, potentially very confusing
  • Not super accessible, could feel dull, tiring or frustrating if it doesn’t click with you.

Review in Full:

Phoenix Point is a masterpiece of the turn-based strategy genre. It offers an ambitious vision of what the genre can be, with layers and layers of intricate design that all work smoothly together. Everything about the game, from the sweeping grand details to tiny minutiae feels like they’ve been meticulously designed to serve the game’s overarching vision and the result is a game that elevates its tactical gameplay to the most rich, nuanced tactical experience I’ve ever encountered.

Something of a surprise to me, though, is how that consistently thoughtful design work applies to areas outside the turn-to-turn combat. The game’s world map isn’t just a missions select screen but a whole other tactical layer as you manage resources, logistics, time and threats – and threats that are quite a lot more proactive with the recently-released Festering Skies expansion. Perhaps most surprisingly of all to me, is the quality of the writing. There are a few misses occasionally, but for the most part the writing of Phoenix Point is excellent. Don’t go in expecting a story-centric grand narrative or RPG-style npc interactions or anything; this is a turn-based tactical combat/strategy game first and foremost. But the end result is a wonderfully pervasive atmosphere of Lovecraft-inspired near-future sci-fi. The characters and their ideologies are nuanced, thought-out and well-established, and the world they inhabit feels, at risk of repeating myself, rich and nuanced. I was just as often excited for the lore and narrative around new developments as I was for the new tactical options they afforded me.

Post launch support continues in the form of patches, updates and expansions all of which so far have been positive additions to the game. Quite positive, if the reviews of 2019/early-2020 are to be believed. I’m a relative newcomer so I can’t speak personally for the initial state of the game, but I’m glad the Phoenix Point seems to have at least outgrown a shaky launch.

So, uh, yeah, I like the game. I could go on for hours about the subtle little details that all work together to make the gameplay sing. But I won’t. Because Steam’s reviews have a word count. I like it, you get the idea. That said, there are some things that I feel are important to mention.

First and foremost, this game does NOT have ‘a little something for everyone.’ It is an uncompromising vision of highly in-depth tactical gameplay. There’s a lot of potentially high-impact decision-making, and a lot of things you can manage. If you’re into it, this affords you deep gameplay without precedent. If you’re not into it, I can see how it would become tedious or tiring. And there’s a LOT to the game, which means that you’ll be Phoenix Pointing quite a lot over the course of it. I mentioned that the game is an uncompromising vision of the type of gameplay it provides and I mean that as a compliment. However, this is another one of those double-edged details because in being so unapologetic, it doesn’t end up being as broadly accessible as its peers like the modern take on XCOM. Some people may find some of its mechanics overly fiddly and dull, or the game’s numerous and potentially high-impact decisions exhausting or punishing. I love these aspects of the game and think it would suffer immensely without them, but I can see how they would be negatives for some – especially over the course of a long and challenging game that deliberately emphasizes them.

Secondly, there are some rough edges. Technical performance is vastly improved from release. It’s never crashed for me, at worst suffering from very occasional 5/10 second delays while the game processes something. There are also a smattering of highly uncommon but annoying bugs. In about 150 hours at time of review I’ve encountered just four or five and only a couple ended up mattering. Plus the game autosaves at the start of each mission, so even a worst-case scenario isn't going to cost you more than 20 minutes.

The big rough edge, though, is that the game can be confusing. There’s a richness of systems at play here that are all at least good, but unfortunately not all of them are communicated well to the player and thus can kick in unexpectedly. Until you learn how these things work, they can make the game feel more punishing or even more buggy than it really is – the most glaring example being how the remaining ammo from a partly-full clip that is unequipped between missions isn’t lost, but is rather stored hidden until it’s used to automatically top up a clip after a mission or accumulates to the point of becoming a full clip and dropped back into the ‘visible’ inventory. Good system, but more confusing than it needs to be and can lead to steep and confusing learning curve if you’re not really on the ball. I’d have liked if the game more comprehensively explained itself – perhaps expanding the useful ‘phoenixpedia’ which is always accessible from the world map. In the meantime, if you're ever lost, I recommend libral use of the ability to save, test how something works, and then re-load. (Although, of course, I caution against proper save-scumming. Nothing kills interesting decision-making like brute-forcing it.) Google can be your friend as well, but because the game has changed so much even recently, a lot of old discourse just isn't useful anymore.

Speaking of learning, I do want to also throw out that unfortunately the tutorial and its overly earnest voice actor is the weakest part of the game’s presentation, and begins what is a mostly confident enough stride with a bit of a misstep. Similarly the first heavy weapon you’ll encounter is easily the least aesthetically satisfying weapon in the game. And, uh, also shards of destroyed terrain mostly have the physics of lightweight plastic, as well as sometimes jittering about as they catch on other terrain for a moment before despawning. The pre-generated soldier's names wear the game's crowdfunded nature on their sleeves. I find finding survivors of the apocalypse in twenty or so years with nicknames like 'OldWhovian' or 'FreeHK' more endearing than jarring, but it's worth pointing out. Minor cosmetic issues, sure, but because they’re mostly front-loaded it’d be easy to form a bad first impression of the presentation. Plus, a little more map and voice actor variety would be appreciated.


But overall, Phoenix Point is a comprehensive masterpiece of a game, but it very much may not appeal to everyone. If turn-based tactical combat is of interest to you and some part of the game sounds appealing, give it a go – especially if you catch it on sale. There’s a lot here for 30/40 bucks and you’ll probably find something that’s at least interesting. And if it clicks with you, it can offer the one of the richest, most compelling strategic experiences you can have anywhere. Just be aware going in that if it isn’t your thing – and it’s not going too far out it’s way to be any sort of thing beyond exactly what it wants to be - you may end up bored, confused or generally rather put out.

Niche game for sure, but I give it an enthusiastic 98% in that niche. But maybe, like, a 65%-75% experience if you’re not looking for that niche, so be aware.\t\t\t\t\t
