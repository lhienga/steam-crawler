I have played Xenonauts, Phantom Doctrine, Xcom 1 and 2, UFO trilogy and I can say that this game doesnt deserve to be so underrated.

I would say it is not as straight forward as XCOM, it depends more on tactic, than random numbers. I love that, I loved that too in Phantom Doctrine. Random effect is the worst thing on xcom 2 tactical combat. What does it have with tactic anyway? With realism ok, not with tactic game.

Most + so far from early game is: Vehicles, Free shooting in Tactical, equipment diversity rather than Weapons II / Weapons III etc. (Which in Xcom worked like must have new weapons or you will be leaving enemies with 1 HP to next round) from there, i also love that enemies can be weakened. in Xcom if i remember, they are full strenght with just 1 HP.

This is review for early game only at the moment.

It is not copy of xcom. Which is good. Although some animations are very copied - it seems to me like they did it intentionally. Because there could be discussion about who really stole what, from which game:-)

If you like older XCOM like games. dont worry to try.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you like XCOM, this is a really good buy. Be aware that the game uses different features and you can't always rely on old XCOM tactics.

Phoenix Point has innovated the standard features you get in an XCOM-like game, while retaining the same spirit of the game. This cuts both ways; the new features sometimes increase risk and other time decrease it.

I played >1000 hours of XCOM (combined) and had gotten stuck in a rut, using the same tactics based on certain game features. Phoenix Point changes its gameplay enough that it caused me to reevaluate my usual playbook.
Over all, it's a very good game if you're looking for an XCOM-like game and need a fresh take on gameplay features you're experienced with.

An overview of changed features relative to XCOM:

* Gone are the hit chance percentages. All weapons have a bullet spread, and when you shoot, each bullet is randomized within the weapon's spread. The scenery blocks shots but can be destroyed in the process.
* Similarly, cover does not grant a flatout percentage defensive chance. It's all about how visible your target is. Even in full cover and not flanked, an enemy that is close to flanking your unit is going to see most of your unit and have a good chance to hit. The game still shows you half/full cover icons but you have to use your brains: a handrail is obviously not going to block as many bullets as a waist-high rock.
* Overwatch is conic. You get to choose the direction, distance and width of the overwatch cone. It allows you to focus overwatch on specific targets. But you can only have a 180° field of view instead of XCOM's 360°.
* Similar to Fallout's VATS, you can target limbs, heads, weapons, ... Disabling certain body parts confers certain debuffs on the enemy. Disabled legs lower movement speed, disabled arms prevent melee or wielding two-handed weapons, certain enemy abilities are disabled when the relevant body part is disabled.
* Enemy AI is about as complex, but each enemy uses their own advantage better. The flanking units really scurry around and hide from sight, making it hard to take them out before they get to you.
* Soldiers get skill points which can be invested in stat increases or the skill tree. There's a fixed class skill tree but each soldier also has a few personal randomized skills to choose from.
* Soldiers can multiclass.
* Geography matters for travel time and ability to respond to emergencies. You start building multiple bases across the globe, and you can build multiple ships and have multiple squads running around.
* For the most part, technology unlocks are vertical. That is to say that new weapons are usually not flatout better, they just have different stats.
* Consumables are consumed. The game makes it very easy to resupply your soldiers after every mission, but each ammo clip you empty or grenade you throw costs resources to resupply. By extension, soldiers need to carry ammunition if they want to reload.

There are some minor bugs and glitches, but they're mostly cosmetic. I have not encountered anything game-breaking. Comparatively, I have encountered about the same amount of issues that you get in XCOM or any similar game, maybe slightly less, so it's par for the course.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tUpdated Review @ 297 hours of game play

My early initial feeling of being transported back almost 30 years to the original X-Com was pretty close. Phoenix Point is what I had hoped the X-Com reboot would be. It was a rare combination of nostalgia and new at the same time.

Game play felt extremely familiar, but in a good way, not the feeling of 'this again?’ There are enough new gameplay elements to prevent it from seeming like you have already played the game, and the story is only similar on the surface.

The one part of the original X-Com that I missed, UFO interception, is already planned for the next DLC (which is free).

Even at normal/standard game play level, the game is challenging (sometimes boarding on frustrating). It took multiple play troughs in order for me to win a game. And unless you like restarting battles and having lots of saves, expect to lose people. Much like the original X-Com, it takes very little to cause the death of your troops. If you hate troop loss this is probably not the game for you.


Review @ 4.3 hours of game play

This game feels much more like the successor of the original X-COM series than the X-COM reboot. I still have not experienced much of the game but what I have is good. If you are a fan of the original X-COM (from the 90's) then chances are that you will like this game.
