\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHad several dozen hours on the X-Box App before buying it here.

Phoenix Point is a nice deviation away from the linear formula of Firaxis' XCOM and XCOM2. If you found the lack of strategic layer a flaw in the Firaxis games, then Phoenix Point will give you more to do, although don't count on it being very complex. Technology progression is more of a web with more subtly defined power level progression. For instance, the enemies will develop armor plating, but you can get weapons that have more armor penetration.

The setting is more interesting, but again, don't expect anything ground breaking. The overall production value is nice but is clearly outproduced by Firaxis' much bigger development budget. Overall, the game's technical details are passable.

Is it a solid recommendation? No, but it's still worth playing, especially if you found XCOM2 to be lacking in that open decision space that players of the original X-Com games enjoyed. The lack of a pod system and strict turn limits on missions alone makes the game worth checking.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFun game. Worth the asking price. A steal on sale.
What are you getting for your money? An enjoyable game with turn based battles and a decent story.
What aren't you getting? X-com. THIS IS NOT X-COM.

I've played all the X-Coms, and while I enjoyed them, the big combat maps ( especially in X-com 2 ) could drag on and on. Maps are smaller in Phoenix Point. Enemies have their own agenda - sometimes they are gathering supplies, sometimes destroying buildings, sometimes killing citizens. sometimes gathering intelligence.

It gives a nice variety to the game. And that's the area where Phoenix Point shines - in its variety. Land on an "explore area" and you could wind up with a text encounter that yields supplies, faction, or moral choices. Or you could get ambushed by raiders, or critters, or one of the three factions.

You can help the in game factions or choose to raid them yourself. Why build an aircraft when you can steal it?

In some ways the game improves on the X-com series. You can always choose to "free aim" . This brings up a targeting ring with two areas - an inner area and an outer ring. Bullets will always land somewhere within the two ( unless they hit an object on the way to the target ).
No more RNG miss madness. If you aim center mass and both targeting areas are on the target, you are going to hit something. You may not hit the little squishy body part that fills only part of the two rings - but you will hit something inside those two rings. Specific body parts have specific functions. Wound a leg and the enemy will move slower. Blow off a giant, pointy arm and that hulking creature can no longer stab you with it. The targeting and "real balistics" are a great improvement over X-com .

Even though it's not X-com, there are still some traditional X-com moments. Don't have time to use the front door? Blow a hole in the back wall and charge in to rescue a citizen from hot alien death.

Tactically, your starting squad is fairly weak on skills and abilities. As they level up, your combat options expand. One great feature is that your heavy weapon user starts out with a jet pack. Swoop down on an enemy and bash them to death with your auto cannon ( this is pretty much a necessity because firing your auto cannon takes three of your four action points but bashing only takes one ) .

A plus - or minus depending how you view such things - is the presence of a "doom timer". If the human population falls under a certain percentage, it's game over. If the "skull bar" fills up fully... not sure what happens, hasn't happened yet, but probably nothing good. What it boils down to is that time is not your friend. Enemies continually get stronger and have improved skills. A red fog of infection spreads inexorably over the map. If a constant sense of doom or the unfairness of alien infestation excites you, then Phoenix Point is what you are looking for.

Some players have experienced bugs. I haven't come across any. The game has been very stable for me.

Is Phoenix Point X-com or X-com plus ? No. And that's not a bad thing. Phoenix Point is a fun game with varied elements and enjoyable turn based battles. Give it a go and see.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really like the XCOM games (I played a ton of the original Enemy Unknown as a kid) so I spent a ton of time trying to find the fun here. For the most part, I was unsuccessful. There are some things here that work, but lots more that don't.

In some ways, the game is very concerned with being "realistic"; eg. the "realistic bullet physics". The idea sounds promising, but it doesn't play out well. Aiming for body parts is often a trivial decision, or a non-decision. Often your shot/throw gets blocked by a random terrain feature that was impossible to see/plan for. In reality, often you could work around these obstacles - you could lean over a little bit, or throw your grenade on a different trajectory. Here, you're just randomly screwed.

I remember when XCOM 2012 came out and a few people were disappointed that each gun didn't have its own ammunition.. turns out that was a good idea. PP lets you waste your time buying and scrapping 100 different ammo types, clogging up the crappy UI, and never gives you any gameplay payoff for doing so. XCOM lets you have some custom ammo types - but at the cost of a scarce equipment slot which makes for an actual interesting choice, unlike any of your inventory decisions here (which are basically non-existent, as you normally have tons of slots/weight to give). Equipment is mostly lame, with way too many variants of very similar weapons. You very rarely get the satisfying feeling of a "next generation" weapon upgrade like you do in XCOM (moving to laser/plasma/whatever).

The level design is just terrible. The maps are bland and repetitive. You spend a ton of time slowly crawling over empty distances, especially when the map has one enemy left that you can't find. It took me a half hour to find a stupid worm in a pit once. The levels are full of overhangs/squigglies that block vision and hinder gameplay in random/tedious ways.

Mission difficulty is all over the map; some "story" missions were bizarrely easy, some were fine, some were easy but with "traps" you had to work around (or bad random setups that you just wanted to reroll). In general the difficulty is too low, but spikes to "very frustrating" (eg. the orichalcum shield ancients, or the final boss), especially if your strategy doesn't line up well with the arbitrary, unpredictable mechanics of the mission (which you then solve by reloading/re-equipping: fun!).

There aren't many "real" enemy types, and what's there is super bland. The enemy evolution feels arbitrary/meaningless, and doesn't pay off in interesting decisions or gameplay. I paid no attention to whether something was a Arthron Spork Wimpleclip or a Arthron Wankle Splink Engine. The only gameplay difference they made was whether they had a shield and/or a gun. The variety of enemies in XCOM is one of the core reasons that game was great, and one of the core problems with PP is how bland the enemies feel.

The AI in XCOM 2012 was reasonably simple and predictable, making the game a contest between your superior tactics vs. their superior power/numbers. That worked out fine, it's a perfectly workable design. It's not necessary for your opponents to be tacticians - but it would also be OK if they were. Instead, the AI here is mega-random. Sometimes enemies dodge around 3 layers of overwatch to have every enemy on the map focus fire one of your dudes (which makes you shrug and re-load). Other times a strong enemy (eg. a Siren) runs out in front of 4 of your people and does nothing. Just waits to die a completely unsatisfying death.

Your tactics against this random level of opposition tend to end up in one of two directions: you play ultra-cautious and tediously crawl around the map... or just save every turn and YOLO around every corner. Neither is fun. I really missed the perfectly implemented timed missions of XCOM 2, where you had to balance out caution and speed. Or the terror missions. Most missions here have no timer, and the equivalent of the terror missions still generally just feel like "kill all enemies".

I much prefered playing XCOM 2 on Ironman mode; here the randomness would make that very ill advised. I imagine most people save scum pretty hard. That's lame, and drains the game of tension - but it'd be way too frustrating to play PP that way (even the "tips" pretty much tell you to save often). One of the great mechanics in recent XCOM games were how injuries predominated over deaths. You paid a penalty for taking big hits - people wouldn't be available for some time so you'd have to rotate through people - but you could reasonably muddle through.

Here, you can lose a few people randomly (and permanently) in an instant (especially to the stupid "umbras", which are a huge random difficulty spike in lots of otherwise easy missions). So when you lose people, you reload. That trains you to see losing people as unavoidable, irrelevant, random, and tension-free. Ran around a corner with no actions left and died? Who cares, you just reload.

PP does have an injury/stamina system, but it rarely came into play for me. Serious injuries are rare - usually people just die if they get caught out, and otherwise you heal up with medpacks (which means they're instantly ready to go for next mission, bad design decision here) - so you never feel the same pressure to rotate through members. You just wait at base until everyone's healed up, then go out again. The game loses a significant source of interesting decisions (which is a theme with many of the design decisions here).

The upgrades are lame. XCOM gave you choices of which branch to take - and often you wanted to take both because they were strong and interesting. But having to pick one was an interesting decision, and meant your people (even of the same class) felt different as they took different choices. Here, you can dual class and eventually get every upgrade for a character. This makes your soldiers feel bland and samey. The upgrades themselves are rarely interesting, with a lot of slots that are just stat boosts or generic abilities (eg. Dash, which you'll get on everyone who can take it). The one interesting tactic I found (rapid clearance + melee weapon discount, meaning you could easily kill 20 enemies in a turn with one character, especially if you had 4 allies standing around donating action points) felt mega stupid, completely outclassed comparable strategies, and was completely at odds with the tone the game is aiming for.

I could spend another few paragraphs talking about how much I hate the geoscape, how tedious it is to jet around putting out fires, and in general how the decisions you make in base building/flying around are less interesting than the equivalent decisions in XCOM. The research tree was bland, and I never felt I was making an interesting decision about what to get next. It's very easy to get too many research stations and have nothing left to research (because it's all locked behind Ally garbage).. that felt lame and against theme.

Overall... I have endless more complaints and I'm tired of writing. I feel like lots of design decisions here were guided by weird "principles" or something, choosing (arbitrarily defined) "realism" and "ideas that sounded clever when we were getting started" over "whatever decisions makes for an interesting game". Over the whole 40 hours (or whatever) I played, I very rarely felt anything other than "glad I don't have to do that mission again".

If this was the first entry in a new genre (like Enemy Unknown was in 199X) this would seem like a promising game with a bunch of great ideas. Instead, it's running against a vastly, vastly improved version of itself (in the form of XCOM2, especially with the DLC) - and that makes all of its flaws, shortcomings and outright failings visible in very sharp contrast.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe return fire mechanic is simply broken. Whenever a character is taking a shot, he will step out of cover, fire, and then the enemy will return fire. Only then will your character move back into cover. (If he's not dead by then)
Basically, the game is punishing you to fire at your opponent. In a game... where you have to kill your enemy... This mechanic, as it is implemented, has no place in a turn-based tactical game. It makes cover next to useless. Your characters will also sometimes destroy their own cover, while firing at the enemies.

The line of sight and ability to free aim is a good idea, but unfortunately, it is, just like the return fire, not well implemented. Sometimes you can shoot your enemy, and they can't, sometimes the opposite. Why? The line of sight doesn't seem to work properly. The structures around you can be glitchy as well, and you can sometimes shoot through wall too.

It is hard to believe the creator of XCOM made such a poorly designed game...

It is a shame, because it has otherwise good ideas. I like the idea of managing the inventories of my soldier, of exploring the globe and building bases.

But as it stands, Phoenix Point is barely playable because of these two issues.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOn the surface this looks like a XCOM clone and it basically is.

The Good:
The most drastic change from XCOM is the way hits are determined. Instead of a percentage you take aim and have 2 rings, around half your shots will land in the inner ring and all of your shots will land inside the outer ring forcing you to think about angles and what exactly you are aiming at. Oddly this mechanic is not taken farther with the concept of aimed shots (Higher action point cost for smaller ring) to vary your tactical options. Riot shields are also added for both you and the enemy and this works very well with the new aim mechanic. The concept of having to explore locations on the world map for missions and resources is also nice and the story is decent.

The Bad:
Unfortunately just about everything else is wrong with this game. The difficulty curve even on the lowest setting is insane. Anyone that has played XCOM Enemy Unknown/Within knows it is possible at least on the lower settings to not lose a single nation or soldier and win. Good luck with that with Phoenix Point. By the end of the first in game month The enemy has received at least a dozen upgrades while there are literally no true upgrades to be had for your forces. I am not really even sure what the point of research in this game is considering there is so little to research. There is also the fact that equipping soldiers costs a small fortune per soldier and takes days to produce the equipment. This is not helped by the fact by the end of the game your people might as well be made of paper and you have to split your squads across the world. You will NEVER be ahead of the game when it comes to having equipment or manpower. There is also the fact there is no real way to protect the populations in havens because even if you do manage to keep the infected and raiders off of them they do not produce any food and starve to death for some reason leaving me wondering why I am even bothering, The range on your transport is also painfully short making travailing to new hot spots extremely time consuming and difficult. What makes all of this together so annoying is they really could have followed the XCOM formula here and come out with a very good game

Over all I do not recommend it as the bad far outweighs the good. I would refund this if I could but there is just enough here to make you go past the deadline before you see the games numerous problems.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWALL OF TEXT: After the equivalent of one workweek playing Phoenix Point I've hit a wall. On one hand the game is a great tactical turn based sim -- but on the other hand it's a busy mess that punishes more than it praises. Victories bring little reward while failures are crushing and brutal. One mechanic in most of Xcom's iterations (and spiritual successors like Phoenix Point) have in common is a research tree that allows you to take your victories and shift the balance of power back in your favor. Enemies have deadly weapons? Kill them and reverse engineer them. Powerful new aliens arrive? Research powerful new weapons. In Phoenix point it seems like this would be the case too -- but the better guns never arrive. You start out against a foe more than fairly matched with the initial evolutions of your foe being easy to knock over, but they rapidly get every advantage you do, plus a whole lot more and they just get harder and harder without the player (despite a complex research tree and factions to share research) giving you anything that really helps you overcome your enemy much less turn the tide. The closest to a weapons breakthrough I was given was a powerful but woefully inaccurate cannon weapon but even that got outstripped by the endless march of the armor and size of the opponents you face. The game gave me resources I didn't even know how to use or have an avenue to utilize, and had numerous "artifact" resources I only learned how to find by the time it was too late. You can never have enough men to cover all your bases, and unprotected bases if raided are totally destroyed making it economically unfeasable to restore them to functioning. There are lots of different craft to choose from to move your squads, but the ones that allow you to move big squads are too slow, and the fast ones don't move enough troops into an engagement to win. You'll only be able to maintain about 1.5-2.5 good squads of troops, and losing anyone will be devastating forcing you to save scum (though I consider this standard in the genre) ... what is NOT standard is how 1-2 squads simply cannot get done what is called for to be done and that's all you get. When I hit the "Wall" I had as stated only two squads/vehicles, and the map was COVERED in plot advancing quests on the other side of the earth Where it took forever to get to and back from, with multiple havens getting raided at the same time. You inexplicably don't pick up most of the guns off the people you kill forcing you to spend/waste resources restocking after every mission and frankly it's a losing battle even keeping your troops supplied. A good metaphor for this game is a 4-star meal served to you with fine presentation only to learn your silverware is the size of toothpicks and the bites of the food are huge and you're incapable of cutting them into manageable pieces until the waiter starts grinding your face into your meal by stomping on the back of your head with a combat boot and as you slowly lose consciousness things start to get REALLY mean.

Seriously -- I felt like I had access to scores of weapons in my playthrough -- but none of them were effective against the aliens. Shotguns that promised to do deadly damage at close range PINGED off of mid-late game enemies armor. Only sniper weapons and unwieldy cannons seemed to harm foes AT ALL, with opponents cleaning your friggin' clock with comparatively similar weapons... because your armor isn't nearly as broken as the aliens.

The faction dynamic in this game is really flavorful and dare I say cool on the surface, but it boils down to a ridiculous game of "three-stooges" with all of humanity on the brink of extinction and each faction being more absorbed with hurting each other over conflicting ideals than fighting the monsters that are LITERALLY EATING THEM. It's like a synedrion scientist would be getting devoured from the waist down by an alien while he is ordering you to sabotage a New Jericho factory for reasons that are completely unexplainable.

Humanity is in the same boat, but everyone is bailing water into each others parts of the rowboat, with predictable results. It looked like there was plenty of game left when I stopped playing because like I said, it just kept getting more and more brutal. It ended for me when I finally faced an alien that was as big and armored as several vehicles with a health meter to boot, that pooped aliens that mind controlled your characters and if you even grazed it would put up a smoke screen to hide it -- and there I was looking at the same damn toothpicks and inaccurate cannons to take it on.

I gave up, unwilling to waste another 40 hours to hit the wall again because even with everything I learned in that long of playing a campaign I didn't see hope in trying again with knowledge and optimization. If I were you, I'd move on, and give up in advance. If you want a good Xcom game play the actual Xcoms or try PHANTOM DOCTRINE which is a refreshing departure from the genre, instead. This game is just a pretty Xcom clone that turns you and your danglies into a punching bag.

edit: gave the game a second chance on the easiest difficulty. This just made the tactical combat boring, but did nothing to resolve the greater issues, just takes longer to for things to become unplayable. Game is a mess. Discovered Cybernetics and Building Mutants but didn't even bother because you needed to build facilities and have your troops occupy them when instead you have to run around. Found three new resources, only two of which made any sense. Artifact resource is not explained how it's acquired, and how you think you get artifact resource you get unrefined mutagen (with no obvious way of refining it.)

This game stole two work weeks of my free time.\t\t\t\t\t
