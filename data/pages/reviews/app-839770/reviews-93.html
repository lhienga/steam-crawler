\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIm a big fan of this game and ever since i got it, ive played it everyday after i came home from work, and on some day offs. While the game is a great example on a tactical strategey game, it does have a few issues. Before i write down my critics, i want to let you know im playing on Veteran (Medium) Difficulty, as i have experience with RTS's.

1. Balancing - Early game: its exceptionally hard to compete against other factions , which seems to be the best way to get resources in the game, enemies from factions such as Diciples of Anu have an ease to one-shot your average soldier, which early game is very punishing. If you were to play the game without fighting or making enemies with another faction, it would make it too time consuming to gain resources to build your bases.

Mid-game state: the game sort of forces you to have a second squad of high ranking fighters as well as having soldiers at all the bases you own, which isn't that hard to manage. However, equipment and ammo management is a nightmare, when you have to keep track of all the ammo for all sorts of different guns that enemies don't usually use (which means they have no chance of dropping that ammo type). Second is skillpoint/skilltree management, you'd be forced to send them on missions so they'll be able to level up their abilities, otherwise, they're targeting dummies whom only have good gear to complement them, which later game, is very hard to play missions with as they're too slow or weak in stats to fight efficiently.
Another issue early game is that there aren't any hints or tips that tell you that satellite structures in the base with its scans in fact, only scans the explorative locations around base and NOT Pandoran Lairs, info on how you're supposed to repel the Pandorans from infecting more of the planet is unclear, unless by the use of Mist Repellers, which appear late in the game, and doesn't even give you the ability to build ones yourself. Enemies such as "Scourge" (I think its called) enemies whom if you kill a Pandoran wraith, will be auto-revived in their next turn. These enemies are imbalanced, as such are the pandoran enemies with pinchers, because they have the ability to walk around in a 1 action point radius, and attack 3-4 times in a row after walking. Enemies such as Pandoran vehicles (The four legged big monster thing) has a variant which causes stalling for turn after turn. There is a variant with a goo launcher, the imbalancing with this enemy is that it can goo once every turn, which if you have regular soldiers and characters on the map, will indefinitely get stuck in the goo, causing to changes to many turns forward, until the monster itself, loses all will-points.

2. Levels and classes
Classes in the game come in minimal varieties (Unless you've unlocked Diciples of Anu as allies) You start with three classes in game, which all other factions have as well. The diversity of classes are very minimal as the each faction has 1-2 unique classes except the Phoenix Project (Unless unlocked late game). There's not much else to say, but that classes feel a little bit slow and not very diverse compared to each other.

Leveling is fun , and is crucial for characters in this game to become stronger and unlock skills, the leveling is balanced, but feels a little bit unrewarding, essentially, a player could camp out their level ups and only spend the points in skills late in the skill-tree, which unappreciates the other skills, potentially making them worth less or less useful at least. ranking up characters in general is fun, and id recommend adding more levels to the characters, and maybe add skills to them that would make them stand out a lot better towards other classes. A good example of class diversity is comparing the technician class to the soldiers class, which might/might not emphasize on different play-styles. soldier and heavy though feel too similar, with very few unique differences. Infiltrator feels like a technician in disguise (They're very similar).

3. Vehicles (Special Units?)
What would make the game incredibly interesting and fun is to have "unique" characters in the game, with slightly different alt to the class they have. A great example is you could have a sniper character in which his class is "bounty hunter" which would be a sniper class, with some unique scavenging perks. or getting bonuses at damaging specific units! This a passionate example ^u^
Vehicles, we have to cover this... the vehicles feels like a double-edged blade. In it self, it is a bigger unit which can do powerful attacks. They feel kind of lacking though, as that's where the line ends. they're units with stronger attacks and bigger hit-boxes, the main critic would be there's barely any special units like the phoenix project APC or Diciples of anu's mutated dogs. special units in the game form each faction are 3x3 grid big, it would be very interesting and diverse in play-style if 2x2 units were added (1x1 are regular units) to the game, they could be soldiers in mechsuits or a steed / a warthog with more inventory space (for example). 2x2 units would see lots of play as its perfect for balancing squads Surprisingly there aren't any ground vehicles with a light machine gun mounted in the game.

4. Feedback
With all these comments i've made, they're only criticism! I don't hate or dislike the game, in fact this is the best game of mine in recent weeks since i got it! I fully support this game and all its efforts, and wish i could help them more seeing as this is a game , which may not be perfect, whom i believe have what it takes to reach perfection.\t\t\t\t\t

There are many good bits, the aiming system, the hybrid classes, the factions, the technologies. But where the game succeeds in originality in its gameplay mechanics it fails in one very important area DIFFICULTY BALANCE and 'fairness'.


Games like this should be played on ironman, choices should matter but the 'difficulty randomness' of Phoenix point makes this impossible. Some missions are a breeze others are literally impossible to succeed in forcing a restart and the loss often feels 'cheap' rather than a learning point (like in XCOM)

Difficulty 'Randomness'
I will give an example - before writing this review I was playing a 'solider rescue' mission - by the second turn of the mission before it was even possible for me to reach the soldiers, they had both been killed- it was literally impossible to succeed. It is not possible to have a good experience unless you are willing to rely on save files and restarts to ensure a successful outcome

Nerfed Progression
The Campaign is also tedious and there is little that makes you feel like you are making meaningful progress or becoming more capable- you start the game overpowered- easily killing the pandorans in one shot and by the time you are 20 hours in with top level soliders and upgraded weapons it takes half the squad to take down the basic enemies and even basic missions (non campaign missions) can be near impossible to complete clean. Why would you make this design choice, it feels like the squad is becoming less capable as they advance, certainly not like the top ranked XCOM veterans.

I really wanted to love Phoenix point, and there are moments where it shines but on balance it is a unbalanced frustrating and often tedious slog and after 50 hours invested I may never finish the main campaign....even during lock down!

Time to go back to XCOM2 classic ironman difficulty!

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPhoenix Point is effectively a crowd-funded, indie-game version of XCOM from the creator of the original XCOM game, Julian Gollop.

While Phoenix Point lacks the big-budget slickness of the 2K/Firaxis XCOM reboots, I prefer it as a game.

Why? In my opinion, Phoenix Point has a better story, and a broader game-world. The game features diplomacy, allegiances, and decisions which go beyond "kill all aliens".

I bought this game when it first appeared on the Epic Games store a year ago, and bought it again when it finally appeared here on my preferred digital platform, Steam.

Phoenix Point's mixture of story plot, strategy, and tactics may not be for everyone, but I would suggest giving the game the 2-hour tryout to see if it's a game for you.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPhoenix Point, recently released on Steam, runs very well.
Most missions in Phoenix Point can be taken on at any time. Exceptions are ambushes and stopping attacks on the facilities of the other factions. There a few other missions that appear that have to be responded to in a limited amount of time.
There are three other factions actively fighting the threat. Each has many bases around the globe and a player's reputation can increase or decrease based on actions taken with factions as a whole and with individual bases. The player can recruit soldiers from the different factions but the more important goal is to get a good enough relationship with one or maybe two of the factions so that their technology will be shared with the Phoenix Project. At some points diplomatic missions must be undertaken in order to increase status with factions.
Improvements in weapons technology are not dramatic in Phoenix Point. Rate of fire, effective range and accuracy all have a part to play in selecting weapons. Armor comes in three parts, helmet, torso and legs. Weapons can be destroyed in combat by enemy fire and consumable items like grenades and ammunition must be replenished between missions. The game makes this quite easy after each mission overall but some equipment must be picked up at a base after manufactured. Items are not manufactured instantly.
Units have far more hit points so it is not possible to wipe out all enemy units in an ambush easily. It is also possible to target individual sections of an enemy unit. Over-watch is not 360 degrees and units have four action points that can be used in any order. There are no pods in Phoenix Point and when a unit is discovered movement is stopped so a player can decide how to handle the situation, another difference from some other games of this type.
There are many story line type missions since each faction has a series of missions to compete in order to gain influence with them. Phoenix Point has a good variety of missions.
Phoenix Point allows the player to have more than one base. Bases are needed to detect alien complexes and for increasing manufacturing and research capabilities. Base development requires a lot of resources which are tight in Phoenix Point.
It is possible to have more than one aircraft and fighting vehicles.
Research is tied to acquiring certain items, killing certain aliens or capturing them. If influence with a faction becomes high enough then their research will be shared with the Phoenix Project.
Phoenix Point will definitely scratch that strategic/tactical itch. It plays very differently in some ways but is an excellent addition to games of this genre. I can easily recommend it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI enjoy the game, but the amount of tactical combat, ironically, can be too high sometimes. I frequently find myself queuing for three encounters in a row, which quickly gets repetitive (especially in the end-game, when there are usually 3-5 pending haven defense events, so at some point I just stop bothering). With this amount of encounters, the game doesn't let me spend nearly enough time on the global map, which also slows down any research and construction, making the game to progress extremely slowly. I realize it's a bit unorthodox and maybe defats the core gameplay purpose, but having auto-revolve mechanic for some battles would be really helpful to deal with the sheer amount of repetitive encounters, such as clearing nests and defending havens.

Other than that, there are some balance issues, especially related to the antediluvian weapons. In fact, the entire Legacy Of the Ancients DLC doesn't fit the plot too well, ending up in a situation where we're looking for a proof about antediluvian existence AFTER having excavated their artifacts and even manufacturing their weapons. The weapons themselves are too overpowered and more or less easily obtainable, so by mid-game I have most of my troops carrying end-game weapons that don't even require ammunition. Most of the advanced weapons tech comes way later, and by then it makes no sense to research or manufacture them, because they are weaker and need ammo.

Despite the abovementioned flaws, I've had my share of fun with the game and would like to thank the developers for making it! Great work, guys! I especially enjoyed reading The Briefing volumes during the development, which was likely the main reason I was waiting for the game to finally come out on Steam.\t\t\t\t\t
