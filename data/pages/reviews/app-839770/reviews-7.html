\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is everything that XCOM 2 should have been.

I loved the original 1994 game and played it many times over the years. I quite liked the first XCOM remake a few years back, and I was hyped up for XCOM 2. I tried to play it, but I was let down. That game was an utter mess in terms of balance and difficulty, with plenty of systems that just didn't work well together and huge issues that made it close to unplayable on higher difficulties.

But when I tried this, oh my god, this game has everything good about the XCOM remake, all the ideas of XCOM 2 implemented properly, a lot more of the feel of the classic 1994 game, and even some additional stuff in the shape of very intriguing, quite realistic and just the right amount of funny political drama and sociological fiction. None of the other games ever had this aspect.

This game has so many systems and layers interlinked with each other, and almost all of them feel balanced with the right relation between risk and reward, they feel like they have a purpose, they do not feel the same, they are mostly not tedious and there is also a right balance of systems you are forced to participate in and optional systems that you can opt in and out of with the corresponding advantages and disadvantages.

To mention some specific changes from XCOM 2 that I think absolutely make the difference:

- The simulated geometry accuracy system is one thousand times better than the percentages in XCOM 2, where you would often miss on a target right next to you with a shotgun, with no control over it, leaving your operative completely exposed and making you want to break the keyboard.
- The action point system is a mix between the modern XCOM games (with discrete points, easier to understand), and the classic 1994 system (with continuous points, giving you a lot more control and ability to fine tailor what you want your soldiers to do). This allows you to do things like take a step, shoot, then take a step back; instead of having to choose between shooting from a bad position or leaving your operative exposed in completely artificial ways.
- The way that hitpoints are managed in itself is incredibly more balanced than in XCOM 2. While both you and your enemies can one-shot targets, this only happens in extreme cases where someone is incredibly exposed or is a really weak target. In most situations, characters don't die so quickly and this incentivizes far more strategic combat that is not as simple as "who shoots first/who gets close first". While you definitely are incentivized to get into someone's face to blast it with a shotgun, this is not the end-all be-all of combat anymore.
- A big big pet peeve of mine with XCOM 2 was the way that squads and alerted enemies worked. It was a complete mess. You would run into the range of a squad, alert the entire squad, and they would get into position and then have full action points next turn. In this game, instead, you spot enemies further away (and so do they), much further than from close range, and you alert them even closer. Moreover, enemies are individuals rather than squads, meaning you can alert them one at a time rather than 3 at a time. This makes the overall flow of new enemies entering combat a lot smoother and feel way more fair, while still having to watch out for getting yourself out of position or not properly caring for your back side.
- The skill system itself is a lot more balanced and feels like you actually have a lot more choices. Most weapon types feel like they have a use (with some exceptions) and you definitely want a balance in your squads of different types of soldiers.
- The damage type system is one of the most complicated in the game, definitely the most complicated in any XCOM game, and it works wonderfully. Every damage type has its pros, cons, strengths and weaknesses; and they are all relatively intuitive to understand. It incentivizes a lot more varied combat and very innovative approaches to combat. Sometimes you want to do as much damage as possible quick, sometimes you want to slowly shred the armor, sometimes you want to use viral damage to eat at their will power, sometimes you want to paralyze them because their hitpoint is too big. Great great stuff.

All in all, this game is an absolute win and in my eyes the best in all of the XCOM series, even better than the original 1994 game, which I would classify as the second best. Absolutely recommend, and probably the best game there is far and beyond in the turn-based strategy genre.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBest substitute for X-Com out there. Lots or replayability and strategy choices. Main issue with tactical game is the maps are too small for the reinforcement method (reinforcements can appear much too close to your units from any of multiple directions making cleared maps potentially deadly). So saves are a must if you don't want to just get frustrated as heck.

Managing your supply is a critical part of the game and if all you want to do is shoot things you won't like this game as you have to buy and sell quite a bit to keep the supplies rolling.

The is an overall story arc with various battles triggering events which allows you to manage when those events happen after your first run through (not sure if they meant for this to happen) but it is kind of nice to be able to NOT trigger a bad event and have it run a long time until you can end it.

There are are a lot of ways to combine the abilities of units to achieve some interesting results like some maps are mazes but one unit blasting through walls can cure that and change the whole map.

It isn't X-Com but what is? This is definitely the next best thing IMO.\t\t\t\t\t
