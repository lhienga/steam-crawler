\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a difficult recommend. I wouldn't just recommend this to anyone, but if you are a hardcore turn based tactical fan then I think you can get a lot of enjoyment out of this title. The graphics are decent and the tactical combat has some surprising depth.

The reason you might not want to pick up this game can be described in a word, JANK! There are all these paper cut issues that remove some of the enjoyment. There are huge pauses all over the place, so turns take much longer than should be necessary. The interface often feels laggy. The world map is clunky, requiring all these unnecessary clicks to activate locations. Line of sight can be deceptive and a hundred other minor annoyances. Nothing in itself is a deal breaker, but all added up can turn off the casual gamer.

If the developers would give this game another polish pass it would be easy to recommend. Until then this is mainly for the hard core turn base strategist looking for their next fix.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis review is based on Phoenix Point: Year One Edition, excluding any subsequent DLCs. This game deserves one play-through if you like the turn-based tactical combat games
Pros:
  • Aim Like a Pro: The aiming system is a game-changer in turn-based combat. It might seem bit complex at first glance, but it is quite straight forward to get grips on. When you aim at an enemy, two circles appear – a big one and a small one. The big circle guarantees a 100% hit, while the small one gives you a 50% chance. The circle size depends on your distance and weapon type. It's not super realistic, but it's better than a dice roll.
  • Free Aim: Get tactical by targeting specific body parts on enemies. Disable or slow them down – your call!
  • Strategic Positioning: Your position and line of sight (LOS) matter. Optimal LOS from a high vantage point means a clear shot. Weapon effectiveness varies with distance and type. Unfortunately aiming from the corners is still a problem.
  • Environmental Effects: Phoenix Point's environmental effects outshine XCom's. No wonky weather here – it's done right.
  • Tougher Foes: Enemies level up, keeping the challenge alive as you progress.
  • Combat Fatigue: Handles troop fatigue better than XCom.
  • Bugs Sorted: Year One edition is stable, with most bugs squashed.
Cons:
  • Repetitive Missions: Missions can feel repetitive quickly, lacking significant story progression. A bit like deja vu.
  • Not So Engaging Story: The alien tale is refreshing but lacks charm without captivating story characters. Feels like talking to oneself.
  • Dim Atmosphere: The game's atmosphere is on the dark side. XCom does a better job infusing personality. Characters are as exciting as a plain potato.
  • Lackluster Enemies: Enemy designs lack excitement. Giant crabs? It's like fighting a seafood buffet from X-Com: Terror From the Deep.
  • Geoscape Challenges: Managing multiple teams and aircraft can get complicated on the geoscape.
  • Armor Variety: Different armors look distinct but are similar in stats. As opponents get tougher, your armor doesn't keep up.
Final Verdict:
Phoenix Point is a worthy playthrough. Looking ahead, a fusion of Phoenix Point's aiming system with XCom's character-driven narrative, an enriched storyline with multiple endings, and an awesome soundtrack could birth a new tactical combat king.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tComparisons to X-COM are unavoidable.
Tactical, customizable, squad-based, turn-based combat in a near future world beset by an "alien" invasion with global base and political management.
Of the two, I prefer this game.

The ways they differ are nice. The biggest one being: no fudged % based targeting. The targeting is more precise. If you select a target to shoot, you will get an "expected damage" value. Or, you can make a more precise "aimed" shot (doesn't cost anything extra) to target a specific area on the target. This area determines where 100% of the shots will land (within a larger, outer ring) and where half the shots will land (within a smaller, inner ring). Great for single-shot weapons to cripple or eliminate foes. No more missing every 90% chance to-hits.
I also like the quests and missions with multiple factions that are surviving in this apocalyptic world. Upon gaining their favor, they will offer you unique unlocks; things to research, unique squad member types, weapons, & gear.

The reason I don't have much time played is that I always get to a point of being overwhelmed flying all over the place trying to manage the world.


I like the fights but dread the policing.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI want to like this game, but there are so many minor things that bother me as I struggle to enjoy the game.

Your characters do not aim around corners, step out of cover, or aim over ledges to take their shots, this might not be immediately apparent but your characters' shots are all based off of their "first person" free aim mode. This sometimes leaves you in an uncomfortable position where you *should* constantly have a clear, advantageous line of sight and you are unable to actually capitalize on your tactical positioning and planning. The enemies though are able to step out of cover to attack you.

To emphasize how silly this is, if you swap a sniper to a pistol they stand up straight, and have a better line of sight because they can actually see over minor obstacles instead of being crouched hunchback like a gremlin. There have been an unfortunate number of instances where I left my sniper with their pistol drawn to Overwatch and I was falsely led to believe that they had a line of sight at an enemy with their rifle, only to realize in horror that their posture slumps back down and can no longer see the enemy they previously had a clear line of sight on. I like the free-aim mode, and aiming down the sights as a sniper to pop a shot off into some bug-eyed monster's round, glossy head behind the chitin shield they're hiding behind should be a highlight of the game, but I think its been implemented to the detriment of the rest of the system.

Battles feel like a chore. Of the 8 or 9 non-tutorial missions I've played, all but 1 had infinitely spawning enemies. From what I'm reading from others, this is normal. Battles are slow and choppy, enemy turns take too long to resolve, movement is interrupted constantly by enemies appearing from the fog of war and wasting your time.

I have no idea when my team is hidden or not. Stealth is apparently a mechanic in this game, there's even stats for it. I can't figure out how it works for the life of me though because it doesn't ever seem to actually come into play or matter for more than the first turn. It would help if there was literally any UI indicator.

I want to keep playing this game, I haven't even seen or fought a fraction of the genuinely cool-looking monsters or Kaijus, but I keep finding myself frustrated with actually just playing the game. The story is engaging, the threat is interesting, and the Lovecraftian elements are great. I like the rival faction mechanics, it's a bit like how I was hoping the War of the Chosen factions would be, but it all feels so scattered. Why do these factions not just "own" one part of the world each so we can have more time to get to know our neighbors before we meet their rivals?

TL;DR it's not XCOM, and that's okay, but it's got a lot of quirks and frustrating behavior to iron out if there's ever going to be another game in this franchise.\t\t\t\t\t
