\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game didn't have millions upon millions of dollars of budget that firaxis xcom 1 and 2 had, but it delivers so much more.

My entry into the xcom-like games, in a genre of games I'm supposed to love, I played those two xcom games, and completely, utterly despised them both. Masochism (which I don't mind, and made me play them as much as I did on impossble/legend ironman) in it's purest form, but without "fun", and reward. I was unsatisfied for a number of reasons, and feel nothing but bitterness when thinking back on my experiences.

Then Phoenix Point came out in early access, and despite how buggy, janky, 'less spectacular', and my savefiles literally corrupting left and right, I loved it immediately, and was everything that I wanted xcom to be, and more things that I didn't think I even wanted, or needed.

However, the game came a very long way since, and most of those horrible kinks have been hammered out, and honestly this game is amazing. I found the fun that I couldn't find in firaxis xcom.

One of the core principles and features of this game is "FREEDOM". Free-aim, unrestricted action order, segmented movement, classes don't define roles, MULTICLASSES. The sheer amount of freedom you have in all these aspects teases, and encourages your creativity, because you can play in countless different ways, both in-combat tactically, and strategically preparing.

Units have actual inventories, carry weight, ammo and other equipment all has to be manufactured, and neatly packed into your backpacks as much as they can lift. They can access their inventory anytime to access extra packed supplies. Units have stats, and many abilities- passive and active. Especially as you get into multiclassing, each of your units start becoming art in how unique they will function. Besides the up to two classes of abilities, each soldier has an additional 3 unique, randomly rolled unlockable abilities.

Research is interesting, there's a lot of it for variety of effects. You are excited for researches, while in xcom they made me depressed. This is particularly true for the unique researches of the 3 big factions that are amazing, and you need to work to get, and you want to get them. They too, independently research their respective trees, and can only share what they have already researched, but once you are close-knit allied, you straight up get access to their tech-tree to research as if it was your own.

These 3 factions are interesting, and alive, along with the entire world and the story. You're not a deus ex machina hero, in fact the entire morality of the Phoenix Project in any given playthrough is only defined by your decisions, and your actions. You need to depend, and work with these factions for a good solution to the pandoran threat, and there are 5 endings, for 5 different paths- although 2 of them are sort of close to each other. You might decide to ♥♥♥♥ them all, and take matters into your own hands if you wish.

Back to combat, individual bodyparts with their health and armor, specific effects for specifics bodyparts broken- yours and the enemy, a bunch of different status effects and types of damage, I need to stress how open-ended it is in how you approach combat. You might excel in particular aspects in combat, but be very weak in another depending on your setup, but your setup is never rigid, you have a plethora of choices all across the game in all of it's parts to make up how you shape your squads.

Phoenix Point is complex, intricate, packed full of features and fun, with the enemies providing a changing, dynamic threat from mission to mission. It is janky, but it is a passion project, and has so much going for it. It is not an easy game, but that's just how it is in the genre. Balance is constantly worked on and tweaked, and bugs are being fixed. It's not a perfect game, but one that I enthusiastically play when I do, and given all the sandbox-y freedom, it is quite replayable.

The DLCs are a bit sketchy, but the base game is all worth it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm recommending this game, because it is by no means bad, and brings some interesting new mechanics to the table - a blend of the action point and turn based approaches, a new take on accuracy and manual aiming and a wide variety of enemy types and loadouts, which admittedly got a little confusing at the end.

However, it pales to what XCOM2 and, even the XCOM EW, can be, and suffers from bloated systems with a bolt-on approach to DLC and expansions. There are too many small annoyances as well, such as incomplete documentation and guidance, anomalies in the aiming system and some interface challenges.

If you really like the genre, and want something a bit closer to UFO than the more recent Firaxis titles, but find Xenonauts a bit *too* old-school, go for it. Otherwise, you might better enjoy XCOM:EW or XCOM2 with the respective longwar mod installed.\t\t\t\t\t

That's why i advise to play game without DLC enabled at first so not to get overwhelmed and frustrated by even more stuff that they add to already busy game

Game's soundtrack consists mostly of ambient music, but i enjoy the melancholic and empty feel it has
