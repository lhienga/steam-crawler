\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe fact that I have sunk nearly a hundred hours (not counting a chunk more in offline mode) into this game without ever coming close to beating it should tell you everything you need to know.

If not, allow me to illuminate. This game has the uncanny ability to not only kill runs as soon as they drag on into mid-game, but also the player's will to live. Normally that would be a great thing. That usually indicates challenging gameplay, which is rather important to a strategy game.

Phoenix Point is not challenging. Not in any desirable way. It's main approach to difficulty are increasingly bigger numbers. Sure, it sometimes throws a curveball your way by introducing somthing new, but 90% of all enemies are simply reused, more tanky versions of prior ones that deal more damage/Have a scarier weapon/one gimmick added that three other types of enemies already reused. However, despite the inflated number of enemy types, the optimal approach basically never changes. It usually boils down to a certain body part being implicitly marked as "Shoot here to win". There's little actual strategy involved.

Now, by itself, all that is tolarable. The real issue starts arising once this is combined with Pheonix Point's addiction to speed. I usually prefer to speak for myself, but I know for a ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ fact that many enjoyers of turn based strategy games do not appreciate being rushed.
Phoenix Point is a game all about rushing everything you do. Everything is on a strict timer. Enemies evolve rapidy, mist spreads constantly, Havens are being attacked over and over and over again, every time only providing a very limited window to assist them. Meanwhile the player is tasked with also juggling expansion, resource procurement, exploration, upgrading, faction relations, Story missions, research, troop management, troop logistics and so on and so forth. None of that would be half as bad if it wasn't asked of the player constantly, all the time, at the same time. Working the Geoscape is absolutely tedious and involves a lot of micromanaging.

However, it gets worse. This tendeny extends into the tactical game as well. Most missions involve getting somewhere or killing something as an inexhaustible amount of enemies pours onto the map from all sides. Whoever decided implementing this type of mission into a turn based strategy game with limited ammo reserves is a good, fun idea might want to think about switching professions.
I assume it's meant to add a bit of pressure and tension, but in essence just turns into a tiring exercise of getting from point A to point B as quickly as possible before too many bullet sponges spawn... Which can fairly easily be accomplished thanks to the many exploitable abilites, units and mechanics.

I guess this is as good a time as any to mention how utterly broken this game's balancing is. There are so many fairly obvious ways to completely trivialize aspects of this game that even my dumb ass found most of them organically. Some of them are so blindingly apparent that I seriously wonder if they were intended, otherwise I have no idea how they didn't come up in playtesting. For instance, bringing a vehicle to any type of scavenging mission basically removes all danger from it as you can literally drive-by crates and even enemies by briefly popping out with the units inside, effectively never exposing your designated supply hauling soldier to enemy gunfire. The fact that by virtue of having a vehicle, the player can drive around the map, taking pot shots at the enemies they are kiting with relative impunity is just silly.

Even without vehicles, it's fairly easy to just have a soldier literally cross the entire map in a single turn, attack a ridicilous number of times, get immunities to use an OP ability without any of it's downsides and snipe many objectives in very short order without much risk or resistence.

This game isn't difficult or challenging. It's just tedious. There's no other way of putting it. I've caught myself actively avoiding missions not because I lacked proper equipment or my soldiers needed rest but because the tactical game feels lika a chore. A formality. I've literally restarted maps to have the objective spawn in a better spot so I could be done two rounds earlier.

To compound this issue, despite it's affinity for speed, the game feels so DREADFULLY slow. Due to the relentless unit spam and the game's need to play animations for eyerything, enemy turns take ages to complete. Entire EMPIRES have risen and fallen in the time it takes Fireworm #23 to slowly crawl it's suicidal behind slightly closer to the frontline. There is a noticable delay to everything. A delay that becomes more noticable with every passing hour of play.

The worst thing about all of these issues and annoyances, the reason I even bothered writing this overly long review.. Is that this game has a very solid core design. If it weren't for what seems like avoidable mistakes, this game could have been a worthy successor to games like Xcom. Some It's innovations over it's predecessors are downright genius. The dynamic action point budget system is a great idea, allowing for much more flexibility when moving units. Manual Aim and the weakpoint system are something straight out of fallout and it's a novel concept worth exploring in this type of game (as long as it's not an instant win button, of course. Looking at you, Arthrons.) The game's story, art direction and faction designs are also incredibly well done. Each faction feels distinct, has it's own goals, interacts organically with others and the player faction and makes sense in the setting.
That's the issue. There is something here. That's what makes me so UTTERLY LIVID. This game had all the potential. All the chance to swoop in and step into Xcom's footsteps as it's unique, own franchise while it's designers were off on their coke binge, designing their own ill conceived, bastard heir to the series.
No, I'm not a fan of that game. How could you tell? Aliens are for shooting. They're not something to ally or coexist with. Not in the xcomverse, at least.

So in summary: "You were the chosen one, Phoenix point! You were meant to fill the void Firaxis has left, not add to it!"

Holy hell, this turned into an essay. If you actually read all this directionless, mad rambling and ranting, absolutely do play Phoenix Point! Because by god do you ever have the patience and disposable free time to do it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tReview for Year One Edition.

Feels good:
- Aiming system. Possibly the best thing the game has going for it. Unlike XCOM, what you see is what you can hit. You can even check from the soldier's POV.
- Limb targeting. Shoot off the legs and it can't move. Destroy the equipment and it can only run around helplessly, like a chicken
- Almost fully destructible walls and floors - bomb an enemy on the roof and watch them fall 2 storeys down to their death.
- Graphics, sound and music are quite nice, in my opinion.
- OP builds where a single soldier can clean out the whole map on turn 1. It's not balanced, but it's fun.
- "Will" system - very interesting combat system where your character's "will points" count as its capacity to do extra things in battle, like abilities, but are also a representation of its mental state - a mentally tired character is easier to mind control, for example.

Feels bad:
- The game is too drawn out. You'll be doing copy-paste missions a lot. It took me over 70 hours to finish the campaign, and I wasn't having fun the whole time.
- Copy-paste enemies. Modularity adds some newness, but they mostly feel boring. Nearly all of them, not counting the humans, are some kind of crab. In comparison, XCOM had more effective visual variety.
- Boring flavorless research. Part of the appeal of XCOM (and even the old X-Com) was the discovery and research of strange new things by your scientists. Here, it's quite bland, and you never quite look forward to reading any of the flavor text or seeing new content unlocked by research. It's a combination of not getting any new visual reward from having completed the research (even old X-Com at least had a full-color spread of the alien being dissected!) and the fluff not being interesting enough.
- Bland story. Interesting premise and execution but not a lot more than that. There are "characters" in the game, but you mostly don't care about them.
- Not very well-optimized, so it can run well only on new graphics cards. Also, no native anti-aliasing for some reason - jagged edges on 3D objects.
- No flying units in combat.
- UI has a lot of room for improvement.

I still recommend the game for the more faithful X-Com and XCOM fans because in my mind, the pros outweigh the cons.\t\t\t\t\t

The end goal of all evolution is crab.
A world-ending invasion and infestation problem that can only be solved by a multi-national team of soldiers and researchers, who are supposed to save the world via application of overwhelming firepower and reverse engineering, respectively, through multiple layers of turn-based and real-time strategy? It checks all the basic boxes, but Phoenix Point is decidedly not an X-Com clone. No really, it isn’t. It’s kind of its own thing, for better or worse.

Indubitable inspiration at a quarter of the technical budget.
This isn’t to say that this title’s presentation is poor, just that it’s hard to deny the amateurish feel conveyed at almost every step. Starting with the good, all the models, textures, lighting and general art style are rather solid. Special praise given to the latter, as the enemies are one step away from looking properly Lovecraftian. On the other hand, UI is messy to look at, even messier to interact with, and there are plenty of instances where animations bug out in rather amusing ways. Your soldiers could run from cover to cover, with their upper bodies contorted as they’re leaning down or up.

When it comes to performance, a Ryzen 9 3900X, 64GB RAM, RTX 3070, Windows 10 Pro system is decidedly overkill. Usually performance is a rock-steady 60fps at 1440p, though there are some rare drops to the 30s or 20s (plus awful visual glitches) when free-aiming towards fire and smoke. Loading times for saves are rather long, but after that it’s not too bad.

An eerie, tense soundtrack plays on both the Geoscape and in the Tactical battle portions. Were it not for the very basic track mixing and general repetitiveness, it only heightens the atmosphere and instills dread into the player, making one doubt just what monstrosity or horror you’d fight next. Sound effects are of decent quality as well, and I’ll give particular praise to the booming echo after firing your weapons... And then there’s the voice acting. It’s fine in diplomacy screens and cutscenes – very well done, actually – but in gameplay you can definitely sense some early-draft grunts, screams and death cries, both from humans and from Pandorans.

Sapere aude.
In the year 2022, melting permafrost has allowed a completely unique prehistoric virus to invade Earth’s oceans and mutate the ocean life. Following wars amongst ourselves and against the hostile seafood, this Pandoravirus has driven humanity to near-extinction, huddled up in isolated havens across the globe. Now, split between three ideologically-conflicting factions, mankind’s only chance of survival rests upon the Phoenix Project, who must preserve the remaining population, analyze the threat, and come up with the necessary solution before it’s too late.

Putting a small spin on the “alien invasion, gotta reverse engineer fast” formula via diplomacy is clever. As we’re about to find out however, it’s imperfect… and you’ll really wish people would get along better when the extinction of humanity is at stake.


Keep in mind that there's no secret plan, no arcane knowledge, and no guarantee that we'll succeed.

As with the X-Com series to which this title is a spiritual successor, Phoenix Point’s gameplay is split between multiple strategy layers. Its tutorial introduces the player to the Tactical layer first, where you control a squad of up to eight operatives on a small field. Your goals generally are to neutralize enemy forces, though sometimes you also have to recover a vehicle, other people, defend MacGuffins, or simply extract with necessary resources before being overrun. Subtle gameplay twists significantly change up the usual turn-based tactics from its inspiration, however – in many ways acting like the classic, 1990s titles. Per-region health and armor add important tactical decisions to be made with every combat engagement. Adding onto this are the granular action point system, which lets you peek from cover, shoot, then duck back in; and the cover system itself, which is only as effective for protection as its physical durability and coverage.
https://steamcommunity.com/sharedfiles/filedetails/?id=2907732177
But what sounds good on paper doesn’t work perfectly in reality. Line-of-sight and line-of-fire are sometimes exclusive; this means your machine guns and heavy cannons, held waist-high, can’t be used in the same manner as other weapons. Or you’ll be notified of a target in sight, but you can only take the shot on its toe or whatever. Most annoyingly, enemies quickly develop strong armor. Couple this with their annoying counter-tactics and abilities, and you’re backed into a corner, unless you pay close attention and make use of every available skill and gear.

What saves the punishing mid-game combat loop is your troops’ own advancement. From both personal and common point pools, you can improve their base attributes and unlock skills. Dual-class to get proficiencies in multiple weapon types, allow instant reloads, attack for fewer action points, lockdown enemies and provide morale for your own teammates with each kill. Max rank Assaults, especially when dual-classed, are my favourite because they can pull off some serious cleanup on damaged or low-ranking troops, getting back action points per kill.

After the tactical layer, we move onto the strategic one. You’re supposed to send aircraft with troops aboard to discover havens, scavenge resources and fight back against the Pandorans. The more planes you can send around the world to examine sites, the faster you can expand and build up your resource depots. This is also necessary to undertake story and faction missions, or engage in trading. Everywhere else you can scavenge for resources or items, or rescue potential recruits.
https://steamcommunity.com/sharedfiles/filedetails/?id=2907727177
However, scavenging sites can be visited only once, at most twice with a mid-game refresh. Equipment, vehicles and even recruits cost a large chunk of your resources. Playing with a single plane is either a major strategic error, or a serious challenge.

The most immediate problem is that the generic faction missions – where you’re tasked with sabotaging rival havens – drop your relationship with the victims too much to be worthwhile. You’re genuinely better off letting these havens suffer attacks from the fish monsters, then rescue them from attack. It’s both easy and a good idea to befriend every faction – you unlock their own tech trees once allied.

Race against time.
Just protecting humanity won’t be enough. The end goal is to research how the Pandorans came to be, and how to put a stop to them. The quintessential alien capture and base destruction are critical mid-game tasks for this purpose, and it might not be too easy when you’ll eventually have to neutralize towering monsters. Eventually, you’ll gather enough intel and have a strong enough crew, that you can wage the final assault and save humanity. The Phoenix Project can go about it solo, or ally with one of the other factions, for slightly different endings.

By the time I finished the game at the roughly-50 hour mark, on the default Veteran difficulty, I was exhausted. Missions are repetitive, and even combat turns tedious eventually. Small haven events are randomized, but the general progression is similar between playthroughs. Downloadable content doesn’t feel balanced or fun to engage with.

But next time... next time we'd be ready.
A solid dual-layer strategy game with significant faults in user-friendliness, late-game tedium and mechanics. If you can get past those, Phoenix Point will keep you hooked in its gameplay loops.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game has plenty of little issues that nagged at me while I played. The progress is mind-numbingly slow, especially when you're just waiting for important story events to occur near the end. Many missions are same-y, with identical environments and limited variance in objectives. Factions are largely one-note, and what nuance they attempt to give them is largely lost with how goofy their personalities are. The story is plain and uninspired. Some enemies bordered on unfair and would kill my units before I had a chance to detect them. The overworld lacked some expected quality of life features. Sometimes enemies would hide in random corners and I'd spend several minutes looking for them after killing everything else. And many, many other things.

Why then am I recommending Phoenix Point? Despite all of this, I was having fun.. The depth of the gameplay means there is a lot to master, and almost against my will I found myself thinking about this game all the time, what I could do better, how I would approach a new playthrough on a harder difficulty. I was playing this game in almost all of my free time for a bit, and I'm not sure if that means it's a good game, but it certainly clicked with the part of my brain that likes being good at things. Eventually I made it halfway through a playthrough on the hardest difficulty before deciding I had had enough of the bad RNG and frankly BS difficulty curve and putting the game down for good. And I don't recommend playing on that difficulty -- but the rest was a good time, to my surprise.

Would recommend on sale if you're the type that likes X-COM but wishes it were a lot more complicated and perhaps wasn't quite as good.\t\t\t\t\t
