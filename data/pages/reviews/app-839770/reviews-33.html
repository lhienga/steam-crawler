\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn the first few hours of the game I thought the game had potential and could be a good X-COM like game.
Oh boy, was I wrong. This games has so many flaws, it felt more like work than playing a game.

Inventory management: basically not existing, everything is put into an unending list of all the crappy items you have found. There are some filters, but they are bugged and don't work most of the time. And even if they work, they are not that helpful.

Difficulty scaling: Enemies become stronger the more time passes in the game. However, there are only few ways to improve your soldiers. New weapon technologies are mostly similar in strength to the weapons you already have. They just have a slightly different mix of stats.

In most missions there are endless waves of enemies spawning. Plus there are some enemies that spawn additional enemies. In these missions it feels like you are always outnumbered.

I am so disappointed in this game. It feels more like a game from 90s in terms of comfortability.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSnap Shot's Phoenix point has been out for several years and early developed since 2016. The property of the Embracer Group. I took a fast look at this now-classic game and gave you my quick thoughts on whether to invest or not.
What I initially liked:
Aesthetics: It is Xcom Chosen all over the place. Crisp beautiful story scenes, look, and design. An Xcom isometric player's dream.
Game Play: If you love X-com and cannot get enough of "Longwar". Point will help that X-com itch appeased initially. This game is for you.
Graphics and Sounds: Pure gorgeousness. Clean sounds, music, mood, doom, and gloom Apokolips "annihilation" film theme stuff. Each faction and even the world map, are clear and precise. Nice.
All in one: You like tactics, in, you want global strategy, done, You want app quick decision gameplay, like Prego, it's in there. Customizing your soldiers, Kudos. Cool icky aliens, in there.
Lore Stories: Short lore stories add to the gameplay not distract from it. Nicely done.
Controls: Not bad, simple, and to the point.
What I did not care for:
Redundant: Phoenix Point could be X-com Chosen and vice versa. Unrefined experimental games.
Genre: Nothing new created here. In fact, you find out that the micromanagement of creating buildings, reverse engineering, and crafting items is more wasteful in this game than in Xcom. You soon learn this is about building efficient teams, air fleets, and faction management than building towards something. Negative for me.
Pricey: With all the DLC nonsense and the game. It is an expensive entry for playing something similar.
Lacks Character: First if you are going to have a story told by the soldiers, like your tutorial and other parts. They should be playable characters. One of the biggest issues, especially for gamers who love and get personal with their soldiers is they are irrelevant to the main task of the game. You can give them all the personal touches you wish. They are meaningless banta fodder and developers have failed to address this growing base of players. Mods do not mean development gold to players.
Isometric: It's an old format, isometric to third-person/first-person mode continues to keep you away from the action. Keep players in Third Person mode. Get things in the action. Mordheim continues to be the precedent no one is learning from.

My last words for now:
Phoenix Point is a fun game. It is a crude version of Longwar, where team management and fleet management is more important than the strategic overall aspects because like most of these type of games. It's about getting to the finish line, not anything you do with your time in between. Until a developer figures out that chess tactical gameplay and strategy gameplay has to matter in character development and story where they must unify with one another. Then you will continue to have these fragmented developed games. See the video above discussion to see how the game plays before investing. A Sale would be wise. Phoenix Point will tickle your itch, but you will want more than the game can give you.
My Score 3.2 out of 5

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBest way I can think of to describe this game is a blend of classic and new X-Com with some interesting elements of its own to help it stand out from the crowd. I say it's a blend of the previous games because the interface is fairly similar to the newer games and it features a half cover/full cover mechanic.

That said though, the actual combat is more akin to classic X-Com. Rather than having two action points and every action you take consuming either one or all of your action points, you have four turn units. The distance your units move determines how many TU they consume rather than just having a simple move or sprint that consumes either one or all of your AP and different equipment, skills, and weapons consume different amounts of TU as well. Shooting is also based on line of sight and projectile trajectory as well rather than being based on an RNG system where your shots either hit or miss. I mentioned it has X-Com's cover system but it functions quite differently here. Rather than just being a factor in the RNG system it actually determines how much of your unit is exposed and their stance. Units in full cover post up against some tall cover like a wall or tall stack of boxes which allows them to shoot around corners and as long as the enemy doesn't have a clear LoS on them, said cover is going to actually protect them. Units in half cover are in waist high cover and crouch behind them which allows units behind them to (somewhat) safely fire over their heads without the risk of blowing their heads off (yes there is indeed friendly fire here).

The game also does a few of its own things as well. It uses a locational damage system where each body part has its own separate amount of HP. In addition to dealing damaging to the unit's overall health, you can also disable that body part which affects them in different ways while also inflicting bleeding to organic units which deals damage over time to the unit at the beginning of their side's turn (also it stacks which can lead to some pretty satisfying moments where you disable multiple parts on a enemy to the point where they're taking 40 damage per turn and just drop dead in one or two turns). In addition to that, there's also a free aim mechanic where you go into a first person view of your units and manually aim their weapon which opens up a window for a lot of strategic opportunities. You can line up a shot to destroy a specific body part, use a burst weapon that fires in bursts, aiming it so it'll inflict damage to multiple parts, or even line up a burst that'll hit multiple enemies. Personally, of all the X-Com style games I've played, this is probably my favorite system. To me, it just feels more natural rather than being based on hit percentages.

The only real complaints I've had about the game so far is that haven defense missions (basically the game's equivalent of terror missions) can sometimes feel pretty cheap at times. I've had a few haven defense missions where enemies started off right next to the objects you have to protect for said missions while my units started clear on the other side of the map. When it comes to the geoscape, I'm not particularly fond of how establishing new bases works either. Rather than just building new bases, you have to find and reactivate bases so actually establishing (or even finding) a base in a key location literally just boils down to pure luck. There's also the weapons as well. Don't get me wrong, they're generally satisfying, but as others have pointed out, a lot of the weapons just feel like sidegrades. A big contributor to what made X-Com such an intense game was the constant need to stay ahead of the curve in terms of technology. Bigger and badder aliens were constantly being introduced and they didn't stop being introduced just because you were putzing around shooting down the occasional UFOs. You were in a constant arms race with the enemy and that went a long way in creating tension. Without the need to research and pump out superior equipment as quickly as possible just to keep up with the enemies a lot of that tension ends up being lost.

Aside from those issues though, I've been thoroughly enjoying the game. The main thing to keep in mind before you buy it though is that this is indeed an X-Com style game so you're in for a rollercoaster ride of victories and defeats. When your soldiers die, they die for good. You will lose soldiers and you'll probably even lose some of your best soldiers at times. There's going to be times when most of your guys don't make it home from a mission or maybe even worse, they all get wiped out. There's also going to be extremely satisfying times when you pull off flawless victories that are insanely satisfying (and let me tell you, there's few more satisfying than steamrolling sanity destroying eldritch horrors so badly that they actually try to flee the battlefield entirely as their buddies bleed out and try to limp away on mangled legs in sheer terror). As long as you can stomach the deep lows you'll face enough to enjoy the satisfying highlights you'll experience, I'd strongly recommend this game.\t\t\t\t\t
