Intro

While this is generally a positive review and I do recommend this game, Phoenix Point is quite a mixed bag. It feels like that for every design decision that is innovative, interesting and exciting, there is a design decision that is just bafflingly bad and frustrating.

As someone who have poured many hours into XCOM2, Phoenix Point does a lot of the things that I wished XCOM2 would do, but on the other hand it ruins a lot of the things that XCOM2 did really well. In very broad strokes, I would say that Phoenix Point excels at the strategic layer, while XCOM2 excels at the tactics layer.

The Strategy Layer

The strategy layer has a lot for me to like because there are many different play styles. You are basically unlimited by the amount of aircraft and strike teams you can make and you can divide your forces between exploring points of interests, doing supply raids to gather resources, defending havens and doing story missions. The faction system also helps varying the strategy layer up and offers several different approaches to advance the story and research.

There are many, many customization options for the soldiers - both from class creation and combos and also with gear. You get several weapon choices that aren't direct upgrades, but just offer different advantages and disadvantages (like range vs damage) or different utilities (like flamethrower vs grenade launcher).
You also get many armor options, including mutations and cybernetics, to further push the customization.

Something that I really disliked was that your aircraft requires a chain of points of interest in order to reach a specific point of the map, but those are only revealed inside the radar range of a base, so you quite literally might have to build 3 bases and wait for their very slow scan time to finish just to reach a mission on the other side of the planet. You can get one of these missions in the first hour of the campaign and it was very frustrating for me to basically stop progression that early on.
This gets even more frustrating with the Festering Skies DLC which adds aerial combat and enemy flyers on the map. You can only engage in combat over a known point of interest, you can't just intercept the flyers on their way to or from one. A bunch of flyers can spawn on an unexplored section of the map and you simply can't do anything about it other than pouring a lot of resources on building bases and scanning the area. This DLC is full of very bad design decisions.

The Tactics Layer

The tactics layer innovates with the free aim system and also the limb shooting mechanic, both of which I enjoyed a lot and gave more aspects for tactics considerations.
I liked the enemy AI for the most part. They don't just carelessly run into overwatch or environmental hazards, and they always manage to move and stop in places that would be hard for you to reach with weapons.

The variations in enemies come from the fact that each enemy type is composed of modules - the same enemy type can spawn with a machine gun arm or a huge melee pincer. It can spawn with heavily armored legs, or more lightly armored legs that allows it to jump up buildings. This seemed like a cool concept at first, but I did find that I constantly needed to check each enemy info because I was never sure just from looking at an enemy what it could to.
In contrast, in XCOM2 every alien is very visually distinct and has a specific set of skills. Once you initiate combat in XCOM2 you can immediately start devising your tactics and setting kill priorities. In Phoenix Point I just found that whole process to be slower and less fun.

I mentioned that you can customize a lot of the soldier classes and skills, however, despite this, I just found most of the skills and abilities very unexciting to use - again in contrast to XCOM2 where most of the abilities felt very impactful and satisfying to use.

General Polish

Calling Phoenix Point unpolished would be an understatement. Phoenix Point really suffers from bad UI. Important stats are not easily displayable, tooltips don't accurately describe abilities, some mechanics - especially those introduced by DLCs - are very poorly explained if at all and it's just generally unclear what you can or can not do. I've had quite a few visits to wiki pages for the game, but even there a lot of the explanations are missing or are unclear.
The DLCs just stacked even more poorly explained mechanics on top of those existing in the base game, so it's probably best to start a new game with all DLCs disabled.

Despite steam workshop support, mods are almost non existing sadly. This game badly needed some UI mods to help with providing information and circumvent some of the odd design choices, much like XCOM2 got.

Conclusion

This review is ridiculously long but I still feel like I've only scratched the surface. Phoenix Point was a just few bad decisions away from being an amazing game. There is still a lot of fun to be had here and some truly innovative and fun mechanics. I do hope it will get some sort of well polished sequel.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI want to like this game, and I want to recommend it, based on the strength of the writing alone. My God. There's a whole website full of short stories for this game that exists just for the purposes of world-building, and the content is better than a lot of A-list novels these days. Unfortunately, while this game had me chomping at the bit to discover its secrets, I just don't have the will to play it anymore...and I've only invested 50 hours!

There are three factions that you need to interact with in order to gain the technology and resources that you'll need to save the world. Each time you encounter one for the first time, you are given a mission to help them deal with some kind of internal problem. Have fun having your level 1 squad with starter equipment faced off with a unit of elite soldiers wearing advanced armor and carrying Gauss weapons or laser guns. These are often the very first points of interest made available to you, and it feels like getting kicked in the teeth every time.

This utter lack of balance persists and gets worse as the game progresses, and is compounded by a whole lot of QoL issues like having to manufacture and equip your soldiers with ammunition that somehow occupies 15% of their inventory space PER MAGAZINE while costing 15% if the resources that it would take to build the gun you'll be shooting it out of, once again, PER MAGAZINE. Bullets aren't that big, and they don't weigh very much. Every time you're scrounging for resources in an alien-infested junkyard at the beginning of the game and can't pick up a resource crate because you brought that extra magazine for your pistol? Feels pretty bad, man.

There are mods available that provide some additional content and try to help with some of the quality of life problems, but using them just kind of makes the whole game feel like a cobbled together mess. The developers themselves released a mod onto the Workshop that allows you to disable a lot of the more frustrating "features" of the game, but it's not great. For example, I turned off ammunition, which is supposed to eliminate the ability/need to manufacture ammo while still requiring your soldiers to reload when their weapons are empty. After every mission using this mod, the game still prompts me to replenish my soldiers by manufacturing ammo. Even though I can't, because I disabled it.

If this had been released right away on Steam with full Workshop support, it might have evolved into one of the greatest gaming masterpieces ever created. Sadly, its exclusive year on the Epic Game Store killed a lot of the enthusiasm that it might have had, while the XCOM games launched with full mod support and gave us amazing community efforts like the Long War mods.

There are some amazing bones here, and I love some of the ambitious and innovative features that they tried to provide for us. Armored transports, the ability to aim at and disable individual body parts, true line-of-sight, a constantly evolving enemy, and a number of other great mechanics stood this title apart from XCOM and should have made for a unique and fun experience. Every line of story and dialogue is masterfully written and fully voice acted, the art direction and graphics are fabulous. Sadly, all of this great potential is cut down by gameplay that is so frustrating and demoralizing that I just can't do it anymore.

There is a type of player that will probably enjoy this. Maybe this title is a love letter to them? If you played the original X-COM: UFO Defense and smiled every time one of your soldiers was killed immediately after opening a door or stepping off an aircraft, you'll probably like this, too. I know that there are people who will suffer through this on legendary difficulty for the right to say that they did it, and that's absolutely fine. If you play games for fun or leisure rather than a sense of achievement, however, I'd consider giving this one a pass. If you really want to play the best the genre has to offer, use the funds you would have spent here to buy XCOM 2.
