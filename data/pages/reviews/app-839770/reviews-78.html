\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTLDR: Phoenix Point is a solid but imperfect turn-based tactics game that, if you enjoy the genre and can live without sophisticated strategic gameplay or a compelling story, probably deserves your dinero.

The free aim / visual targeting system during tactical combat is by far PP's best feature - much more fun than the RNG 'dice roll' mechanics of previous games in the genre and, I hope, something we see more of. In essence, you freely direct a camera representing the selected character's POV, zooming in and out and panning around until you find something you want to shoot at. The 'accuracy' of the weapon you are firing is represented by a circle (which scales as you zoom) within which all your shots will land in - the smaller the circle, the more accurate your weapon. Cover is just whatever blocks you or your target from view - your units will duck / flatten themselves against what is nominally highlighted as 'low' and 'high' cover but really the important thing is what proportion of your character's body is hidden behind something that can't be shot through.

With this free aim system and individual hit location effects, combat becomes about surgically disabling enemy weapon systems, targeting unarmoured limbs, and finding shooting angles that keep you as little exposed as possible. The enemies develop stronger and stronger armour as the game progresses but, by finding each enemy's weak points, carefully thinking about the order your units attack in (some weapons penetrate armour, some don't penetrate armour but shred it, some don't do much damage but can stun enemies, etc, etc) and using a small handful of special abilities that higher level characters get, defeating them always feels like a mini puzzle game rather than an RNG dice throw.

It does feel like this base system could be built on much further but, as the first game (that I know of anyway) to use this sort of system, it's a great new way to approach turn-based tactical combat that feels fun and satisfying.

Likewise, the pseudo-Lovecraftian lore and way it combines - eerily, given 2020 - mass pandemics and climate change into a horror-survival narrative also feels like a fresh take on a familiar theme... though equally somewhat under-utilised. There are some nice world building text descriptions during 'random encounters' on the strategic-level map but a few more details that supported world-building in the tactical maps would have been welcome.

The main problem with PP is that, with all love for the game, it does become pretty boring during its longish midgame. There aren't enough variety of enemies (the much vaunted 'adapting enemies' means not much more than sometimes the same basic enemy will have a grenade launcher and sometimes they won't) or of biomes / map types or of mission varieties so that, by the midgame, you've seen nearly (nearly...) everything the game has to offer.

Added to this, the minimalist soundtrack, while brilliantly atmospheric at times, is generally so minimalist that you're often playing in near silence and a limited palette of customisation options and little ability to give your units personality meant I was never as invested in individual characters as I was in, say, the later XCom games.

Finally, on the negatives, while the tactical game is great, the strategic layer is very minimal, 'diplomacy' not worthy of the name, and - most of a problem for me - there just wasn't enough character or narrative to keep me hooked. There are few NPCs to provide emotional content and a very thin overarching story that doesn't have enough dramatic hooks to keep you invested in a story.

In conclusion, if you're looking for a game with a story that will keep you captivated (such as Banner Saga) or on the edge of your seat (such as XCom 2 et al) or involved strategic or diplomatic gameplay, then I think you'd be disappointed in PP. However, if you think you'll be satisfied with a solid tactical turn-based game with some light strategy elements and a focus on innovative, if not earth shattering, combat mechanic then I reckon you'll be happy enough.\t\t\t\t\t

Very X-COM Like, but not X-COM
This is for the Year One Edition. So it means reviewing the game with the DLC and all the patches and improvements. Unsurprisingly the game has a lot of X-COM feel. but there are some key differences. It's possible you won't feel as invested in the game as with X-COM and I'll touch on the why's of that a few times.

I do recommend it, but there are reasons why you might still be disappointed. So do the "reading all the words" thing if part of what you liked about X-COM was really making your team super and you actually felt your individual soldiers had personalities and you really loved the story.

On the other hand if the X-COM combat felt a bit tedious at times or formulaic or you found there was a certain soldier class you just wished could do a little bit more, PP fixes that. If you wanted a different type of geoscape/base building meta. If you thought X-COM didn't always feel grand enough, you too might want to read all the words.

How it's different from X-Com
Pheonix point pushes you to not care about your soldiers as much. It provides mechanisms that allow you replace them faster and more readily. It pushes you to develop multiple teams. This is still a tough thing for me since I don't like losing soldiers and I don't like save scumming so when you get a bad mission draw and things happen that you have very little chance to avoid that get soldiers killed it frustrates me a lot even though I know it likely won't really stop me from progressing. It's not a bad mechanic, if you embrace it I can see how it makes the game feel like a more desperate fight. But this is a hard point for me and I can see how it would make you like the game less even you don't really get why.

All Your Bases Are Belong to Us
Lots of bases, and you'll need them. You don't need to develop them all, but with how scanning works in the current version (it's automatic and "constant" once you have the facilities compared to having to manually trigger them in earlier versions) you do need to active a few bases. So it's a very different Geoscape meta from X-COM. I'm OK with this, I just wish that controller navigation worked better. I just have too many issues with not realizing I have a aircraft selected when I'm going to check out a base and since they don't just give specific buttons to the actions when you have a base selected I can accidentally redirect a flight to a base when I was just intending to inspect it to start a repair/upgrade. This isn't really an issue with KB/mouse. But I actually prefer the controller for the combat. I'm odd.

Combat
You have more actions in Pheonix point, that alone gives more flexibility. You still have the issue where range is way more important than accuracy, but the free aim does help a little with this. There are still times where your full aim circle is completely within the target and you still miss, but it's not as common as a 99% hit chance missing in X-COM once you are used to it. I certainly like combat better in Pheonix Point than X-COM This is also aided by smaller maps quicker engagement speed, less need to hunt for that one last jerk of an alien. But as mentioned everything is more fragile in PP compared to X-Com. You and the enemy both, even the big armored enemies you face will go down faster, sometimes because of the teamwork you can bring to focus on them more readily. All of your soldiers have more flexibility than in X-COM so it easier to adapt to most situations. Though you do still run into issues where you just get flattened and you don't really have much chance to respond.

You aren't meant to save everyone
You can, but the way things work they want you to side with just one, maybe two, of the three factions and then you the other as your punching bag to get the various resources you need or want. It really is a lot simpler to just raid one of the factions for a vehicle or tech or food or supplies than just about anything else at certain stages of the game.

Healing is weird
Your broken limbs heal instantly after battle, but your HP need a medical center. Your stamina is supposed to matter but it doesn't matter until you get to a really a low break point. So this does lead to some situations where you hang around at the end of missions using medpacks to heal the HP damage on everyone so that you can have that team in action again much faster because the fact that one soldier had both legs and arm and their head disable doesn't matter, you can go fight an Pandoran incursion 30 minutes later and she's all good. The mechanics are in place to make it much more interesting, and with how they push you to not care as much about soldiers and develop more of them the missed some opportunities. The simplest for me would be that having a limb disabled is a stamina hit. You have 40 and it really doesn't matter until you get under 10 and a mission only takes like 2 most of the time. So if you lost 5 for a disabled limb then yeah the BS stuff would suck, but it still doesn't stop you from rapid deployments unless your getting disabled a lot. So flying back to a base and just dropping that soldier off to recover and grabbing another would be another strategy layer to add.

If you like X-COM you'll probably like PP
There is more combat, it will be with more squads, this will force you to learn new tactics because you just won't have your A-Team all the time. This will actually make you enjoy things more and make you more effective. You may get tired of it, you may realize that certain mission types just annoy you with risk reward and you'll skip them. But in it's current state, that's OK because there is always something to do for most of the game. You may feel more disconnected from the game than you did with X-COM. The story is not delivered as forcefully, you don't get as invested in your troops. You might get annoyed with how black and white all three factions see things when you sit there seeing all the obvious ways that at least any 2 of them could combine and make even more awesome stuff, admittedly it's hard to see how all three could coexist.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a thumbs up but I want to let people know what to expect and highlight some flaws. In general, this is a fun game and you can sink a lot of hours into it and enjoy yourself.

However, if you're coming over from modern Xcom you need to know a few things. They have the same framework but their mechanics are very different at times. Cover really only acts to conceal your soldiers from gunfire by making them bigger or smaller targets. You don't have a % chance to miss a shot, you have general odds of EACH ROUND going into a specific zone and if one or multiple enemies (or allies) are in that zone they are at risk of getting hit. Free aiming is newly implemented and let's you pick off certain body parts or weapons (very cool and useful). You also get to pick which lanes your soldiers overwatch, which is extremely helpful, but the enemy will take notice and attempt to thwart you through movement. Also, make sure you don't set yourself up so an enemy can pull overwatch through one of your own soldiers (a hilarious up that will cost you a soldier to friendly fire.)

This game lacks certain quality of life features. Examples: your soldiers use action points (AP), which are represented by a bar made up of 4 slivers (each is an AP). The game gives you general information about how much AP will be used moving a character or performing certain actions but there are times (infuriating times) where your character will be left with what looks like 1 AP, which is sometimes all you want or need, but in reality you actually have just a teensy bit less than 1 AP (which generally leaves your character dead,

The overworld HUD will draw your attention to, audibly alert you to, and constantly mark faction havens that are under attack. You will not be alerted in a similar manner when your own bases are threatened. You instead receive a silent text alert in the top right corner of the HUD that you can click on. This cost me several bases.

You will need to remember the names and locations of your bases because the game does not do a great job of easily making it known which bases are where and what resources you are sending to which parts of the world. This caused me to many times back out of what I wanted to do and click around until I found the base, briefly memorized the name, and then clicked back into what I was doing.

When a friendly faction comes under attack you will see two numbers: Attacking strength and defending strength. As far as I can tell, these numbers only mean something if you're going to let the battle autoresolve because you might think that a defending strength of 11 to attacking strength of 3 means you're going to have significant ally support. You'd be wrong. You might have 3 allies on the map and sometimes they will just plain skip their turn and get massacred. Do not rely on them.

Remember how pissed you were when one of your soldiers got mind controlled? Brace yourself because certain enemies can mind control multiple characters (they will then smartly hide so that you cannot break the mind control easily). In short, enemy mind control rocks, yours does not.

Do not expect the constant feeling of progression in technology that you got from Xcom. The weapons you can get pretty early will pretty much be what takes you through the game. I found the other weapons to be more lateral moves than upward ones. Useful for niche encounters or enemies but not going to make your strong characters feel mightier.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tUnfortunately I am unable to fully endorse this game. It is certainly playable and to some extent enjoyable but ultimately I decided not to devote any more time to it for a number of reasons.
1. The overall direction of the game isn't clear. What should I do next? What is the most important area to research? Which missions are the most important? In the end I couldn't decide whether my overall strategy was largely correct or doomed to ultimate failure.
2. There is a certain lack of polish when compared to games such as Xcom2 and Gears Tactics. This is reflected in the graphics in the missions which are very basic to say the least.
3. It is tough, probably too tough. Playing on veteran.
4. Overall I found it to be quite 'fiddly' with the different factions and too many weapon types etc. I would have preferred it to be more straightforward. I.e. fighting a single enemy, the factions for me representing something of an over-complicated story line.
Had this game been released 10 years ago it would have been incredible but in 2021, not so much. It's a shame as the game is far from being a 'turkey', just lacked direction, graphics, mission level design and being way too fiddly. It was however very stable, no crashing issues and just one minor bug with my troops entering/exiting an APC on a certain level. After 44 hours I have decided not to invest any more time in what has proved to be an ultimately quite frustrating game, which having played every single XCOM game, I had wanted to adore.
