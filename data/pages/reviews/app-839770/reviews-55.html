\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLet's star with positives. Shooting mechanics is fresh and interesting. You have to see target or its parts and aim at it. Damaging body parts disable them. this is especially useful against enemies with guns, as you can break arms so they cannot shoot you.
Sadly, that works for you too and there is only on class (technician) that can fix broken arm. To make it worse, most common type of enemy, can regenerate. So disabling them has no effect.
Other thing in tactical combat is continuous spawns. On some maps, you have to do the thing and retreat. This wit combination that you have to pay for ammo (this is main resource drain) makes it quite annoy.

Overall, the game quality decrease over time. Early game, research has lots of flavor text, but longer you play, you get almost now extra info after you finish research. Also most research is irrelevant, it gives nothing interesting or better. Like having +15% damage on enemy that needs one shoot to kill. That is just lazy. You will also get most of the weapons and armor early game.
Mid game, you found out, that nothing really matters. Things happen on Geoscape, but no matter what you do, there is no progress. You just play, or stay at home base. Saving havens only matters for reward.
In the late game, you will found out, that all 4 ways to finish the game leads to the same final mission. It doesn't matter who you ally with. You can just let the game flow until you get to experience the worst part of the game. Final mission is arena, with bullet sponges enemies, artillery enemies etc. It is just designed to be exploited.
Overall the game feels rushed and unfinished. Late game is boring, you can see how quality declines and early amazement is gone.
DLCs add something that were planned but didnt make it to the game. Like air combat. That said, there is no research on flying enemies, weapons are questionable. And some dont even work on enemies, like EMP damage. This whole thing is confusing.
Next DLC expands on the precursor civilization idea, but it is implemented too early, so in main story line, you get shocking reveal, yet you already mine precursor tech.
And corruption DLC adds enemy that calls reinforcements, because thats what we needed.
So yes, the game needs year or two of development. It was already delayed on lunch and thanks to epic, released in this sorry state. Late game needs complete rework, as well as final mission.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWell, time to write another review. I usually only do this when a game has either been incredibly good or incredibly bad, in my eye. This time, it's been an incredibly good one.

Now, despite me calling this a good game, there are a few glaring flaws that bug me. I will be starting my review with those, in a list.

-The Acheron enemy introduced in Corrupted Horizons is hilariously overtuned. I often find myself instantly restarting a mission to reroll the enemy counts if I see two or more of them on my first turn. They need to be nerfed.

-This one is a pro and a con. The amount of voice-over and instruction you get is highly variable. Sometimes you'll be going around doing mission after mission without any NPC saying anything, just commanding your squad and murdering aliens (or humans). Other times you'll be given voice-overs and cutscenes after every mission. Sometimes you encounter something new and the game tells you about it. Sometimes the game expects you to figure it out. I honestly like this, but it needs to be consistent. You're a small military outfit fighting an unknown threat. It makes sense you have no clue what the enemy can do until you experience it more or research it.

-DLC is poorly integrated into the main game. What I mean by this is that the DLC missions will often overlap, with varying levels of quality put in. A strong example is the introductory mission to Festering Skies. Athena, the main spokesperson for Synedrion, contacts you. There is no voice over for her speech here. In almost every other thing she says in the entire game, it is voice-acted. This can be... offputting, to say the least. At least that was my impression. Additionally, each DLC adds new things to research that may lead to specialized gear or equipment that only helps you in that DLC. Again, Festering Skies is a good example here. Most of the research projects give you weapons to put on your aircraft. You will not be using these weapons when you engage in content from any other DLC.

Now that I've gotten that out of the way, let's talk about what I love about this game. You start out with one aircraft, a handful of soldiers, and a few places to visit. Already, the atmosphere here is that you're venturing out into the unknown to explore. As you poke around you find some scavenging sites to try to loot resources from, or meet the factions who you can either befriend or try to loot resources from. I love the choice here. I typically end up allying with all 3 main factions, but it's excellent that I can just antagonize them for profit if I wanted to. The Pandorans, the alien threat you fight, start off hilariously weak. They fall before your soldiers easily even if you have barely a clue what you're doing. You simultaneously feel like you're a badass, yet there's still this big world to explore. Then the Pandorans learn how to hold guns, and you see them showing up with guns from the main factions you've met. You get the impression that they've killed them and taken their weapons; that they're learning to fight you just like you're learning to fight them.

As you explore more of the map and your sphere of influence expands, you'll start getting more aircraft on the field. More squads of soldiers. Soon you have several squads operating at once, with one campaign happening on one side of the world and another happening on the other side. The Phoenix Project slowly grows into its own faction on par with the others, able to carry out missions all over the world and operate from multiple bases at once. Even with all this range of influence, you start seeing the red mist spread on parts of the world you can't reach yet. You start to think that the Pandorans are over there killing people. People who are either your friends or valuable targets for raiding, depending on how you've handled the game thus far.

Soon enough, you've expanded to the rest of the world. You have the entire globe under your careful watch and you're looking for your next target - whether that be a Pandoran attack to defend against, or a weak haven ripe for a raid. I'm suddenly realizing I've been sitting here more or less writing a story. This is a story that the game tells through the gameplay. You write this game's story with the choices you make. Sure there are only 4 endings (5 if you count getting a game over) but the path you take to get to those endings can take many forms. The vast amount of things you can do at any point in time can be absolutely overwhelming and terrifying to a new player (and for this I suggest you play without any DLC on your first playthrough). This game is a sandbox in the best way.

I should probably say something about combat and character customization, though most people talk at length about this. A soldier has 4 Action Points (AP). Moving a certain distance drains 1 AP. Firing a gun can cost anywhere from 1 AP to 3 AP. This means that if you have a gun that costs 2 AP, you can either fire it twice in one turn or use some AP to get into a better position and then fire. This might not sound very impressive early game, but once you have a max rank soldier with two classes maxed out, they might do 5 or 6 actions in a single turn, or maybe kill half the enemy team. Yes, you can multiclass your soldiers. Want a sniper/assault that returns fire and takes an arm off an enemy every time he gets shot? You can do that. Want a berserker/heavy that barrages an enemy halfway across the map with a grenade launcher 4 times a turn? You can do that. Want a berserker/infiltrator that assassinates enemies in melee from stealth? You can do that too. There are tons of options. Anyone can equip any armor, anyone can equip any weapon. Armor is purely for stats, and isn't class restricted. Weapons, however, will carry an accuracy penalty if the soldier doesn't have proficiency with the weapon they're using. That doesn't mean they can't kill something with it though.

Anyway, I have no clue how long I've been writing this review. tl:dr good game, do not regret the purchase. As a closing note I'll address the fact that this game gets compared so much with the Firaxis XCOM games. I don't think they should be. They're two different beasts. That said, if you enjoy XCOM:EU/EW or XCOM2 you'll probably enjoy this. I've played both games extensively and they're some of my favorites.\t\t\t\t\t

the aiming system is way more interesting, i think. same applies to the cover system imho.
there is not simply a certain percentage chance for success -- the systems are way more realistic.

edit: while i still think that the aiming system as well as the cover system is superior, phoenix point gets a bit too repetitive for my liking. and the strategical count-down-timer is nothing i like too much.

still a really good game if you like the genre. right now i would prefer xcom with long war and long war rebalance mods on top, though.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is gold. It delivers where the X-Com series has always failed me a bit: at the balance. In X-Com it is too easy to screw up. Countless times I found myself biting the keyboard for failing a die roll or losing cover to light enemy fire. Not so in Phoenix point. Instead of two turns, each character gets four. Instead of a random number generator determining a hit, all bullets hit in a predefined circle. These two aspects alone make Phoenix Point more predictable and, thus, less infuriating than X-Com. It's still possible to ♥♥♥♥ it up but it's easy to tell beforehand, how risky a move is. In Phoenix Point you interact with various factions looking for a way to cope with a alien invasion caused by a virus. Both helping and antagonizing these factions are valid strategies with their unique advantages. To cope you either eliminate the aliens, find a way to coexist, or evolve into something alien yourself. Each playstyle comes with different benefits and costs. It seems there is only one thing that Phoenix Point does not leave up to choice: to counter the alien threat you need to leave your humanity behind.\t\t\t\t\t
