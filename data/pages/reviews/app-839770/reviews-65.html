\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI think this game solves some of the pacing problems that XCOM 2 had, and its unique mechanics are really fun to play with and the game is visually cool.
There are Three mechanics I really like. The first is how aiming works. Instead of doing the XCOM style (95% to hit) and its all or nothing, you have two circles, one inside the other. Half your shots are likely to fall in the smaller circle, and ALL your shots will fall into the larger circle. Your guns shoot some number of bullets per shot (the basic gun shoots 6 bullets) so, sometimes you hit with all 6, sometimes one missis, or maybe your taking a long shot from across the board and most of your shots miss. It is much more satisfying than the XCOM % to hit mechanic.

The second mechanic is damage to body parts. Your arms, can take damage, or your legs, or your head. If they take enough, you start to bleed and the limb becomes disabled, providing some kind of detriment to your character. But you can shoot at enemies and disable some of their special attacks. That baddy with a gun for an arm? Shoot the gun off and he is defenseless and runs off the board. That shield is in the way? Shoot the arm holding the shield and he drops it.

The Third Mechanic I like is a simple one. Your soldiers get exp AND Skill Points. Meaning, if your guy has gotten to a high level on simulations alone, he still isn’t going to be as strong as the guy who gained his experience through combat missions. To make up for the fact that someone can be higher level, but lower skill, you also gain Skill Points you can use freely on any soldier every time you complete a mission.

All in all, I am enjoying the game quite a lot.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI want to love this game, but it has serious issues that make it difficult to enjoy. The game’s story is set in a science fiction / cosmic horror setting. The story follows a linear progression with a narrative that largely fails to benefit from its setting. Except for perhaps Synedrion, you are given no options to meaningfully influence the story.

Ground battles are turn-based and require thoughtful positioning and action point usage. The game tends to reward blitzing and run-and-gun tactics. Mechanics like cover, flanking, and height advantages are not present or have so little influence to not be noticeable. Weapon firing uses a snapshot or a quirky free-aim mechanic that takes some getting used to. The free-aim mechanic functions much like your typical RNG to-hit-chance with a per bullet / pellet hit calculation. I say this because a noticeable number of shots where the reticle completely covers the enemy can still miss and do no damage. This is particularly noticeable with sniper rifles. The to-hit-chance does go to a 100% if all of the pixels in the reticle and around it are only the target, but this often requires you to be right on top of your target.

[Minor spoilers] The Festering Skies DLC adds a rock-paper-scissors style aircraft battle system. It also includes helpful modules that increase your travel speed, and can allow your soldiers to rest while travelling. These additions are nice, but they come with base and haven bombing raids that require resources to repair and can destroy havens. The late game enemy aircraft also field many more weapons than you and start to partially heal after each battle. Unless you are willing to risk significant air assets equipped with advanced weapons they are functionally unbeatable.

Diplomacy is a practice of picking two factions to be allies, and using the other as a full time loot pinata. To really be successful you will need to treat them all as loot pinatas. You should be performing regular raids on faction havens to steal resources, technology, and aircraft. There is no other way to reasonably get the resources and number of aircraft you will need quickly enough. As long as you balance raiding with haven defense, destroying enemy bases, and completing faction diplomacy missions it is not hard to keep positive faction standings.

There is little reason to care about your bases or soldiers. The bases play no significant role in the game’s narrative outside of the initial tutorial and the occasional random base raid. From a game mechanics perspective you will need them to increase your research speed, production capacity, and extend the range of your aircraft. The bases feel more like a resource and time sink, just something you need to spend resources on to progress the story. The soldiers do not have biographies and their customization options are extremely limited. Their personal perks can same what differentiate them them, but they are implemented in a random way that often results in no to limited synergy. For the most part, they are interchangeable. Besides the solders I received during the tutorial, I would be hard pressed to remember any of them.

[Minor spoilers] The last mission’s boss battle is what flipped my recommendation from a tentative yes to a no. Already many of the game's enemies are not fun to fight. Expect to encounter multiple disabling, mind control, and stun mechanics supported by stealth or high armor / hit points. The game’s last boss fight is worse, it breaks the existing mechanics to offer a new battle experience. The boss has only one attack, it cannot be blocked, mitigated, or disabled, and the damage stacks if your soldiers get too close. It does this while endless spawning additional enemies to attack you from the sides and only occasionally being vulnerable on select angles due to rotating invulnerable shields.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHonestly, there are some elements that I've really enjoyed, tactical battles are nice, the free aim and attack style (where you can actually aim and have them hit where they're aiming, as opposed to having a dice roll say you missed from a foot away) give the game a good tactical feel. Unfortunately, that is probably the shiniest part of this otherwise pretty dull game. It's not a bad game, but it has a lot of details that can mess with your enjoyment.

Just about every item in the game has to be manufactured, from armor pieces, to ammo, to guns and grenades. This leads to a lot of micro, where you're unequiping entire squads to give another their guns because it's cheaper to hotswap than to make more.

Diplomacy is more of a lesson in cheesing and exploits, as to any true meaningful actions. You are encouraged to try and play nice, but also that since you are fighting for the greatest good, that you should also just take what you need from them. which puts you in this loop of raiding for resources, then grinding for rep by wiping the Swamp Monsters, then repeating. If you're feeling particularly good/micro intensive, you can play nice and constantly have you planes fly around and trade, but like I said, it can be really micro intensive. The 3 factions that will always end up trying to kill each other, nevermind the horrible Swamp Monsters that are killing them all. None of your legitimate actions can stop their wars of extermination. Even if you max out diplomacy, there's no option to have them chill until after the extermination threat has passed. The best(?) thing you can do is to steal all/most of their aircraft, which will hamper their efforts to kill everyone.

Base Management is shallow, for a game that is about having Havens against the mist, and named your first base after the game, they're more like truck stops that contribute to the list of ♥♥♥♥ you got to pour resources into. At least the other Havens sometimes have events when you arrive. I'm not looking for a colony management game here, but there's literally no point to caring about them beyond the radar they provide, and that you can heal troops there.

There's more gripes that I have, though it feels like nitpicking now, but I suppose that's the point: the fun and interesting parts of the game were overshadowed by the micro intensive and cheesy geoscape, and it now just feels like I'm slogging through a quagmire, hoping that something interesting comes along\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAll the most frustrating parts of XCOM with none of the heart.

This game seems great on paper but the execution is just not there. PP:YOE's main selling point is essentially, "No XCOM rng madness, instead we give players grounded combat and realistic gun physics."

Cicada, cicada.

PP:YOE replaces "85% chance to hit" (XCOM translation = you're gonna miss) with two circles and your rounds will travel anywhere within the circles. So the enemies, depending upon weapon and range, take up a certain... um... percentage of the circles. Does it look like 85% of the enemy is in the circle, well you've got an 85% chance of hitting that enemy with each round. For an assault rifle one round might hit where you intended, 4 might hit an arm, and the rest might miss all together. Okay, I guess that's better and XCOM's approach? But use a 3-rounder, like the heavy class' weapon, or a single round weapon like the sniper rifle... well, let XCOM translate the result for you...

Last thing before wrapping up is the overwatch system: It is broken. Enemy AI detects your overwatch cones before they "arrive" on the map and skirts the edges of the battlefields avoiding stepping into overwatch. It makes it almost impossible to set up a decent, effective ambush. Overwatch activates when you're able to paint enemies with the cones after encountering them, but mostly, the result is just random and janky. PP:YOE utilizes the XCOM "slowdown" when overwatch is activated, but it seems you and the enemy are moving at two slightly, but consequential, different speeds. The result more often than not is your unit firing too late (enemy dropping a level or, more frequently, running past cover) and missing. Overwatch only seemed viable when an enemy is charging in for melee under 15 feet. Its just not good, y'all.

This isn't a trash game, which makes PP:YOE all the more frustrating. Take a look at what other reviews have to say about the core game and most of the DLC - especially Festering Skies. The game feels rushed and unpolished; it feels like early access content. But its not. I realize I'm posting a review after only five hours, but really that's all it took to recognize what this is: diluted XCOM with smoke and mirrors marketing.

Also, this is just, like, my opinion, man. Maybe you'll love it. Maybe you won't. I really suggest reading the reviews on this one. I should have...\t\t\t\t\t

Tips are confusing and seem contradictory. Tips repeat themselves. Game crashed on me twice in my small play experience.

The good? The mutated NPCs are cool, the ability to disable parts of a body is a cool mechanic and adds some variety to the combat.

I dunno, it looks and feels like XCOM when you first start but man, does that wear off quickly.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNo.

Phoenix Point is why certain game mechanics belong in the dustbin of history, and the new mechanics it brings aren't well thought out.

For context, I've attempted four playthoughs on Rookie, all of which have failed for varying reasons. This is coming from someone who managed to beat X-COM 2 on Normal, and X-COM Enemy Unknown on Commander. I've also played my fair share of the off brand X-COM games from the 2000's era (UFO Trilogy anyone?). I should perhaps also mention I was a backer for the game before they announced moving the game to Epic, and cancelled it then. The reason I bothered with it now? Writing this review of course. That and I bore easily. Rookie was picked out because of how difficult this game was intended to be, and I wanted to learn the mechanics.

Anyway, I'll start with the Tactical Combat. Dear ♥♥♥♥ this gets repetitive fast. It's also quite frustrating. There's no hit percentage chance, and even then shooting...well we like to pick on modern X-COM's soldiers for missing those 99% chances to hit. The only way I can describe shooting in Phoenix Point is "my soldiers are drunk". They take to the battlefield sloshed out on enough booze for TF2's Demoman to say they've drank too much, and tend to shoot at everything around the intended target. Even the Fallout-esque limb targeting doesn't improve their accuracy much, as they still have a tenancy to miss short ranged shots with no cover between the target and the shooter, and will often pop off the limbs you didn't target.

Cover is also worthless. Unlike what was advertised, even explosive weapons tend to do next to no damage to cover. You can't - say - blow a hole in a wall with a grenade like you can in X-COM. Also no, conventional weapons don't appear to shred cover either. More often that not, you'll also be moving soldiers out of cover to get better positions. And I don't mean X-COM-esque moving from one piece to cover to the next - I mean you will literally leave your soldiers standing out in the open to get good firing positions, a tactic that often ensures their own death. Speaking of, losing someone in X-COM is more often than not a painful experience. Losing someone here? "Eh, I can always recruit more cannon fodder".

The Geoscape isn't much better. I'll start with the thing that bricked three of my campaigns - the economy is completely ♥♥♥♥♥♥. To wit, you can't passively generate any of your three resources - Food, Materials and Science - without some technology that pops up in the middle of the game that lets you make your own Food, and you only get if you raid or ally with the Cult of Cthulhu - more on that in a moment. Unlike either X-COM game, the game cannot support the economy, since everything like Enemy Unknown is manufactured individually. And new recruits often come with no gear, unless you go to a settlement and hire a soldier they offer, which is often way more expensive. This means most of your resources will be spent on making gear for the recruits, and you may have to send underequipped Rookies into combat. This system was so frustrating and game bricking that I had to resort to mods just to play the game. On Rookie. That's usually a sign a game is not well thought out.

On top of this, you'll be dealing with the bands of Humans, Cyborgs and Psio- I mean the Arch Fanboys, Cult of Cthulhu and your local sociopathic Science Club. Dealing with these three groups isn't particularly interesting and mostly amounts to Arch yelling at me when I research certain upgrades on the tech tree. That being said, you "engage" with them by defending their Bases from enemy attack or occasionally doing missions (which usually involve raiding the other factions, which feels like a ♥♥♥♥ move). Even better, raiding a faction's research centers is the only way of getting certain technologies, which requires a Base to have a Research Center and favour from RNGesus to get the tech you want. So if there are - say - a bunch of Cult of Cthulhu havens near you and none of them have Research Facilities, good luck getting that food making building.

You can also ally with them. In theory, you have to pick one and gun to ally with them - it is not possible to unite all three groups, trust me I have tried this very thing. What usually happens however is I'll end up aligning with the Arch Fanboys or Cult of Cthulhu, and the Science Club usually end up allying with me as well. Something seems off there.

This system has also just lead to me asking "Can't I just buy/trade for the Tech?". The end result seems to be railroading you into a faction war, because once a faction's opinion of you starts to plummet, there's no way to improve it, and the Arch Fanboys are especially bad about this.

As for the DLC? Yeah, it's terrible garbage material. Hope you like fighting near invincible cyborgs with shields or a giant monster that'll deplete the human population (which will game over you if it drops too low) faster than Godzilla goes though Tokyo.

tl;dr: Phoenix Point reminds me of all those Classic X-COM knock-offs we got during the 2000's, and is filled with the same problems - a boring, unengaging mess that just resulted in me giving up on it. You'd have a more fun time with the more recent X-COM games or even the UFO Trilogy (of which this one heavily borrows ideas from UFO: Aftershock, particularly in terms of the three faction system). Avoid.

Oh, and this game also records your data for some reason unless you disable it in the options menu. Make sure to use a VPN when playing!
