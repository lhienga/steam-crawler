\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really wanted to recommend and like this. I love the genre, I really enjoyed my first 8 hours of it, but it just started to feel like a slog that became unfun.

5/10

Pros:
- Aim and fire: Taking out the right parts of enemies limits their effectiveness, adding a great amount of strategy
- Diplomacy: Help the right people to get more tech/gear/etc
- Trading: The ability to buy/sell supplies (and with careful checking, even making a profit moving stuff from one to another)
- The differences between factions, they're really interesting and well thought out, all three have their own unique takes on survival and their own tech; and the android people can be considered a sort-of fourth faction
- You feel like you're losing; you're up against an unbeatable enemy and really trying to survive/thrive
- Ammo system - having to carry ammo is great, and limited inventory. The missions where you grab gear are quite interesting too
- Mission variety - from attacking cities to defending them, to attacking pandora lairs to rescuing people; there is a great variety of interesting missions

Cons:
- Story: 25 years after the virus hits the game starts, and you start with -nothing-. There are factions that manage to have global communication, and while it makes sense they don't work together, it's strange that they basically suck at existing.
- Tech: Basic assault weaponry (but also rocket packs?) and basically useless armour. It's like taking army soldiers from now, but you forgot how to make shotguns or other basic gear of today, while having amazing vehicles. It's a weird juxtaposition of forgetting today's tech, but inventing a few less useful pieces. A lot of tech doesn't even tell you what you get, you just research things and hope you get something useful from it.
- The Factions: To befriend them you have to babysit them. You're clearly just a small group of soldiers on a single airship yet they expect you to be everywhere and save all their cities. They have -lasers- and yet a few people with assault rifles are supposed to make this big difference? At least give me something for saving you, like some ammo for the laser guns I bought from you. What's more annoying is that as soon as you befriend them you get shown all their world conclaves and are expected to save them all, even if you're on the other side of the planet.
- Enemies: All the factions are rather unique, and fighting the three human factions is interesting. However the main enemy, the mist, basically just has everyone's abilities. You mostly fight the pandora, and it feels like fighting the humans is like fighting a weaker pandora. Especially once the pandora start generating unremoveable mist (except with explosives) in large areas around you, and cloaking every time they get hit while also healing when they shoot you. The pandora actually end up with better weapon tech before you do, and yet you can't steal the weapons back.
- Cybernetics: Cybernetics are awesome, but apparently helping the androids means they automatically hate and want to murder you. Why aren't they just another faction like the other three?
- The map: Everything is so spread out and you lack the resources early to easily outfit a single squad, no matter multiple. So you lose faction influence just for existing.
- Aiming: Aiming is almost mandatory, as a lot of times you can fire at an enemy but have a 1% chance of hitting and as far as I could tell the game didn't tell you that unless you manually aim\t\t\t\t\t

Soooooooooooooo boring.

-----


Despite Phoenix Point's legacy in Gollop's own design; it feels.. off. There are great systems, but they feel unbalanced against each other to the detriment of moment-to-moment gameplay. As such, and much in the same way as XCOM's own spin-off, Chimera Squad, the game leaves an impression of being a clone of something better: a shame, given the reality of the game's roots.

There is plenty of innovation to the developer's credit, but the combination of these systems falls short of what makes Firax's games so compelling. Many have put this down to the drab environments and weak customization elements. They are not wrong; a lack of connection to the world and your squad is one of the great successes of the XCOM series. However, the main problem with Phoenix Point is how loosely the individual systems fit together.

Despite XCOM 2's RNG map design; most maps worked well with the characters, providing an experience that was both tight and open, with pod positions feeling crafted for either maximum challenge or maximum power-fantasy. No turn felt wasted. By contrast, Phoenix Point's few visually appealing maps are open shooting galleries, won only by attrition, with little scope for tactical flair. The least visually appealing maps, the "mist" areas, are drab corridor shooters, made worse by what seems like a good system on paper - the friendly fire mechanic.

And this is really what sums up Phoenix Point; great ideas, but a failure to really understand why less is sometimes more. This leaves Phoenix Point feeling like less than the sum of its parts.

Replacing RNG with a manual aim and specific body targeting does little to enhance gameplay due to the map design. The evolving enemies seem promising, but enemies feel indistinct and unpredictable, which although counter-intuitive, is essential in a tactics game. Despite the increased action points, characters feel immobile, leaving the game feeling more like a cover shooter, rather than the refined, perceived-high-risk, plasma-infused game of chess that XCOM has become.

I won't say much about the overworld; it feels well crafted - there is some additional complexity, but much of it feels like busy work, with little indication as to what is a priority and what isn't. The sense of relevancy may be there in subsequent playthroughs, but that doesn't help sell the game on the first run.

To summarize; if it were a board game, I would have created my own rules after the first game and then found great enjoyment in it; let's hope future workshop support or updates makes this the case. Do I dislike it? No. Do I feel like it has the potential to be better than XCOM? Sure. Will I be playing more? With the workshop support for XCOM, probably not anytime soon. Would I recommend it? On sale, yes. Am I being harsh? Perhaps, the long journey to steam was unlikely to help manage expectations.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tQuite simply Phoenix Point is what I would have expected out of X-Com.

I read some negative reviews and I was on the bench about this game. After getting the Year One Edition and finally giving it a go. This is what I was missing out of the new X-Com II game.

Don't get me wrong, I love X-Com still, the community and mods are great, and I have come to accept it's quirks when it comes to accuracy (if you played it, you know what I am talking about :P )

But Phoenix Point gives me that feel of base building and expanding, and additions to ground combat that I would have expected. It feels more complete and open to giving better stats to make the right calls. Plus the story is pretty nice in how you can make choices that will affect things down the road. Like I said, it feels more complete.

At this point I am a fan of Snapshot Games... and will assume the negative comments are from the X-Com fanbase. I have found no issues with this game thus far and am enjoying the heck out of it. I look forward to their next game.\t\t\t\t\t

Bases are a joke, and defense missions are just the same over and over again, with your guys somehow placed everywhere but where the fight is going to be.
The game explains little about whats happening, and its difficulty scales way too fast. Also relies heavily in RNG - and that its just stupid.
The game relies o you playing it 5 times from scratch to understand most thing, and that's just .
It tries so hard to not be XCOM that fails miserably at only reminding you how great that series of games was.

Its a game that "feels right" but its just that, a feeling. Overall is a mediocre, unbalanced and repetitive game inferior to similar (and older) games\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo I just cleared my first lair (tier II Pandoran Nest) and have to say: It was a blast!

Booted up my game maybe two weeks ago on Hero difficulty. First Playthrough, XCOM and Xenonauts Veteran, all good. I love the Overwatch mechanics and rely on it pretty heavily. Playing it safe. Lost no soldier so far *tap on shoulder* and eager to keep it that way.
In the lair though... Jesus. The Devs really did a great job here with level design. So I do my usual business, planning out a route to take through the rocky maze, search&destroy the objective, setting up overwatches and anticipating those creepy fishmen to poke around the wrong - or right - corners. Jokes on me!
The angles in this stupid lair were so... alien - literally - that they were jumping all over their supid stalagnites, taking podshots at me and retreating behind a solid wall with no easy way for me to retaliate... And I loved that. It's their home after all, they should have the upper hand, right?
So change of tactics, more rush, got the lair, all good. First mission to have all but one of my six soldiers injured, a couple even seriously hurting.

Why am I writing this? Because I feel like people are not giving Phoenix Point the credit it deserves. You get a lot of bad feedback on google that is some years old, and people still complain in this comment section that the game is not as far as it should be so long after its first release. I take a look at this game now - with fresh eyes - and I see a solid game. I don't see the balance mess it might have went through. And I think it's important to judge the game in the state it is in now and not how it came here.

Plus I can't really relate to much of the bad feedback I've read about:
I don't feel my troopers are cannonfodder or oneshots. As I said, I haven't lost anyone yet. But I also try to give them 20 strength asap - totaling to 200HP. Maybe I haven't encountered the one-shot enemies yet though. I don't know. The biggest thing I came across was a Chiron so far. And they are... annoying, but far from deadly - on their own.

I don't think there are too many ambushes or missions. If you don't want missions, what do you play the game for anyway? Skip to Outro? And besides the tutorial does tell you that there will be much to handle and you can't possibly be everywhere. So feel free to let that haven decide its own fate.

Tutorial - another curator mentioned that the game is really sparse on info? Cannot relate. While the little intro story arc is great and puts a lot of info up front, basically everything new is explained to you as soon as you encounter it in the open.

Much to do. That's right. Especially with all the DLCs enabled I feel there is a lot of front-loaded content and I did feel a little overwhelmed on what to do. And what matters and what is just fluff. Getting a second and even third team asap is probably mandatory. Have them do the explore and haven defense missions, while your Alpha team does the quest and nest missions.

Reddit super-soldier guides? The game being punishing on "casuals"? I don't know about this. I am for certain not a casual, but I haven't read on guides or anything (besides of weapon stats in the wiki) to keep afloat. I'm not even multiclassing much, because often I feel those 50XP are better spent on the base stats than just a second class.
My A-Team is rocking 4 assaults with the ARES-AR1 (standard) assault rifle and an Odin (standard) grenade
1 heavy with a Deceptor MG, the Neurazer for stunning and an Odin grenade
1 sniper with a Pythagoras VII Laser sniper rifle and the CYPHER standard pistol
Throw in a medkit and a spare magazine for everyone and you are good enough to go. Only multiclass is one Assault/Sniper. I'm pretty damn sure this is far from any Terminator meta build. It is possible is all I say.

But I don't want to sound like too much of a fanboy. There is of course downsides.

The "strategic" UI - especially for manufacturing - is a mess. Lots of scrolling, little quality of life. I would much prefer to have all your active ships with shortcuts on the side instead of having to iterate through them. Hell, I play the geoscape on "slow" because I lose track of what's happening otherwise. With just 3 Manticores (planes).

From time to time the game stutters. It's probably bugged, because it doesn't always happen with the obvious elephant in the room (like a mist sentinel covering half the map in fog), but often when coming out of the Free Aim or after throwing grenades... But since this is a turn based game I don't have much issues with this. Let it lag out a couple seconds and you're good to go. If not, then most likely after your next shot it's good again.

The game somehow makes you feel bad. Kind of. For example I am on 6 bases now, meaning 12 plane and 12 vehicle capacity. But I only have 3/12 planes and 2/12 vehicles. Which at the start made me think I am progressing way too slow. But lacking the materials there's really nothing you can do about it.
The actual truth is: Less is more. You will probably have a lot of spare capacities and building slots at all of your bases. Feel free to even demolish excess stores or facilites you won't be using. The materials are better spent elsewhere. And that's the premise of the game I feel: You are scratching together a last line of defense. You are not dominating a virus.

Lack of progression. That's kind of true. You don't progress upwards much. There's no better weapons. Just different weapons. You progress wide, though. Up that one 4-man team to a 6-man and two 3-man/1-car teams and you know that you made progress. Starting to cover the area with scans (which are by the way another materials trap!!! You don't need them everywhere) and mist repellants also does feel like establishing a foothold. But not really progression.
Lore-wise I try to explain this with the timescale. You are dealing in hours in Phoenix Point, other than in XCOM or Xenonauts where you deal in days and months. Of course you won't make technological leaps in that timeframe, but rather finding something different to more suit your needs - for example the Synedrion laser sniper rifles.

Just think about it. There are 60+ People STARVING per day in each haven because Pandoravirus makes agriculture and scavanging almost impossible. That should give you a good idea of what state humanity really is in. The game could maybe do a better job at setting the stage, but once I realized this for myself almost all of the aspects of the game made much more sense to me.

TL;DR
Phoenix Point is an uphill battle. And it feels like it.
I appreciate it. Retrospective I would have also spent 50 on it.\t\t\t\t\t
