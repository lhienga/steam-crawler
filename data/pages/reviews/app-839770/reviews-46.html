\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGame can be quite fun at times, but more often than not it's just frustrating. Even a year after release, it's still full of absurd bugs and shoddy balance. Here's a fun one; there's a good chance you won't be allowed to use any of your abilities upon loading a save game. Reloading doesn't help either, it's baked into your savefile somehow. Here's another; the A.I. will just randomly not take it's turn after loading a save. Both completely destroy the flow of the game, and the only option is to just play through every single battle in one go. Need to take a break? Too bad.

The A.I. is mediocre and very predictable. After a couple of battles against each enemy type, you can tell how Every. Single. Battle. against them in the future is going to play out. They don't change tactics based on the environment, friendly or enemy troop compositions, or really anything at all. The only thing allowing them to provide any sort of challenge is outright cheating. Either in the form of RNG alterations (I've seen the A.I. miss one, and only one, sniper shot out of several dozen battles, whereas friendly snipers miss at least a couple per sniper, per battle under better circumstances), or outright ignoring game mechanics such as paralysis, ability points, and movement restrictions.

The game has a lot of potential, but unless the devs focus on fixing bugs and the core gameplay instead of adding more DLC, it's not worth the time.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI definitely recommend this game, but with some cautions. Also, this review is of the base game, without enabling any of the DLCs, and it's for the PC, not console.

The game overall is an entertaining combination of tactical, squad-based combat and an over-arching strategic layer that will be familiar to anyone who has played XCOM (of course), or Fort Triumph. The soldier development is interesting, with available multi-classing, and the abilities are varied. Some have been changed or nerfed since release, so take older tips about character builds or strategies with a grain of salt, but never mind that - you'll figure out which abilities are worth taking. The tech tree is similarly interesting, with a range of weapons and abilities that are not "one size fits all"...unlike in XCOM, where you will always pick a laser rifle over an assault rifle, and a plasma rifle over a laser rifle, there are a lot of "side-grades" in PP where different weapons have different strengths and weaknesses. (From reading other reviews it sounds like the Ancients weapons are overall better? But I haven't played that DLC yet.)

Aesthetically and thematically, the game is great. The Pandoravirus makes a great threat. The storylines and factions fit the setting. The cutscenes are of still pictures, not animated as such, but I like the style. The ending(s) is (are) a bit anticlimactic IMO, though the final mission is fun enough.

That said, the game can definitely become a slog, and the missions get a certain sameness quite quickly. There will be times when you're just waiting for something to happen. (This can occur in the endgame in other, similar, games too, but it seems more prevalent in PP.) While your soldiers can be quite fragile, even after levelling up, the effort in levelling them is significant (so be prepared to lose some level 7's or, more likely, to restart the level...). The game can definitely be challenging, depending on the mix of opponents you get on a given map, and Legend difficulty can be punishing (as it should be, of course).

It's lacking some quality-of-life features as well - for example if you want to swap out a soldier's armour or weapons, it's a piece-by-piece thing - there's no "unequip" button. If you take a vehicle (like an Armadillo) into a scavenging mission, you can't have a soldier one-button drop the scavenged supplies into the vehicle - again, it's piece by piece. If you're looking to raid or steal research, there doesn't seem to be a way to find out what each faction haven has other than by clicking on each haven in turn. Things like this aren't game-breakers, but they contribute to the overall fiddliness and slogishness (?) of the game.

A final point is the voice acting and character customization. The soldier voices are...not great. Some of the voices are like nails on a chalkboard, and the range of voices available is very limited. Soldier customization is also very limited. (as a result soldiers with two mutations will always end up looking like dorks). I'm not asking for XCOM 2 levels of customization (where the modding community has added all manner of voice packs and clothing packs) but a bit more variety would be welcome.

Overall, as so many others have said, it's not as polished as the new XCOM series but it is polished enough, and it's definitely fun, though I'm not sure it will have the same replayability. I've seen complaints about difficulty etc. - to be honest, from my perspective, I like the game fine as it is; if any changes were to be made, I'd prefer more quaIity of life or (please!) voices/customization. I got the game on sale, but I think I would have got my money's worth even at full price - but it won't be for everyone.\t\t\t\t\t

Decent story and feel of the world they created, it does feel like something small is missing that will solidify the canon, but they did an admirable job in bringing the universe to life. \r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm going to recommend this game, but with a lot of caveats.



Firstly, comparisons with XCOM are inevitable, since it's developed by some people who worked on the original UFO: Enemy Unknown, and is a sort of much more granular XCOM spiritual-not-quite-sidequel...?-something. As such, I'll try to point out what it does better and what it does worse.

If you liked the base XCOM as it was, this is probably not the game for you. If you liked the Long War, however, this might be your thing.



Overall impression of the game: it's a bit on the raw side. There are graphical glitches, mostly when viewing the battlefield from third person shooting perspective. A lot of black void backgrounds. Occasional aiming hitbox glitch. A lot, a lot of cheese in abilities and tactics. Some missions can be solved pretty much only by cheese. Ending is somewhat underwhelming, but I felt the same for XCOM-2, although XCOM presents it better.

On the flip side, things that XCOM-2 does, Phoenix Point tries to do much deeper, but as a result seems to have spread itself too thin in the development.



Let's talk about the aiming system.

PP uses realistic ballistic cone of fire based on the point of the shooter, and limb-based damage system that can disable abilities, using weapons, walking and eventually, living. You can autofire, but almost never want to. This prevents the "95% chance to hit" problem of XCOM, since point blank the enemy is physically in the cone of fire, but can lead to other problems.

Enemies are designed around it, using shields and armoured body parts to protect weaker bits of their bodies, you want to prioritise disabling body parts that can cause the most damage, your own soldiers can become useless if one of their arms gets disabled and they only have two-handed weapons, but so can the enemies.

This system is great for tactical approach to overwhelming enemy numbers, letting you take out most dangerous implements first, mop up the disabled enemy second.

Half-way through the game I've realised a fundamental problem with the system. I can't even think of a way they could have developed around it.

IDLE ANIMATIONS.

Enemies have idle animations, moving around their body parts. The game is entirely about shooting body parts. It's possible for an enemy to be hidden completely behind cover, only for 10% of their animation loop to have their head sticking out. All you have to do is wait with your sniper for them to poke out, then, before animations loops back, shoot them while the game is in the aiming slow-motion.

At the same time, because there's a delay between confirmation of the shot and shooting animation, it's possible for a perfect shot on the enemy to go wild because they idle-animate their way out of it in the meantime.

Second problem with the aiming system, which is also it's feature, is that unlike fuzzy XCOM obstacles to the shot, everything in the environment blocks your shots. It's especially bad in New Jericho maps, I've noticed, that are full of girders and other mechanical bits. Flanking is always an option, but this realistic aspect gets annoying pretty fast.

As a side result, Overwatch fails half the time because your own operatives decide to activate it right as an enemy runs behind some enviromental obstacle, and all your shots hit it instead of the tree.

When the system works, however, it WORKS.

I've cheesed my first Scylla (boss enemy) with my C-team without taking any damage by basically shooting out it's legs one by one and bleeding it to death, keeping distance, rather than going for more armoured body parts.



The rest of the game is similarly granular.


Research a weapon in XCOM, and you can equip it on all soldiers without limit.

Research a weapon in PP, and you need to manufacture them one by one and hand them out to your operatives. You also need to craft ammo separately. You can run out of the ammo on the mission if you don't bring enough. All the stuff you loot from enemies, or your outdated gear, you can dissassemble back into crafting materials.


XCOM has only one team plane and a mission you are heading to at the time. You are mostly going to have just the A team with some second-row troops for when A team is injured or fatigued.

PP has several types of aircraft, each with benefits and downsides, you can either build them or steal them, and you are going to need several operating at the time each with their own B, C, D and so on teams on it all around the globe putting out fires.


XCOM-2 WotC has three factions (I haven't finished that game, so I might be wrong on details) that you can slowly ally with, each of which gives you one unique soldier. Factions disagree ideologically but otherwise just give you bonuses and missions.

PP has three factions with vast ideological differences, troop compositions, aircrafts, dozens of bases, their own research trees that they progress down, and you can either ally to get that research or steal it, and by end game all three go to war with each other and possibly you and start actively attacking and messing up the globe.


XCOM has a linear progression of armour and weapons, with Tiers of 1 to 3, with some alternatives on each tier.

PP has a jumbled mess of factional armour and weapons, with only some weapons eventually getting a Tier 2 analogue, and some having a Tier 3 in Legacy of the Ancients DLC, while armours are for the most part sidegrades, split by class-specific restrictions.



Quickly on the plot and setting:

It's nonsensical mix of X-files level spy fiction and very blunt Lovecraft. The game builds up a lot of intrigue at the start, with old conspiracies, failed moon mission, etc, but becomes laughably unsustainable in the later parts.

For me, the plot lost me when I found out that the ancient antideluvian civilization that apparently ran into the Pandoravirus before, the civilization whose existence humanity outright ignored beforehand and didn't bother investigating, that same civilization left around a whole bunch of ruins with magical floating rocks, exotic materials and stone laser-beam-shooting robots that are still active two million years later. Humanity is dumb in this game.

At the same time the last million of people on year are somehow sustaining the incredible industry of New Jericho, sci-fi of Synedrion, and giant temples of Cult of Anu.

It's nonsense, but so is the entirety of XCOM-2, and XCOM-1 to some degree.



I can keep rambling on, but I will end on this.

All the reviews that say that the game is a grind are true. Question is whether you are up to it. First 20 or so hours feel good, as you explore the globe, factions, tech and new enemy variants appear in almost every mission, but mid-to-late game drags on with endless Haven Defence missions (I played for 72 hours, trying to max out factions and side missions, and according to end game stats I did Haven Defense 40 times), and the worst part is that they are your only evergreen source of resources for manufacturing and resupplying.



Strong start and a lot of cool ideas of granular design.

Grindy middle full of joggling different teams and bases and loadouts. Only 5.7% of players finished the game on any difficulty as of this review.

Final boss is a bit of a plop and I think can be cheesed by snipers from outside it's chamber without activating it.
