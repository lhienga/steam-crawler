\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTLDR: Difficulty heavily RNG dependent, most players will probably have to cheese the mechanics to win consistently, battles are repetitive, and enemies are not very interesting. The story, most of the voice acting and campaign setup is pretty good.

While this game seems to be way better than it was at launch, the difficulty level and the AI seem pretty broken, you have to really plan ahead and spend a minute or 2 on every single move since the AI seems to always know where your guys are. If you leave any units out of cover for a turn, or they just get bad luck RNG in low cover, they'll probably get killed immediately, even on Rookie difficulty it's extremely unforgiving.

I expected better enemies, but the mutant alien monster enemies basically just feel like any other faction that's been re-skinned for the most part, they get access to everything you get and more (shield guys, rifle guys, even snipers), but they also seem to get stuff faster than you can unlock and manufacture it sometimes, which is pretty frustrating and doesn't lend itself to interesting counter-play. You pretty much have to fight all the enemies and factions using the same tactics to win without losses.

I did think the story was more interesting than I expected, the factions have unique aesthetics, the voice acting is mostly pretty good (though some of the characters are pretty corny). The world map campaign style is basically the same as XCom which I have always liked.

One could argue I'm not very good at this game and that's why I don't like it (which is fair, I don't play many turn based games these days), but if 20+ hours of practice isn't enough to have fun and not feel like every mission is a slow frustrating slog unless I run one of the really specific cheesy builds, then I don't think it's really worth playing. The animations and the terrain destruction also look pretty bad for a game at this price.

I've had some fun between some of the more frustrating missions, but it still feels like a pretty average early access game in quality; if you're looking for a good turn based tactics game, XCom2, Battletech, and Phantom Doctrine are great. I'd say only bother with Phoenix Point if you're really good at turn based tactics games and have already played the ones mentioned above, and are looking for a challenging experience.\t\t\t\t\t

Green energy projectiles coming from the fog of war where you rookies had no line of sight to... the first time one of your troops got mind controlled... the original remains as playable today as it was 28 years ago!

I love Firaxis's take on it as well, don't get me wrong! The modern version is more refined and accessible. But I prefer my challenge coming from creatively designed enemies and gameplay mechanics over random dice roles. So I looked forward to this title due to Julian Gollop's involvement.

Phoenix Point is somewhere between X-COM and XCOM.

I have the year one edition in EGS and bought the complete edition here in Steam. The modelled ballistics, creative enemy design, more complex meta game and the ability to cross-class... this is a great game that I can easily recommend. Snapshot isn't great at creating DLC however, Legacy of the Ancients is the only DLC I would enable in my next play through in fact. Kaos Engine does add some good vehicle upgrades as well...

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPhoenix Point is a weird one to review. There is so much to like about this game but also so much to hate. It is a good overall game if you can ignore the scads of blemishes that keep it from being a true gem. If you love the X-com series and tactical games like I do, it is absolutely worth picking up during a sale. Just understand that it will require some patience here and there.

Pros:
- I've been addicted to X-com since the original game (yeah, I'm old) and have played a number of X-com clones like this one so I don't say this flippantly... there are some mechanisms within PP that I believe outclass Firaxis's X-com series and I wouldn't be surprised if they are adopted in the next iteration of X-com. Those mechanisms:
o Overwatch - The way PP implements overwatch is with a narrow cone, which allows your soldiers to create overlapping fields of fire. There is nothing better than watching a larger enemy force bum-rush your kill-box only to be decimated.
o Focus fire and Line of Sight - Instead of relying on a 100% top-down set up and a Rng percentage roll for hit chance, as X-com does it, PP instead opts for a 3D FPS line of sight setup a kin to the Fallout series. Percentages to hit are based on distance from the target, weapon type and the ability of the soldier in question. The FPS aspect makes lining up a target much more intuitive while the focus fire mechanism adds a puzzle-like aspect to the gameplay as your Pandoran enemies often have specific vulnerabilities that need to be targeted for a successful kill. This makes positioning your troops to attack from various angles very important during combat.
o Skill Randomization - At first glance, the soldier level up mechanism is overly simplistic with soldier classes all going through the same skill trees. What can be easily overlooked, however, is that each soldier starts out with three random "specialties" within the tree that when combined within the right class can become the difference between them being cannon fodder or a killing machine. While there is certainly room for improvement in the soldier creation functionality, I found this randomization to be great for building out different fighting styles.
- The story line of PP is great. Interwoven throughout with multiple endings, it drives you to your next fight in order to discover "what happens next?". Even after I tired of the combat, I found myself pushing through in order to unlock the ending.
- Blood and Titanium, Corrupted Horizons and Living Weapons DLCs - Now I wouldn't advise picking up the DLCs individually but I do think that the reviews do them injustice for what they add to the base gameplay. When I picked up my game, all DLCs were included and at the advice of some forums, I disabled them my first play-through (which I think was the right call). Once I ran through the game a couple of times, however, I found that the content they added was refreshing. I especially enjoy augmenting my soldiers with mechanical body parts (B&T) and have put together some great mechanized squads.

Cons:
- Global Map & Resource Balancing - There is no nice way to say this, the Phoenix Point meta-game is currently unfinishable (at least on higher levels) due to what are severe resource balancing issues. Now to be fair, I refused to play the game on anything but Hero (level 3 of 4) due to combat on lower levels being ridiculously too easy. After restarting the base game some 10 different times using experience to make smarter choices, I found that I always ended up in the same dilemma around the same point. Eventually, (usually around the time scylla begins to appear), I ran out of resources as well as Points of Interest on the map which resulted in a sudden struggle to keep my team supplied with ammo. The solutions online recommended aircraft theft and farming/trading to trickle in cash but it felt like a subpar hack when I tried it. My solution - and one I would recommend once you've given the base game the college try - was to download a mod which gave me infinite resources from the start, taking that part of the gameplay completely out of the equation. Once I could actually fund my team as needed and Mutagen became the only resource I had to manage - the game became infinitely more enjoyable.
- Combat AI - The combat AI in PP ranges from absolutely engaging to just plain ridiculous depending on the enemy you are encountering. The sad reality is that the combat AI is pretty dumb. Enemies rarely use cover effectively (often not using it at all), never move to flank your position, and regularly perform actions that are not in their best interest. Mostly, the enemy tactic is to simply bumrush you, allowing you to wipe out large hordes by setting up a defensive perimeter and picking them off one by one. Adjusting the difficulty level higher does not increase tactical difficulty but rather just adds more enemies to the screen. Now for the Pandorans (your main enemy), this AI tactic works fine as it lines up perfectly with the storyline. But watching human enemies regularly leave great cover and end their turns out in the open where they were easily picked off was painful to watch and I found many missions ruined as a result.
- Combat Animation Bugs - While they weren't game breaking, several bugs in combat stole from the immersion. I noticed two primary animation bugs that regularly bothered me: 1) Often times a soldier (your own or an enemy) faced the wrong direction *away from the enemy* when crouching behind cover. This may seem small but compounded with the AI issues above was an immersion killer. 2) There exists (at least for my AMD video card) a dancing soldier bug where when one of my soldiers who was originally behind an upright wall was hit with return fire, they would get stuck while trying to move back into cover. This would cause them to jiggle back and forth for two full seconds before returning to place.
- Character Randomization - The character randomizer algorithm is overly simplistic and it was rare that I didn't jump into the character screen after recruiting a soldier to fix what felt like an immersion breaking result. PP did a decent job of providing enough different names, faces and voices to create random characters but the tool used to randomize those options either (1) often overly used the same faces/names while ignoring others or (2) created ethnic/racial voice, name and hair combinations that felt pretty wacky.
- Story Pacing - Despite the cons above, Phoenix Point really is a fun game but it is too d*#$ long! The amount of missions you need to complete in order to see just one ending let alone all of them is too much and the game ends up wearing out its welcome. As a completionist, I wanted to see the ending for the game but eventually I got sick of playing the same missions over and over.\t\t\t\t\t
