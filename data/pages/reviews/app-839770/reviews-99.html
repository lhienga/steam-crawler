\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI wanted to like the game but instead I hated it.
Everything that got me hooked where things I ended up hating, I really recommend you try this within the 2 hour period, if you don't like it it is not going to get better.

There's just so many design flaws that I cant understand the reasoning behind. No fog of war so you don't know where your soldiers can see and not. This is a big deal, I need that info. I cant see behind walls in real life so when monsters just pop up because I had to assume I could see behind a corner its incredibly frustrating.
There are apparently places with fog of war so the technology to have that is there. I wouldn't know though I gave up before that, and its not the norm and no mods/options to change anything about it which is weird because there's like 3 DLCs out already and a 4th one is on its way.

The strategy part takes forever because it insists of locking the camera to the soldier you're moving and showing you how they run to where you told them to go. It feels incredibly slow. Even the original X-com had tricks to speed up sending soldiers from A to B.
I don't need to watch my guy run to see what a good job he did reaching where I told him to go, speed that ♥♥♥♥ up if you have to show me that or give me the option to do so at least. Now I have to watch people accelerate, run for 4-5 seconds and then de-accelerate and get into position 4-5 times per turn.
Xenonauts did this right, your guy will be where you tell him to be, and he will walk there and you're free to keep assigning orders in the meantime. if he runs into an alien he will stop and alert you.
Going from that to how Phoenix Point handles things feels like a step back. I mean - it LOOKS cool, the game has really nice graphics and animations but the information provided is not important.

The cover system is also annoying. I thought it would make the game deeper but it just makes it feel more shallow somehow. Everybody is running around to get inside of cover/get into the enemies cover. I don't like it and that's a core feature.

It's incredibly hard to get an overview of how the battle scene is and after a while I just ended up with the good old "pixel hunting" with my mouse, looking square by square at which square be the best square to move to in the case of having cover/being able to shoot the enemy instead of actually looking at area.
I would say that relying on this information is a crutch for a fundamentally flawed system. I think I would have prefered just some kind of matrix version where I would just be able to see that info at all times. It feels like the "real world" graphics that is not a bunch of lines telling you where safe is is just cosmetics. It's really annoying. The battle scape has too much information and too little information at the same time if that makes sense.

It's a weird decision because I saw a game designer for X-com 2 say they scrapped this design because of the reason I just mentioned. You don't look at the world, you look at where the lines are the most optimal.

There's a lot I can say about the game but the reason it hurts is because I know there is a great game in there but its being held hostage of these for me horrible mechanics. It's possible it's not my kind of game but I don't know who would enjoy this.

I wanted to "get" the combat so I could finally enjoy the game, but the only way apparently is to just "keep playing until you get it". I'm all for "git gud", I love punishing games but in this case I just feel like there's a huge mountain of actual frustration, not challenge to get over and I gave up. Everything is so vague and the combat is so "loose", the more I learned about it the less I wanted to play.

The game seems to know this as well because combat early on (my only point of reference) is extremely easy and I feel that also holds me back from learning how to play it. None of my choices matter really. I tried to play as strategically as I could, trying to learn the new cover system but it only ended up with me feeling that I wasted time because when I would just run up to the baddies and blast them in the face point blanc they died all the same and I had to spend less time being forced watching my guys run.

Permadeath is an awesome mechanic but you are going to have to punish me at some point for doing something wrong so I can learn and get a feel for how to play the game. Reading guides etc still just give me the tip "get a feel of how to play" and if that's the best a game that's been out for one year can give me then I'm sorry the battle design is just too vague.

It took me on average 6 hours of studying up on how to play the game, and this is by alt-tabbing and preparing by late night watching youtube videos about "how to play to be able to enjoy the game".

Eventually I I just cut my losses and played another game of Xenonauts instead.

It just hurts because I would love to like this game so much but theres just so many things you just "have to get over" for me that I will never enjoy the awesome things I really hope future games take inspiration from.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhile I love the physically-modeled projectiles, this game is seriously flawed compared to the original 1994 game by this designer, and the Firaxis remakes. The maps are small and not varied, and what tactical options are afforded, if there are any, favor the enemies. (My favorite is the fortress map where you start on open ground against enemies with rocket launchers, because your troops are too stupid to approach through the buildings.) The constant onslaughts of ever-more-powerful aliens, while your troops stay unchanged except for higher damage numbers, quickly become tedious instead of fun. Play the 1994 original, or play XCOM2 with all the DLC, for more nuanced challenges.

Oh, and turn off Legacy of the Ancients. It brings nothing but more tedium, with the prizes being weapons that, by the time you slog through to them, aren't *that* much better than what you ressearched without the grinds required.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Good: The free-aim system this game implements is unique and interesting. It requires you to think about a tactical situation very differently from XCOM EU and XCOM 2, where each individual must be placed carefully so they don't get caught in the cross-fire as opposed to bullets magically passing through. Its also important to consider cover and line of sight with the free aim which impacts the success of your shot. There is also the added benefit that even if you miss you still can score some damage on your opponent. I also like the fact that the soldiers have accented English in accordance to nationality, something XCOM has not really done except for very specific characters. Plus the story has a cool premise that is not boxed in by the precedent set by XCOM UFO Defense.

The Bad: Unfortunately, it seems this game has put all its bets on the free-aim system and it has definitely not paid off. The strategic layer has nothing more to offer than XCOM 2. Base building is boring, and resource management even more so since your involvement with either component is pretty surface-level. The various factions aren't that interesting because acting on behalf of one makes the others unhappy. Not that this makes a difference in the overall game as the negative impact is negligible. The game looks and feels like a bad XCOM 2 mod. The character models and customization is bad. The collision physics are bad since the environments feel like its all made of cardboard when it is destroyed. The controller support feels like something the developer slapped on last minute. The mouse pointer is always in the middle of the screen even during dialog and cutscenes. The cutscenes seem to be entirely comprised of concept art and textboxes. I have not finished a full campaign as of this review because the thought of going back to this game makes me consider what I would rather be doing. The game has committed the worst sin imaginable; it's boring and the developer wants you to pay $60 for a game that's realistically worth $20-15. It's highway robbery.

The Ugly: The mutants are, of course, pretty ugly. So is the EULA, which admittedly I did not read very carefully.

Conclusion: The free-aim system is a cool and interesting spin on the classic XCOM dice roll as was advertised. I was waiting for this game to be available on Steam for a year and was happy about it being on 50% sale. However, it seems that the year was not spent wisely on this game. Its pretty buggy, the gameplay is boring, its badly optimized, and its a far more expensive as a game than what is called for. Plus, its like all of the things I did not like about XCOM EU ended up here. I wanted to like this game but sadly I cannot recommend this game and I have a feeling the situation will not improve with time.\t\t\t\t\t

At the global scale it's a good game and the storyline is fantastic, the multiple factions makes it really interesting and the multi-base system is a good idea ... but I just keep coming back to the combat. I don't want to fight anymore, the RNG for your troops is pathetic while the enemy can snipe you from halfway across the map with a pistol or shotgun. A great example is where I rescued a bandit who was laying waste to the forsaken ... until I took control, then he couldn't hit the broadside of a barn.

I've tried modding it and seeing if the tweaks on offer help but it barely covers the core issues, X-Com 2 Long War is far superior to this. It is a nice attempt to continue in a different direction with X-Com style games but it's only half finished with poor quality improvisations to make it across the line to call it finished.\t\t\t\t\t
