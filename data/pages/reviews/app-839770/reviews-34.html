\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo far I'm not even through my first campaign in this game, but I already know I love it. In short, it's XCOM improved with more flexibility, more strategy and more depth. So here goes my review.

Phoenix Point is a turn based tactics game about building up an organization capable of saving the last 1 million human inhabitants on a planet that has been devastated by the horrifying and alien Pandoravirus. The first two assaults by the virus have already devastated the planet, and now a third wave has begun in the shape of a red mist that spreads to cover more and more of the globe. The virus transforms infected humans into Pandorans, aggressive bioforms that your teams of operatives will need to confront in close combat. As the mists spread, the scattered human settlements increasingly come under attack and humanity faces the real threat of extinction. Meanwhile the Pandorans send ever new and more lethal creatures in their attacks, which also increase in frequency.

With this gripping setup, the Phoenix Point campaign is a merciless race against time. You control the last remnants of the Phoenix Project, an organization formed to investigate and combat the Pandoravirus. Winning the fight will require advanced technology, bases on every continent, multiple aircraft carrying elite teams of soldiers to respond when the Pandorans attack - and the game gives you nothing for free. Even basic rifles and body armor need to be manufactured by your workshops to equip the new soldiers you recruit. All this is in service of a plan laid down by the original great minds of the Phoenix Project, which must be reassembled and enacted before humanity is wiped out.

The resources to fund your efforts are largely gained by exploring the world map and scavenging in various ways. However there are also the 3 factions of human survivors for you to deal with, living in roughly 100 havens around the world. In your quest to save humanity, you have the option of actually raiding human these settlements for resources, technology or (crucially) aircraft. On the other hand you can ally with them and gain access to their technologies based on friendship rather than robbery.

In a fantastic mechanic, each of the 3 factions also has its own plan for dealing with the Pandoravirus. So instead of following the Phoenix Project's plan you can end up with completely different solutions to the crisis by entering an alliance with your faction of choice.

As you can see, on the global strategic map you have a lot to consider in terms of where to send your limited aircraft, where to establish outposts, what facilities to invest in, who to ally with and who to raid, what missions to accept and what crises to respond to. By the mid-game you will have multiple teams simultaneously traveling the globe, factories and labs running full tilt to equip your troops, and base structures being built at many different outposts.

The other half of gameplay is the tactical map, where combat encounters are decided. This combat involves turn-based movement of a team of soldiers on a square grid in the XCOM style. Firearms simulate every individual bullet fired with a trajectory and the possibility of hitting different parts of the target or missing entirely. Furthermore the game allows you to freely aim each shot or burst to try and focus on the specific part of the target you want to hit and disable. It's a fantastic improvement on the XCOM formula with a deeper and more realistic simulation of hit chance than XCOM's percentage-based rolls. Phoenix Point also offers a lot of flexibility with the use of each soldier's actions as you can freely shoot, move incremental steps and use other abilities in any order. The troops also have a Will statistic that can be used as a resource to fuel special abilities. But beware of letting it fall to 0 as that will trigger a panic in your over-stressed soldier!

Beyond the basics, tactical combat features a wealth of cool mechanics, including but not limited to: an armor system with different level of protection for each body part, the use of troop carrying vehicles, an inventory and carry weight system, a large amount of very different weapon types, various damage types including paralysis, poison, fire and Virus damage, directional shields, the ability to target and destroy enemy weapons, strategic map points that can be capped for a morale boost, a stealth and detection system, an overwatch mechanic targeting a specific fire arc and range etc etc. Furthermore your soldiers themselves are also wonderfully customizable with 7 distinct classes and the option to create cross class soldiers that can master 2 classes simultaneously. On top of which every individual recruit comes with 3 randomly chosen unique skills.

Having written an excessively long review gushing over this game I obviously can't recommend it enough. For any fan of XCOM who wants a more grounded, gritty and hard core experience. For those who wished the XCOM games allowed for greater depth, more strategy and more replayability. For those who always wanted to manually aim the shots of their badass soldiers into the enemy's weak spots for those perfect kill shots.

Phoenix Point might not equal the production values and graphical fidelity of XCOM 2, but in every other aspect it more than matches that level of enjoyment. It is maybe not as easy to get into as XCOM 2 however, so watching at some youtube guides can be a big help. Personally I got a lot of use out of the guides put out by Perun and the beginner's tips he gave.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tXCOM 2 is currently my all-time favorite game, but Phoenix Point is growing on me.

Things I like:
- Free Aim: Being able to target specific areas and shoot around shields and obstacles is a great enhancement to this gameplay genre.
- Realistic ballistics instead of dice roll shot chances. One of my biggest complaints about XCOM is missing shots at close range that have a 95%+ chance to hit.

Things I don't like:
- Maps are very static. One of the joys of XCOM 2 is the dynamic map building.
- Limited enemy types. So far the aliens are two primary types that just morph to have additional abilities. Similar to having more enemy types, but not the same.
- Lack of (so far) variety of character types. This may change some as I get deeper into the game.

Things that are different that I am adjusting to that I haven't yet decided are better or worse:
- Less linear story line. On one hand, I think it will create some interesting possibilities for encouraging going through the game more than once. One the other, it can be very confusing and unclear what you should be doing. Perhaps more playing time will reduce some of that confusion.
- Much more detailed approach to weapons. Again there are pluses and minuses. Soldiers can carry more, but the management of "stuff" is for time consuming in the game.

All-in-all, my initial reaction to the game was a little negative because of the added complexity, but the more I play, there are aspects of the game that I like and will miss the next time I play through a game of XCOM 2.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPhoenix Point is really good, a solid 'A' in the F-S scale. It's an unforgiving strategy game with turn-based combat where you command the last Delta Green cell (with the serial numbers filed off) in 2047 during humanity's extinction event at the hand of cosmic horrors. If this sounds up your alley, I recommend buying the game even at full price (something I rarely do).

I am playing on Veteran (normal) difficulty and it's challenging. If you're doing a blind playthrough (and you really should for maximum enjoyment) you can expect a lot of "ooh, I wish I knew that when I started" moments. I've restarted three times so far with no regrets.

I get strong Eldritch Horror vibes from the gameplay loop. You have to aggressively stay on top of constant crises while exploring, trading, researching, building, and growing your squads. The time pressure is constant and the "doom counter" is the human population which decreases continuously. The moment you think you have it handled the game throws more complications at you. I love it!

I won't give any spoilers but if you are playing blind I recommend turning off DLC for your first playthrough.\t\t\t\t\t
