\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOne thing that grinds my gear about the franchise since LAD is that RGG Studio, in an attempt to deepen players' engagement with side content, tried to fuse them into the main storyline one way or the other. For LAD, it was one particular huge difficulty curve introduced in a boss fight that was supposed to be solved by grinding an "optional" ladder tower, and the job system whose biggest fault was simply being unbalanced. For Lost Judgment, in order to finish the school stories to their fullest (which, while are totally detached from the main storyline, felt very much like the second most important piece of narrative in the game), you have to suffer through some of the worst minigames ever introduced in this series so far. This catering-to-completionist design mythos is one I've always been mixed on, and I was hoping that RGGS actually takes time to flesh out the more player-oriented mechanics instead of forcing mediocre minigames on us in order to access the best narrative pieces of the game.

And I guess LAD Gaiden is meant to be the answer to that? This 20 hours trip through Sotenbori - historically the smallest and most structurally simplistic city in Yakuza, lasts 5 chapters where the story probably could have been told within 6 hours at most, while the rest of the game acts more like a reintroduction to the city which hasn't been a big focus point since Kiwami 2 and probably will play a role in the third Judgment installment. Gone are the substories that you encounter naturally just by roaming the streets, now they are presented as jobs given by your fixer, a mechanic lifted directly from Judgment's request board. Next to those are the fetch quests/menial tasks that contribute to Akame Network's progression bars and level number. Everything seemingly feeds into another positive feedback loop where the more money, xp and funny looking numbers you earn, the more benefits Kir- I mean Joryu gains. You have much more agency here compared to LAD, no pressure to grind mindless fights, no moment where I found myself being forced to do anything I disliked.

Even with a better approach towards quantity/quality balance, the act of completing side content remains hampered by a few legacy design decisions. The unnecessary menus where you can't take more than one sub story at once and have to talk to Akame to hear her exposition on the situation.every.♥♥♥♥♥♥♥.time came off as the most egregious to me. Sotenbori's simplistic layout and bland atmosphere turned exploration into pure boredom after 10 hours of playtime, and the new slingshot gadget that let Joryu grab objects out of reach, while is a nice touch, never got its usability expanded outside of combat and can be safely tossed into "one-off shallow mechanic" bin that includes Lost Judgment's parkour. Despite all those relatively mild weak points, LAD Gaiden easily beat out the two last main installments by simply ending the journey before the game itself got obnoxious.

Gaiden's combat shines again, and RGGS even threw in a fighting arena to please players thirsty for brawler bloodshed, an aspect that Lost Judgment did well yet had very little space to unleash due to extremely low combat difficulty. Sadly, the new combat gadgets like drones, explosive cigarettes, and the sling don't spice up Joryu's combat as much as I thought due to imprecise control and extreme heat mode being way too overpowered to the point it thwarted even the best combat moves in the game. Power fantasy catharsis remains high thanks to the much higher number of enemies in each pack which feels like... a Dragon Engine capabilities flex at this point, and hopefully RGGS can polish some leftover kinks to make brawler combat even smoother to engage with in the future.

For fans, the mysterious premise is of course Gaiden's biggest selling point. The main storyline is actually pretty uninteresting and predictable here due to various factors: the Daidoji being painted as a morally ambiguous shadowy organization that feels too mustache-twirling cartoonish in its way of operation to be believable, the two main antagonists are surprisingly cookie cutter in their personality and writing with the only saving grace being their excellent acting performances; the story once again gave Kiryu an easy way out in its midpoint climax and almost became a soap opera. The overall storytelling lacked some of the harsh edges from the Judgment series, or the elegant gut punches that's been a staple in Yakuza's writing since 0, but it nicely filled in some of the missing pieces that led to Kiryu's appearance in LAD which I guess is the whole point of the game. On the bright side, Gaiden introduced some of the most likable allies in the franchise, alongside an ending that both hit you like a spaceship and, strangely enough, teased a continuation of Yakuza 5's unsolved threads. I DON'T NEED SLEEP, I NEED ANSWERS.

While I came off Gaiden somewhat mixed, overall this is still strictly a game for fans and more of a companion piece to LAD and Infinite Wealth first and foremost, so it's easy to chalk it off as an engine experiment for bigger scale combat and preparing side characters for the next installments. Can be placed somewhere in the middle of the pack, not bad but also not very memorable outside of the cast and the ending. Kaito Files still holds the crown for budget short story here.\t\t\t\t\t
