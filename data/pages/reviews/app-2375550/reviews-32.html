\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLike a Dragon Gaiden feels to me like the perfect distillation of the best parts of modern Yakuza games, compressed down into a package that's much more compact than other recent entries while still providing the same mix of compelling story, incredible combat, and wildly varied mini-games and side content. As a companion to Yakuza 7, it provides a wonderful feeling of familiarity, and as a standalone entry in the franchise, it stands tall among the better entries in the series.

By nature, Gaiden is a much shorter game than 7 or either of the Judgment games - it's designed as a side-story, returning to Kiryu to fill in the gaps in his narrative between his exit in Yakuza 6 and his small side-role in Yakuza 7. Despite clearly being a shorter narrative, though, I found that it felt plenty meaty, moreso than several of the pre-5 entries in the series. I think a big part of this is that its story is split across just five chapters, making each of those chapters quite long compared to how these games are usually structured. The end result is that the narrative has very little downtime, with only a few periods where the story gives you the time to poke around and do whatever you want for a little while. The story is typically split between serious, heavily-written drama and absurdity, centering Kiryu's struggle to continue on in a life that, in his perception, no longer has meaning, and this narrative thread carries him through to where he seems to end up in Infinite Wealth. It's well-written and feels like it does Kiryu much more justice than 6 did, while setting him up for a potentially perfect true departure from the franchise with the next game.

The combat is not quite peak Dragon Engine combat - Yakuza Style still feels weird and retread-y even with the tweaks to combat borrowed from the Judgment games, but Agent Style is an absolute revelation and completely justifies the weird narrative framework of the Daidoji faction. Seeing Kiryu shoot out Spider-Man webs and whip people around relentlessly, zip around on rocket boots, summon literally fifty drones to irritate every enemy in sight, and blow people up with explosive cigarettes is both hysterical and deeply satisfying. The group/1v1 split in the styles is still clear, with Agent ideal for clearing out large groups of enemies and Yakuza best suited to one-on-one showdowns, but it often felt like a necessity to switch to Yakuza rather than a choice I was glad to make. On the whole, though, it's still modern Yakuza combat, and it still just feels great.

On the whole, Gaiden is another worthy entry in the Yakuza franchise, far more essential than its side-story nature suggests. As a brawler-style palate cleanser between the turn-based 7 and 8, it's extremely fun, and it provides a great mix of connective tissue between the games and standalone entertainment and intrigue. Pick it up.\t\t\t\t\t
