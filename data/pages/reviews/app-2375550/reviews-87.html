\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAfter playing all Yakuza/Like a Dragon games that are available on Steam, I can't say either this is a must play or a skippable entry.

Like a Dragon Gaiden: The Man Who Erased His Name takes place in parallel to Yakuza Like A Dragon (Like a Dragon 7) and you play as Kazuma Kiryu (now using a fake name "Joryu"), the classic protagonist of the series. Planned as a DLC package for Like a Dragon 8 Infinite Wealth, Gaiden is shorter than most entries and feel like a lot of its parts are made with less budget and time.

That being said, this is still a very entertaining game from RGG. These guys know how to make the most out of a small open world and the grounded setting. Plenty of minigames, progressions, and side stories that are worth sinking your time into, not only because they're fun and have depths beyond the surfaces, but they also give you resources to unlock upgrades for Joryu in combat. And trust me, you'd want to have some upgrades.

The combat system in Gaiden is somewhere between Judgement, Lost Judgement, and Yakuza 5. Joryu has 2 fighting styles, Yakuza and Agent. Agent is a brand-new style to the series, it focuses on fast and long combo string, 3 consecutive side-stepping, and sci-fi gadgets like drones, Spider Man web wires, rocket-powered shoes, and cigarette bombs. Yakuza is a combination of Dragon Engine Kiryu's Dragon of Dojima style and some moves from his older games. Yakuza style's attack speed can feel quite slow, but not overly clunky like Yakuza 6, similar to Judgement. This game also features enemy-juggling, which was first seen in Judgement and streamlined in Lost Judgement. Joryu's combat rhythm and capabilities can open up even the stronger foes for some nasty juggling combo. While juggling and gadgets sound fun and powerful, enemy AI in Gaiden seem tampered to be more aggressive and opportunistic in their strikes. Lost Judgement (the latest Dragon Engine game) seemed to have AI's that are afraid to hit the player and often leave you alone while you combo attacks either regularly or while juggling. Meanwhile Gaiden's enemy AI doesn't care where you are in the arena or what you're doing, they will try to hit you and get you off a combo. So most of the time while you're fighting multiple foes, you'll have to utilize everything to create yourself some space or dispatch the weaker enemies quickly. The Heat mechanic also returns, but it is nerfed pretty harshly. The Heat bar that allows you to use Heat actions or activate Extreme Heat goes up rather slowly in Gaiden. So, either you have to stock up on heat-regenerating items or use it very sparingly, either way you cannot spam it like in the older games.

The reason why I can't say this is a must-play is highly dependent on a player's investment to the series. I'd say that if you only played 1 or 2 Kiryu games, then Gaiden wouldn't be as worthy to you. But if you're invested in the series, the story, the gameplay, and universe, I'd say this is a must-play, implying you haven't played it already. This game tells a very interesting and emotion part of Kiryu's life story but not necessarily required to enjoy future or older games.

One more thing I find weird (and noticed when it got worse) in this game is the lighting. Most RGG games are fine with their lighting, even with Kiwami 1&2's green filter, scenes from those game don't look out of place and offer a consistent imagery experience throughout each one. But Gaiden has a focus on darker shadows and overall color level contrast of the image. This might be to some people's preference but I just find it a bit jarring. Seeing bright particle effects over a dimly lit environment, while contact and cast shadows are sometimes too dark and sometimes too bright that they don't seem to exist, threw me off from the experience a bit. Some scenes nailed the high-contrast lighting perfectly, such as the prologue scene with blue and red lights on each side and the shadows are dark and threatening. While some scenes like the final boss fight has less detailed lighting, overly reliant on baked and static textures and colors. It's a good game for screenshots sometimes, but not most of the time imo.

I still give it a 7.5/10, a good Yakuza game, through and through. Even with shortcomings from short development time and branding as a side-game, it still offers a completed Yakuza experience through good story, memorable characters, cool set-pieces, abundant side activities, soundtracks, and satisfying combat system.\t\t\t\t\t
