\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWeird how I seem to be playing Like a Dragon games more often than I’ve ever played the typical Ubisoft games, but the burnout is far less noticeable… Seriously, though, RGG Studio has been putting out games set in this universe not just annually lately, but often with several games coming out the same year. And in situations like with this game, I’m not sure if there was the point. Not that I disliked Like a Dragon Gaiden, quite the opposite, but it does feel almost unnecessary at points…

Like a Dragon Gaiden: The Man Who Erased His Name is a new entry in the long running series of titles that erased their old international name (which was Yakuza), but remained mainly open world action adventure titles with beat ’em up combat and cinematic stories. This entry, as the title itself says, is a side story that is set during the events of Yakuza: Like a Dragon, but despite the short visit to Isezaki Ijincho (the main setting of that game), most of the events happen in the classic Sotenbori that was originally seen in Yakuza 2.

At this point I usually write that the “open world” section is not big but is otherwise dense with stories, but it isn’t exactly the case in this game. It’s not fully embracing a typical open world onslaught of icons pointing to busywork tasks, but for whatever reason the substories do go in this direction this time around. Not that there are no longer more deep side stories for you to explore, but most of the optional content this time around is far closer to how substories were in the earliest entries, with simple “bring me X item” tasks. While the longer more involved substories rarely do anything interesting even when their premise is good. There’s one wonderful crossover exception, but apart from it, the substories are pretty weak in this one.

Same goes for the main story, really. You do get to learn more about some secret agent-like society that was first hinted upon in Yakuza 6 and some of the characters shown there seem to be set up to reappear in a later game. But as far as the current story goes, you get some minor details and some new emotional scenes talking about things you already knew from other games and it’s not particularly fulfilling. Some of the new characters are really nice and Kiryu, for a change, has more range in his personality, but it’s not a game that will interest anyone with its story but the biggest fans of the series.

So what did I like about Like a Dragon Gaiden: The Man Who Erased His Name? It plays really ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ well. Just like with Lost Judgment, combat controls are extremely fun and better than they’ve ever been in the series. Every fight just flows well and provides enough challenge (on a highest difficulty) for those who like it, while providing a perfect amount of challenge (on other difficulties) for people who just want to have fun. Granted, some mini-games still go really unfair, unless you grind or look up best solutions, but those are an exception and most of the time this game is just really fun to play.

Yet it puts it in a weird spot. If you like RGG universe games for gameplay and it alone can interest you, this is a good game for you. It’s unlikely to become your favorite, but it’s really really fun. But if you like the RGG universe for the story, you really can skip this one and not miss out on anything the games won’t recap if needed later on. I liked it, but at the same time, it was a rather forgettable even if enjoyable time.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really don't like how RGG Studios couldn't help themselves from double-dipping on Kiryu's saga when 6 was such a great send off, but I guess that ship's long gone by now. The story we get here is at least interesting, with some new (mostly) compelling characters and the same dumb RGG plot twists we've come to love. As for Gaiden itself: it's okay. I had fun but mostly wished I was playing a Judgment game instead. The gadgets didn't do much for me which is unfortunate considering that was one of the biggest angles they were pushing. It feels like any other agility-based moveset (can we pretty please get 0 Majima's backbreaking back?) with a few gimmicks to sweeten the pot. But it's still a Yaku- Like a Dragon game so you're in for a good time if you're already a fan. I think $35 would be more reasonable for what you get here (a fun but unnecessary entry). As such, I would recommend waiting for a sale. But also POCKET RACING BROS WE ARE SO ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ BACK.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t"This is a shorter side story", they said. "We only took six months to make this", they said.

Riddle me this: how is it that a game that was thrown together in six months - PALTRY amount of time by today's standards - has far more content for $50 at launch than most games that cost $20 more and took YEARS to develop? Ryu Ga Gotoku Studio. That is all.

Let's get one thing out of the way real quick: this is easily the shortest story in the series by far. Only five chapters to get through, most of them can take you - assuming you prioritize the story and occasionally upgrade your abilities - about a decent seven to eight hours, tops. That being said, story itself is as rock solid as you'd expect. Though be warned, if this is your first foray into the series, you might be left out of the loop on some things, so at the very least, watch a story recap to get caught up (bare minimum; play or watch Yakuza: Like a Dragon). To keep it as spoiler-free as possible, this is a short and sweet adventure that - especially towards the end - is a perspective flip on a certain part of Y:LaD (set at the same time as the latter, to be exact) and leads directly into the next game, Infinite Wealth.

As for the game itself, well... it's more Yakuza. Or Like a Dragon as it's called now. More of that beat-'em-up goodness that you've come to know and love is still present and accounted for, and given some of that much-needed polish from Judgment, 'cause RGG have pretty much mastered the Dragon Engine by this point that - without exaggeration - this is THE BEST Ki-er, Joryu has played in DE by far.

Spoiler alert - no, the styles from 0 and Kiwami don't return, and Kiryu's still using the same style that he did from 6 onwards, but two things - it's given a proper name now (Yakuza) and, perhaps most importantly, it just FEELS so much better to play. And if you want to be on some real goofy ahh Roger Moore-era Bond chicanery, then the Agent style's got you covered. It's a little cumbersome to get used to (the Rush Combo in particular is a little sluggish), but the gadgets more than make up for it with how hilariously fun they are thanks in part to the DE's wonky-as-ever physics.

As for side content, there's still a fair bit to be had. For all intents and purposes, this is basically the Kaito Files from Lost Judgment if it were turned into a standalone entry. Short story to get through, but this time around, the content is a lot more plentiful (Substories are present, for one), having all the usual staples you'd expect (Karaoke, Pocket Circuit, Hostess Clubs, Completion List, etc). Realistically speaking, though, you'll probably be spending A LOT of your time in the Castle, which has the best iteration of the series' coliseum (last seen in Kiwami 2, fun fact) yet. Trust me, it's better to see it for yourself if you haven't already.

Yes, this game is worth it. Whether full price or on sale, you can never go wrong with an RGG Studio product. Just that simple.\t\t\t\t\t

+ Final boss is really REALLY good.
+ The final scene at the shrine made me cry.
+ The Castle is pretty cool.

+/- The combat, while an improvement over Y6 and Kiwami 2, is extremely odd; Agent feels like a cut down version of Crane and Yakuza is mismatch of 5's Kiryu and 6/K2's Kiryu that attacks far too slow.
+/- Professional difficulty is surprisingly actually hard but I didn't find it particularly fun.
+/- Akame is okay.

Lot of the same issues The Kaito Files had: Most bosses are fought like 3 times at minimum, two styles don't blend very well together, very little focus on side activities.
- Lots of issues with combat that were already present or even solved in previous games are in this game: Most bosses are fully immune to grabs/spider grapple/basically every gimmick, bosses constantly get super armor, style switching is really slow for whatever reason, and a ♥♥♥♥ load of bosses with bladed weapons.
- All side content is recycled from other games.
- Despite the very short run time of the story, the plot manages to spend most of it having very little happen other than Kiryu repeatedly making Omi or Daidoji upset and then being interrupted by Akame making you go do a substory.
- The story desperately needed some sort of personal throughline like Kiryu's depression from erasing his old life being shown more.
- It's an alright game but nowhere near enough unique content for the price point, it should have been 30 dollars.\t\t\t\t\t
