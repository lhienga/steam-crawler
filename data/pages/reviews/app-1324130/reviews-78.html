\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSuperb game! It's like Rimworld in 3D, but that's just the short answer. While some things aren't randomized/procedural (colonist options; there's a big list to pick from) there are a lot of ways in which that improves the game. For instance, there's an analysis feature whereby you can research technologies involving certain things in the world only by observing them. You get flavor text about that thing when the observation is done, and it seems to be different for each observer, adding to replay value. At least I assume it works that way, or it just so happened my vegetarian wanted to tame and not eat the animal.

Further, I love the spin they put on some familiar things. For instance, the storyteller function in Rimworld is served by your choice of moon, with cryptic messages giving you an idea what kind of game you can expect, and the moon actually physically appearing in your world.

I love the modularity of construction and I love the way power and storage work, and... Ugh, I just love so many things after only an hour and a half. I can see myself sinking dozens of hours into this easily. If you're like me and you modded Rimworld until it broke, this is an EXCELLENT place to go until you work up the will to fix your broken Rimworld.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTons of potential, but there is still a lot of work left to be done. Replayability could be great with the different characters/biomes/maps/seeds but the poor mechanics at present spoil it. You end up having to micromanage every character every hour until you reach a late game where you're so OP and over supplied that it doesn't matter when your characters waste 75% of their time.

Main complaints:
Survivor AI is terrible. Sometimes they'll commit to a task until they overshoot their scheduling window and accrue multiple hunger and sleep debuffs, sometimes they'll run a recheck of their tasking once they reach the 90-99% completion range and then sprint off to do a lower priority task.
When ordering them to complete a harvesting task, there is no way to ensure that they also carry the goods back.
If you order a survivor carrying 10 oil to craft fuel, they will throw their 10 oil on the ground and find an alternate source of 10 oil to use.
1 hour before their sleep schedule begins, they will set off on a three hour hike to harvest something. When they arrive at 2am , they will complete a few percent and then return to base. After one hour of sleep and already suffering debuffs, they will again set off on that three hour hike.
The frequency of severe weather is obnoxious, even at the base difficulty level you'll be lucky to get two normal days in a row. Over the course of one hour, the thunder storm will turn into an extreme heat wave, which will then overlap with toxic ash and last for multiple days. Half a day after that clears you'll have solar flares, then rain, etc...
Animal attacks can spawn inside your perimeter, which is plausible when it's flying bugs but less so when it's a herd of aggressive camels.
Survivors have the personalities of reality tv contestants. They bicker and insult each other every time they're in the same room. They can also go through the process of falling in love, getting engaged, getting married, and then a week later they're trying to hook up with somebody else while they're all standing in the same living room.

Some minor annoyances:
Building is fun but pretty limited, and you need to build much larger than you should due to not being able to overlap some things on the grid system. A door can't open next to a wall vent, can't build a window in a wall because there's some wheat growing 6 meters below it...
Distances and the time it takes to move between them are silly. It takes half an hour to walk to the animal that you just killed with your bow and arrow, and an hour to walk back carrying the meat.
Tamed animals are prone to bugs, some will be unable to find the feeding trough and starve until they attack you.
You can craft cpus and railguns by hand but can't make a baseball cap or beanie that will survive for a year.
Popup notifications get really repetitive and can't be disabled.
When people wake up early and don't have immediate tasks, they walk into other bedrooms and stand over the still sleeping survivors like creeps, giving them a disturbed sleep debuff.


If they can fix the AI and adjust the frequencies of events then it could be great.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI was very close to giving this a thumbs down but decided it held enough merit on its own to warrant a recommendation. Its impossible to not compare this to Rimworld because the mechanics are a near carbon copy. If you are familiar with Rimworld you will feel right at home, but it does do several things differently - some of which actually improved on the formula. I played through two scenarios - the trading post and the survival scenario. After selecting the scenario you can select two maps and, in a nutshell, one is basically a desert map where wood is scarce and rock is a preferred building material and a forest map which is essentially the opposite. Most other objects of each map are just analogues of each other with different labels but the same function. The weather cycles are different for each map as well, but there is no randomly generated world in the same vein as Rimworld. Also, there is only one other scenario for a total of 3 and that is all there is in terms of content. There is no sandbox mode. No other modes at all so that is disappointing for a 40-dollar game which is why I almost gave it a negative review.

How its different from Rimworld:
-The way the map is handled is much different. There is no world map so to speak and, instead, you will build a hot air balloon and choose from randomized events that appear on the horizon and the outcome is randomly decided by dice rolls. This aspect of the game was underdeveloped and really just served as a means of gathering resources when youre having trouble finding them in your own map. It reminded me of Frostpunk, but after a while the hot-air balloon mechanic felt repetitive and the events were recycled too quickly to be exciting. I didn’t mind this system and I also appreciate that it can feel a bit more cinematic and interesting than the randomness of Rimworld, but it was really too underdeveloped to be very much fun.

-OMG the music is terrible. Just a repeat of twangy banjo music that made it feel like I was playing a cheap Ice Road Truckers game. I turned it off after 20 minutes.

-Getting new survivors is very tough since you are only attacked by a variety of insects seemingly inspired by Starship Troopers so there are no human attacks and therefore no prisoners and no opportunity for recruitment like in Rimworld. The only way Ive seen to get new pawns is basically just to get very lucky in the hot-air balloon events. You can save scum the events until you get a favorable dice roll but the odds are so low and it takes far too much time.

-The object movement mechanics are awesome in this game. You can build and move anything with ease without resource penalty or a need to rebuild that object. A pawn will simply pick up and drop anything even doors and windows anywhere so this was a great mechanic that allowed you to rearrange everything at will.

-I hated the way you stockpile items at first, but I realized it was an improvement because it is more simplified. Rimworld has a complex stockpiling system that can get a lil overwhelming at times. There is no priority system in AD which allows pawns to prioritize which items go where if the stockpiles are the same. This sucked at first but then I realized it was largely unneccessary since objects only fall into two categories - small items that can stack in chests and shelves and everything else that must go on the floor. Once I figured this out it was easier to streamline your inventory and maximize space. I think Rimworld should also find a way to simplify stockpiling a bit better. I always felt that items take up too much space in Rimworld and it was a nice change to see that I could stack 3-5x more stuff in a chest.

- Obviously its graphically better in almost every way.

- Despite that there are not very many scenarios, I do liked that they existed. Rimworld really just has loose objectives that seem secondary to the experience whereas the objectives in AD are the core of the experience. For example, in survival you must accomplish building a landing pad and every so often you will get a random chance to evac a survivor but the catch is that you can only evac one at a time. What this means is that you must alter your strategy over time, automate defenses, and shift to a lone survivor strategy until your last evac takes place. This prevented the gameplay from getting stagnant I also really enjoyed the Trading Post scenario where your objective is just to make a million dollars, but I was greatly disappointed by how few the trading partners were and how non-organic the trading system felt. Once again, I felt this aspect was really underdeveloped and the lack of development for a 1.0 release is a common theme in this game.

- The science gathering is a fresh and interesting approach to gaining new tech. Survivors must "observe" items, animals and plants all over the map to gain their abilities. For example, researching the tech to make fuel can be accomplished traditionally through a research bench and tech tree or you can "observe" the manure left behind by a cow and you can simply make fuel through processing manure instead. What this means for game play is that there is more than one way to skin a cat when it comes to acquiring new resources. This was a very inventive and fresh take on the process of gathering tech.

- A lot of people compare this to Sims, but I didn’t see anything that spoke to any sort of depth into survivor interaction. Just very basic interaction like you see with Rimworld but with not as much variation and less noticeable effects. Survivor interaction is far more advanced in Rimworld.

-It felt like the pawns were more efficient because they were less hampered by their emotions and seemed to sacrifice sleep a lot more to a bare minimum at the expense of their mood, but even with a very poor mood it was rare that they suffered a severe effect from them.
Overall, I liked more aspects of the game then I disliked and I felt they had a pretty good foundation to work with and mold. I do think that there is far too little content for a 1.0 release. If you have no idea what Rimworld is and youre interested in colony-building games then I would start there as it far more fleshed out and with far more depth, but if you want to be more casual and want something prettier then AD this isn’t a bad place to start. It needs a lot more modes or even a sandbox mode that blends all aspects of the 3 scenarios. 8/10\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI have played more than a thousand hours of Dwarf Fortress. I have sunk hundreds of hours into Rimworld. I've played RTS games, base builders, survival sims - this is my favorite genre of games to just dive into and let my stress fade away. Farming, exploration, management, resource harvesting, socialization - I love all these mechanics, and I love seeing innovation in the genre. Even if the game has a few flaws or bugs, I love being able to dive in and have to spend time mastering new gameplay elements and figuring out all the little eccentric twists a game has to offer. Graphics mean nothing, popularity means nothing. Only one thing matters - is the game better, in some way, than the others that came before it?

With that in mind, I am completely confident in saying that Stranded: Alien Dawn was an utter waste of my time.

There is no mechanic in this game that is not already in Dwarf Fortress or Rimworld. Somehow, the game plays even slower than both of those alternatives, especially if you actually start collecting more survivors. Survivors will always hate each other until they want to bone. Crafting mid-game weapons and armor will take literal days worth of time, at which point the game will undoubtedly throw a wave of enemies at you, meaning that you need to have certain people work on nothing but crafting weapons while another just rebuilds defenses and turrets. The mechs are damn near impossible to build and not even needed - a simple maze filled with traps and turrets over the walls will do the job 100% of the time. This means that you spend way too much time on the mid-game building up the supplies you need to automate your defenses, constantly remaking the same weapons taking up months worth of time just to survive long enough to complete the turrets. Nothing memorable happens in the meantime, it's just farming crops, collecting scrap, making clothes, cooking, and moving beds for all the jilted couples. There is no point in pursuing any branch in gameplay, because the time-investment is so steep that it actually hurts your progress in the game to do so.

Taming animals takes up so much space, defending them becomes even more costly than defending your base. Using the balloons is only worth it to grab nearby scrap or dismantling Tachyon sites for CPU and Electronics. Ore is practically impossible to get, mining is ultimately pointless, diversifying clothes does nothing (just make summer and winter sets), and if you bother with armor you're an idiot because you should never leave your base's defenses during a wave anyway. Diversifying food is also pointless, as you only need one chef meal, one tasty meal, and one quick meal to give survivors adequate diversity while also accommodating lower skill levels. Drinking is pointless and does nothing, even when it's stim tea or coffee. You spend so much time making smokeleaf pipes because they are consumed so quickly that your crafters will spend more time packing pipes than doing literally any other crafting task.

The game inflates the amount of time needed to reach endgame by making you have to repeat the same tasks over and over. If there was more diversity in the enemies, tactics, or in the required resources, this might not be as noticeable, but since you'll be repeating the same actions each season year after year it gets very stale very quickly. I had forcefields and carbon fortifications unlocked and never even bothered because wood walls in a maze with traps and turrets destroyed waves even 200+ in number every time. Once you observe the basic crops the game essentially plays itself and you're just on rails and can't lose.

They make you remove one survivor at a time because it's so easy to get the orbital beacons. I had beacons up before I had researched pulse rifles - that's mid-game. I just kept the beacons off for ten years to see how much else I could do, then turned them on with a base so over-prepared for assault waves that there was nothing left for me to accomplish. Turning up the difficulty after the map starts isn't and option, and harder difficulties just kills you faster, it doesn't actually change the gameplay. I'd still be having the same problem.

I put nearly 150 hours into this game. If I had done that in Dwarf Fortress, I could make a series of lava pumps that melts every invading army that pissed on my lawn. If this was Rimworld, I'd be sending out raiding parties and making cults. In Stranded: AD, I watched Rita divorce Jack to shack up with Edmund, who divorced Ken, who then went to shack up with Umyar. That was the highlight of my endgame experience.

For a game that had an EA release and is now in full release, I was expecting so much more actual content.\t\t\t\t\t
