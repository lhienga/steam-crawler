\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo I have been playing through this game for the past few weeks and have completed 2 of the difference scenarios. It plays pretty similar to rim world in the sense you have survivors who have to farm, research, gather and manufacture resources to complete a central Mission. In the base game you have the option to run 3 different scenarios: Crash landing , Trade outpost, and Military outpost. I played through both crash landing and Trade outpost.

The different scenarios start you off with 3 - 4 "survivors" and caps you at a total of 6. You gain more people by heading expeditions and encountering them randomly. The "survivors" aren't randomized, and have set starting stats, traits and backstories. There is a pool of 30 to choose from (This can be expanded via the seam workshop).

Survivors all have tasks they do that can either be ticked on or off or be weighted via priority levels like Rim World. The main difference is the task list grows as new things are unlocked. The different crafting, cooking and manufacturing layouts also allow you to link work stations to further customize work flow.

The tech system is pretty interesting, you have the stuff you learn at a research table and then there are things you can only learn by sending out a survivor to "Observe" local flora, fauna, and rock formations. This is how you unlock new crops, animals and minerals that you can utilize. As far as the research tables go, you have basic research that branches off as you unlock more tech as well as a special section that is randomized at the start of every game. This random section is called "Break throughs" and serves as a way to improve existing tech. These break throughs can also be unlocked through expeditions and through trading (depending on the scenario).

Building is pretty fun, since its a 3d space you aren't just limited to a 1 floor base. Using different materials that you unlock along the way, can build massive forts guarded by traps and turrets. There is no internal risk to you base, meaning things wont blow up and set everything on fire, at least that didn't happen in any of my play throughs. However, that doesn't mean that your base is safe from destruction. Waves of angry animals and giant bugs will periodically attack your base, damaging structures and knocking down buildings if they deal enough damage to the foundations.

Combat in the game is pretty straight forward: equip gun, draft survivor, they shoot. The combat stat helps determine their over all effectiveness in combat, but equipment and visibility come into play a lot. The good news is the characters with a high combat stat hit there target pretty often. The survivors can also equip melee weapons along side their ranged weapons and will switch to them if the enemy is too close.

Over all its a pretty solid game. It has its flaws. The Animations are pretty limited, pathing is s a little weird some times, and I did encounter a few bugs (nothing game breaking) . Defiantly would recommend 7/10\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA shameless Rimworld rip-off with less to do, smaller research / tech trees, and none of the weird humour or wacky charm.
To be fair, the visuals are very nice (though not without bugs) but, without the depth of the original, it ends up feeling tedious. Desperately needs intelligent human or alien enemies. Anything but yet another style or colour of insect.
Character choice is from the same pool of 30-odd pre-set survivors who unfortunately tend to follow "traditional gender roles". Very futuristic and sci-fi. Needs to do away with the backstories and instead have a random skill (and name) generator to create a unique pool of survivors for each playthrough. Nobody cares if Bob was a farmhand who enjoyed sunsets and ice cream: the story begins when they crawl from the wreckage...

It's ok, but if you can deal with its odd-ball visual style, just play Rimworld.
Edit: After 82 hours, and finishing the game, my opinion remains unchanged.
