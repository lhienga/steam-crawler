\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIgnore the haters who claim Stranded: Alien Dawn is Rimworld and then hate on it for all the reasons it is not Rimworld -- after playing eleven hours. RW is a fine game, but if the haters try to go back and play RW, they will wish it was more like SAD.

Part of the genius of Stranded: Alien Dawn lies in the struggle to get your survivors off the planet one-by-one. Attacks from local megafauna increase in intensity and frequency, so you find yourself always calculating how quickly you can evacuate, who should go first – and who should be the last person standing to hold off the worst attacks.

\t This game mechanic creates a real sense of urgency and tension as each survivor bids adieu. It’s nothing you cannot handle, as the game supplies you with a variety of automated turrets, traps, fortifications and weapons.

\t Ideally, you reach a point where your defenses do all the heavy lifting. It can be great fun to watch as your flamethrowers and traps dispatch several hundred bugs without you so much as lifting a finger.

\t But to get to that point, you have to collect resources, build up a food supply, establish a power supply, work your way through a research tree – all the things you associate with a first-class survival simulation game. If you have dabbled in the genre before, you will find much familiar in SAD, but the beauty of the game is in its accessibility.

\t Even if you have never played a survival sim, you can have great fun learning the ropes at the easier difficulties as you ponder tackling Insane/Chaos mode.

\t The downsides include skill degradation, which the game never warns you about. If you have a survivor who specializes in, say, research, but you give them tasks on occasion to help build up a secondary skill in crafting, e.g., you can come back just a few days later to learn that they have lost all those crafting points and more, because they are not constantly using the skill. You never lose a skill level once you attain it, but any progress toward the next level can vaporize.

\t Steam discussion threads are full of folks who complain about travel times to distant places. The most obvious response is that it is not an exploration game. After you crash land, click and drag across wide swaths of the map and all the resources in the highlighted area will be revealed. If essential resources are not relatively handy, pick a new seed. There are ways around any shortage, but especially if you are just beginning, you might want to minimize the challenges.

\t And then there is the micro-managing. If you set up the Activities schedule and the Priorities menu reasonably well, and you are playing on easier difficulties, everything will get done eventually. I would suggest that this is actually the most fun way to play the game. But let’s just agree that efficiency is not your survivors’ strong suit.

\t When one of your survivors almost, but not quite finishes scavenging a wrecked spaceship, and instead of completing the task decides to meander back to camp to eat a fruitcake, the temptation is to jump in and direct the survivor back to the wreckage. Odds are some other survivor is already heading out to finish the job. But you have to be a certain kind of person, that I am not, to just let things unfold, however inefficiently.

\t Finally, allow me to praise the online community, which is extraordinarily helpful and generous with its time and wisdom. Flin Fredstone, Bjorn, tommy_b_schmidt and many others are the unsung heroes of this game. The devs are a pretty constant presence on the forums as well, which is always a good sign.

\t I have replayed the game numerous times without a single glitch and every playthrough has been different and uniquely challenging. There are many different seeds, different teams that you can choose, different biomes, and two additional scenarios (Military Outpost and Sow and Sell), not to mention an active modding community, that make the game almost endlessly replayable (although if there are glitches, mods are almost always to blame). I appreciated that the achievements could be obtained, more or less, just by playing the game. Beware, though, that a Perfect Game with all achievements can become imperfect when they add content and new achievements, even after the full release -- and after you have moved on to some other game.

\t This is one of those rare games that appeals to casual gamers, but as well to those who prefer a game into which they can invest many hours. There is always one more thing to research, one more fortification to build, one more preparation for winter. So feel free to blame me when you look at the clock and are surprised to see it is two or three in the morning. Enjoy.\t\t\t\t\t
