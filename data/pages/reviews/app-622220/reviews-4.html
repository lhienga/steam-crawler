\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'll start with the conclusion first. If you like musou games, Extella Link is fine, but it doesn't reinvent the wheel and everything that it does other musou games have done better. It's not bad, but exceedingly average within the genre. If you like musou games or love Fate, it can be worth it. If you don't like musous, this won't change your mind.

Now the text dump.

GAMEPLAY
Strategic Layer
The main idea of every single mission is to take control of certain key segments of the map by defeating mini-bosses within each zone. The enemy can also attack your zones with the idea of it being a territorial push and pull: do you push forward to gain more territory or try to defend what you have? Do you do a deep strike and take out their core areas to cut off reinforcements or steadily push forward conquering everything in your way? Sadly, these types of thoughts aren't really developed as most of the time your decision is attack the thing that's most threatening or fulfilling whatever main objective you have. Sometimes it's defeating a boss level enemy to prevent them from escaping, sometimes it's a long distance attack against your commander you need to stop, etc. There are some small benefits such as taking out neighboring zones to a fortified one to prevent needing to defeat a moderate amount of mobs before the mini-bosses show up allowing you take that zone quicker, but the standard mobs pose such little threat that even the small bit of additional time it takes doesn't make a difference. The strategic level of the game is sadly underdeveloped as while there's a framework there, it doesn't really offer a lot reason or benefit to trying to apply strategy in which zones to conquer and whether to attack or defend.

Character Control/ Customization
You have basic combos which are executed through a variable amount of light attacks with a heavy attack to cap off with unique moves based on how many light attacks were executed. Along with that, there are skills that you can activate at any time with relatively short cooldowns to get unique one off attacks. Some do not like this as they view it as firing off all your skills then just using standard moves until your cooldowns are done, but I find a lot of joy in blending in the skills with your standard combos to find ways to quickly move around a zone while attacking or to funnel enemies into one area for a AoE skill. There's seven skills per character and you can only have four skills equipped at one time, so there's a small bit of customization as well. Cap that off with Install Skills which are constant passive boosts you equip before a mission and you can have differing characters well suited for different roles, which is great.

Combat
We have different characters that have different specialties, different skills and passives we can equip, different high power moves and possible combos we can learn to quickly move around while still being aggressive. How does it all blend together against the bosses/ minibosses?

The grand reveal here is that it doesn't.

Most of the enemies that are supposed to be a higher threat can be taken out rather mindlessly. Before they start attacking there will always be a big red flash alerting you to block, or maybe dash away if you're already at a distance. Afterwards they have a big pause where they wait for you to punish them. They're stunlocked whenever you're attacking them and to make matters worse, the most efficient way to kill them is to hit them a bit, then use a specific type of skill based on your character to lock them into a QTE that involves you button mashing to deal damage uninterrupted. If you boil it down, it can be block their attack, attack back, use specific skill, mash button. Rinse and repeat. If they block, you can just keep hammering away until you break their block anyways. There's sadly no learning any enemy patterns or sneaking in attacks between moves, no constantly aggressive enemies you need to get space from, nothing. The biggest mixup is occasionally they power up giving themselves immunity from being stunned or knocked back. This results in you trying to keep away and hit them with long distance attacks or just doing the same yourself to legitimately turn it into a slug fest. There can be fun to be had, but it's usually from self-imposed restrictions and the huge cast of enemies feel the same to defeat.

Difficulty
This may not be a concern for some, but this is painful for me. The game is not difficult in the slightest. Most failures I had were due to me missing something or because of self-imposed restrictions. The game can easily turn into something mindless to beat every single mission, even if you are underleveled. Install skills, the passives mentioned earlier, can dramatically trivialize things with tons of easy combinations. If you don't mind a game that you can play while turning your brain off, then this section won't be an issue for you. If you do like a challenge or want to keep on your toes, you'll be left disappointed.

STORY
Moving away from gameplay the easiest thing to transition into is the story. If you are new to Fate and know nothing about it, you will be happy to hear that prior knowledge is not necessary whatsoever to understanding what's going on. The reason for that is because very little is going on.

The story starts off with a small recap of the important bits of the setting and why the main character is where they are now, then things happen. You get strung along very quickly and while the reason you are fighting the battles you do make perfect sense, there's nothing really going on beyond that.

Despite that, the story is passable. The characters are all well known for Fate fans and are represented spot on. All the usual voice actors retain their roles and do fantastic. If you want to see more of a certain servant doing their thing, that's exactly what you'll get. If you've never seen the characters before, you'll be able to see who they are on the surface level but sadly not much more beyond that. The saddest part about this is that the characters themselves already set the stage for lots of good stories, but nothing is expanded upon. It stings especially hard as Fate/Extella did a fantastic job with its story almost all the way throughout whereas Extella Link comes by as just average and forgettable.

PRESENTATION
This is where I start to become happy again. The presentation in Extella Link is absolutely spot on. There's a good variety of maps with different landmarks and effects, lots of color palettes, and each character's moves fit perfectly with their personalities and abilities. The music is great all throughout with a few surprises here and there. Overall fantastic.

PERFORMANCE
Performance for me was solid with the major problem being the camera. I experienced no real frame drops or hitches even when there was a lot going on. Instead, most problems come from playing with M&KB. The camera hitches, jitters, and slams against walls by default. It can be smoothed out dramatically, but requires tinkering with settings and possibly even graphics driver settings to get things to relax. If you don't mind controller, that's the easiest option.

CONCLUSION
Despite all the negativity in this review, I like Extella Link. It dramatically improved upon Extella in terms of mechanics, but took a step down in terms of story. There's improvements that could be made, but it's a step in the right direction. If you want another musou game, there's worse options to be had, but I wouldn't recommend it as a first experience for the genre. If you love Fate then it does a great job of presenting the servants and letting you feel as if you're them causing mayhem. Turns out, I love Fate and that feeling was exactly what I wanted.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's fine. Worse than Extella TUS, better than a lot of other musous. If you like Fate you'll probably find something to like here and can freely ignore the below.

Why the first game is better:

* TUS has super-jumping, movement is great - jump out of the early frames of a dash, you go flying. This game has no movement tech whatsoever. Mashing the dash button is the fastest you're going to get.

* In TUS, you have standard Koei-style charge movesets, which move you use in each scenario matters. I'm convinced that anyone saying otherwise is either being intellectually dishonest or spent almost no time experimenting. This game, on the other hand, is about *spamming active skills.* Once you have a decently-leveled character and solid Code Cast with Cooldown Reduction, no reason to ever use any other moves again. The best character (Nero, by the way) is the one with the fastest projectile active skill animation combined with the best movement speed (hers is 110% of the base value, 4th fastest movement speed). If they'd done a resource-based system like Pirate Warriors 4's active skills, maybe there would be more of value here.

* TUS has minimal dialogue/system messages blocking mission progress if you play too quickly. This game locks you behind dialogue constantly. Mission 01 alone has a total of two and a half minutes of arbitrary waiting before objectives progress. Granted, the speedrun community has a mod on the SRC page to fix that called FDM, but that's not a base game feature, and this only applies to the PC release.

Many elements of gameplay that impact player experience get overlooked, and then other, unrelated elements are blamed despite not being the cause. I understand that most people playing musous are not looking for high-intensity, high-skill-ceiling experiences but the irony is that the same things that contribute to doing that well tend to also create better casual experiences in this genre because both of those play into the power fantasy of wrecking thousands of enemy soldiers. Or programs, as it were.\t\t\t\t\t
