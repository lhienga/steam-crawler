The second time was much faster but still cool - I enjoyed having the chance to see another perspective. Good eerie-to-creepy ambience, and a lovely escape-room type game to play with someone over an evening or two.\t\t\t\t\t

Even though the 3D part is a breath of fresh air, the puzzles barely deserve to be called that way. As a bunch of other people already said, they mostly look like this:
Player A: "go there, do this"
Player B: "ok, I got the item, now you do this",

and so on and so forth for an hour or two.

I played through both routes (butterfly and bee), and both timelines, and you really don't gain anything by doing so. If you're on the fence about buying it and want to play the game just for the story, here, I'll save you the money:
Rose Vanderboom and her future self (or her past self, depending on your point of view) work on resurrecting Albert, they succeed, Albert uses the black and the golden cube to merge the timelines. The past is never dead, it's not even past.

If you really want to get it, at least wait for a discount. Other Rusty Lake games give you much more bang for your buck.\t\t\t\t\t

14. The Past Within
Past and future come together in this excellent two-player experience. You both play as Rose (one of you as her past self, the other her future self) as she follows Albert's instructions through time itself. Cool puzzles for both timelines with an interesting narrative and a very cool cooperative presentation. A really good game but, if you are a completist like me, you might be a little frustrated as you'll need to play through the game at least four times (two paths for the past and two for the future). Not only will you have to recruit a friend for each of these playthroughs but it can feel a little repetitive after the second time as the story is fairly self-contained without branching outcomes and fairly linear gameplay.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThere's another co-op puzzle series out there called We Were Here that has a pretty similar set up to this. It feels a lot less polished than this and perhaps even incomplete, but it does understand how to make the idea a bit more challenging. In The Past Within every puzzle in exactly the same. One person finds a code, which is always right in front of their face, then relates it to the other person who then inserts it into the only place on their screen the code could possibly go. then they repeat this 3 to 5 times (I suppose to prove that the person has actually done it and not just guessed?) and then maybe some little things happens like a jump scare or plot development and then the process repeats.

The big problem is that you're never asked to figure out a puzzle together. And, of course that there's only even one code and one entry station at a time so there's never any question about what you should do next. There are escape room games that do this quite a bit better, and even in We Were Here, which was very similar, at least there was some difficulty in thinking about how to relate what you were looking at to your partner. Here it's "Z3X1Y2" or "bee, person, house". The one section where this game starts to come alive at all is in a section toward the end where you relate the codes under the threat of timer, only then do you feel that you and your partner are actually working together on the same thing.

The story is a lot more obvious than in the Cube Escape games, and it doesn't appear to me to expand the universe in any way. I guess if I were you, I'd skip this one.\t\t\t\t\t
