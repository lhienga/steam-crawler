\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTl;dr: Pixel Cafe is the perfect game. If you’re on the fence, buy it. If you’re not sure, at least download the demo. I can’t imagine a gamer alive who wouldn’t want to spend an hour (or fifty) playing Pixel.


Every once in a while your gaming library starts looking a little dull, so after scrolling through all the titles you can’t muster up the enthusiasm to play, you grudgingly turn to your discovery queue, knowing full well there probably won’t be anything there you haven’t already heard about and dismissed or that you’d ever be remotely interested in playing.

And then: wait, what’s that one again? Hmm . . . great pixel art . . . intense challenges . . . well-crafted story . . .

The promo blurb for Pixel Cafe doesn’t begin to capture the subtle genius of the game, but then, despite spending more time on this review than I probably should have, neither can I (and words are my money game). The closest comparison I can make is to Touhou Mystia’s Izakaya, but that only goes so far. The core gameplay for both games is somewhat similar, in that you’re operating a cafe of sorts, and you have to manage memory, expectation, timing, and, hopefully, a little RNG luck in order to get through a shift.

But while TMI doesn’t take itself too seriously, the story at the heart of Pixel Cafe is one that resonates, even for someone who’s a decade or two beyond the intense turmoil one feels when trying to answer the questions that plague all of us at some point in our lives, namely, why the f- am I here? there’s no answer, sorry And what the hell were the people who raised me thinking? they weren’t; they were just trying to get by, too While nothing you’re going to find in your Steam library is going to be able to answer those questions for you, Pixel Cafe lets you abandon your own tortured path for a while and enjoy someone else’s.

Oh, and they are not joking about the ‘intense arcade challenges’. I am, when possible, almost exclusively a kb/m gamer, but I can’t fathom trying to play PC without a controller. I’m sure it’s possible, but you’re going to need to be more dextrous than Rachmaninoff to pull it off. On a controller, however, when you fail (oh, and you will), you’ll know it’s your own fault, not an accident of unfortunate finger manipulation.

Add to all of the above that the developers of PC have included every option one wants when playing a game with arcade-style challenges -- level selection, easy restarts, difficulty options, upgrades that carry over from location to location, and more. The game is beautifully conceived and perfectly executed. Pixel Cafe’s creators seem to have thought of everything, except what I’m going to do when I’ve finished my umpteenth playthrough and have to go back to my own existential crises (you aren’t really living if you don’t have more than one).

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhen I first played this game's demo during a NextFest, I immediately knew I was going to buy this game the day it came out. I did, though I didn't get around to playing it till a week or two ago. And I was glad to find I had made a good choice.

Pixel Cafe is a stellar little experience from start to end, and with the updates that they've been making in the past two months, there weren't any notable frustrations by the time I played. That said, I played with an xbone controller, and cannot speak toward mouse or keyboard controls.

From front to back, I loved every moment with Pixel Cafe. Beautiful presentation, a soundtrack that I'm eagerly awaiting the standalone release of (partially so I can add its songs to my TTRPG character playlists), and gameplay that perfectly sunk me into that flowstate of just playing Plus, a story that definitely did not need to be as good as it is in a stellar time management game, as Cook, Serve Delicious has proven. I'm so glad it went well above the bar, though. When the only thing I can complain about is a few typos, I can't recommend this game enough.

Baltoro, thank you for making my favorite game to come out in 2023. I loved it down to the last drop.

Minor note: You should definitely try the demo first, if only to help you find your ideal difficulty. I was comfortably 3-mugging every level, and thus I started the full game on Master Barista, which seems to be the same difficulty. This was perfect for me, comfortably passing every regular level with 3 mugs, and even managing to do the same on many of the Nightmare levels. After my second bar I began to just try the Nightmare stages until I completed them with at least one mug, and I came back after I beat the game to try them on the medium difficulty. Still a fun challenge, but much more fun for me that way.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis was a blast to play. A great game for around Christmas and New Year's Eve.

I played this game in its entirety with a mouse on the easiest difficulty setting and it worked out great. But you do need a controller if you want more efficiency or if you want to play the game on medium or hard mode because that's not going to be doable with a mouse past the second bar unless you have supernatural tolerance for things not reacting the way you want to/expect thus having to redo things or not being fast enough.

And the dialogue needs to be edited one more time. There are spelling and grammar errors throughout. Nothing major, but using dresses (plural) instead of dress's (possessive) or misspelling entire as enitre should not happen in a plublished game. Those are the two examples I remember but I do remember there being more throughout the game. Some of the mistakes definintely read like the devs are either dyslectic or not a native English speaker/writer. So it just needs a bit of extra love on that front.

I really enjoyed the story and the gameplay here and I wish there was an option to have several save slots so I don't have to overwrite the game I already played to start a new game (although I might have overlooked that being a thing).

I just wish I could get keys for this game to give it away in a giveaway though, but I can't seem to find them anywhere online, which is sad.\t\t\t\t\t
