As a consequence I was frustrated with a lot of the features that were not yet available in Mafia and the resulting limitations on gameplay. For example you cannot replenish your health through the use of food and drink items, you cannot explore the city at all and you are not able to purchase weapons. The player has to stick strictly to the chronology determined by 2K Games and can only use weapons that appear during the mission. All of these features were later improved on in Mafia 2.

Having said that, it is far better than Mafia 3 and remains an enjoyable game. I beat the missions in around 13 hours, though I did not collect all the items. The missions are challenging and varied enough, unlike Mafia 3 which had a lot more missions but these were boringly repetitive in nature. Great cast of characters with Salieri (The Don), Paulie who is desperate for his big break and Sam who will ultimately turn his back on Paulie and Tommy (the playable character). It's also nice to have all vehicles available to choose from one's garage during the game instead of needing to steal them.

Radio stations were so-so, far inferior in that area to both Mafia 2 and 3. I found the ending rather abrupt: I was expecting having a few more missions to beat and it didn't feel as though the story had been fully explored, particularly regarding what happens to Don Salieri as he tries to manage the fallout from the deaths of Paulie and Sam.

For anybody that is new to the Mafia universe of 2K Games, I strongly recommend you start with this one then move on to Mafia 2. The experience will be definitely more enjoyable.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWith the exception of one particularly painful mission, this is a brilliant all-round experience, with the driving being an absolute standout among a number of positives. I hadn't touched the Mafia trilogy prior to this title, but I'm glad it stayed off my radar long enough for this to be my introduction. Mafia: Definitive Edition follows the journey of Tommy Angelo, a humble cab driver in the city of Lost Heaven, as he becomes part of a new family. It's not a protracted story, but manages to feature great character development, interesting historical insights and a number of unique and challenging missions.

While having an entirely linear story progression, the game does an excellent job with its pacing, so you never feel like you're being pushed along. There's plenty of opportunities to take a drive between missions and explore at your own pace, plus a few different collectibles out there for completionists to uncover. Unfortunately, there's not much in terms of side missions or distractions, but the main story does enough heavy lifting to make up for this.

As I previously mentioned, Mafia: DE also features some of the best driving physics I've seen in any game and flying around Lost Heaven in Lincoln Clay's Samson Drifter from Mafia III is unbelievably satisfying. The combat isn't that far behind, either; not as memorable, but certainly not a negative.

Nothing stood out to me as an area where there was significant room for improvement, with no major graphical/audio issues or glitches to put a dampener on the experience. It's simply a very well-rounded game, which exceeded my expectations in almost every regard.\t\t\t\t\t
