\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInteresting concept with poor execution.

More of a interactive story-telling than an RPG really. And even though the story is quite interesting, the gameplay becomes very tedious after a while. Having played for roughly 12 hours, I reached a point where the only thing that keeps me going is the 'completionist syndrome'.

The game progression revolves around running around the map and "discovering" clues by reading stuff and talking to NPC's. It boils down to right-clicking every once in a while for the 'thaumaturge sense' to lead you where you need to go, after which you might as well just mindlessly click all POI's (highlighted to the player either automatically or through the 'right-click magic') to obtain clues and draw conclusions. Of course the game does not attempt to challenge your own deduction and instead makes the main character conclude things automatically after you click enough diamond-shaped icons.
Some quests (side ones especially) seem to be poorly developed as if they were glued together in haste, with scene transitions feeling like some content was cut/missing. Definitely didn't help the immersion.

The game looks quite nice visually but could sure use some polish here and there (some animations feel a bit 'wooden', especially in cutscenes). The game supports DLSS but turning it on repeatedly caused my entire OS to freeze after a while of playing, forcing me to restart the machine. Seemed to be quite common issue when I looked it up online. Disabling the resolution scaling sorted the problem with little impact on the game's performance.

I liked the music but there isn't much of it outside of combat. Voiceovers are mostly mediocre with some really poor examples once in a while (Piotrus from Powisle for example).

All in all, I had huge expectations for this game and got let down unfortunately. Definitely would not recommend buying at full price.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis year started off like this - games I had high hopes for disappointed me, while those I approached with caution turned out to be pleasant surprises. From the latter category emerged "The Thaumaturge," created by just five people.

To explain what the game is about... um...
Imagine Poland in 1905. Pressured by the Russian Empire, torn apart by socialist uprisings, and plagued by thriving criminal gangs. Poverty, misery, violence, corruption.
Now, add a dash of magic in the form of thaumaturges - people with the ability to read the thoughts and emotions of others, and to control spiritual beings called "salutors," whose powers they can influence others with through tricks, quite reminiscent of Jedi. And of course, these thaumaturges don't live in peace with each other. Oh, and there's also a proto-Nazi organization that wants to exterminate them.
So in this world, you seek your place under the sun - Victor (with a double W) Zshulski, heir to a financial empire, a man with a dark past and an unclear future, and yes - a thaumaturge from a family with centuries-old traditions in magic.
Your job (aside from, well, surviving) is... um... complicated.
Overall, the fate of Warsaw, all of Poland, and perhaps the entire world depend on you, but while you deal with global problems, you still have time for smaller ones. Like catching a serial killer, stopping a sadistic usurer, saving the lover of the Russian czar fleeing from authorities, and finding out where exactly they sell that notorious American drink, Coca-Cola.
Seriously though, the game is cool. The mix of criminal investigations, "choices matter" storytelling, and (don't laugh) Pokémon is entertaining. For at least 30 hours, which is how long it took me to reach one of the few possible endings.
It's not perfect.
The graphics are slightly rough, some of the voice actors sound like they've had 1-2 extra shots of vodka before recording, and besides, even on the highest difficulty, the battles are too easy.
But it's worth it. Definitely a chance to play it again, as well as to buy the (very likely) sequel.
Rating: 8/10\t\t\t\t\t
