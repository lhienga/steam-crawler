\r\n\t\t\t\t\t10/10

Pros:
-Absolutely captivating story, occult mystery and historical fiction blended perfectly.
-Gameplay is somewhat unique, doesn't feel boring or tedious.
-Combat is really engaging, requiring some strategy and knowledge of your Salutor arsenal.
-The Salutor's designs are incredible and their animations are extremely well done.
-Very detailed environments that actually feel alive.
-Rasputin's Introduction and involvement it's really well done and takes a very different view from the revolutionary image that demonized him, really great to see other perspectives of historical figures

Cons:
-A few technical glitches and funny errors, but nothing gamebreaking.
-Animations and facial expressions are a bit stiff, to be expected from a small studio but still very enjoyable and also very promising for future projects.
-Combat can be somewhat confusing but will get way easier after a couple times.

Overall great game, Looking forward to a sequel already and I haven't even finished the game.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFirst off, it's a great game. Great intrigue, interesting combat system, choices can have interesting unforeseeable consequences (My first playthrough I managed to lock myself out of everything but the "screw up" endings due to poor choices and then not having enough skillpoints). Also the soundtrack is great.

However, it is a rather rough cut gem. Certain issues can fixed with more polish (pun not intended), and others not so much.

Such polishing issues as follows:
Minor performance as in frame drops.

Certain events just sometimes lock you into an infinite black screen.

Quest themselves have various bugs i.e. not properly marking completed objectives, bringing completed quests back after interacting with something you missed, and sequence breaking by accidentally talking to certain people.

Animation on models break a lot during dialogue. It's like there's an invisible chiropractor behind the characters and at any their necks with violently snap in a random direction. Other times a character is supposed to perform some animation and their bodies just stiffly stand there.

Issues that might be more ingrained:
Voice acting varies wildly, and I mean wildly. I can say it's serviceable especially with all the other languages they just randomly drop into dialogue, it doesn't ever become grating (expect for the kids).

There's some certain lacking "impact" when it comes to salutors sound design both as allies and enemies. That could be intentional given as they aren't physical beings but still comes off as unsatisfying.

Still despite its rough edges, I definitely thinks its worth it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhile I didnt have high hopes for The Thaumaturge, it seemed potentially interesting. The setting, the time period, the topics and how mystical dark fantasy story could be integrated in all of that was promising. And the mix of story driven adventuring with turn based battles had a lot of cool ideas about them. But, unfortunately, the end result is quite underwhelming for the most part and outright bad in a few spots.

As mentioned, the concept is strong. 1905, Warsaw under Russian Empire control, rise of the Rasputin cult presented on the edge of history and dark fantasy. With everything being dramatic and also veiled in the mysticism of thaumaturgy and people who practice this magic. As a result you get a cool mix of historic drama, Witcher-like demon hunting and Sherlock Holmes-like case solving. Its all played from the top down perspective, controlled similarly to a point and click adventure or RPG in the vein of classic Infinity Engine titles or the far more recent Disco Elysium.

You spend most of the time running around huge maps that represent different districts of Warsaw, finding people to talk to, items and collectibles. Youre also constantly clicking the right mouse to reveal the elements in the locations that have strong emotional traces that are usually tied to either minor collectible quests or to main or side quests, where they provide you with additional information and alternative means of dealing with obstacles, usually in the form of unlocking new dialogue options. To give the developers credit they put quite a lot of big and small variations in their quests that lead to unique consequences and callbacks later on. Which mostly works fine, with only some seams being quite obvious.

Sadly, in practice all of this is quite uninteresting. For example, the game often provides you the connections and conclusions for finding different items and traces and it really tries to emulate an almost investigation/detective game feel. But everything is so streamlined and automatic, that its faster and sometimes outright more beneficial, to just click past all of that until the game gives you a new trail to follow. For one, despite the overwhelming amount of the conclusions and items, it always boils down to 1-3 simple choices they unlock. For two, even then the game gives you a name or an address to follow theres nothing on the map to use this information on, so you still have to follow the objective marker instead of using your head. And for three, clue items and collectibles simply do not exist until the game brings them into existence somewhere in their gigantic maps, leading to pure pixel hunting. The more I played, the more similarities I saw to Sherlock Holmes: Chapter One, a game I worked on. One that, I think, turned out quite well, but also one that could be improved in a number of ways. Yet everything The Thaumaturge borrows, it does much worse

At least the combat seems very promising in the beginning. The idea here is that of turn based combat where each action has a certain time for activation and may have additional effects of delaying or outright cancelling turns for the target. There are no items, no equipment and health is always reset to full at the start of the fight, so everything is always decided via smart usage of said actions. And as you get stronger and get more salutors, your abilities increase, leading to different strategies for battle focusing on damage over time, making enemies scared, dealing pure damage, varying types of actions or building combos. Combat system is pretty cool on its own, but a lot of the time battles seem to exist just to spice up the story. And in those cases, combat is rather tedious, while the only narrative reasoning for it is that some random people come up to you and say that they dont like your face.

Biggest disappointment, however, is the story. For how cool the premise is, for how interesting the setting is, the resulting tale is just a messy nothing in the end. It does have highlights and better written characters, some moments and scenes are genuinely well delivered. Its just that most of the time you go through the game, both gameplay-wise and story-wise, on complete auto-pilot and its hard to care about whats going on. It does look nice and the music is quite pleasant. At the same time, however, the usage of Unreal Engine 5 here is quite unrefined and even ignoring that, the game can be quite buggy. I got stuck a few times, requiring a reload and crashes are also a possibility.

While theres nothing outright bad with The Thaumaturge, theres very little genuinely good about it either. Almost every cool idea that the game has, remains undeveloped or taken in a direction that feels unsatisfying. While the ending of the story is presented as if there could be a sequel, I just dont see why anyone would want it. That said, a new take on this idea could be interesting one that could be more refined, even if that would require the game to be slightly less ambitious.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo after finishing the story for the first time, 30 hours playtime, i'd like to add my review.

Rating see below.

If you love polish history lesson garnished with okkultist detective and interesting and deep fights (some where really hard) go for it.

I really liked almost everything about it.

There are a few issues. Although i finished all (or most of the) quests i couldn't get all skills to max which i didn't like much.

Fights can get pretty intense (had to throttle down difficulty for 3 fights) but are engaging and require you to think about your build (stick what passive effects to what attacks, use what Salutors for what enemy)

The UI could use some improvments both in fights (no hover/information what debuffs on you mean), map (what quests are on which area) and the constant pinging for directions/clues was a bit annoying.

Overall i'm totally blown away by the game and finished it in like 3-4 days (yes, long session) and i loved almost everything about it.

The price is more than fair.

Great game, thanks!
