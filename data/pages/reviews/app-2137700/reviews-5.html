\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA good Observation Duty game that could've been great. Definitely worth buying at full price and playing, though!

Below I will give my general critique of the game. It's only meant as positive and constructive feedback, and should not be taken as me just bashing the game. I love the OD series in general, including the flawed but interesting OD3 that a lot of people dislike, and this game. This is a good game, and my points of critique below should only be read as me wanting to see the next game in the series improve upon OD6.

GAMEPLAY
This one does experiment with the gameplay a bit, and I think that's good. Most anomalies are reported by clicking and holding on them, while others must be reported through the menu that appears by right clicking. It works fine, though I do have some critiques about it. And don't think this means the game is bad, it's just things that were tested out with the gameplay in OD6 that I think stops a good game from being a great game:
1. For people new to the game, it's easy to think you're only supposed to do one of the two. Yes, the controls are shown clearly on the loading screen, but a lot of people don't actually register it. I've seen several youtube videos where the players were confused about this for way too long, and I was so myself. And then you have to learn what should be reported by clicking or by going in the menu, and as what menu item, after that, basically doubling the amount of stuff you need to learn before getting properly into the game. So while the new system seems to have been made for easier gameplay, the fact that the reporting system is split into two like this is actually more of an extra hurdle, or a roadblock, for getting into the game.
2. While the click and hold to report in itself is an easier system than the "open menu and choose type and room", I think it feels less satisfying and fun. At that point you've removed the feeling of actually reporting the anomaly, the idea or fantasy of being the person that's on Observation Duty. And you've turned it into more of a pure "find the difference" game, because the tiny bit of puzzle gameplay that was there(find the right combination of anomaly type and room) has practically been removed, and with it also the fear that comes from having to choose correctly in a stressful situation.
3. The right click report menu does its job, but while half the time it's used for stuff that can't be pointed and clicked on(camera malfunctions or anomalies that take up large parts of the screen, including where other anomalies might be), the other half is when the game just arbitrarily decides that you can't click and hold to report specific anomalies on screen(like ghosts or abyss presences). Meanwhile, the "smoke/fog" report button is almost never used, as there are a bunch of anomalies that are either smoke or fog like in nature, but you just use the click and hold system to report instead. So the whole thing just feels inconsistent, illogical, and forced.

DIFFICULTY
When the game released there was no difficulty choice. And like a lot of people, I thought the game was too easy. I don't know if this was intentionally done, or if it just turned out that way, but that did take away from the fun of it. If something's too easy you don't get that feeling of accomplishment. That makes the game less fun.

But then the developer said they were working on an update that would introduce Hard Mode. Normally I review games immediately after finishing them, but I waited until that patch came out, and until I'd had the time to play through it. And Hard Mode certainly lives up to its name! Not only are there some new, more difficult anomalies added in, but existing anomalies have a more difficult to spot version in Hard Mode. I enjoyed this a lot, and I think it made the game overall a much better experience.

I also really liked how the Hard Mode had the "three strikes = timeout" system, which I thought was very, very elegant! It adds a bit of tactics to the gameplay.

That said, I think I still would've preferred just a single difficulty that was somewhere in between the two. I don't think difficulty modes generally add more fun to a game in and of itself. On the other hand, I think there being a completely optional Easy Mode for accessibility would be a good thing.

ANOMALIES
The anomalies this in OD6 felt more all over the place in quality than any other OD game: From the highest highs, to the lowest lows.

I think the worst offenders were the repeating anomalies that showed up in many, possibly all, stages. Sometimes basically completely identical, sometimes just using the same assets. Gone are also some of the weirder, "normal" anomalies like things that stretch and the like. On top of that, a lot of the "main intruders" were just kinda boring, and a lot of the unconventionality that's been a pillar for the OD games was gone. Instead we have "standard ghost lady", "standard living marionette lady", "just a guy", "Silent Hill shake body again", "regular demon", etc.

The sum effect of all this was again that the game felt less fun than previous titles in the series. It felt less imaginative and weird. It felt "safe". Like everything had been overanalyzed, overworked, and revised to the point where originality was lost somewhere in the process. It reminds me of myself as a traditional artist: When I do quick drawings or paintings, finish them, and move onto the next, they might have flaws and not be perfect. Yet they're interesting, intriguing, creative, fun... But if I spent a lot of time making sure everything is perfect, well planned out, nitpicking details, cleaning up every little line, working on the picture for ten or twenty times the amount of time I normally would, then the result is always boring, sterile, unimaginative. It might be much better on a technical level, but it has no soul.

The upside is that it feels less like this in OD6 when playing on Hard Mode. I don't know if the developer also fixed some of it in Normal Mode, but it's definitely a bit better on Hard.

OTHER (MAINLY PRAISE)
There are some things I think are genuine improvements. I mentioned earlier how I liked the "three strikes = timeout" system, which I think could easily replace the old "three strikes = game over" system, or could be used basically as it is in the future, like it is here in OD6.

The addition of music during the final hour was also a nice touch. It helped increase the tension for the final stretch, which was great!

Every level was wonderfully designed, and I loved them all! Though it was a bit weird that they all had six rooms, except for the pool which had seven. Wouldn't have minded a bit more variation. Also, it felt weird every time on the Summer Cottage level, when switching from the balcony to the warehouse and then to the sauna. Almost like when movies break the 180 degree rule. "At the water, not at the waterback at the water!" Kinda jarring, but also something that might've been done on purpose specifically for that jarring effect.

I also like how colour camera feeds were done this time around. Because the saturation was so low, it sorta gave the feeling of black & white screens, despite being in colour. Very nice!

And I think the anomaly report when failing a level is a nice touch. It doesn't tell you specifically what it was, but just enough that you can go back and maybe, if your memory and observation skills are good enough, possibly find what you missed in the previous round.

FINAL NOTES
I do think this is a good game. It just suffers from what feels like, "what if OD, but we base its development on statistical data and current market trends".

This entry in the OD series just doesn't feel like the developer had fun making it. It feels like they did a lot of things because they had to, not because they wanted to. It's a good game, but I hope the next one actually feels like they had a ton of fun.

100% in ~35 hours, no guide.\t\t\t\t\t
