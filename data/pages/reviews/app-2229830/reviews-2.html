\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI grew up on the original C&C. I mind playing it for hours upon hours in Skirmish. So when this was released, I instantly bought it! I'm sure I have it on Origin but who uses that ;) Installed the original C&C and memories flew back at me.

The game really lacks options for graphical settings, it kinda just jumps you straight in to the game. Since then I've been playing the GDI campaign which I never really did back in the day.

The game plays good on my average laptop and no bad on the Steam Deck but thats no surprise as this game is from 1996? or somewhat. But the main thing it runs. No work arounds, just runs.

The gameplay is simple, which is great for my simple attention span. The AI in campaign is pretty easy so far and pretty predictable. Able to figure out where they're coming from and what they will send.

The FMV/cut scenes are a blast from the past, they're cheesy but still good. I wish more games did it.

The music is just fantastic, Mechanic Man is top tier.

Overall, if you're wanting to get into RTS genre, this is a fantastic place to start. Granted you will be missing some quality of life features are lacking but its a pretty short campaign to get through and start your way on a great series.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've skipped almost everything of the RTS genre back in the 90s (except Age of Empires and some of it's clones).
I thought that CnC is a primitive boyish strategy that relies mostly on quick reflexes and full of sad grey/brown colours in the graphics (close examples: stracraft 1, Dominion, Tzar, Age of Wonders 1 - why everything was so grey back than, while such colourful games as Heroes of Might and Magic 1/2 and Warcraft 1/2 existed already??).

Recently I've started a Grand RTS Crusade and since the anthology is released all of a sudden, I've decided to give it a try.
From my point of view, 25 years later it's still a primitive boyish strategy that relies mostly on quick reflexes and full of sad grey/brown colours in the graphics.
But for an adrenaline RTS where you only need to build and destroy, it's addictive a lot.
I've started to think why. It look like the reasons are:
1) Good unit specialisation system, that is almost intuitive to catch. You start to understand what is countering what from the moment you see unit in action. And the type of the unit really matters a lot (which is lacking in Warcraft 2, Age of Empires 1 and a bigger list of the junk clones of the latter from the end of 90s).
2) Mission designers increase the gameplay complexity and introduce new possibilities step by step very skilfully. This part is really a masterpiece.
3) The good old holywood plot about "free fascist nations" and "autocratic fascist nations" confrontation, where both "good" and "bad" sides actively involved into some colonial business in some other countries, that are seen as brutally "oppressed" from the both sides and absolutely require freedom. Alvvays exciting.

The fact that Westwood made such a big step from Dune2 in the interface design and game controls is astonishing. Most of the elements used in this game are now widely used in the genre and became a standard by default. From the historical point of view, just to compare the changes is to feel that you touching the history. It's like visiting the museum with the old fossils in it, but you still can catch the idea without the professional scientific help.

For the wide claims about the need to install fan patch, I disagree.
Why do you need some shady .exe in your computer which do what? I don't even know. This is insecure and not an option.
Why do you need to play Remaster instead of the original (while this version of the original is running on Win11 and modern hardware without any problems)? The Remaster has different balance, it's for someone who already over-played the originals. Me, I'm thinking this is strange, because I want to play a game as it's designed originally, to receive an original experience that developers wanted to share with players initially. Not a re-imagining by some 3rd party.

In the end, I didn't lose anything by skipping it in 90s. But playing the game now, I'm still receiving challenge and interesting experience so it's a good game even nowadays. I'm just wondering how mind-blowing it was back then. But reading reviews where guys writing about this game being life defining is a bit of overreacting I think. While everyone know that life defining experience is Age of Empires 1, because it's beautiful, historically more accurate and provides more actual knowledge for a kid. Ha-ha.

Ho-ho.

Ehe-he.



O-ho-ho.
