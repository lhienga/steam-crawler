\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDo you need a game to de-stress from daily life? If so, this could be just what you're looking for.
The goal is simply to build a landscape by placing down pre-determined tiles (given to you in a random order) to score points, with bonuses given for adjacent tile placements (ie trees next to more trees etc). Scoring points is necessary to keep the flow of tiles coming, otherwise it's game over when you run out of tiles to place.
You also get given various blueprints to place (amongst the sequence of random tiles) and those generally require tiles of certain types to be placed next to them to generate bonuses.
As you score more points, you can also unlock other structures (which again, need certain tiles placed adjacently) to unlock more bonuses.
And that's it really ..... you can just keep building the landscape by placing tiles and keep going until you run out. There's no time limits involved, so you can play at your own pace, and can easily step aside from it whenever you need to.
There's a tutorial to get to grips with the mechanics (although they're fairly simple to grasp) and there are various challenges too, as well as the normal game mode, and a sandbox mode.
Judging by the achievements (I've earned 9 on my first hour of playing) not many people are actually playing this game (ie only 24% completed the tutorial, and some other achievements which can be earned just in the tutorial alone have similar low completion percentages).
So, if you fancy a simple game to unwind after a hard day, then this could be one to try.
I picked this up for just £5 (as part of a bundle of similar games) and it's well worth it.

NB there are also similar games out there like this (DorfRomantik, TerraScape) and they're equally as soothing but this is the one I'm currently playing just to chill out.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPan’orama is a delightful game that lets you build cities in a laid-back manner. It’s all about strategically placing hexagonal tiles to craft stunning landscapes, ensuring that each piece fits perfectly to create a seamless panorama. What makes it even more exciting is that the game experience varies with each play, as it randomly mixes up the tiles, keeping the challenge fresh and engaging. Pan’orama shares similarities with the charming game Dorfromantik.

In Pan’orama, players are given a set of hexagon tiles, each depicting various city elements or landscape features such as windmills, houses, and rivers. The objective is to place tiles with matching attributes adjacent to one another, forming a continuous landscape. Each tile placement garners points, with additional points awarded for aligning similar tiles, enhancing the strategy as you aim to optimize your score while expanding your idyllic world. The game also introduces quest tiles and special buildings, each with unique effects, adding layers of strategy to the serene gameplay.

Visually, the game is a treat, boasting an aesthetic designed to soothe and relax the player. Complementing the tranquil visuals is a soundtrack that serves as the perfect backdrop for relaxation. For those who enjoy crafting their own scenarios without constraints, Pan’orama offers a sandbox mode that provides complete creative freedom.

Pan’orama is the ideal game for those looking to unwind and express their creativity. It masterfully balances the addictive nature of turn-based games with a tranquil atmosphere, making it a game you’ll want to return to time and again for both its calming effect and its engaging gameplay.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is one super addictive tile placer-style puzzle game. I've really enjoyed some of its thoughtful gameplay additions, like added blueprints for unique buildings that boost your tile scores, gameplay modifications and bonuses, and spawning tiles that require you to combo them with several other tiles of the same type that serve as sorts of in-game quests. The fact that you can prioritise aesthetics over chasing the high score is also quite nice, because obviously creating a pleasant-looking area is much more important than anything else. Hunting for animals is also a fun little activity, and I like how placing tiles next to each other upgrade their look. I can see how this game can get repetitive, but as long as you treat it as something to play in 1.5hr sessions, you'll hardly ever get bored of it.

I've ran into some visual bugs where new tiles don't visually appear as you place them but that's just a minor thing. The game runs well on my toaster even when I create a really huge map.\t\t\t\t\t
