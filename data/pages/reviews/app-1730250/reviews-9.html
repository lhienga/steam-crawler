\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPan'orama is a very streamlined and capable boardgame/city builder that perhaps isn't worth its asking price, but it really depends on if it speaks to you. It has a very pleasing aesthetic, bright and colorful, and the gameplay is very simple and no-frills. If you're looking for something to occupy idle hands, it might be just what you're looking for.

Put simply, this is a solo tile-laying game that focuses on optimization of its adjacency rules. In other words, your goal is to earn the most points possible before your deck runs out and it is entirely based on where you place your tiles in your landscape.

Starting with a single tile, you will build outwards and earn points based initially on adjacency rules with advanced placements making themselves apparent as you gain special structures and complete 'quest' tiles. For the most part, when you plunk a tile down, you will gain one point, but you will earn more points if you place the tile next to similar tiles, which will level up visually. Placing a tree, for example, touching two other trees will get you three points (1 for the tile, 1 for each adjacent tree) and the two next to your new tile will level up (trees will grow taller, rocks bigger, water will gain features, houses will get bigger, etc). This is why when you look at screenshots, most tiles are grouped together because that's the most straight-forward way of earning points.

Randomly in your deck, you'll come across "quest" tiles which is usually a grouping of tiles in a specific configuration with one or two that are looking for specific adjacencies. For example, you might get a shape of 2-5 tiles that you need to fit onto your board, but maybe one or two of those will be marked with what it wants next to it, hence your 'quest'. Completing these demands will net you a lot of points and usually adds more tiles to the bottom of your deck, giving your game more longevity (your game is over when your deck runs out).

Finally, earn enough points (marked in the corner of your screen in a blue hex) in an unending series of milestones and you'll be rewarded with a choice of one of three randomized structure blueprints that you can put on your map to earn a bonus. These are specific configurations of hexes that can make use of existing hexes or be laid out completely unconstructed. When completed, you'll earn the structure's bonus as well as put a unique building in your landscape. For example, one building might randomly dump a bunch of tiles within a radius for free or one might level up a whole bunch of existing tiles in its radius for a points bonus, or yet another building might make future placements more valuable than they normally would be, etc.

As you develop your landscape, you'll be treated to on-tile animations, larger features, and other things that make your landscape come to life, so to speak. These are just minor visual rewards for a job well done, but otherwise have no impact on your game or your points. You don't have to worry about routes or citizens, supply chains, roads, taxes, or anything of the sort. This is simply a tile-laying game where the goal is to min/max your placements so that you can earn even more tiles to place and keep your game going. You could theoretically play indefinitely if you're good enough or have the patience to keep it going.

Overall, this has joined my collection of "I'm stuck on a meeting" games that I dawdle with in the background while I'm listening to incessant corporate droning about how unimportant things are going to destroy the world as we know it because someone is going to make a few hundred thousand dollars less than what they were expecting to make and thus it is imperative that I personally see to it that the thing, whatever it may be, gets done in a timely fashion so that some dude I don't know can buy a piece of crap instagram car and not share a single cent with me.... you can see why I need games like this to just chill and put my mind elsewhere lest I find myself in prison for more appropriate-yet-unbecoming reactions to my daily life.

I don't believe this is something that I would just sit down and play. This is a complimentary game, meaning that this is something you do while you do something else. Want to listen to a podcast, but don't care to snack and stare at the wall? Forced to sit in a three-hour town hall talking about the state of your particular corporate nation, but don't actually give a hoot? Want to go to town on some munchies while playing a game that only requires one clean hand? Did your mom call you to tell you a riveting story about how she saw someone she didn't know at the store being crazy enough to buy grapes at an inflated price when her favorite brand is tastier and cheaper by ten bucks for several extra ounces? Is your wife or husband telling you a ribald tale about how Pete at work made a horrible blunder with a slice of chocolate cake in the kitchenette and had to spend the rest of his day looking like he ♥♥♥♥ his pants? Are you perhaps reading an overly verbose review about a very simple time-waster of a game? Then Pan'orama is an excellent thing to have going in the background, keeping your brain and your hands occupied in an entertaining and occasionally-challenging way while other things get done.

To be fair, you can find yourself under Pan'orama's spell from time to time as you get harder quests or fancier structures to complete and in its own right, it's a very capable solo game of just plunking down some hexes. Is this something you really need to get right now and play until you're exhausted? Nah. In some ways, it has the same appeal as a jigsaw puzzle, but perhaps with less challenge. There are going to be few (if any) instances where you don't know what to do and yeah, you can totally bung up the works and run out of cards. But even then, there's a handy undo button that you can abuse to your heart's content and really get that min/max itch scratched if such a game works for you on that level.

Ultimately, this is just a fine, chill time with more to do than watching rubber ducks go down water slides so it certainly has its appeal. I certainly don't dislike the game and it absolutely performs a very specific function for me, especially when I'm stuck on long-haul activities that only occasionally require my attention. I haven't encountered any bugs and the aesthetics are ultimately pleasing so the game accomplishes exactly what it sets out to do. Some achievements might have been nice, but those are also ultimately meaningless so really, if you're the type that might sit down at a table and play a solo boardgame or you find yourself in situations where you need to actually kill time, then Pan'orama will probably speak to you.

One important note that isn't made clear when you're learning the game - flower tiles level up everything around them even if they don't match, so they're great for filling otherwise-valueless gaps in your map.\t\t\t\t\t

Dorf, while a great game, didn't invent the tiles and its placement and Pan'orama does everything pretty differently from Dorf. Grouping doesn't matter much, it's all about direct adjecency.
