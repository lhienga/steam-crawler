Despite having beaten Katana ZERO twice times - once in Story Mode, once in Speedrun Mode- my opinion of the game remained unchanged. I bought into it expecting fast-paced, high-octane action, similar to what I'd seen in Hotline Miami and DEADBOLT. These games share the same comfortable aesthetic, fantastic soundtrack, and story revolving an MC being forced to do their masters' bidding. Unfortunately, Katana ZERO plays nothing like those games, and this becomes clear rather quickly.

You start a new game. A short tutorial introduces you to the controls, then the phone rings to inform you of your next mission. You enter the level; after the MC puts their headphones in (this takes 7 seconds) you confirm you truly want the level to begin. You play through 4 screens of minimal action (each 20 seconds long), with an unnecessary Replay screen and an intermission in-between. A scripted cutscene kills your target and the mission ends.

You return to your apartment. You are given two options - drink tea, or go to sleep. The two options are at two polar opposites of the apartment, and 'walk-excruciatingly-slowly' mode is on, the one usually used in other pretentious, "story-rich" games. You have to choose ‘Yes’ for each option, despite the fact that the game won't let you go to bed without drinking tea and watching TV first. You go to sleep. An interactable dream sequence pops up, where you are required to continuously press LMB in order to continue playing.

You wake up. It's time to meet your employer. The sequence spawns you at the doorway, in 'walk-excruciatingly-slowly' mode, asking you to reach the armchair in order to start yet another sequence filled with dialogue options that all lead to the same conclusion. If it's not the employer, it's a bar that has you talking to random strangers, a casino that has you betting money multiple times before you can start your mission, cooking a meal for a child, searching for a toy, or speaking with a homeless person.

If this part of the review feels like it's dragging on, I'm managing to get my point across. This is what it feels like to play this game. I will never be able to recommend a game that willingly sacrifices the flow of gameplay for exposition. The first mission foreshadows each cutscene, each mindless interaction, each conversation the game forces on you throughout the next 10 missions and in the next three or so hours of gameplay. This is not the "breakneck action" the game’s store page seems to try to advertise.

There is conversation in-between missions, during missions, before boss fights, between boss phases, after bosses die, it simply never stops. This exposition is neither compelling nor engaging, quite the contrary - the conversations are awfully cringey, deliberately written using overformatted text to make the characters seem quirky and fun. This game, that is mostly based on its storyline, simply does not give you enough time to connect emotionally with the cast, or at least feel remorse for the actions you take.

The story seems to revolve around a veteran of some war fought using a drug that helps slow down time. This is because the fight only happens in the user's mind with the consumption of the drug, and allows to see the outcome of battle, as well as avoid real death. This is never explained throughout the game, and neither is the cause of said war, the nature of its opposing forces, the history and science behind the drug, the consequences of using it ("withdrawal"), and what the characters you encounter have to do with it all.

I still have no idea what happens in these continuous dream sequences, who the little girl you always interact with is supposed to represent, how the characters you kill are related to the distribution of the drug, what the Districts are and why Three doesn't allow children, what role the other agents you don't fully engage with (Fifteen, SNOW) play in this story. These hint at the making of a sequel, which is disappointing in and of itself, considering this 3-hour long, $15 title is not marketed as a multiple-part serie.

Immediately after the introductory mission, you are forced into a stealth minigame - where if you get detected by one of the four guards, you have to restart the entire mission. Four is apparently too many to handle at once, unlike the forty S.W.A.T. officers you mow down a few missions further down the line. These stealth mechanics are then never revisited again.

There are two engagement options - your katana, which has a generous cooldown on each of its strikes, and objects the devs deliberately place around each stage. These objects directly correspond to the amount of enemies the game wants you to take on at once, e.g. 4 objects for a group of 4 enemies, or a Molotov cocktail in front of explosive barrels leading up to an enemy - that way you don’t get too creative with your approach.

Throwing objects at enemies with guns, who have already spotted you, achieves very little. Your regular, machinegun-toting, half-drunk bouncer will laser-target the cleaver you threw at his face with US military strike drone precision. The second shot that soon follows targets you directly if you don't cut LoS, and if you're unlucky enough to have used your katana on a different enemy, or are in the air / just finished rolling, you simply have to die and restart.

Enemies are sprinkled around the stage in two-man squads, directly overlooking doorways, forcing you to use slow-mo. They don’t spawn in the same place each time, throwing any and all muscle-memory out of the window. Rooms are too small and narrow to maneuver around freely, and as your only option is to break doors down, you get detected instantly. It's enough to get parried once, or kill an enemy at the wrong time, for the katana to enter a cooldown and for you be sent back to the beginning of the stage.

There are no alternative ways to kill bad guys. No gadgets, guns, rating or point systems to unlock new equipment a la Mark of the Ninja. No new movesets or a choice between lethal / non-lethal options, like in Hotline Miami. Your katana is never fast enough to recover after striking, and enemy gunfire is unnaturally accurate. You don't even have the most basic of mechanics closing doors behind you or hooking onto ceilings / walls. It's a race between the enemy who detected you and you reaching them to strike them down first.

There are 4 enemy types in the game - goon, goon with a parry, goon with a gun, and riot shield S.W.A.T. officer who can only be engaged from behind. This, along with 2 turret types you find in about 3 different missions, will be the only challenge you encounter throughout the game. Worse yet, some sections are quite literally "on-rails", riding a motorcycle slashing bad guys or clearing a path for a trolley you're riding.

The best way to play Katana ZERO is through Speedrun Mode, checking all the boxes that remove the aforementioned annoyances - Skip Cutscenes, Skip Replay, Action Only, Stable Seed. But, by that point, you have already consumed the content the game had on offer and there’s no incentive to replay it. Hardmode further highlights everything wrong with this game; it increases the number of enemies per stage, completely stumping any and all progress as you struggle to dispatch enemies with efficiency and precision.

I can’t imagine how this game received 98% overall positive ratings on Steam. It's incredibly linear, with the story taking up almost 80% of clocked game time, and the story is not even that good to begin with. The combat system leaves a lot to be desired. You only "play" for about 40 minutes total, which is a joke. All in all a poor experience. Steer clear of this.\t\t\t\t\t
