Slashing Through the Shadows…
» Katana Zero is a blisteringly fast indie slash-em-up with action that is an absolute adrenaline rush. Taking more than a few cues from Hotline Miami, it is largely about a drug-addicted neo-noir-inspired samurai who sticks a bunch of needles in his body. It wears its inspirations on the sleeves of its kimono but refines the tried and true one-hit-kill formula in a manner that makes it feels fresh, exciting, and innovative in surprising ways.

Gameplay
» This game puts you in control of a nameless samurai assassin with the power to manipulate time and see into the future. In an extremely clever and fun framing of the action, every level is contextualized as being the planning of an assassination. So, instead of your character dying, you are told “No… That won’t work,” as your precognition rewinds to the start (complete with old-timey VCR effect), giving you a chance to find a solution that does not end in your death. Every level ends with a security camera recording of what “actually” happened, which serves not only as a way to see your moment of triumph played back but also to subvert expectations in interesting ways later on.

Working up to that moment is a blast because Katana Zero’s action is fast-paced and empoweringly flexible. Like Hotline Miami, it is one shot, one kill for both you and any enemy that is not a boss. Your character can slash in eight directions, slow down time, use a dodge roll to avoid bullets or reflect bullets back with his sword. There is a great flow and feel to your character’s movement thanks to small touches like how each sword swing gives you a slight boost of momentum, or how you can cancel out of any roll, and the way each kill gives a satisfying screen-shake and pause to the action.

However, the most iconic feature of this platformer is the slow-mo effect – specifically, how optional it is. It never feels too strong; it is on a pretty strict cooldown, and since enemies will hear whenever gunfire rings out, you will rarely have time to stay safe long enough for it to replenish. This puts it in a perfect spot as a powerful ability to get you through a tough situation while still being limited enough to keep the action quick.

Story
» The story of Katana Zero does not quite hit the high note that its action does, but it is at least carried by strong writing and a few great characters, such as your boss/therapist that guides you through your traumatic past while also providing you with the dossiers of people he wants dead, as well as a psychotic Russian adversary named V. It unfolds in a dystopian neo-noir setting, where you are entangled in a web of conspiracies and government experiments. Exploring deep themes of trauma, identity, and the consequences of one's actions, Katana Zero poses itself as a dark and mature tale, artfully woven into the fabric of the gameplay experience.

For instance, there is an innovative dialogue system that allows players to choose unique options based on their timing and interjections. By rudely shutting down conversations or patiently listening, players can unlock different dialogue paths, adding entertaining depth to the game. However, while these choices provide multiple playthrough opportunities, the story deviations feel superficial, and later dialogue options can be inconsistent with previous decisions. Additionally, the game’s narrative leaves much unresolved, hinting at a potential sequel or DLC, but the lack of payoff in this first chapter may leave players feeling unsatisfied.

While Katana Zero will not take more than four to six hours to complete, it is a very tight four to six hours that cuts out any fluff. It never feels like it is running out of ideas, and every level does a great job of introducing small twists to its simple formula, from an underground minecart level to a motorcycle chase sequence on the city highway.

Visuals
» It also helps that everything looks and sounds great. The slick neon aesthetic and fantastic sprite work go hand in hand with some legitimately impressive 2D lighting effects that give this game a unique visual style and personality. Accompanied by a fitting and catchy retro-electro soundtrack, the audio elements perfectly complement the intense action.

Conclusion
» All in all, Katana Zero’s time-bending samurai action is the star of the show here, but it's unique presentation and entertaining dialogue go a long way in supporting a plot that suffers from feeling like it is saving its best ideas for a sequel. As action games go, the game’s shorter length makes it a quick fix, and I’ll no doubt suffer withdrawal while I wait for the next hit.

Score: 8.7/10

The game's story and storytelling pleasantly surprised me, keeping me engrossed throughout, eager to see what the next mission held. It was the kind of game I finished in one sitting, later reflecting on the details I may have missed. The ability to shape the main character's path and personality was a standout aspect of the narrative.

The game mechanics and art design are commendable. Despite some portions being repetitive, it exceeded my expectations overall. I appreciated the challenging scenarios reminiscent of souls games, particularly the boss fights. However, I found the motocycle episode unnecessarily prolonged and somewhat out of place in the overall game experience.

The music, narrative, atmosphere, and storytelling are noteworthy. Given its price point, I would recommend this 4-star game to anyone.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTLDR: Lightning fast 2d slasher, cyberpunk themes, and a very high difficulty curve. It's worth the money.

Gameplay
Fast paced, and very flashy. You kill instantly, but can die instantly too, and in the hard mode speedrun, you're gonna die 500-1000 times at minimum. Thankfully it's portioned out appropriately so there's no worry of having to redo long sections.

Achievements
All achievements are fairly easily doable, although the method to get the keys was quite obscure and I needed a guide for that since there was no in-game indication any of these keys even existed. The 2 most notable achievements were the gold medal and the hard mode achievements. The gold medal was doable, but took quite a bit of practice and using guides on youtube to improve my time. The absolute hardest, obviously, was the hardmode achievement. Getting through this can take literal hours and requires insane amounts of skill to get through, especially towards the end. It's definitely a challenge, if you're up for it.

Story
Very intriguing story, it seems well integrated into the gameplay itself and peaks your interest by deliberately leaving many things unsaid. I do hope that more will come of this series, as I actually quite enjoyed the story as well as gameplay.

Hate
A small criticism might be the lack of spammability with the katana, however you just need to learn the game and get better. A nice addition would be to make each bullet parry not cooldown your slash, something I found to be an annoyance especially in hard mode. The devs did a good job, but they also need to produce much more content to fully sate the players hunger.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNever have I been more genuinely and instantly intrigued as I was when I first played this game. The trailer hyped this up perfectly, and the game delivered everything I was hoping for and much more. Very underappreciated for how amazing the story and gameplay is. While the combat is mediocre the game makes every level feel intense and heart-racing. Planning out how to complete a level based on enemy AI, your own abilities, etc. makes you feel intelligent and makes the gameplay phenomenal. That layer of having to think makes Katana Zero almost feel like a puzzle game. The quick decisions matched with the rougelike resetting once you die allows you to make educated decisions and try different approaches. When you die (Which I personally did a lot) you gain further knowledge and so can try again. Dying doesn't feel like as much of a loss as it does a new step in completing the level. Combine this with the difficulty of the game and a challenging but fun and genuinely amazing new experience is born. It's art style and animation flow perfectly with the gameplay, making everything feel real and stressful. Katana Zero's gameplay, while amazing, is only half of what makes this game worth buying however. Without spoiling the story, all I can say is that you will be instantly captivated and motivated to find out everything. What's great is: YOU can. The dialogue options genuinely feel realistic and allow us to put ourselves into the shoes of the MC flawlessly. Dialogue from NPC's and how they react/how the story progresses is entirely up to you. If you want to rush through everything, go ahead, but there will be ingame consequences. I was able to step into the shoes of our MC and genuinely connect with the story. Choosing dialogue options felt good and felt like a real part of the game. One of my personal favorite parts takes place when our main character is tied up to a chair without his sword (Trying to avoid spoilers so I won't say anything else.) The dialogue options in this section feel perfect. You know exactly the type of situation you're in, and that's reflected on your character. It is a perfect section. Throughout it, a smile grew on my face, that of smugness because I felt what the MC was feeling, it was like being totally in sync.
Overall, it was phenomenal. would recommend to literally everybody, and I'm 100% sure that the only people who wouldn't enjoy it are people who hate living.\t\t\t\t\t
