\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTLDR: YES. Play it. It's good.

Katana Zero is a 2019 indie stealth action platformer from Askiisoft, of Tower of Heaven fame. Yeah, that old gameboy-themed indie that got really popular in 2009.

It centers around two main mechanics: Time-stop and precision dodging/parrying.
Your moveset is basic; you run, you jump, you can slash in any direction (be it with a mouse or with an analog stick).

You have a dodge roll button that makes you briefly invulnerable to basically anything (with a few exceptions in hardmode), and can be cancelled into a slash if you need to attack immediately (although that removes your invulnerability as you're not rolling anymore).

You also have a recharging energy meter that's used to activate the timestop mechanic; as long as you keep it held (or as a toggle, you can change it from the settings), the whole game slows down to a crawl, as a sorta faux-bullet-time. You generally are not REQUIRED to use it, but it synergizes perfectly with the other mechanic, the parry.

ALMOST any projectile in the game (that isn't lasers or similar) can be parried with a well-timed slash. Depending on your inputs, it'll either go back to its sender or bounce off in whatever direction your slash was aimed, if you're aiming too far away from its shooter.
(the only catch is you can only parry one projectile per swing, so if you try to parry an entire shotgun blast you're gonna eat every other pellet except the single one you hit).

Not just that; parrying a bullet INSTANTLY removes your dodge roll cooldown, making you able to cancel that slash immediately into a roll... which can be cancelled back into ANOTHER slash, and so on. With proper timing (or with clever use of the timestop to make it more feasible) you can just ZOOM through hails of bullets, rolling through some and slashing others to give yourself another roll to go through even more of them.


At first you'll generally rely on the timestop to be able to time those things well, see when enemies shoot you and react accordingly by immediately bouncing their bullets back in their face.
But as you get used to it and master it... you can just STOP using it altogether. Every enemy type has a specific attack pattern that you can learn, and a specific timing to their reactions and firing. So the more you play, the more you'll get used to these enemies, and the snappier your runs through a room will be as you effortlessly roll and parry through everything that's thrown at you while seldom using the time stop when it's REALLY needed. It's IMMENSELY satisfying to master, if a bit obtuse to learn when you're just starting out.



Now putting that aside, Katana Zero is a pretty short game. It's a cheap indie, so yeah, it's kinda short. Took about 4 hours on my initial playthrough, without skipping anything, and a good bit of it was story and dialogue.
That said ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ is the story and dialogue here good. Almost every cutscene in the game gives you dialogue options, be it for flair, for potential humorous effect, or actually affecting the way other characters treat you in the long run.

To give an example (kinda spoilers but this happens literally in the second area you visit)...
There's a receptionist lady in one of the early levels that you can befriend depending on what you say, and she ends up not just helping you at the end of that stage, but also appears later on and can be talked to again, after she got fired from the hotel you slashed up and got a different receptionist job.
But if you antagonize her on your way in, she'll call the cops on you as you're on your way out, and your organization ends up taking her out as she's an unwanted witness. Then the later area just gets a different receptionist instead.

I know a lot of games love to brag about """your choices matter trust us they actually do for real this time""", but this game's length genuinely helped a lot in actually making that semi-feasible to do. There's unique secrets to be found depending on how you act and what you choose to do throughout the story, including (something super secret you can get at the very end that i won't spoil but it has to do with how your psychiatrist sessions go).

...The story DOES unfortunately end on a cliffhanger which left me desperately wanting more though, but apparently there's going to be a DLC (?) or something like that coming later to give it closure. Free for owners of the game so, sure, alright. It's definitely not an Asura's Wrath moment where they just make you pay extra to see the ending, and I appreciate that.



So yeah, you have a pretty short game, with a REALLY solid gameplay loop, and a really cool story that gets contextualized into the gameplay too (as every failed attempt of an area is just the protagonist "planning out his route" through and realizing that's not the way he should do things).

The visuals are REALLY good too; pixel art, yeah, which you've seen in a trillion indie games by this point, and the sprites aren't particularly big, but all the backgrounds and actual areas you walk through are RIDICULOUSLY detailed and atmospheric. From jumping through rainy rooftops at night, to climbing through a hotel while taking out an entire mafia's worth of goons, to the lit corridors of an abandoned slaughterhouse repurposed for... other things.
Almost all the areas stand out from each other from their theming and visuals, and the synthwave soundtrack also really helps set the mood.

Mind you, I didn't walk out of the 100%ed game actively REMEMBERING many songs, there's not a lot of melodies that I could really say had that... oomph? that makes game songs stick with me, but trust me, all the songs work really well in setting the dystopic-ass mood this game was going for. They work REALLY well in gameplay, it's just not something I would be particularly inclined to listen to outside of it.


Anyway, all that said. It's a good game, get it. It's short but it's worth the asking price.
MAAAYBE get it on sale if you're not convinced by the short length and potential lack of content (if you don't like finding hidden collectibles and secrets on replays of the story, or the speedrun mode), but either way it's worth a play.
Idunno if it's worth the 100% for everyone, getting that perfect speedrun in was a pain in the rear end, but it's worth AT LEAST a play.\t\t\t\t\t
