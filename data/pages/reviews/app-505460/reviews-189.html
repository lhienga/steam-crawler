\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFoxhole is simultaneously the source of one of my greatest experiences in gaming in the past 10 years, and a great game and great concept marred by several flaws.

Pulled by peer pressure into the Colonial faction during the last days of War 96, I began my Foxhole experience under heavy artillery fire, spawning in a town base with my fellow Collies. A few things caught my attention almost instantly. This was the first game where people were so willing to use their mics and speak. Men called out for fire support, enemy positions and movements, officers took charge and led desperate countercharges to blunt the Warden advance even as we lost trench after trench, pillbox after pillbox, with nothing but rifle, stickies and Bomas in hand. The war truly came alive for me then, dragged into a massive Dunkirk scenario where we were charged with holding the line as we evacuated the seaport of valuable supplies, and pulled out our vehicles to avoid a complete encirclement and loss. Foxhole gave me stories to tell my friends that I had never had before. It introduced me to its wonderful community of veterans that I had managed to integrate with, and fight alongside in hopeless charges, desperate defenses, daring raids and rousing victories.

Side note: I still remember the first ever armored action I took part in, a six hour tank graveyard just outside The Blemish as an armored car gunner, helping to keep anti-tank infantry off allied tanks. I remember most fondly the hospitality of the random SSgt that took me under his wing and commanded our small detachment of ACs and medium tanks to a resounding victory.

In a game where artillery pieces take two men minimum and three optimally to fire, where tank drivers require callouts from their commanders and gunners to function; vehicle crews and small squads function all at once. Things go smoothly or horribly wrong in a second- tanks can get swarmed, surrounded and ambushed. Infantrymen take two or three shots to kill, and bleeding mechanics keep Leeroy Jenkins-es at bay. You learn first to unlearn the FPS mentality of charge in, take the fight and damn the consequences, then learn again to seize opportunities, be daring and inspire your fellow comrades to turn the fight back in your favor.

I had the distinction and honor of fighting alongside remarkable men and women all around the world that I would not have had the chance to meet elsewhere, shoulder to shoulder rushing trenches with bayonets fixed, shouting commands in tanks as we took part in massive tank line battles, or simply chatting with as we brought logistics from the backline to a nearly cut off frontline base. There is a sense of solidarity and friendship that I have so rarely experienced anywhere else online with strangers that I found comforting. In the next wars or so I found my calling, as a frontline battle medic. I indulged my inner rambo at times, charging into fire to save downed teammates, picking up weapons off the corpses on the ground to make my last stands with my Collie brothers in arms. As a lesser-picked but valuable role, I never once felt like what I did was not impactful. No matter how small of a cog in the machine I was, I still felt vital, be it to the squad, or to the front that I fought with.

It's no surprise then that I got heavily invested into the game. During the next few wars, I started to get more serious with the game, learning advanced mechanics and the complicated internal politics of clan alliances, high commands that ran the show from behind the curtain, tagging along risky operations with bigger, heavier toys and staggering logistics requirements that took days to prepare. These operations were amazing, regardless of the outcome, but I also got to see some of the dark sides of Foxhole (that any other community would reasonably face).

Alting, toxicity, divisions along clan lines, my experiences were consistent with that of many other reviewers. Not only that, logistics and facilities are a drain to do. I remember at one point spending my entire free day farming up supplies and materials for the front, only to realize I would have to spend 10 hours a day, every day, if I wanted to play with the big toys that larger clans could muster. Until then, I could only hope to be lucky and join an open call for a battle tank if I wanted one, or forced my small band of friends to grind with me until we got a battle tank of our own.

Yes, one could say this is by design. You aren't meant to make the super heavy behemoths on your own and en masse spam them everywhere. If you did want to play in one of those big tanks you could always ask, that is true. This is where divisions and the aforementioned problems come into play though. Big clans won't give you a battle tank for free. You have to be trustworthy enough for them to let you have one. Yes, whereas crewing one doesn't really demand much in terms of credentials or credibility, but if said big clans don't operate in your timezone, you're out of luck unless you really band together people to do just that. I couldn't blame a big clan for guarding their prizes though, these expensive monsters are amazing to play, can turn the tide of losing fronts, but are also made with the blood sweat and tears of dozens of people across weeks. Losing all that to someone that turned out to be an alt, or a numbskull, it hurts. This isn't helped by the long, inane logistics systems. Take fuel production for instance. Nowadays, if you want to bring diesel in to power a facility, you would have to, as a solo player or a small clanman: get scrap with a truck or flatbed, refine into diesel, get a flatbed to transport a fuel container that needs to be filled at a refinery with your diesel and drive down to your facility, to get perhaps 2-3 hours of operation power depending on what you want to do. Following that, there are garrison supplies to queue to transport, basic materials to refine and transport, all of which requiring specialized equipment to efficiently haul their base components to refine, and specialized transport equipment like shipping crates and ships to bring to the designated location.

This massive investment is possible as a solo man, but rarely done- only highly dedicated players take on the task of producing and running their own facilities, producing their own specialized equipment, etc. You have to join a large clan to play with some of the tools of war then. Now there isn't anything inherently wrong with that, but these commitments stack, and ultimately drain you day by day, if you invest your heart into the war. Scrap gathering is calming, therapeutic, even. I loved hammering away and scrooping with my fellow logimen. But scrap gathering day after day to keep your clan's facilities running feels more like a second job than a game.

Given all this of course, you don't have to get into the logi game if you don't want to. You can choose to do whatever you want to do- though the tradeoff is that you may not get to play with ALL the things in the game that you want to. That doesn't take away from the fact that I love this game. It's a grind, as all MMOs tend to be, but it's fun, rewarding and the most unique experience I have had in a long time. Though, one job is quite enough for me, so I'm taking a temporary break from the war, and making a promise to not get so involved in the war when I do return.

The memories I had from this game are something else, as are the wonderful personalities I met while serving in the Colonial Legion and the Warden Army. I hope to return to the fold soon, now as a Second Lieutenant with enough experience to bumble my way through commanding a line or help my fellow comrades learn the game.

If you remember that this is just a game, and treat your fellow men with respect and patience, you will go far in Foxhole, and this game will be well worth the money and time you spend in it.
