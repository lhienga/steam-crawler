\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is stressful, with no guarantee of either a short-term or long-term payoff.

From my personal experience, the praises about this game are true: there are complex mechanics, requiring players to consider supplies to wage war as well as the limitations (and any gameplay-enabled advantages) on waging war. For example, just about any type of supply has to be manufactured, and that would mean harvesting raw resources to be eventually converted into supplies. For another example, there are border bases that can be claimed at the edges of enemy territories, if only to surmount the problem of getting supplies into those territories and forming a beachhead in the first place.

Unfortunately, a steep learning curve mixed with lousy quality of life designs means that the inexperience of players, players' lack of ability to learn, players' short attention spans and language barriers, can adversely affect any chance of deriving any satisfaction from this game.

For an example of inexperience players inconveniencing everyone else, an unwitting player can build a structure in a location that should be clear, such as the front of a resource mine. (The area in front of a resource mine has context-sensitive coding that allows players to place resource containers for easy retrieval of resources.)

For an example of players' lack of ability to learn, there are players who repeatedly send supplies to the frontmost and newest forward base - which also happens to be the most vulnerable to being overrun - regardless of how many supplies that it already has.

For an example of players' short attention spans, there are players who just leave vehicles around, as soon as something else catches their attention and they leave to redeploy elsewhere. ("Redeployment" in this game is a simple matter of despawning the player character and respawning it elsewhere.) These vehicles inconvenience other drivers at least, and at worst, they can be stolen by enemies that have sneaked past the frontlines.

For an example of language barriers, there are many people from different countries who are players on the same side; do not expect them to understand each other when they should for the sake of tactical coordination. This could be surmounted with skill built from experience, but until then, the barrier is an obstacle to instruct and teach each other.

Having said all that, there is now the matter of long-term gameplay in this game; the default game mode is "Conquest", a war that spans across multiple territories. Strategic decisions are necessary for either side to make any gains; these decisions won't come if players play alone and/or are incapable of strategic thinking.

For example, it can be very frustrating to watch another team fail to follow up on their otherwise successful thrust, e.g. failing to build a new base on top of an enemy base that has been cleared, even after its surrounding area has been secured.

If you have read up to here, chances are, you have read other reviews too. Some of these might mention the "toxicity" of the community of players in this game. This is unfortunately quite true, and it stems from all of the above.

Expect Western players to have a poor opinion of Eastern ones, and vice versa. Expect players to bring nationality bias into the game, and steal from other players of the same side to give to other players of the same side who happen to be of the same nationality. Expect experienced but bitterly burned-out players to treat new players poorly (e.g. suspecting them of being "alts", i.e. double agents currently playing with different accounts to sabotage the side that they are currently on).

In summary, if you are looking for a game that you can play with other people or against other people, this isn't it - unless you are somehow addicted to stress.\t\t\t\t\t

Imagine Running with Rifles, but as a cooperative MMO.
No money, just a common pool of resources, and a big scary bad guy. The game has two major factions: The Wardens, and the Colonials. Each faction has it's own tech tree that is unlocked slowly as the war progresses, with different battle tactics that their commanders can call upon. This makes the game feel very immersive, since you really feel like the enemy is another country entirely. Foxhole takes place in a "World War" style universe, primarily influenced by the second world war.

Every player is just another soldier
There's many ways to contribute to the war effort. You could grab a rifle and go shoot bad guys, or get in a tank with some friends, and shoot bad guys with a bigger gun. But the main pull of Foxhole is it's comprehensive logistics system. Every time you spawn, you use 1 Soldier Supply, and you'll probably grab a rifle, 2 magazines, and maybe bandages or grenades. All of those things need to be made and shipped to the front by other players, and that's just the absolute basics. Players also need to ship building supplies, maintenance supplies, artillery shells, even tanks and other armored vehicles. One of the most overlooked roles, however, is the role of the engineer. These guys are the guys digging trenches (PLEASE do not ONLY dig trenches at first, trenches by themselves are only any good in very specific situations), building bunkers, and even giant fortresses. Just grab some bmats, a hammer, a shovel, and ask where you're needed. 9 times out of 10, you'll find someone engineering.

TLDR: If you want to feel like a cog in a machine, this is THE GAME for you.\t\t\t\t\t
