\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is like many games rolled in to one-- it can be either an intense team battle, or a chill logistics simulator, or a combination of both. I find it relaxing to chill down with Truck Simulator sometimes, and Foxhole can be the same way. Except that when you haul a load of goods in Truck Simulator from Flagstaff to Las Vegas, there's no real point to it. But in Foxhole, you know you're bringing things to the front lines that they need.

Every shell, vehicle, gun, trench, barbed wire, uniform is made by people and brought to the front lines by people. A lot of interesting game mechanics are at play here. For example to shoot artillery, you need a couple of people operating the gun to move it, someone outside of the gun with binoculars to see where the enemy is and direct the shots, and someone to load the gun. You can sort of get away with doing it with 2-3 people instead of 4, and with multiple guns, you might be able to change the balance of who loads things for truly optimal efficiency.

It's also like factory and truck simulator game, in the back lines, as well as trains and freighters in the water. And it's also possible to infiltrate enemy lines and mess with their logistics. There are trucks, trains, factories, flatbeds, cranes.

One thing that surprised me is that things like boats, tanks, and planes are far more accessible to a new player than I expected. The challenge is learning and figuring out how to do it, which is fun in itself. For example, you might think as I did that something like building a tank is only for people who assemble in a large team and work on it for a long time. But in truth, you can build a low-end tank yourself in, say, 1 hour. But for me it took me days of learning and experimentation to figure out how to do that. The trick is don't try to gather the materials by hand; start by building something else that makes it easier to do the next step. Similar to how in Ark Survival Evolved building a stone house is a big ordeal, but if you get yourself a wood gatherer and a stone gatherer first it's not such a big deal.

Another thing that really struck me as unusual was the community. Driving a truck around the back lines, whenever we'd accidentally crash into each other due to high traffic, people would say "oops, sorry" via voice, which is always active by default. Once when I was in the training ground shooting artillery, there was another guy learning there, and after we missed a shot, he said "Hold on a sec. I need to do some math." These are not typical gamers.

Rank and promotions are solely a function of how many people click to give you a commendation. Each person is limited to commending someone else a certain number of times per day. It felt really great to figure out how to do medical and spend some time out there keeping our people going and discovering I'd been promoted at the end of that session.
