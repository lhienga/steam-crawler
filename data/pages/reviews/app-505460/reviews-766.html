\r\n\t\t\t\t\tYou: control a single soldier able to shoot weapons and mine scrap to build guns and ammo to shoot weapons and win the game

The Game: complicated tactics and emergent gameplay based upon player vs player interaction. Soldiers must maintain supply routes to active battle sites as well as not suck and lose them.

The way it plays: Like no other. Because the flow of battle is often resource based as much as it is skill based players must band together to make sure many needs are met, you as a player decide how to play but after 1000 + hours it's very clear what needs to be done, and are you willing to do it?

Because it's a persistent realtime battlefield this game suffers from low population to booms throughout periods of the day. A War may last several weeks but the map can radically change from day to day depending on how many people were active while you were offline. This is the main draw and the biggest downside in one. You are only a single soldier and can only do so much in a war that will continue whether you are online playing or not. On top of that the resource mining and logistical nature of supplying the front lines with much needed resources many twitchy gamers seeking instant gratification would find boring or unrewarding.

It's a game that demands an active playerbase 24/7, you can play for an hour but you won't truly appreciate the inns and outs of the game if you aren't actively participating in the war. Every day you login and hold your breathe to find out what that state of the game is in compared to where you left off. It's incredibly rewarding when things come together and very unrewarding to a person looking to make a serious contribution or unwind for just a 30 minute gaming session. For these reasons Foxhole won't ever be everyone's cup of tea. I'd compare it to the battlefield games or planetside 2 but with much more depth and a significantly smaller needier playerbase. The Dev's have been very active over the last two years adding content and fleshing out the game which is great, but many glitches remain, server lag and connection can sometimes be a bit sketchy, and the player base fluctuates dramatically for the mentioned reasons.

It's not for everyone, but you'd find it difficult to find a better in depth tactical war game that isn't just spawn and shoot 'em up.

2022 edit: since the last I played this game has been expanded on in so many ways making it a much a greater game in theory, but the player base has become uh... "roleplay centric" while it still has all the parts of what was once a great game, the game is not there anymore, I don't believe the majority of the player base is populated by real players anymore, and while I still think of it as a great work, I no longer recommend it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBefore recommending this game to anyone, I feel it is important to remind people that this is an early access game. That is clear on the label, but I feel that many people underestimate the importance of that fact on the game as long as it is playable. The developers are currently adding lots of features to the game which is good on the surface level, but since they so much left to add they're not taking the time to properly refine the player experience. Ordinarily, this would actually be the correct thing to do for an unfinished game, but for an early access game it means that the game isn't always in optimal playing condition.

Logistics is unequivocally the backbone of Foxhole. Since 98% of the game is based around player built structures, weapons and vehicles, it is essential that there is always a chain of players collecting and processing the materials to make those resources. Unfortunately, logistics is also the aspect of the game most affected by my preface. Back in June 2019, Clapfoot released a massive update that added resource scarcity, moved essential logistics buildings farther apart, and reduced the ratio of usable materials extractable from the resources. The full justification for why the developers made these changes would be too long for this review, but in short they hoped that the increased difficulty of soloing logistics would make managing the limited resources well as a team. These changes have been the most controversial in the game's development and the community's sub-reddit has been predominately logistics complaints ever since.

To solve the coordinate problems of the game, players organize themselves into clans. I've never been apart of a clan personally, but they are the main way of organizing plans of attack and other operations. Unfortunately the clans often emphasize the harmful in-group mentality that permeates many aspect of the game. There have been instances of clan members defending the actions of their clan mates (including but not limited to griefing, tking and racism), along with the creation of hit lists against members of other clans. On a similar note to community related criticisms, at the time of this review, there is a scandal surrounding the community manager alleged abuses of power in the discord and in game. As evidence by use of the word "alleged", I am choosing to reserve judgement whether his actions constitute abuse, but I encourage you to look into the controversy yourself and come to your own conclusions.

Despite its short comings, this is a truly unique game. Combat has a far slower deliberate pace. In no other game I've played can you and 4 other randoms desperately hold out on a bridge for 2 hours against a force better supplied and 3 times your size. You keep killing them, but with each charge they make over the bridge your defenses weaken. From the bridge, you are pushed back to the houses and as reinforcements finally arrive the two sides break into an intense block by block brawl. Instead of fighting, you could instead choose to play a medic and increase the life span of your units.

Logistics even in its incomplete state, can still be plenty fun to play in small doses. It has a relaxing aspect to it in the vain of Euro Truck Simulator or Stardew Valley, just doing your part through a set of small straightforward tasks like driving, picking up scrap metal, and digging holes in the ground. Not for everyone to be sure, but it can be nice to put on some music and drive around for a couple hours here and there. Just be sure to read chat and look at the map to know about ambushes.

The Developers are very open about their plans for the game, and host Q&A streams biweekly along with blog posts on the alternate weeks. They might not change direction based on community feedback or take their time when doing so, but it is clear that they truly care about the game and want it to be the best it can be. In no small part because of the transparency of the dev team, I believe that Foxhole will overcome its momentary shortcomings and become a great game. If this seems like your type of game, for only $20 you will probably be satisfied with your purchase.\t\t\t\t\t

The devs aren't afraid to experiment with new ideas in order to reach a better, more engaging result. Most of the negative reviews stem from a minority of frustrated (but extremely vocal) players who'd rather see the game stagnate into a top-down Unreal Tournament. Or players who don't realize that certain things need to be tested on a global scale (and not just in devbranch) to ensure they work well with the playerbase the game actually has. Don't listen to these people: in-game, you will find a plethora of people who enjoy working cooperatively. You'll also run into a few jerks, but don't let them discourage you from trying a very cool, very ambitious game that you probably dreamt would one day exist as a kid.\t\t\t\t\t
