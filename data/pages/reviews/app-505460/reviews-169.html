All I have said is what I recognise as a relatively new player, and unfortunately I don't think this would be the case if it weren't for having a great deal of assistance whilst learning, and whilst I continue to learn. I was lucky enough to have an experienced friend who ensured my experience was as good as it could be. From what I've both seen and heard from other new player however, a persistent problem is the labelling of newer players as alts, ruining their experience.

Another part of the problem is the lack of an effective way to deal with said alts when they appear. I feel these are part of the same problem, with experienced players too quick to label anyone acting differently as an alt or a griefer due to the abundance and repeat offences of alt accounts.

I enjoy this game, more so with every hour I spend playing it. The community is one of the most dedicated I've come across and there's a genuine sense of comradery from both teams. I love it and recommend it, my only fear being the current dilemma may not be healthy for its longevity.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEDIT: I won't be changing my review now that the game has been released in 1.0. Despite the addition of some new content, the core design and technical issues remain. The tutorial remains unfinished, and many of the mechanics are not explained in game and probably will never be.

TL;DR Foxhole is a very unique game, with some innovative ideas for an MMO. Unfortunately, the execution leaves much to be desired, specifically when it comes to game design, technical issues, and community management/moderation. Therefore, I can't recommend the game in its current state as a whole to the gaming community in general, although some gamers may wish to try it just to experience the unique, albeit flawed, experience the game has to offer.

Foxhole has been my love hate relationship for years. I was originally attracted by the promise of a large open world team game with strategic aspects. As a fan of RTS games, I knew I was most interested in the logisitics side of gameplay. Making strategic decisions about which supplies to make, where to send them, etc. can be extremely fun.

The problem is, the majority of the gameplay is NOT spent devising strategies and making moment-to-moment tactical choices. Rather, at least on the logistics side, 95% of your time will be spent in the most utterly uninspired, grindy gameplay to ever be included in a videogame.

For example, gathering resources, which is the baseline activity needed to create everything else in the game, consists of driving (very slowly) to an in game location, then holding down the mouse button to gather resources until your inventory is full, then driving back, rinse, repeat.

It is incredibly tedious, it does not offer any interesting challenge beyond a sheer test of patience, and it is so boring that virtually everyone who does this part of the game has to have something else - a YouTube video, or a podcast - going just to keep them going insane from boredom. When when of the core mechanics of your game is so boring that people can't even engage with it without distracting themselves with something else, you have a pretty serious game design issue.

For me personally, the grind is the most egregious issue in Foxhole; it permeates everything, and it is obviously a very intentional design choice by the devs, but I personally think it's a pretty bad design choice. Not only does it not respect the players' time, but it increasingly requires so much grind that many tools and mechanics are effectively out of reach for solo players. So the game has become dominated by clans, and while that's not necessarily the worst thing in the world, if you are a casual player like me that doesn't like taking orders in a videogame, you are going to find it difficult to experience everything this game has to offer.

There are two other major issues besides the glaring flaws of game design itself. The first is bugs. Although the game is reasonably stable, there are still many bugs left to iron out. And because the game is so time-demanding, and punishing, even an infrequent bug can make the game borderline unplayable. A common example is for a player to spend a lot of time grinding resources, turning them into supplies for the frontline, loading up a supply truck, only to encounter a bug when trying to cross a bridge, or move from one server to another, that causes them to lose said truck. Boom, hours of work just disappeared thanks to a bug. You just wasted precious moments of your life grinding without even getting to enjoy the fruits of your labor. When the game wastes your time so blatantly like that, it's hard to stay invested.

The final big issue is a lack of moderation. Although moderation is not totally absent in the game, there are no dedicated moderators. And the grindy nature of the game makes it ripe for griefing. If you're playing a match of COD, and a teammate kills you a few times, it's nbd, you can just kick them from the server and that's that. But if a griefer in Foxhole takes all your stuff and dumps it in the river, that's hours of work that you will never get back. This leaves an extremely sour taste in the mouths of players, and it's arguably the number one reason for recent dissatisfaction with the game.

Having said all that, you may be wondering, if the game is so bad, why did I put in 1,000+ hours? There are two answers to that. First, the game is so slow paced and grindy that even just giving it a chance means playing it much longer than other games. This is not a game that lends itself to casual 30-60 min play sessions. Logistics in particular demands pretty huge investments of time. So I have more hours on this game than on other games that I have enjoyed considerably on the whole - because those games don't waste my time and allow me to get what I want out of the game much more quickly.

But more fundamentally, there is a strong dichotomy in Foxhole that is the root of my love-hate relationship with it. There is no denying that the game is innovative. There are very few games it can be compared to, and I might go so far as to say Siege Camp (the devs) have almost created an entire new genre of game. Parts of that experience - delivering supplies to the right base at the critical moment to help the defenders hold of a wave of tanks, or coordinating a large armor assault across a whole region - offer a totally unique experience. And when everything is going right, it can be almost magical. Unfortunately, these magical moments are buried beneath an absolute mountain of grind and downright terrible design choices.

If the unique aspects of Foxhole so intrigue you that you must try it, and there's no other games that can sustain your interest right now, give it a shot. In some ways it is worth experiencing the very innovative ideas that the game is presenting. But be aware that in terms of execution, this is a pretty "bad" game. The mechanics are unpolished, and the visual design is adequate but uninspiring. The devs are not super competent, nor super professional, and they don't communicate well with the community. They refuse to add microtransactions to the game, and while many may see this as a plus, I believe that for an online game it makes it harder to sustain and improve the experience in the long run, since you don't have ongoing revenue to support the game. For all of these reason, I can't recommend the game in its current state to most gamers.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFoxhole is a very unique and special game. Blending elements of shooters, MMOs, and other strategy based games into title that has no comparison. Foxhole has so many different types of game play to offer that it is nearly staggering. From participating in massive tank battles, spending days perfecting base designs, to sneaking around enemy territory, foxhole has so much to master. As such it is no surprise that myself and many others have sunk countless hours into this game and its community.

Many will point to the games community and developers as a point of criticism, and while much of that is valid, every large community will have bad actors. Ultimately, foxhole has allowed me to create friendships that have lasted for years and I wouldn't trade any of my time playing this game for any other. Like any game certain mechanics are lacking, back line logistics remain complex and arduous, but none of those activities are requiered. I can always hop on foxhole knowing that if I want a simple hour of fun I can go to a front and play infantry, or if I want to get into the weeds of more complex strategy than I can rely on my clan to provide fun and engaging operations, one of many clans present within each foxhole faction. I have not spent more time playing any game more than I have with Foxhole and I dearly recommend that others try it out.

Joinsom.com and stay foxy!\t\t\t\t\t
