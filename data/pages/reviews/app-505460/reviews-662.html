\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIs this game any good?

The short answer is yes. The long answer is more complicated.

Is the community any good?

The short answer is yes. The long answer is more complicated.


Overview

Foxhole is a fairly unique animal when it comes to online games in general. It fills a very specific niche, and it plays like it. Every day, hundreds of players, organized into two different teams, fight over one massive map instance for guts, glory, and the precious resources that keep their war machine going. However, unlike a game like Planetside 2, every bullet that is fired, every gun that fires those bullets, every bunker that bullet is fired from, and every hammer used to build that bunker is painstakingly produced by a player. Combat is very much a war of attrition, and communication with allies is essential. "Good" players are determined, not by an abstract "kill" counter, but by how much of the enemy's crap they can destroy before going down. Truthfully, Impressive performances on the battlefield may never be remembered or noticed by your allies, but when it is, it feels like you've really achieved something. Which reminds me....


Progression

There are only two forms of progression. The first is pretty straightforward: commendations. You do something impressive, your allies can commend you. If they do, you will get a rank next to your name to lord over other players. It doesn't do anything intrinsically, but it gives you bragging rights, which is cool. Often, you can use this proof of bragging rights to coordinate players beneath you, which is equally cool. The second form of progression is research, and ISHA'S TITS IT IS A GRIND. Research unlocks improved equipment for every member of your team on the server, and acts as an enormous force multiplier for hundreds of players. Unsurprisingly, the grind is scaled to match its power. Rare iron and aluminum drops must be mined from finite scrapyards scattered around the map. Each scrapyard realistically has about 100-250 of these resources, and it can take upwards of 600 for decent researches. Even then, the research must be done in a specific order, or risk failing. IF your research succeeds, you will then get a prototype kit, which can be used to construct a prototype vehicle. These are often astronomically expensive to construct, and require rare "components" to construct, instead of the ubiquitous "scrap". Once the prototype is constructed, your team will receive a massive power spike as the recipe unlocks for everyone. "But how will I get this shiny new tech?" I hear you asking. welllll

Logistics

Logistics gameplay is the backbone of Foxhole. For every hundred soldiers on the front line, there are dozens of players (known as logis) mining and refining raw materials, building those materials into usable supplies, and transporting them to the battlefield (often through enemy fire) in a variety of different utility vehicles. I was honestly surprised by how much fun running logi could be. I'm not normally one for playing a trucking sim, or mining asteroids in EVE online, but something about producing and transporting desperately needed supplies to my buddies in pseudo WWII appeals to me. Maybe it's just the feeling of contributing to the team, and knowing that your grinding could be the difference between victory and defeat for hundred of other players. That said, if this aspect of of the game doesn't appeal to you, don't worry about it too much. In the end, Talented soldiers and tankers are the players that win battles, and logis are the ones that win wars.

Base-building

Another essential aspect of Foxhole is base-building. In practice, it is best comparable to the survival game Rust, with just a smattering of Steel Division thrown in for good measure. Bases are used as both defensive and offensive tools. Trenches can be dug pretty much anywhere with nothing more that a shovel, but they will eventually decay if not connected to a properly supplied base. Bases are built by construction vehicles, and supplied by logi trucks. With enough resources and players, a base can even get an AI garrison to help guard it when the owner is offline.


Combat

I'll be honest, I'm no connoisseur of top-down shooters. The only one I can really claim to have played extensively is Helldivers, so keep that in mind as I describe the coordinated confusion that is Foxhole combat. The game has a detailed "fog of war" system which ensures that you only see enemies on screen when your character can "see" them. About to round a corner in a trench? There might be an enemy on the other side, and you won't know until you take a peek. Don't worry, you won't die just taking a quick look (probably). The only thing less accurate than the enemy's description of your mother is the rifle they're shooting at you as they yell it. Most weapons in foxhole struggle to hit targets when firing in the heat of the moment. If you want to take down a running enemy, you're either going to have to either stand still and shoot a lot, or get lucky. Learning to maneuver in Foxhole under combat conditions is a real challenge. Movement seems slow and laborious, and it gets even slower as you take on heavier equipment. You have a stamina bar that depletes as you sprint (or walk when you're encumbered) and managing that can be as important as your ammunition. Fighting feels realistic (relatively speaking) but I'm still not sure whether it feels good.

All-in-all I've found combat in vehicles to be the most fun. All the tanks I've used have spots for three crew members: a driver, a gunner, and a commander. Communication is essential. The commander has a pair of binoculars that let him see much farther than either the driver or gunner. This means he is able to direct the gunner to targets, even if they are BVR, and tell the driver to GTFO if enemy anti tank weapons get a bead on you. Land mines are rampant, and can only be spotted and disabled by infantry, so combined-arms operations are a must. Tank v Tank operations are intense, with both coordinating to try to outmaneuver and outgun each other. It's a lot of fun, even if grinding to get the tank in the first place is a chore without help from other players.

Other Players

I was actually pleasantly surprised by the community here. That said, I hope you speak français, because a large percentage of the playerbase is French. Thankfully, most of them speak English too. As with any game that requires team coordination with randoms, you need to have a fairly thick skin. ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ aren't nearly as plentiful as I initially anticipated, but they are certainly present. I also recommend buying the game along with a friend. The gameplay can be dull at times, and playing with a buddy can really spice things up. Likewise, don't be afraid to talk to other players. I've found many of the fanbase to be genuinely fun to be around, and there will often be a veteran willing to help if you have no idea how to do something.


Summary

TLDR: I recommend this game to anyone looking for an economy sim along the lines of the original EVE or Rust, as well as those looking for an in-depth coordination based game along the lines of Swat 4 or the original Planetside.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI find myself hesitant to write a review for Foxhole not because of any faults it may have, but because I usually do not write reviews. It has grown on me quickly, and I hope to truly capture its value in words. Foxhole is not your typical war game. You might think it is like any other shooter where you spawn somewhere and immediately get into the action with some pre-set class or kit you have saved. No, it isn't like that. The scope of this game is unlike most or any others; the way hundreds, or thousands of players come together to achieve common goals is simply awesome.

Chances are you will first spawn somewhere completely lost, hoping for someone to help you navigate the seemingly vast and unknown land ahead of you. As you walk down a road, the distant sounds of artillery fire and destruction pique your interest. Just what IS happening on this battlefield? That's when you chance upon a friendly truck—logistics has arrived, taking desperately needed supplies to the nearby front. The driver offers you a lift, and you accept. Suddenly you see the world from a new angle: trees, bunkers, trenches and the like pass by almost in a blur as the big truck surges forward. Once you are at the forward operating base you are advised to pick up a rifle, and a couple clips of ammunition. You put on your brave face, follow your fellow soldiers into battle, even score a kill or two before your life ends in a graphic and tragic tale of explosions and ill-fated attempts at medical recovery.

Thus begins your journey into the world of Foxhole, where every logi truckload delivered and every pillbox built puts your faction one step closer to victory... or perhaps defeat. Whatever the case may be, every one of your brothers are there with you until the bittersweet end.

Get immersed already in this alternate-reality set in a distant future, where technology and perpetual war not unlike that of the World Wars reigns forever supreme! For the price, it is worth it and more.\t\t\t\t\t
