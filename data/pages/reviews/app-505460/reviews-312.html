\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is the first review I've done for a game on here before. I normally am not into writing reviews such as this, but I feel so strongly about Foxhole that I felt it would be a disservice not to. I believe strongly if there is a community, or game, or whatever it may be that is truly doing something great and that you believe it, to support it and be outspoken. As such, I truly think Foxhole is a great game and that the developers are working their best to make the best, most fun, and most exciting game and experience as possible.

Foxhole is a persistent war game. A "MMORTS" as I've heard it called at times. You play one soldier in a war machine of millions. Every choice you make affects the war, and the world of which we play in. Whether it be making some shirts for our soldiers to wear, going behind enemy lines to sabotage them, or just being a basic infantryman. Everything you do works towards fighting this ongoing war.
If you are someone who enjoys isometric games, simulation games such as ATS, or RTS's then most likely this game is for you.

Foxhole offers a game experience that thus far no other game has offered. A live action, timely, persistent war. And I cannot recommend it more!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI was a strapping young lad back then, before the war. Or so I was told. I had a decent job at the local haberdashery, which while far from glamorous was secure and useful. I had few problems with my family, my two sisters and mother and father. In just a few short years that youthful sense of stability and promise, that peace, came to look more and more silly to me.

When I arrived home after my first tour of duty my mother screamed for me to get out of her house. Dutifully, like any soldier, I began to obey before she finally recognized my face and bid me back inside. I had become gaunt in my time at the front, and calloused. Scars covered my body like cracks in old concrete. I no longer laughed, for there was nothing I felt worthy of it. The jokes of old friends and my sisters annoyed me for their frivolousness. In time I took to drink, wasting away hours in one dreary speakeasy after another. In what, in retrospect, seemed like an instant I had been cast out of my parents house, banned from the premises of the local church, and lost my profitable haberdashery employment. So beholden to the demon drink was I that I the details of many of these and other occurrences remain forever lost to me, a small mercy given the pain of the things I DO recollect. Besides, a story of sullen and cruel solitude is one without much real plot. There is instead no melody or rhythm in the foul music of men's demons. Only the noise.

It was until I saw him that day, in a portside establishment of ill repute named "The Scabby Strumpet". Through a haze of poor quality swill I gazed upon his face, clear as day. Sgt. Edward Dongle in the flesh. Same lazy eye and hideous scowl.
Oh that scowl, that sneering and violent look. The same one he gave us as he lead us into one massacre after another. One wave of gore and mutilated bodies to the next until finally one more foot of land could be stood upon by us and claimed until we were so horribly driven back to whence we came, mangled and bruised to the level of near death and blind from the stings of the gas!

And that bridge....that ungodly bridge. He volunteered us, he wanted us to go there. He wanted to be the first across, everybody knew it. Wanted the medals, the recognition, the sheer power of others dying for his own glory. My god! Why did none of us kill him when we had the chance? We accidentally killed our own all the time, none questioned it. But why not him? Why let him destroy what was left our humanity?

On that bridge, pilled high, thousands ran into the fire. A mountain of rotting chop meat stood in the broken pit in the center, growing ever larger and more minced as machine gun fire strafed back and forth across the space day and night.

30 days. 30 real world days!\t\t\t\t\t
