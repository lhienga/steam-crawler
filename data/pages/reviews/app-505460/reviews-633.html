\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI bought Foxhole on the 21st of March 2018, and I've always found the game to be enjoyable and ingeniously unique. Siege Camp, the group of indie developers that have worked on this game for nearly half a decade, has continuously worked on this game, bringing out new edition after edition, showcasing wonderful armoured vehicles, firearms, gameplay mechanics, visual effects, and a ton of other interesting additions.

When it comes to shooters, particularly the stagnative nature of triple-A games where the main objective is wildly shooting and running around under a constant barrage of flashy colours, Foxhole stands out as a game with a very new and unexplored concept, basing itself on a period of warfare anywhere between WW1 and WW2, doing so before other, larger, game designers chose to do so (Battlefield 1, Call of Duty WW2, et cetera). Before I get into my favourite bits about this game, I'd like to say that I'm glad to have bought this game when I did, and I'm proud to have supported an indie developer like Siege Camp in an age dominated by big fish.

Now, regarding the game: I could get into the way warfare is dynamic and invigorating, with every firefight being reliant on a player's natural environment, their skill at arms, awareness, instinct, and, most critically of all, other players guarding their backs. I could talk about how this game is so multi-faceted, that any player can choose a manner of options; he could coordinate an attack with his mates, fight in the trenches, demolish defences in a heavy tank, or produce and transport ammunition to the frontlines, so that his brothers-in-arms have the means to fend off the enemy. I could mention that this game's graphics are wonderful, that the developers actually wrote and constantly contribute to in-world lore, that Foxhole has a vast and active player base, and that the game never ceases to excite me.

However, the single greatest selling point of Foxhole to me is the community.

In no other game are players so reliant upon one another. Teamwork is an absolute necessity, on the frontline or in the factories. I've found that Foxhole lets me connect with people from all over the globe, allowing me to pick up parts of their opinions, their culture, and, at times, even their language. When a large group of French players picked up Foxhole earlier this year, it worked wonders on my French, as communication and verbal back-and-forth is critical to the nature of this game. I'm not saying that you'll never be able to fluently speak any language until you buy and play this game, but I do believe that games like these can have a profound impact on us, and I would certainly recommend practicing another language by chatting with other people who actually speak the language, in a manner that is fun and productive, as opposed to relying solely on a dusty textbook.

Before I end this review, I should clarify that this game isn't about languages necessarily, but it's a detail that I've found whilst playing the game, and one that isn't discussed frequently. In truth, the game is about communication moreso than language barriers, and that also means that, if you're fundamentally opposed to cooperation and communication with other people over video games, then Foxhole isn't for you.

However, if you struggle with communicating and speaking to strangers, This game might help you get more confident and willing to talk to others, because the community is generally tame, and offers up a safe and controlled environment for people from all walks of life.

In short: I staunchly believe that Foxhole is a game too special and great to leave hanging. Thus, I'd heartily recommend this game to my friends.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tStarted playing after the Winter Army update. Game has a unique persistent war concept where two sides battle it out, and everything is player built; the grind in the game is farming/crafting/transporting war supplies and materials to support the front lines. Overall I did enjoy my time playing, but there are major issues plaguing the game. Players get very tribal for their team, and the experienced players have 1000s of hours vs the average player with approx 30ish hours. Newer players can accidentally grief somewhat easily, this leads to a lot of bad blood and toxicity. Im probably done playing this game, and had a lot of fun. A few of the major gameplay loops are rough around the edges, and I am not satisfied with the attention the devs give to those areas vs adding new stuff.

Pro:
* Unique and fun game concept
* Thrives with organized groups
* The look, sound, and gameplay is mostly tight and feels good.
* Immersive

Con:
* Small dev team
* Devs focus on adding new guns/vehicles, leads to power creep, feels like devs do not play their own game enough with decisions they make.
* Balancing is BAD. Each team has different gear, but generally one side has a few superior tools that make the game unfun. Balance in Foxhole is switching which side has better tools.
* Game was not designed with the increased player count, queues are always high in the most important/active/fun areas.
* Voice chat within the game is awful. Finding clans within the game/official discord channel is awful.
* Learning curve is not suited to the average player
Players use alternative accounts to grief, devs have failed in creating anti griefing mesures.
