\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm updating my review from a week ago because somewhere between hour 10 and hour 40, the genius of this game really clicked for me.

My reviews are notoriously "stream of consciousness / ramble" style, so bear with me:

This game is truly one of a kind. It somehow manages to scratch the itch for both community-based MMO gameplay, classic solo RTS gameplay (i.e. Command and Conquer series), and even match-based shooter FPS gameplay (i.e. Battlefield) at the same time, and it marries them in a really novel way that feels completely organic and enjoyable.

It feels like the modernization of the RTS genre honestly, doing away with rinse and repeat formulas for base construction and replacing it with a living, breathing war environment that involves all the same core aspects you love about RTS games: resource harvesting/refining, munitions creation/stockpiling, logistics and planning, combat that feels intentional and strategic, and also with the grittiness of a TPS/FPS shooter (since you have control over a single soldier you actually care about).

But here's the genius of it all- you can participate in any (or all) of the tasks that interest you. You're not forced to do anything that doesn't appeal to you. Want to build fortifications and trenches? That can be your shtick. Want to focus on scrap hauling and munitions stockpiling? That can be your shtick. Want to be the guy hauling those stockpiles from the backline to the front lines where artillery is raining down and the soil is soaked in blood? That can be your shtick. Want to be a dedicated grunt, holding the line every time you play? That can be your shtick. Want to do it all? Do it all.

In games like Assault Squad 2 (which I love) you DO have a lot of these same options, but because the burden of controlling your entire team rests on your shoulders, it can feel incredibly overwhelming and micro-managey when battles are unfolding so quickly. Instead, Foxhole places you in the boots of one soldier, and you're forced to think and act the way a real soldier would: like a single cog in the wheel. And while this may imply that you never feel useful or like you make any difference on the outcome, that couldn't be more wrong. Because of the incredible community this game has, you're able to communicate with every division of your faction when you log in, and understand what is needed and WHY it's needed, adding an incredible sense of purpose to every task you take on. And on top of that, your peers are able to give you commendations for your actions which affect your rank as well as your communcations rating, which add a ton of value to your time spent working on different tasks.

I know it sounds like I'm just mindlessly praising this game and calling it perfect, but it truly is one of a kind. Because of that, it's so much more than the sum of its parts, and the small technical or design gripes are vastly overshadowed by the strengths within the overall experience. Once you get the hang of it, join a squad and communicate, and contribute to the war effort (and hopefully get to experience the feeling of a victory after all your hard work), there's just nothing else like it. Not to mention that even when you're dying and getting pummeled...it actually feels cool and fun somehow.

One other point of praise I have for this game and how special it is is the sense of "grandeur" it has. Our last war just ended on day 30 (yes, real life days). That's how long these campaigns can take to resolve. And it was a crazy tug of war back and forth until the final days, making every day feel important.

To wrap up this review I want to give a rambly but detailed example of one of my experiences in Foxhole this past week:

I logged in in the middle of the day and my squad lead let me know that we were prepping for a big artillery barrage and push up the western front later that evening and that any munitions and supplies were going to be incredibly helpful in this home stretch assault. So I decided to spend my afternoon harvesting salvage, taking it to the refinery, then on to the factory to create a variety of basic materials (used for construction and tank repairs), small arms and ammunition, medical supplies, and soldier supplies (the game's version of "tickets" which allow for respawns at the base where they're stocked). After a couple hours of work my work order was finished and I took the supplies on my supply truck to the nearby seaport to load them into a couple large shipping containers using the seaport crane. I then built a freighter ship and docked at port, loading up my containers as well as a few transport vehicles for the evening's push. After I was all loaded up I departed from port and took the freighter up river a few miles to the closest western front seaport and off loaded all of the supplies and vehicles for us to use later. I was bummed when I heard that we may win the war any minute, thinking all my hard work may have been for nothing, so I left the supplies at port and joined the boys nearby to watch the conclusion. To my surprise...it didn't end. The enemy pushed back so hard in their final hours that we ended up incredibly overpowered and unprepared. After a couple hours of brutal fighting on the front, we were out of supplies and needed, more than anything, solider supplies (for respawn tickets) and basic materials (for construction and tank repairs), and our logistics chain had been completely severed by partisans during the last hour of fighting, so we were choked out and didn't know what to do. So when I told my squad lead that we still had two containers at port PACKED with supplies, we made it our priority to get those containers up to our FOB to continue the fight. We made it down to port and back up to the front with a tank escort, and a friendly soldier near the port volunteered to follow us back up to the front with a crane (to unload the containers once we got there). We resupplied, reasserted our push at the front, and won the war just hours later. Wow. What a day that was.

TL;DR- this is something truly special for those with any interest in RTS or MMO style games. Amazing work on this one devs. I'm so excited to be part of this community and can't wait to see all the future additions.

Whichever side you choose (*cough* Collies *cough*)- enjoy the fight and good luck out there. See you in the trenches. x\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA wordy, detailed review with a TL;DWTR (Too long, don't want to read) just below this.

Pros: Fun concept, diverse language-based clans so no one is left out (Euroasian, Latin, or English), constant updates and tweeks from devs.

Meh?: Isometric shooter with cover-based system. (Detailed under a mini-rant under section "AIMING" below). "Isometric balancing" meta.

Cons: Increasingly hostile community as of Late August/Early September 2021, exploitable mechanics, questionable commendation and reporting system that can be exploited.

==================
Foxhole is an isometric massly online real-time strategy shooter where you, the individual, join one of two factions and fight the other until a set of global objective are completed and victory is achieved. Themed for some era between WW1 and WW2, Foxhole is placed in a fictional world locked in a constant state of bloody, seeming endless war. Teamwork makes the dream work in this game, and learning to do a little bit of everything can help your side in the long-run by leaps and bounds. However, learning everything there is to do in Foxhole is easier said than done, and some things can be utterly frustrating.

Currently, as of this review, there are three main "assault groups" that I can describe as "naval", "armored", and "infantry". As stated above, Foxhole experiments with not giving everyone the same thing- just like in the real world, the Allies and the Axis didn't both use Tiger tanks just with a different coat of paint. While some assets such as gunboats and field guns are essentially the same in function, they will vary in design. However, armored vehicles and infantry weapons are COMPLETELY different from what you can manufacture yourself. As said, the powers of our world didn't share their technology willingly with one-another and share their manufacturing techniques to both produce the same guns; unless, of course, they purchased it from a third party...

Colonials are describe in theory as conquesting "liberators" that seek to expand their domain and bring "freedom" to their neighbors. They are describe as having fast and rapid approach both in tactics and method, their weapons favoring quick "aiming" and fire rate at the cost of firepower.

Wardens are Northern-born winter-hardened "zealots" who collective worship a deceased God General named Callahan, and are a new and quickly growing superpower of the "modern" world. Forged in cold steel and ice, they are describe as heavily defensive, methodical, and slow-paced fighters, their weapons adopting the same methodology of "hit once, kill one".

Or, supposedly.

As the game has progressed and developed, most conflicts commonly came down to what I can only metaphorically describe as throwing eggs at the flank of a superheavy tank. It's messy, it's getting everywhere, but no one is going nowhere. "Isometric balancing" was a term popularized by the community as a way of describing the non-semetric approach to "balancing the game". Colonials get fast moving tanks? Wardens get slow-moving, hard-hitting tanks. Colonials get infantry based satchel charges great at blowing up structures? Wardens get a moving 250mm shell-spitting bunker-buster. Collies get a revolver thats slow and powerful, Wardens get a fast-shooting mauser-style pistol.

It's give, and it's take, and it sometimes leans in one's favor more than the others in terms of "meta".
But teamwork and communication still plays a critical role in all of the game- NOTHING CAN BE BUILT WITHOUT LOGISTICAL PLAYERS.

Logistical players are people who go to "scrap fields". They harvest scrap, they refine it at refineries, they make your guns, bullets, bandages, plasma, etc all in the same factory, worryingly enough. They supply your respawn point with soldier supplies, or "shirts" so you can respawn. The gun you hold was made by someone. The car you drive was made by someone. The trench you're in, the bunker you're running toward, the shell you just got blown up by, was all made, fired, and destroyed by players. And one of those players could be YOU.

But, now we enter the hazy territory of technology, which has been causing an uproar in the community and a massive amount of paranoia in the came from something called "alting", more on that below.

Logistical players, through their scrapping, will occasionally fine "rare resources" such as iron components or aluminium components, which are used at engineering hubs to research new and better tanks/guns/face masks/SHOVELS/toilet paper. However, some groups/clans can't agree on what to research, and sometimes resort to hoarding these supplies in isolated parts of the map so they can rapid-research technology. Or, even worse, someone will use an alternate account to join the opposing faction, steal from their team, and deliver it to their "real faction". These are the actions of remarkably few (and incredibly ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ idiotic, lowlife, spineless) degenerates, and they sometimes load up trucks and drive them into oceans to destroy masses of supplies or just outright drive it to the other side. The end effect of these actions have caused MASSED HYSTERIA between both sides, new players (meaning you, potential recruit/purchaser) being subjected to many forms of harassment and swift/heavy-handed action that include mass-reporting for temp-banning a player, being team-killed by them, or being verbally berated.

These actions can also come from something as simple as building a trench in the wrong location, carrying too many supplies, standing too close to a vehicle "you don't own", breathing the wrong f***ing way- it's gotten out of hand, and I heavily recommend you quickly find one of the many, MANY people who are more than happy to teach you the game and stick STEADFAST beside them until you're a little more well-known from your daily playerbase.

Now...
====AIMING====
This is, by far, the hardest thing for many new people to grasp when coming to this game. Understand that the map is not flat, terrain comes into play in more ways than "looking pretty". There are walls, benches, holes, divots, trenches, houses, hills, MOUNTAINS to stand on, behind, peek from the corner of, or crawl under. Depending on how well you're covered, you'll have a small shield pop up that tells you just how much of your character is covered.

Let's say Bob and Pete are standing on a flat field. They both have a crouch-level sandbag wall before them. Bob kneels, and gets a full "shield". Pete does not, and he gets none. If Bob aims and shoots toward Pete, its almost a 100% shot if you line up a white line and put the X on top of your target.
Now, if Pete kneels, he ALSO gets behind cover with a "shield". If Bob fires at Pete enough, the shield will slowly turn red from "suppressive fire" which, in theory, should "break his cover" and begin hitting him at one point, keeping players from sitting behind some indestructible wall.

That is, of course, theory.
Not all cover is the same. Remember that the shield is just there to say "yah, like THIS MUCH of you is covered from THE DIRECTION YOU'RE LOOKING". Your back, your flanks, and potentially a portion of your front can still be shot. The cover mechanic has been worked on time and again, and it still has issues.
And that's all ON A FLAT FIELD.

When elevation comes into play, the shield can be an outright damn LIE. Channel your inner Obiwan Kenobi, because having the highground is KING in this game as of this review. So long as you're not getting shelled out of your spot, you're relatively untouchable.\t\t\t\t\t
