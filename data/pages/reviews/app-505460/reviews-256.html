\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWith the Battlefield franchise dead and Farm51s WW3 being janky and unfinished, Foxhole is as good as it gets in the "all-out warfare sandbox shooter" department.

This is the ultimate definition of a "sandbox shooter". Wanna clear trenches and mow down waves of enemies with a gun emplacement? Do it! Just wanna be part of the supply lines and deliver stuff in a truck? Go right ahead! Wanna sneak into enemy territory and disrupt THEIR supply lines? You can do that! And no matter what you decide to do, you can feel yourself contributing to your team's victory.

The battlefields of this game are immersive as heck! The top-down camera view really gives you a sense of scale as you are surrounded on all sides by fellow soldiers, tanks, and artillery pieces. The movement/aiming mechanics in this game take some getting used to, but I got the hang of it after a couple hours of playing.

Finally, the in-game community is wonderful. Everyone is very kind and respectful, and veterans of the game are always happy to help newer players learn some of the less intuitive mechanics. You will be treated how you treat your team.

I can't recommend this game enough! It's everything I've been looking for in a game since the fall of Battlefield. It's a must-try game!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI love this game and in terms of being a MMO team-based game it might be the very best out there. Putting hundreds, even thousands of players to work cooperatively toward a common goal is no mean feat, and this has meant the formation of organic culture in the two factions and rewarding gameplay for good leadership. There are of course problems with in-fighting and suspicion, but to avoid it one can join the central faction Discord (Warden Unity Hub for Wardens and Sigil for Colonials) to seek arbitration by higher-ups. Foxhole is one of the few games to offer compelling artillery gameplay as well as logistics, tanking, infantry and construction. There is a niche you will find if you play it, and all this means is that prior to War 96 Foxhole was one of my all-time favorite games, and I recommended it to all my friends. However, for two major reasons I cannot in good conscience recommend Foxhole, and am in fact advocating a boycott by existing players. Tl;dr, they are massive game-breaking bugs that ruined the experience of hundreds of players.

Let me address the elephant in the room, going by other steam reviews. Because of the community-driven gameplay, there is bound to be abuse by bad actors and there is not much the developers can do about it. Though they could hire moderators to monitor the servers live, I would rather they not do this since, firstly, this takes money away from development, and secondly, world chat is much more fun being unmoderated. The solution implemented instead, vote bans and community reports, is very difficult to balance. Too strict, and it becomes very easy for a coordinated group of players to unfairly ban someone they have a personal grudge with. Too lenient, then if an alt or griefer runs amock destroying valuable equipment it's very difficult to stop that player. The only change I can recommend is that there be a zero-tolerance rule toward alt accounts, permanently banning not only the alt account but the alt-user's main account as well. Although these destructive players are a serious issue, the massive coordination of players identifies bad actors fairly well and keeps them under control.

Now onto game-breaking bugs. These are far more serious because they involve not players but deliberate action by the developers themselves:

1) Failure to balance the new railway gun, the Tempest Cannon, in a timely manner. This railway gun was debuted in the official release of the game, the 1.0 update. There were fewer than thirty days available to test the entire, vast update on the developer server, and only about two weeks (a short time by Foxhole standards) to test the most advanced weapon additions. As such, before the 1.0 update rolled out only glaring bugs were removed and hurried balance changes made. Both in-game factions competed desperately for the chance to win the 1.0 war, which became what I called the "tryhard war," so they sought to use the most effective weapons. As soon as the Tempest Cannon became unlocked, the developers hastily changed the range of AI howitzers so that the Tempest Cannon could not out-range them. The reasoning here is quite basic, they did not want artillery to be able to hit any base without AI counter-fire, provided they were in the same map. The Wardens discussed this among ourselves and most major regiments decided that the Tempest Cannon was not worthwhile producing for its cost, as its purpose would be to attack the strongest fortifications and it could not do so without being vulnerable. However, the Colonials discovered that the Tempest Cannon could shoot from region borders. This offered a number of advantages: first, a slightly longer range which could hit half of a bordering map; second, with the way maximum population works, a large regiment with a tempest cannon is essentially invulnerable to counter-attack and there was no counter; third, and most crucially, no artillery can shoot into border areas, which meant that the longer-ranged, stationary counterparts of the Tempest Cannon (Storm Cannons), could not shoot back even with player input!

Because it takes upwards of two days to produce a Tempest Cannon even with the resources, and due to geographical differences, this exploit benefited one faction nearly exclusively. It took multiple weeks by developers, despite being well aware of the problem, to patch it out, but by then the damage was already done. A well-laid-out concrete base, properly defended, is meant to be a significant obstacle to overcome and building one can take an entire week of tedium, but this exploit meant an entire defensive line could be swept away in a single day with no counter by defenders. Yet it's not the worse of the two.

2) Relatively early into the war (about a week and a half) an odd bug occurred where it was impossible for Wardens to join a map, despite being outnumbered. Population in a server map works like this: there is a maximum number of players (roughly a hundred) who can be on one side in a map, and below this absolute maximum there is a relative maximum that scales with the number of enemy players. This is to prevent eighty players steamrolling twenty in one map. One side can still be outnumbered to a degree, and when this happens the deployment screen will say "Reinforcements Needed." What happened with this glitch was that for some unknown reason Colonials could join the map to the absolute maximum of players, while Wardens attempting to join would be disconnected and unable to join. There were only a handful of Warden players in the map against an entire army (despite this they held well enough to not lose the entire map), because although the deployment map said "Reinforcements Needed" it would not permit anyone on that side to actually provide reinforcement. This happened early in the war and developers were notified about it with documentation.

Fast-forward about three weeks later, and the same glitch happened again, except in two different maps which were each crucial to winning the war, leading to the Wardens losing those two maps and the entire war as a result. There is no documentation of this glitch happening to the Colonials at any point. The developers, despite being alerted to this, stayed silent and recently stated that they "observed no anomalies with the servers." Either the developers are indifferent or they're lying, but in either case it is a slap in the face to the playerbase, as gains they worked hard to take for days or weeks can be lost not to superior skill but a one-sided bug.

I will dismiss any conspiracy theories that the developers deliberately skewed the game to hand a win to one side, because secretly helping one side is exceptionally bad business practice even by Siege Camp's standards, and the only motive they would have would be to bolster the exceptionally poor morale of the Colonials (despite winning the previous war, and having new equipment to play with). For Wardens, late or absent response by developers in dealing with gamebreaking bugs (when they have otherwise been quick to resolve other issues) is the primary reason for defeat, and one cannot be blamed to not want to keep playing for that reason. For the Colonials, it is a victory stained with this knowledge. This is not a factional issue, as had the positions been reversed the Colonials would have every right to be indignant, and every player should demand change.

At this point I have no intention of picking this game up again until the developers write a public letter of apology to the entire playerbase, as it's far too late to merely quietly patch it out. One might ask, "Well you already paid for the game" but that was quite a while ago, and more importantly, a community-driven game is reliant on players. Players are needed to make Foxhole fun and worthwhile at all, so simply refusing to play will make an impact.\t\t\t\t\t
