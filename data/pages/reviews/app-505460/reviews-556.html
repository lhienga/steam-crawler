\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf there is any game which could destroy human civilization as we know it, it's this one.

As of writing, I have owned this game for 5 days. I have played for 31 hours. That is nearly as much time as a full-time job in the United States.

I have made friends, had hair-raising adventures, and found a series of enticing puzzles for my brain to unlock. I've built, packaged, delivered and moved military hardware. I've wrecked house on a machine gun in a brutal, endless siege. I've gone behind enemy lines in daring raids.

This game ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ terrifies me with how much I enjoy it. It stimulates my brain in the perfect way. The enemy is an ever-present threat. Almost every stranger I meet is immediately my friend, because we're all on the same team. I want to help them. Getting a commend for bringing a shiny new anti-tank gun to the front is like a warm hug.

This game simulates perfectly the endless grind of World War 1. There is ALWAYS a fight, ALWAYS a front in need of guns and people to hold them.

You don't "finish" a session of Foxhole. You ABANDON it. Like a soldier finishing his tour in Verdun, you drive meekly back home towards your normal life, the artillery shells still screaming and crashing in the background.

But despite simulating the endless grind of the wars it's based on, Foxhole is FUN. Addictively fun. Crack cocaine fun. You will die a dozen times and you just keep coming back.

You could play this game until you pass out of exhaustion, and not run out of interesting, fun, apparently purposeful things to do.

It simulates comradeship, friendship, brain-stretching puzzles, and pulse-pounding action. If this game had sex, it'd fulfill almost every emotional need a human being has.

This game isn't even a Skinner Box. It's Skinner's Colony. Skinner's endless football match. You're not aware of it being predatory in the same way as a mobile game, because there are no microtransactions. You don't think of it as having maliciously designed time gates. Things take a long time to put together, but it feels like a perfectly natural length of time.

Besides. You have friends. You're not doing this for the dopamine. You're doing this for them... And their commends.

Foxhole terrifies me. It provides a sense of purpose and satisfaction, stronger and easier and more immediately pleasurable than in any other area of my ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ life. It has no brakes, and if you don't force yourself to stop, it will eat every other hobby, relationship, commitment or life's work you hold dear.

Science Fiction has often discussed people "going into the box" - escaping into virtual reality, or the Matrix, or whatever. I think if you were trying to design that kind of psychologically perfect digital heaven, it would look a lot like this.

In a way, it's sort of like Valhalla. You will die, over and over, helping your friends in arms to win and lose in an endless struggle. Nobody ever truly dies. You just come back, over and over, in peak condition to begin the work anew.

This game simulates a world without bosses, hierarchy, or outwardly imposed deadlines. No one is TRULY above you. Your work is self-directed and emotionally fulfilling. You do it because it's pleasurable, or it helps those around you. It's communism, if communism were also a football game.

I'm clicking the recommend button at the end of this review. But honestly, I just needed to put all my thoughts about this game into one place.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's effectively a World War 2 simulator. The names are different, but the aesthetics and tactics feel like how you'd expect them to be for that description.

There are 2 main tasks in the game: fighting and logistics.

The game is all done from a 3rd person 3/4 view. You can change the camera direction easily, and in some cases, it needs to be done a lot. There is a bit of a learning curve for the game, especially since they aren't the best at telling you how to do more than the very basics. Though the starting area does have a training area that teaches you the basics and isn't bad at such.

Logistics is a very important part of the game. Without it, the fighters would have no ammo, guns, or respawns. Logistics (or Logi as they say in game a lot), mostly is gathering materials, refining them, and then turning those refined materials into usable forms. Such as by building vehicles, ammo, guns, respawns, fuel, and so on. Then you send those materials to the front to be used by the fighters there. They are also responsible for doing tech research to unlock new guns and vehicles to use.

Fighting is done on a large map with around 30 regions. Each region is it's own map zone, and it takes a few seconds to load into a new zone (often called a hex). Usually there are several fronts where the two teams meet for battle. This can be forests, coastal regions, plains, cities, and so on. Battles are often brutal, where you fight for every inch of ground. Each side has the ability to build new bases and buildings to aid in their defense. This can be as complex as concrete bunkers with trench lines or as simple as sandbag and barbed wire.

Each side has their own weapons and technology. Usually they are comparable, though they can vary greatly in use and form. Like one side has an RPG launcher, while the other has a portable tripod mounted gun that shoots small tank shells. Both have sniper rifles, but 1 has only 5 shots and a longer effective range, while the other has 10 shots and shoots faster.

The game is a lot of fun overall, but can be quiet frustrating at times (99% of that frustration coming from other players).\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhen everything comes together, it meets the promise and delivers a fun and exciting experience. Unfortunately this is rare and you'll spend most time either fighting stalemates at bridges, taking pot-shots at enemies off-screen, or dealing with terrible UX running logistics.

There's a lot about the game that could be improved, however few issues have been addressed over the game's development period, most updates usually dabbling in further asymmetry which causes unending balance problems. Worse, the devs rebalance the game between wars with knee-jerk reactions depending on who last won without sharing actual numbers of what's changed (and in many cases, no mention that anything has changed at all). Naturally some wars will be won for factors beyond gameplay design, so this leads to balance that's all over the place following dozens of knee-jerk balancing decisions.

The logistics is possibly the most compelling part of the game, if you're into that kind of thing. Unfortunately while it's the only way any fighting supplies exist to support the combat gameplay most engage in, the process of logistics is difficult and filled with community drama. Take for example fuel which, unless you use the refinery in a non-standard way and keep a personal stockpile of fuel in your queue output, you can only transfer a single can at a time - which is also the only way to stock up one fuel truck from another, one click at a time of maybe 100 cans.

The game is also filled with drama. The kinds of games do tend to attract some difficult people, however it's made worse here as the report system will automatically ban a player for a day if a number of reports are made. Naturally this means when someone gets annoyed with someone else, it's not uncommon for them to get help from their clan to mass report them. The clan I was playing in had some supplies stolen from their base, a player suspected another clan, and things escalated into a mass reporting war. I haven't experienced anything like it since Habbo Hotel.

So overall, the promise is exciting, and 1% of the time it delivers and when it does it's wonderful. But I eventually decided it wasn't worth my time compared to other games I could play that deliver either MMO combat or transport gameplay better, more fun, more reliable, and more respective of my time and the players as a whole. I encourage the devs to engage with the community better, communicate more, and give more long-term thought into design decisions.\t\t\t\t\t
