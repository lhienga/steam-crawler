\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFoxhole embodies the creativity and novel game play ideas that small studios are sometimes known to make their splash with, Foxhole is certainly one of those games.

Foxhole is a truly interesting, albeit at times flawed experience and that unique experience begins at the intriguing game play design. This experience is underwritten by two impactful game play design decisions, namely the persistence of the battle and the player driven nature of the game. Persistence is born out by the fact that players build and improve bases to cement gains in battle. Persistence also means that the war rages at all hours, even when you are offline. The battlefield can look really different in between sessions. However, the player driven nature of the game is probably the most fascinating element of the game. Foxhole is like a sociological petri dish, which is lends itself to interesting observations about how people behave and prosecute a war that no one is really in charge of.

To start, Foxhole is structured, paradoxically for a war game, as an anarchical game. As a no-rank recruit from the get go, you are free to wallow around where ever you want. There is no appointed in game grand commander. Warfare plays out more like groups of allied militant insurgents facing off each other in the absence of a dictator to will a grand strategy onto players. Clans, or as they are called "Regiments" in game are core unit for organized players. Good regiments will self organize and execute some of the more interesting plays of the game, they do this often with or without the 'public'. They use their ability to organize players to bring materials to front and conduct operations. As a regiment player myself, I've been able to do everything from "arty parties", amphibious D-Day style assaults, armoured fist operations, QRF (Quick reaction force) defence ops, grand theft auto plays behind enemy lines and other things. Outside of regiments is the public meat grinder. A common observation is seeing groups of random players, coalescing around a pitched defense or offence, making ad-hoc battlefield decisions. There is usually a critical mass of players, that when they come together usually spurs on some sort of semi-organized advance.

The player driven nature bears out in other ways too, for instance your in game rank is determined, indirectly by other players through the use of the commendation system. Pretty much all combat vehicles require multiple players to operate, with some end game tanks requiring up to like 4 players to effectively operate. Tripod based weapons, require someone to carry the weapon and the tripod to tactically navigate the crater ridden battlefield. Artillery requires at least 3 people, and then 2 more people after that for each additional gun (not to mention the logistics effort to move guns and ammo).

The in game economy, often just referred to as logistics, has to be the most bold decision to include in a war game. Every thing from rifles, band aids and tanks have to be made by players and transported to the front by players. This has created a whole "game inside a game", worker bees, players who self identify as logi need to not just mine raw materials and refine it, but also manufacture and bring the stuff to the front. This game play loop has some challenges, like making it engaging and fun for players. But also suffers from the lack of in game tools for organization. At the least though, players can shout out orders in the chat or put markers on the map begging for materials.

Another interesting observation is the limited direct feedback you get from the game itself. If you kill an enemy you won't know it unless you see your foe drop dead to the ground or an ally tells you that you killed someone. The only time the game seems to give feedback is if you are team killing. It was very only recently that Foxhole has some level of stats that you can view.

This leads to some of the flaws of Foxhole. Foxhole as an early release game, and much a "project simulator" as it is a war game, I found that it lacks a lot of in game tools to promote organization. In game, there is a text chat and voice chat and a map where players can pin markers that communicate in game happenings. There is also signs that can be placed in the world, it's quite fascinating to see how players have used signs to try to impose order and organization. One example is player placed traffic signs: what side of the road to drive on, other signs telling players to honk as they cross a narrow bridge. Signs indicating where to park and no parking zones among a whole other hosts of defacto player driven traffic bureau.

In my experience and rolling with a regiment, the vast majority of planning has been outsourced to another tool altogether: Discord. What players have done on other tools is fascinating, I've seen regiments go as far as to build out work order systems using Discord to request supplies. But players also use Discord to organize operations and share intelligence.

This leads to another challenge of this game. Because it is persistent and takes awhile to bear out, it can lead to burnout. Actually hearing some players talk about this game, if you didn't know what they were talking about you would think they were talking about their job.

This segues into the last point: Community. This game is fundamentally about the people who play it. We have quite an roster of everything from the drama people (those who masterfully recite their dying words over voice chat as they beg for a medic), the try hard commanders trying to organize a desperate suicide blitz against advancing armour, and yes ,once in awhile a toxic player might be more toxic than the in game green ash gas grenades. But observing the community around Foxhole has been an interesting experience in of itself. Most notable has to be L.O.G.I Union, an in game union organized around the principle of improving the logistics gameplay aspect of the game, which is using tactics reminiscent of real labour movements to spur on improvement from the developers of the game to promote a better experience for players who are cogs in the big cog wheel that is logi.

Ultimately if you want to experience a fairly unique multiplayer war game, Foxhole is certainly something to try out.
