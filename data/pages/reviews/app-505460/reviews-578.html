\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBought Foxhole: Winter Army after reading about it online. The game is true to its description. It's a real time massively multiplayer persistent war game in the isometric perspective that involves player input at every stage, set in a fictional world incorporating elements of both world wars with many artistic liberties taken. There is no plot or predestined path - the "war" is set in a large, persistent map divided up into hexagons, with each hexagon hosted on dedicated servers with their own instance. The winning side is whoever can inflict the most losses and gain the most ground on the opposing team.

The choices are endless. Some players go directly for combat and arm themselves with a rifle and a few mags to push the enemy trenches, build and operate artillery pieces, plan behind-the-lines clandestine ops or join tank corps to push or defend their team's territory. Others opt for logistics, harvesting and transporting raw materials to player-built factories and the resulting finished products to the front lines where they are needed. Then there are the builders, putting together defenses, trench lines and tank traps, or rebuilding bridges and destroyed towns in captured territories to restore the supply chain. In this open world sandbox, players are free to drop in and out of any activity they so choose, acting individually or coordinating with various clans, regiments, and/or squads that may or may not specialize in certain activities or operate in specific regions of the map.

One of the most interesting aspects of Foxhole is that nearly every structure or item used in game must be produced by players. From the factories, spawn points/bunkers and trench lines; all the way down to the re-spawn tickets, bullets, explosives, vehicles and their fuel; all at one point needed someone to mine the materials out of the ground, refine and process them into finished products, and then transport them to where they're needed most. Which means in order for your team to stand a chance capturing enemy territory or defending your own, your entire cause is reliant on a robust logistics network to ensure they have the supplies they need to keep moving, building, and fighting.

This interwoven, open-ended nature of the gameplay in Foxhole leads naturally to a lot of unexpected and emergent scenarios. In my short 2+ weeks of owning the game, I've witnessed massive, well-coordinated combined arms offensives with tanks and infantry pouring into no man's land, bullets flying and artillery shells going off all around. I've been a victim of sabotage countless times - most notably when skiff-mounted enemy commandos ("partisans") shut down our push into their territory by sneaking satchel charges onto our backline bridges and dumping the contents of refuel stations into the sea, knocking out and denying our supply routes. I've been a part of hectic last-mile logi crews, evading minefields and manned enemy checkpoints, drawing and dodging fire through enemy encirclements to deliver much needed artillery pieces, ammunition, and most importantly shirts (respawn tickets) to very appreciative soldiers in embattled, bombed out bases on their last leg. Few things will give you a rush like supplying your guys with a second wind to end their siege and push the fight back through enemy lines. This game truly has it all.

For the downsides, of which there are few but are still significant IMO, my number one complaint is the way the player's perspective is handled. Since the view is top-down, the game limits your character's visibility depending on a number of factors - time of day, weather, and.. screen aspect ratio? That's right, if you are like me and have a wide or ultrawide monitor on a 16:9 or 16:10 etc. ratio, you are disadvantaged visibility-wise against players with a 4:3 letterbox setup. This is due to the way the game scales your viewpoint as there is no ability to zoom in or out whatsoever. On widescreens and above your longest sight lines will be towards the corners of your screen, with the edges of your in-game visibility being obscured by the top and bottom of the monitor. Players on letterbox monitors on the other hand are able to see 100% of their visible range in a 360 degree area around themselves. Meaning these players can keep their camera static as they move about without losing any visibility, while widescreen users have to continually adjust their viewpoint to ensure the area they are looking in is as close to one of the four corners of their screen as possible.

I've not played long enough to gain a full understanding of this game's balance, but from what I can understand at the beginning of each "war", as the map, players and technology progress are reset, devs tweak and change different technologies based upon the recent wins/losses of the two factions. It seems that Colonials and Wardens each have units that are somewhat overpowered, and so devs will make them appear earlier/later on the tech tree to influence the winner of the next war depending on if either of them have been on a winning or losing streak. This is a complaint I've heard over and over from veterans playing the game and if it's as bad as they say, it makes the outcome of each war seem somewhat artificial.

There is a fairly steep learning curve. The starting area for each faction includes training areas with signs giving basic guides on how to operate within each of the game's varying theaters, however finishing all of the courses isn't mandatory and gives players surface deep knowledge at best. It's also far from a proper tutorial. Clans and benevolent players often offer their own interactive tutorials particularly when new players flood online, if only to lessen their own future suffering via erroneous noob activity. Most players will generally take time out of their day to explain different mechanics to new players as well, so long as one is willing to listen and learn. I don't necessarily consider the learning curve a mark against the game, as I feel the nature of Foxhole can be so complicated it makes sense that learning how to do well and understand all the elements would take time.

By no means however should the last few points deter anyone interested in this niche genre from picking up Foxhole. The game is by itself an extraordinary concept executed well, and you would be hard pressed to find anything like it anywhere else. I've had more fun and experienced more unforgettable moments in 3 weeks with this $20 game than I've had during the whole lifecycle of many AAA games costing 3x as much.

Highly recommend.\t\t\t\t\t
