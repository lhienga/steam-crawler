\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a game that I wanted to make sure I gave it a few hours before I gave it a write-up. There's a lot to the game and while it isn't overly complicated, it is complex. The scope of the game means you will benefit from browsing the wiki, doing EVERYTHING in the tutorial area (seriously, it's there for a reason), and listening to more experienced players. There are some decent Youtube tutorials for the more daunting tasks like logistics and research, and I highly recommend spending some time getting to grips with artillery in the training area before you start wasting precious shells in-game. The more you understand what OTHER people are doing, the better you will be at your own chosen role, seeing as you will understand how best to support them or take advantage of the power, support or cover they provide for you.

The devs definitely made the right choice in making the two sides as similar as possible whilst still giving them some measure of identity. There is almost no thematic or strategic incentive to choose one side over the other, meaning that teams will tend to be balanced overall - yet stalwart loyalists of either side can be *very* defensive and tribal, to the point of being actually toxic. It won't affect your gameplay, but when asking around threads for help and info try not to mention which side you play for if it can be avoided...

Foxhole is the closest thing you'll get to an all-encompassing "war experience". The scale, both in player numbers and group responsibility, is much larger than what you'd see in more action-oriented games, and more visceral and involved than what you'd see in more strategy-oriented games. Every piece of equipment and infrastructure in the game was researched, mined, built and delivered by your teammates. The war never stops, so be thankful that there are guys in Europe who can guard the trenches while you're sleeping.

The amount of freedom and group responsibility does however, leave the game open to some abuse. The reporting system is, as I understand it, much improved over what was there, but occasionally you will see people just being trolls, throwing grenades around in friendly backline factory towns, or even "spies" (or "alts") filling up trucks with supplies and driving them over to the enemy bases (different from "partisans" who are actual enemy players that attempt to infiltrate your backlines and steal things for themselves which is a legitimate and effective tactic). Even just seeing new, afk or incompetent players who waste resources without consideration for the time and coordination that was put into getting them there in the first place can be frustrating. However, to solve these issues any more than they are already would mean putting restrictions and/or artificial controls on who can do what which would ultimately detract from what Foxhole is about and what it tries to accomplish. The key to winning the war is cooperation, from the guy mining scrap in the yards, to the guy pumping out munitions and supplies, to the men who risk it all driving those supplies to the front-lines. If the chain breaks at any point, the house of cards will fall.

The game isn't for everyone - this isn't Call of Duty. Playing on front-lines can mean hours of sitting behind a sandbag with your gun aimed down the road just waiting for friendly armour and reinforcements to show up along with a veteran who can coordinate a successful offensive. Don't be surprised to see bridge stalemates lasting for several days - especially in the earlier phases of a war where heavy artillery and armour are rare.

Foxhole is slow, frustrating, immersive and extremely rewarding. If that sounds like your thing rather than the adrenaline pumping 360 no-scopes of other gun games then give Foxhole a try - there is simply nothing else like it.\t\t\t\t\t
