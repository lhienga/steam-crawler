I LOVE paperwork!

Death and Taxes is a simple game about choices that drowns out the monotony of office work with OCEANS of personality. With its vintage aesthetic and quirky but engaging dialog, it's hard to not have a good time just spending your time in the world. I for one dream of having a simple office job like the one displayed here- show up in a snazzy suit, shuffle papers for a few hours, then go home. No muss, no fuss. There is whole thing about "choosing who lives and who dies" and "your choices affect the direction the human world will take" but honestly I wasn't really bothered with that for my first run. I just like following orders and reading profiles.
I won't say anything about how that went, but uh... let's just say the game does encourage you to think for yourself a bit more than it would initially lead on.

Endings, Endings Galore

There are many things that can affect how your game will end up after your set time as a reaper is over, though the primary way this happens is through the life/death decisions you make at your desk. As you work and earn money you can eventually get tools that will *slightly* clarify exactly what your decision influenced, but that means you only have a moderate level of control in New Game+ and beyond, your first playthrough is almost entirely decided by you.
While I enjoy that there are so many different outcomes, I can't say I'm looking forward to going through the whole game again to get them. Death and Taxes feels like an experience you can't do back to back, as a full 28 day playthrough really is a bit of work. Not that that's a discredit to the game or anything, I look forward to going back eventually and trying out a different playstyle. It just does feel kind of bad not having the drive to immediately go back and try again.

Overall a great experience, with a few minor flaws.

Any complaint I have about Death and Taxes, other than my lack of desire to replay the game right away, are just minor nitpicks that nagged me during my run. Stuff like, voice acting having differing levels of audio for no reason. Some dialog options really not conveying what I really want to say in a situation. Very vague dialog that I assume is supposed to hint at other endings and is probably supposed to be cryptic, being way too cryptic to really mean anything. The elevator being a huge pain to navigate. And the bar being a general disappointment- some good characters to interact with, but stuff like the drinks being prohibitively expensive on your initial run and not having their effects made clear to you.

At the end of the day, I would recommend Death and Taxes. I was inspired by one Mori Calliope to give this a try and I was not disappointed. If you don't mind primarily reading, and making some pretty ♥♥♥♥♥♥ up decisions now and then, you'll have a good time. But if the idea of working in an office drives you insane, then... well, you might still like it given all the weird stuff you can get up to.
Not to mention, for the really weird folks out there, you can choose to be a cat. Or a dog. Or a bird, if you feel like. I liked just being a skeleton, myself.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHaving played the game extensively, for the price point there are collective number of flaws with the game.

The first is it tries to shoehorn the political views of the creators into the game. The game always has it that unemployed and retired people are generally beneficial to kill and are always a burden on society; never is there a suggestion an unemployed person could go on to become beneficial, and rarely is there a consequence for marking retired people for death.

People who express views contrary to that of the creators - people who back oil (used for plastics, ironically used by the 1930s style radio, AKA Bakerite), fuels, hunting, media or corporations are always stereotypically marked evil and are shown to be beneficial to kill. Those of liberal inclinations - pro-climate change, pro-LGBTQ, pro-transhumanism, etc, are generally signalled as beneficial to keep alive. There is no nuance.

Genders are intentionally omitted, with the game forced to explain why 'they/them' is a system. The concern trolling the game tries to explain implies that people might possibly discriminate on gender. But a game forcing you to discriminate on employment status and political views is a-okay, apparently.

Perhaps one could forgive it for 'creative licence' on views, after all, free speech is important, but the game is pockmarked with annoying bugs, user interface flaws, and mediore gameplay with no real achievements.

For example, when trying to click to pick up a form, it will also interact with the item behind it. Insufficient desk space coupled with an overwhelming number of useless trinkets - including a cactus that makes a noise, a fidget spinner, a flipping coin, a pig that takes money and produces more pigs (up to 3 pigs) for no real purpose and a plaque - mean you will be stuffing practically everything into the drawers. The fact drawers were even required should have been a warning sign to the developers the UI is clumsy and addled, but apparently it wasn't.

Other bugs include:
- Items refusing to go into the drawers because of weird overlap detection
- Being unable to pick up the item you can see, picking up the item behind it
- Interacting with items unintentionally (for example if you drag a form slightly on top of 'Deus Fax Machina' it will throw up a prompt as if you're trying to interact with it)
- Having to remove items from a drawer to get to a specific visible, on-top item
- Audio still playing even after dialogue has ended and a new scene has started
- Terrible music loop with no seamless transition (literally just fades out and then fades back in)

The game suffers from dialogue bloat, suffering from similar issues to classic Pokemon games, where you will find yourself rapidly mashing trying to skip cutscenes. The only enjoyable character is Mortimer, who is a side character who the developers made crucial part of the plot at the last minute, probably after they realised he was the only decent character.

Voice acting is reasonably decent, but the talent is wasted on small talk go-nowhere plotlines where characters gripe about their crippling life issues (likely a proxy for the developers themselves) which ultimately have no impact on you, the main character.

There is one exception to the voice acting though, apparently for the voice at the mirror they asked for the 'most annoyingly obnoxious female voice imagineable'. The mirror "dialogue" is the worst out of the bunch, not only is it not crucial to the plot, but you get gaslighted by a patronising voice that offers no real insights and no useful information. It is also buggy in that incorrectly reports you are 'destroying the world' even as other game mechanics clearly show you're saving the world.

The gameplay literally involves bureaucracy. But this isn't an ironic parody in the sense of 'Papers Please', where the point is to make you hate the bureaucracy and see it for what it is whilst being a petty tyrant, alas no; the developers overuse the same 'choose alive or dead' checkbox option like all they ever saw was Excel spreadsheet forms.

You have to pick up papers individually, pick up a pen, check the box, put the pen down, put the paper down, and then pick up the next one. That's it. There's no iteration or innovation; you can ignore the rules (with the exception of killing everybody or saving everybody per day) with no consequences besides a loss of worthless money used to buy useless trinkets. And just in-case you were too dumb to grasp the creators' political message they even literally give you a lamp spelling out what the "correct" choices are.

Before you argue 'this is the point', Papers Please introduced new mechanics, new rules with consequences, and new dimensions, without losing the bureaucracy vibe. For 28 "turns" (days) you use the same system. That's it. That's not a game. The endings consist of basically a PowerPoint slide show of static images showing your "outcomes". The only fun parts are Mortimer's made up stories of adventure, which ironically have more plot depth to them than the entire game does.

In terms of games you could pick up for a similar price (~£10 at time of purchase), this is one to definitely avoid.

Oh, and there's no taxes. Not that would have helped the game fix the glaring issues.\t\t\t\t\t
