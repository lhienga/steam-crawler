\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is so so cool... for about the 10 first hours of gameplay.
Okay, sure, it is a visual novel, so it is to expect, and believe me, the 10 first hours are so fun because you don't really know what happens, some dialogues can be taken differently depending on what is your intention on the current run, and most of the achievements are possible without looking guides.
All the good things told aside, as soon as you start playing you should already be looking to do every achievement for it to not become the most tedious consecutive hours of gameplay you are gonna have, there are so many RNG-based achievements that I've even thought that some achievements were broken, this game is alright to enjoy it, but for real the devs should add a skip text button or something because every re-run is lik 30 or 40 minutes like it or not and believe, you are gonna re-run a lot.
If you only want to get every achievement one after another, plan it from the start and it may be tolerable, otherwise, this game is gonna give you headaches.\t\t\t\t\t

In the Business of Afterlife: Death and Taxes Review

In a world shrouded in uncertainty, Death and Taxes presents a unique twist on the afterlife. As a grim reaper, you'll navigate an office job that involves deciding the fates of mortals through paperwork. The game combines the decision-making depth of Papers, Please with the quick choices of Reigns, offering a refreshing take on bureaucracy simulation.

While reminiscent of Papers, Please, Death and Taxes infuses a touch of dark and absurd humor that keeps the experience from becoming overwhelmingly heavy. The game's progression spans four weeks of work, during which you must fulfill quotas and secondary objectives that shape the fate of individuals. The consequences ripple through hidden statistics like environment, health, and peace. Balancing compassion and judgment becomes an art, and sometimes, bending the rules is essential to prevent catastrophe.

The story unfolds with a lighthearted tone, sprinkled with absurdities like a talkative pirate merchant. While Death and Taxes doesn't delve into the same complexity as Papers, Please, it successfully carves its own niche. The game might be short, but its replay value shines, inviting you to explore multiple playthroughs and endings.

With an outstanding soundtrack and a charming atmosphere, Death and Taxes is a well-crafted experience. Despite some repetitive elements, the game's mix of decision-making and light-hearted narrative makes it an enjoyable endeavor into the world of bureaucracy and the unknown beyond.

Especially when the bark keeper lectured me about sexual harassment out of nowhere. I was expecting a joke, and instead some freak wrote a script to lecture me about basic social cues. I can only imagine that freak, the person that wrote that dialogue. I can only imagine they are just a burden on anyone that is unlucky enough to be associate with them.

I genuinely thought this game would be tongue and cheek. But instead. It just feels like all the characters are intentionally hostile to the player. Not for reasons relevant to the story. But because someone involved with the creation of this game... That someone is just a horribly miserable, and thoroughly unlikable cretin. The music is great, the visual aesthetic is understated but excellent, but if you put in one exchange where a character breaks the fourth wall to ♥♥♥♥ on the player. The person that just paid for your work... It genuinely does ruin the entire product. Its uncalled for.

Don't purchase this game for full price. Or better yet. Don't even /play/ this game. There are better interactive fiction. There are also developers that don't disrespect their audience.\t\t\t\t\t
