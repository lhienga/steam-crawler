\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really liked the concept of this game and as a fan of stuff built around an open choice mechanic like Papers Please and Reigns, I thought it was bound to be great. Unfortunately, I just really didn't vibe with the artwork or writing style of Death and Taxes.

I think a key part of what makes these morality-focused games work is a more abstract aesthetic that lets you project your own feelings onto the characters but D&T's art has a very specific and quite detailed art style that doesn't leave much to the imagination. Same goes for the writing -- it tries very hard to be funny and quirky and just ends up making you feel like you're clicking through pages of a comic rather than occupying a world where your choices can make an impact. I pushed through to finish the game and didn't have a terrible time or anything but I just found that I was tuning out and not feeling like anything I did really mattered.

I think if these developers made a more traditionally story-driven game they'd do a great job but if they try something like this again I'd really like to see them try a simpler/more artistic approach to the visuals and let the game's mechanics speak for themselves.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDeath and Taxes has one of those elevator pitches that's made to lull you in. Play as the Grim Reaper and select who gets to lives and die in a given day? No game concept has any right to be that spicy. In the same vein as Papers Please and Don't Feed the Monkeys, it's proof of the amazing ideas that can form from combining unorthodox gameplay mechanics with interesting moral dilemmas. Unlike those titles though, Death and Taxes is maybe a little TOO simple to ever fully deliver on its potential.

There's a really cool game here and one that doesn't wear out its welcome considering it clocks in at around 2 - 3 hours. But the idea of killing and sparing people never evolves much further than reading their forms and checking off whether they get to keep breathing or embrace the afterlife. It always feels like there should be a little more challenge or prodding of your moral flexibility, especially because the game rarely throws you ethical decisions that aren't "this person's good, this person's evil. Pick who lives." Still, the concept is a fun gimmick and the whimsical setting the story thrusts you into is full of character. I rate that the world changes in correspondence with your decisions as well, whether that's through small news updates on the character's phone or the state of the planet towards the endgame. Getting the bad ending first was genuinely a surprise and made me reflect on all the crappy decisions I'd made during my first, more gungho shot at the campaign. (don't worry guys, I spared Tesco Value Hideo Kojima on both run-throughs. In return, I'm expecting him to start making games with cutscenes that aren't the length of a Scorsese movie).

Essentially, fun game and novel concept, even if it can't quite evolve its central mechanic enough to really knock it out the park.\t\t\t\t\t
