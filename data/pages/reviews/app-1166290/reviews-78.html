\r\n\t\t\t\t\t𝗔 𝗴𝗮𝗺𝗲 𝘄𝗵𝗲𝗿𝗲 𝘆𝗼𝘂 𝗽𝗹𝗮𝘆 𝗮𝘀 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝗚𝗿𝗶𝗺 𝗥𝗲𝗮𝗽𝗲𝗿, 𝗼𝗻𝗹𝘆 𝘆𝗼𝘂 𝗰𝗼𝗹𝗹𝗲𝗰𝘁 𝘀𝗼𝘂𝗹𝘀 𝗮𝘁 𝘆𝗼𝘂𝗿 𝗼𝗳𝗳𝗶𝗰𝗲 𝗷𝗼𝗯. Let me start by saying that I REALLY wanted to like this game. I played it through about 4 times to get a few endings and also some fun achievements. However, in the end, Death and Taxes fell through on my expectations.

I will be drawing direct comparisons to Papers, Please as it is the only game with a similar premise I have played.

• Let's start with the gameplay itself: every day, you are told to mark a certain number of humans to die. At first, this may seem important, since you are told you will be reviewed at the end of every week and that if you fail to mark the correct number of files, you will not be paid at all. 𝗛𝗼𝘄𝗲𝘃𝗲𝗿, 𝗮𝘀 𝗹𝗼𝗻𝗴 𝗮𝘀 𝘆𝗼𝘂 𝗱𝗼𝗻'𝘁 𝗺𝗮𝗿𝗸 𝗲𝘃𝗲𝗿𝘆𝗼𝗻𝗲 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝘀𝗮𝗺𝗲 (𝗲𝗶𝘁𝗵𝗲𝗿 𝗹𝗶𝘃𝗲 𝗼𝗿 𝗱𝗶𝗲), 𝗶𝘁 𝘄𝗶𝗹𝗹 𝗻𝗼𝘁 𝘀𝗶𝗴𝗻𝗶𝗳𝗶𝗰𝗮𝗻𝘁𝗹𝘆 𝗮𝗳𝗳𝗲𝗰𝘁 𝘆𝗼𝘂𝗿 𝗽𝗲𝗿𝗳𝗼𝗿𝗺𝗮𝗻𝗰𝗲 𝗿𝗲𝘃𝗶𝗲𝘄, whereas in Papers, Please, you needed to be very meticulous with every single detail if you didn't want a citation. 𝗠𝗼𝗿𝗲𝗼𝘃𝗲𝗿, 𝘆𝗼𝘂 𝗵𝗮𝘃𝗲 𝗻𝗼 𝗶𝗻𝗰𝗲𝗻𝘁𝗶𝘃𝗲 𝘁𝗼 𝗱𝗼 𝘆𝗼𝘂𝗿 𝗷𝗼𝗯 𝗰𝗼𝗿𝗿𝗲𝗰𝘁𝗹𝘆 𝘀𝗶𝗻𝗰𝗲 𝗺𝗼𝗻𝗲𝘆 𝗶𝘀 𝗲𝘀𝘀𝗲𝗻𝘁𝗶𝗮𝗹𝗹𝘆 𝗽𝗼𝗶𝗻𝘁𝗹𝗲𝘀𝘀 𝗶𝗻 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝗴𝗮𝗺𝗲. You can use it to buy cosmetics (just for show), widgets (items you can use at your desk for fun, although a few of them will help with your decisions later on), and drinks, which I have so far not found any point to (the game even tells you that because you are a skeleton, the drinks fall right on the ground). In Papers, Please, if you don't get paid well enough just for one day, you might have to let your wife starve since you can't afford to pay for everyone.

It essentially all depends on what you want to end up with: do you want to follow the rules to a T, be a rebel and bend every rule you get, or try to save the world be letting good people live and killing bad people (again, it's very rare that you know who is which). But there is no emotion or urgency tied to ANY of your choices.
• Whatever you pick, whether according to a certain plan or just randomly, nothing seems to hold any weight, and part of that is because the game makes it very clear that 𝘆𝗼𝘂 𝗮𝗿𝗲 𝗷𝘂𝘀𝘁 𝗮 𝗥𝗲𝗮𝗽𝗲𝗿: 𝘆𝗼𝘂𝗿 𝗰𝗵𝗮𝗿𝗮𝗰𝘁𝗲𝗿 𝗵𝗮𝘀 𝗻𝗼 𝗺𝗼𝘁𝗶𝘃𝗲𝘀, 𝗻𝗼 𝗲𝗺𝗼𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻𝘀, 𝗻𝗼 𝗹𝗼𝘆𝗮𝗹𝘁𝗶𝗲𝘀, 𝗻𝗼𝘁𝗵𝗶𝗻𝗴.
