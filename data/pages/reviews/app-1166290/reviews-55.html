\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDeath & Taxes - a great game where choices matter and you must agonize over the fates of complete strangers as a bureaucratic Grim Reaper. Your choices start off seemingly simple but become increasingly less clear cut as time wears on. I even questioned why I would send a Pineapple Pizza eater to their death.
(Le gasp!)

D&T is like an ethical trolley problem wrapped a fantasy realism atmosphere.

It has great writing, memorable characters, excellent Voice Over throughout and a lovely distinct art style.

Definitely recommend.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI bought this during sale and though I will be getting Papers, please 2.0. Well, how mistaken I was... let's get this elephant out of the room, shall we.

First of all, the developers of this game are preachy woke hypocrites. Death and Taxes goes to extreme lengths to hide gender on every info card about every human you have to process (and decide who lives and who dies), and refers to every single person as "they", which is, obviously, a gesture towards the non-existent pronoun community. This is explained in-game and by developers on the Steam forums that the Grim Reaper Office simply doesn't care about gender, instead referring to every character as "they". The developers try to hide this incorrect pronoun game as a matter-of-fact that it's simply below Grim's recognition.
Well, if that is so, then why is the cat clearly referred to as she? Why isn't she referred to as "they", if a frickin' plant with a skull is referred to as such? The woke editor probably forgot about this, and it just slipped into the final script, I guess.
The use of incorrect pronouns has one effect on the game. Total dehumanization of all the characters. I couldn't care less about any of "them", because the use of generalized pronoun has made them into faceless nothings. So, if the developers wanted to achieve a total disconnect from the people you kill, then congratulations, but from the tone of the game and its terrible dialog, I guess it's not intentional.

As for the game itself, it's a shadow copy of Papers, please. But unlike Papers, please, it gets more repetitive, very quickly, and its writing is beyond bad. It indulges in trying to question moral, ethical, and philosophical questions, with the grace of an understudied high schooler. The "story" is told through tons of useless dialogue that ultimately goes nowhere, and looks like it was written by a five-year-old with Parkinson's. And most importantly, it's boring. It's simply too boring to chew through.

But hey, instead of a good story and important choices like in Papers, please, you get to enjoy absurd amount of visual customization which is, again, totally pointless, and doesn't mean anything in a game where you never see your character unless you actually look into a mirror. I wish the developers spent less money on artists (who did do a good job, by the way), and spent more on actual writers who are able to convey thoughts through written media.

The game is also very short, about 2 hours tops. Also, replayability is piss-poor, unless you want to try silly stuff like killing everyone / letting everyone live, and so on.

So, for anyone with decent standards, it isn't worth it even during a sale. But, if your idea of philosophical discussion consists of poetically describing cosmological phenomena in a totally wrong way, indulging in bastardization of the English language and human speech in general, or you get high every time someone calls a woman "they", then I guess you will love this.\t\t\t\t\t
