Well, ♥♥♥♥ you then, Boss! I had to go again. And got the Utopia ending. And then I went for it again and this time deliberately set things on fire, just to see what would happen.

I am having a lot of fun playing this. It is story-driven, and it lives off of the fact that you can "redo" the story, retaining your knowledge, which you can then lord over other characters (which is especially satisfying when it's your Boss, obviously, because the Boss in this game has Real Boss vibes).

I still haven't gotten to the bottom of all the mysteries, it's been three runs now. Maybe I won't make it to the bottom of all the mysteries ever. And also, I pretty much kissed getting all the achievements for this game goodbye, since the last achievement is... hell to get. Really not recommended if you're that kind of completionist! But if you don't mind a missing achievement, and this sounds like silly/charming fun to you?

Then hop into the boat and discover how being Death can feel JUST like being a frustrated office worker.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI was very excited for Death and Taxes when I bought it on sale. I'm very fond of menial simulation games like these, and having grown quite attached to Deadbolt's Reaper, I'd thought I'd found the perfect game for me. Especially considering I'm a full time laws student and that reviewing cases like the reaper is doing is my bread and butter.

Unfortunately I was incredibly disappointed within the first and only hour I played. With how much the game emphasises self-determination, I had hoped I could roleplay a faceless, borderline obsequious bureaucrat. I'd follow my manager's orders to the letter not only because I intended on keeping my job for more than a week, but also because such an esteemed, antiquated organisation that dealt with death absolutely knew what it was doing and an intern definitely had no place making any mistakes on his very first week.

Unfortunately, approaching this game rationally was my first mistake. The story reads like a pantomime. Everybody is an inept, illogical clown, and treats everything you do like you're crazy. Quite ironic that this was how I felt as a player, reading the cases before me and seeing the profession in big bold letters "CORRUPT CEO" that may as well have read "KILL THIS ONE" because the game seems hell-bent on making a statement it thinks you can't figure out yourself. You are railroaded so hard that you are punished for any philosophy the developers disagree with, which the game seems to dress up as some kind of intentional design choice. This is exacerbated by the developers' own factual shortcomings, like not knowing the difference between the profession of law and law enforcement, which cost me a day's pay when I saw not a single barrister, solicitor or judicial officer in a sea of corrections officers.

It suffers all the same shortcomings of We. The Revolution. And if you haven't seen Tehsnakerers video on it, I implore you to do so. Just like We. The Revolution, Death and Taxes forces you to think objectively about something entirely subjective. Forget being fair, just, or even a downright menace, the gameplay loop in DnT revolves around predicting what the writers want you to do and think, but unlike We. The Revolution, the writers lost my suspension of disbelief the second Fate opened his mouth, because while he has some very poignant things to rant about scientific materialism to the impressionable new guy, he also makes a firm point that his organisation is very PC, and that people are not judged on the basis of race or gender despite manipulating you into destroying humanity the very next week.

All these characters and the writing seems to shamelessly derive from the writers' own fantasies. Except the swashbuckling pirate skeleton. He was my favourite character not because he was good, but because he wasn't as bad as the others. I never had an opportunity to visit the skeleton speakeasy, but I can only guess that the characters there would be similarly archetypal and uninspired.

What doesn't help is that the game can't take itself seriously. It seems to not be able to make up its mind about being either a grim (heh), serious and richly dark game, or some kind of quirky indie title about a funny skeleton man wearing cat ears. The game had perfectly enough character and thematic balance as it did, but all the shoehorned internet memes, the funny cosmetic items like the aforementioned cat ears and the entire arc with Fate's cat royally pissed all over whatever intellectual value the writing had, because I didn't buy it for the Reddit memes.

The useless dialogue choices give Fallout 4 a run for its money, too. You can be snarky Grim, edgy Grim, funny skeleton or a mixture of all of them and Mr. Fate will be as bipolar as you are in like. I hope you weren't trying to beeline to the gameplay, because Fate is going to make you feel like a real ♥♥♥♥ about it despite urging you to be completely impartial and emotionally unimpeded in your decisionmaking. Whatever "decisionmaking" you can even do. Most of them are hardcoded. Kill or spare somebody, programmed response on your phone next day. While there is something to say about Fate being a capricious and irrational character, my attempt to remain at least slightly thematically consistent with the stone-faced bureaucrat fell apart about two weeks in when all of a sudden all of the dialogue choices turned into snarky Grim and I felt like a massive ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ by cussing out the (On occassion, quite reasonable) musings of my boss as he espoused the virtue of magnanimity and candour. Even the "neutral" options aren't neutral. They're always some manner of "Uhh... okay..?" or "Mm... yeah... uh... yeah... fine * pouts* ". Sometimes I forget if I'm playing a skeleton man who condemns people to death for a living or a spoilt teenage brat basking in the glory of her father's company.

I could go on forever about my gripes with the writing, but credit where credit is due - the art style is charming and very well done and the gameplay was engaging and I poured over every case in detail before I realised it had no direction and nothing mattered. Complements to the music, too, even if it began to get grating by the end of the game, though my frustration with the game likely didn't help that. Probably makes a good tuber game.\t\t\t\t\t
