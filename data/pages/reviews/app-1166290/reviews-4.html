\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn summary:
If you have played Peaper Please and missed being the decision maker by controlling things, this game is for you.

First of all, I feel like I should start with what I expect from interactive fiction games; because it seems like it would make my assessment more logical. My expectation from such games is that they tell me the story without boring me or that the story is very interesting and has literary value.

"Dead and Taxes" is about you working in an office as a grim reaper. You are a grim reaper and you decide whether people live or die at the table with instructions from "Fate". It would not be wrong to say that you are not completely free in this decision-making mechanism. You have to follow certain guidelines, but those guidelines may not exactly align with the player's values. What will you do here, will you listen to the boss named "Fate" or will you be your own person? The story cards the game offers us are very interesting; I also really liked how it presented us with the decisions we made the next day, as if we were reading the news in real time. Even though they give unexpected results in some cases, they are quite exciting.

There are a few issues that bother me in the gameplay part of the game. The game aims to tell you a story. As in other text-based games, the player gets bored after a certain time because he is constantly reading and cannot stay in front of him for long. Listening to this story may be exciting at first, but it may tire you after the second hour of the game. After a certain point, the story could have kept the player more engaged if it had been told differently. Despite all this, the game encourages you to see more than one ending.

A game that is successful in creating atmosphere. The music is beautiful and manages to make you feel like you belong in any environment, but there seems to be a general lack of connection between the tracks. The atmosphere might have been more lasting if the pieces were more in tune with each other and if the environment changed more slowly rather than suddenly when you changed.

The characters in the game were quite adequate and entertaining. I couldn't take my eyes off the bartender and listened to her with all my attention. They make you both question yourself and realize the situation you are in.
We should not forget the mirror conversations that take on the same responsibility.
One of the parts that I found missing in the game was that the decisions I made had a little too much reversal as I approached the end of the game. While playing, I found myself saying: "What did I do that made the world suddenly start to disappear?" The ending seems a bit rushed.
Finally, the dialogue options didn't make me feel like I had a lot of options; My options seemed very close to each other.

General evaluation:
Gameplay 6/10
Dialogue and story 7/10
Atmosphere 7/10
Music 8/10
My overall score: 7.8/10\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game has its merits, despite the premise being a bit basic and the writing not that great. Voice acting was incredible, that much has to be admitted.

The main demerit I would say is the grinding aspect. You can read the profiles you have to mark as carefully as you want, but even then, the only 'real' way to get the good endings is by mindlessly going through a few playthroughs until all profiles are marked automatically for you, at which point, why bother?

It was fun the first few couple of times, when you're figuring everything out and the twists happen, but after that, it's just pretty boring grinding. I still had some achievements to go, and they were fairly easy, but it would require some more mind-numbing marking of profiles in specific ways with no mystery added.

I don't know how the game could even be fixed. It depends on its repetition to learn lessons of the past, but the dullness of the gameplay once you run out of new text to read can be pretty damning.

Still, a fun experience, and for the amazing discounted price I got it for, very much worth my money. Don't expect too much, and you'll do fine.\t\t\t\t\t
