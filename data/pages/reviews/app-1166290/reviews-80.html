\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn my ongoing and likely futile effort to write a review for every game in my Steam library (#509 out of 1000+)... it's time for Death and Taxes.

┛Somewhere On The Border Between Clownlishy Evil And Diabolically Brilliant┏

Maybe it's the Terry Pratchett fan in me, but I am instantly attracted to any game where you play as Death -- or a grim reaper, or any other physical manifestation of an abstract concept, really -- and doubly so if an otherworldly bureaucracy is also in the mix. Unfortunately Death and Taxes never really fully embraces this delightful premise -- you may play as a grim reaper, but one very clearly written from a human perspective. Perhaps best illustrated by the ultimate goal of the game, which is is to save humanity from extinction: the narrative frames this as an inherently positive goal, because this is what the (expectedly human) players are likely to think. This is, to me, an enormous missed opportunity. Instead of taking this for granted, how much more interesting would the story be if the player character had the opportunity to learn about humanity and then decide for themselves whether or not they should attempt to divert fate? Actually grapple with some of the big, existential questions here and examine them meaningfully.

Annoyingly obtuse design encourages -- practically requires -- multiple playthroughs. There are denser systems at play here than you might expect from a bureaucracy-simulator, but they're initially invisible. If you've played any casual management sim before (like Reigns you'll find these systems familiar: there are four different meters to balance, and each choice you make adjusts some of those meters up or down. Unfortunately this information is obfuscated to the point where I suspect many players may not even notice it at all. To see these stats, you must first purchase a desk lamp, then turn the lamp on (I know, right?) -- and then manually move the character sheets under the light to reveal the information. It is, in a few words, an exercise in profound tedium. And even moreso than you might imagine -- those stats? They're invisible -- just question marks -- until and unless you've selected that choice in a prior playthrough. EG if you select John Doe to die on your first playthrough, on your second playthrough you will see how his death affects the management meters, but not his survival. Theoretically, to reveal all of the stats, you must play through the entire game at least three times, taking care not to both spare and condemn every available soul. This is an astonishing bit of game design that resides somewhere on the border between clownishly evil and diabolically brilliant.

Personally I find the core idea of Death and Taxes very compelling -- a simple management game with complex interweaving systems at play beneath the surface. What I find frustrating is the idea that these systems can be revealed at all: I'd much rather the game either conceal these meters entirely, or be transparent from the start.

And if they cannot be hidden, they should be visible. All-or-nothing. No half-measures.

Death and Taxes is built to encourage multiple playthroughs, with various endings to earn and additional dialog scenes to unlock, and one might argue that the obfuscation of these meters helps keep these subsequent cycles somewhat fresh. Unfortunately each run takes a fair bit of time, mostly repeating the exact same tasks -- over and over -- with very little new content until the end (and only if you don't wind up repeating an ending). This makes the bulk of each playthrough feel the same -- so unless you're using a guide (and that's never a fun way to play) you won't even be able to start playing with a 'full deck' until your fourth run, unless you decide to play at random or by trusting your gut (in which case you're doubly likely to see repeat endings).

And ultimately I just don't think it's worth it. There's too little variation between cycles. You're always going to be treading water, mechanically moving through the same conversations and paperwork again and again just to see a handful of relatively short endings. You'd really have to fall in love with this world and these characters (of which there aren't many) in order to justify the sheer time-and-tedium necessary to experience the game in full, and frankly I did not.

Ultimately, though, Death and Taxes is still an interesting experience -- at least for one or two playthroughs. I'm glad for my time with it, even if I've zero desire to go back.

Arbitrary Rating: 7/10

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm not really sure what this game is missing: the essential humour should be there, it's a decent premise, and there are characters which are somewhat interesting. Visually it looks pretty good. I guess my core problem is that this game doesn't really have a 'core' dilemma. In papers please, you choose between being a good person and helping your family, unless you are really, really good. In this game, you choose between making the world a better place for humans, or killing everyone and making it worse ... because you are an ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥? I followed my natural instinct to be a good guy on the first play-through and got the best possible ending for humans without really thinking that hard. There isn't really much of a penalty for occasionally disobeying orders, so if you really want to at any time you can at no real cost to yourself. And the theme of how pointless and boring the administrative work is isn't expressed well enough for how short the game is.

Stick to papers please if you want the moral dilemma, or the Ace Attorney games if you want the structured narrative. This is an awkward middle ground.\t\t\t\t\t
