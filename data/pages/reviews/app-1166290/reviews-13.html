TL;DR
Death and Taxes is an incredible game for the "tourist"-style of gamer who plans to play through it once or twice, but an arduous experience for completionists who try to 100% a game and go for all achievements. The core gameplay loop is sound, but a glaring lack of certain UX elements in the game greatly diminishes replay value.

Fate's Catillion
There's a common rule applied across a lot of different fields known as the 80/20 rule. In game development, it's most often used in the context that it takes just as much time to create the first 80% of the game as it is the last 20%. Death and Taxes, in this regard, is a big victim of the 80/20 rule, having a great first 80%, but lacking refinement in the latter 20%.

My first two or three playthroughs of Death and Taxes were by far the most fun with the game. Discovering the mechanics, reading the funny news stories about your decisions, and interacting with the small host of characters was definitely interesting. However, as I was attempting to 100% of the game's achievements, I found subsequent playthroughs a lot less appealing. This is in big part due to the fact that the story beats do not change. Your actions during the game only has an impact on the ending; otherwise, the story will go on hitting each of their plot points every single time without fail.

Now, this wouldn't be so bad if Death and Taxes featured any kind of "save state", such as the one you find in Papers, Please, that allows you to play from a certain day rather than all the way back at the start of the game. Because so many of the achievements come from dialogue options on only a handful of days, not having to go through the whole game for a single dialogue option would be ideal, especially when that dialogue option doesn't even affect the ending and is just used for a quick joke. This and some other UX improvements become the glaring missing 20% that would've taken this game from good to great.

Breakdown
Pros
  1. Encyclopedia Britannica: There is no shortage of world-building in this game. So many characters have a ton of dialogue trees for anyone that are interested in digging in and chatting. Even though you might not explore every dialogue option or read every news story, the atmosphere building is great.
Cons
  1. Under the Radar: The lamp and snowglobe, which help you track your influence on the world, are two essential tools you need in the game if you're achievement hunting, and absolutely insufficient as the only real tools the player gets. There's no way to tell if someone will drop a stat by 5, or 100, and quite frankly, I don't think I would've been able to 100% the game if it weren't for 3rd party tools.
  2. Answering Machine: So many of the days visits to the main office with Fate are completely unnecessary for the game. I appreciated these being present as part of the world building, but I really wished I could skip going to the office entirely on days where the chat had no bearing on my progress.
