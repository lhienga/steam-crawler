\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe writer uses many wonky non-words.

At the beginning, Asane says she that knows Hinami is in her 20's. Later, Asane treats Hinami like a child. That destroyed any confidence I had in the writer.


Also, the use of morphine as a painkiller for a minor injury was a stupid move on the part of the writer. An application of a less addictive local analgesic would be more appropriate considering the high rate of addiction to opioids. Is the writer trying to drum up buyers for his/her illegal opioid business?

Junnosuke keeps calling Nanase a slut, when she clearly is not. I'm not her biggest fan, but I would never call her a slut. He needs to learn not to judge a book by its cover.

Rei distinctly behaves like a virgin, but her bio says that she isn't. That makes absolutely no sense.

The writer also uses too many expletives when they aren't needed, i.e. baka is translated as a**hole.

Going forward and moving forward. Those sayings disgust me. I wish visual novels would quit using them. In the future is always more appropriate than those recently concocted lines of political drivel. It's like the writer is telling us of their political affiliation. I prefer my eroge to be apolitical, thank-you very much.

Junnosuke: "A'ight I'mma head out." Ugh. The writing in this visual novel reminds me of the writing in Sankaku Renai. It's as though they share the same writer.

A superior story marred with inferior concepts and character writing. It reminds me of a VN version of AKIBA'S TRIP. Not bad, but not excellent. 7/10\t\t\t\t\t

8/10

Looking across the history of visual novels and even the broader landscape of video games, it's challenging to find a work with a utopian setting as wildly hedonistic as NUKITASHI. Yet, contrary to its daunting title and extreme world concept, The essence of this work's storyline, in reality, manages to be simultaneously hilarious and exhilarating, offering a rebellious take on traditional Japanese mainstream Jun'ai narratives. What kind of charm does this widely acclaimed work possess to make such a significant impact in the Japanese visual novel gaming scene?

The atmosphere of this work is absurd to the extreme, with a plethora of crude and vulgar jokes constantly challenging the player's tolerance. However, behind the absurd storyline lies a myriad of the author's metaphors and reflections on order, values, concepts of good and evil, and identity politics. The core of the story has considerable depth, presenting serious considerations of sociology, ethics, and politics in an absurdly light-hearted manner. To simply label it as "Nukige" would undoubtedly be an oversimplification. Besides the impressive voice acting of the main characters, what's truly commendable is the earnest and detailed voice work for every background character, combined with the chaotic BGV in the scenes and the lazy, psychedelic Acid Jazz music, offering players an experience as if they are immersed in this crazy utopian world. Furthermore, the writing quality of this work is also excellent. As a visual novel, it provides a space for players to deeply engage and immerse themselves, which is indeed rare.

While this work indeed has many unique aspects, the narrative experience it provides might not quite meet the expectations set by its 85-point median score on ErogameSpace. Besides the story becoming increasingly lackluster in the middle and late stages, the quality of the narrative across different branches varies significantly. Many plot developments are somewhat clichéd and repetitive, and the story's logic is not flawless. In terms of sociological, ethical, and political reflections on identity politics, groupthink, and concepts of good and evil, the exploration is only superficial and lacks depth. The portrayal of many main characters remains stereotypical, with character development being too superficial to elicit empathy from players, although the three supporting characters from SS are more successfully fleshed out, almost to the point of overshadowing the main cast. At the same time, the abundance of Japanese puns, subculture references, and NETA tributes in the game raises the barrier to entry, making it unfortunate that not all players can appreciate the ingenuity of these clever designs.

I still believe that NUKITASHI is an exceptional game. Its bold and unique theme and world view alone are enough to attract the majority of visual novel enthusiasts and Japanese game fans. The alternative, rebellious interpretation of traditional Japanese Jun'ai mainstream narratives is also a major highlight of this work. However, the storyline may be difficult to scrutinize, and its controversial absurd expression along with its hedonistic world view might deter some players, while for others who come purely for its "title," the relatively depth and Jun'ai mainstream storyline core might not meet their expectations. Yet, if you can accept these aspects, I believe you will find insights in this work that stands out not just in the visual novel genre, but in the history of video games as a whole.
