\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is "insanely" good. If you haven't played it, you absolutely should.

SPOILERS AHEAD (Please do not read if you haven't played the game yet)

Story-based games like this one are usually unable to keep me engaged. I think it's because the game world typically doesn't pose enough of a threat to make me care. I believe that one way to keep the player engaged is to punish them by setting their progress back if they fail. As an example, the souls games accomplish this by making your lose your souls if you die a second time before reaching your corpse.

After the first 15 minutes of this game, I thought this was going to be a walking sim due to the static, albeit gorgeous-looking, lonely world and narration voice overs in the background. Such a high focus on the audio-visual presentation with minimal UI and controls certainly made the experience immersive.

But with such simple controls and gameplay mechanics, how would the creators ensure that the player would be engaged? The player doesn't really accumulate any gold or experience as they progress that they could lose and be set back. And if the player dies, they would just respawn back at the last checkpoint. So how do you convince the player that their mistakes have a real consequence when they actually don't? Well, you lie to them lol

After introducing the player to their first combat encounter (which, you cannot really clear without failing), the game shows you that with each mistake, a black rot will grow on Senua's right arm. It then prints a big text in the sky saying "If the rot reaches Senua's head, she will die and ALL PROGRESS WILL BE LOST".

After reading that, I thought to myself, surely, they can't do that. I am enjoying the game but not enough to repeat my entire journey. I hate to admit that the game actually managed to deceive me and I truly thought that if I died X number of times, I would have to restart. This was enough to keep me on the edge of my seat (couch, really) and engaged in every combat encounter and fire trial. I did some research after finishing the game and confirmed that I was lied to lol

The main character suffers from psychosis and experiences horrifying hallucinations. The devs used visual and sound effects so effectively, that combined with the deception, even managed to make me paranoid about what the character was going through. 10/10 would be deceived again.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOverall, I loved this game and highly recommend it, even for folks who are easily scared Buy this if you're looking for a gorgeous game with a stunning presentation of its gripping story. If you don't care about story and just want good gameplay, then you should look elsewhere.

As a neurotypical (as far as I know, at least), the way this game portrays Senua's psychosis is fascinating. Throughout the game, you'll be hearing a chorus of whispering inner voices guiding you, commenting on your situation, or mocking you for ‚ô•‚ô•‚ô•‚ô•‚ô•‚ô•‚ô• up. As the game suggests itself, I highly recommend playing this with headphones to get the full experience. In addition, due to Senua's own tenuous grip on reality, you'll also find yourself wondering how much of the game is reality vs something in Senua's head.

The game's visuals are fabulous.

The gameplay doesn't quite reach the same bar as the presentation. One of the main immersive qualities of the game is that there's never a direct HUD. The game guides you towards objectives with whispering voices and pretty light effects instead of outright markers. In combat, you aren't given direct 'bars' for your HP or your focus charges, these are instead shown diagetically. The main consequence of this is that the game never directly teaches you the controls. Early parts of the game tell you to "focus", but if you don't pause the game and find the dedicated focus button, you won't be able to progress. There's not a tutorial for combat either, so you'll once again need to pause to learn your options. I went through a fair chunk of the game before realizing I had a kick option to stagger enemies. It would have been really helpful to know that when the game starts throwing enemies with shields at you. As I've hinted, the game has combat. It's overall okay, but it overall feels like less was put into it than the presentation.

Most of the game is solving environmental puzzles, mainly finding rune shapes in the surrounding scenery. The game gives you enough guidance that you'll never be stuck by something in plain sight, but the game doesn't ever do much to expand the idea.

I know more of this review is critical than positive, but I really do think this is worth playing. The story and its presentation are incredible and unique. I'm not aware of anything else quite like this. I beat the game in about 6 hours, so it's pretty short and sweet. I'll choose to take that more as a positive; better to end sooner than be padded with a bunch of crap.\t\t\t\t\t

More recent video games have been almost entirely downhill from here.

Yes, this game is probably at least partially to blame for the overabundance of mental health themes in video games that came later. Yes, the combat in this game is incredibly shallow. Yes, there is a frustrating abundance of unskippable cutscenes.

Even though this game is far from a perfect masterpiece, it deeply engages with Northern European heritage without crapping all over it or bashing the player over the head with "wHiTe MaN bAd" or randomly race-swapping a Norse goddess or making the female characters as hideously masculine as possible or such nonsense. This game even goes so far as to suggest that a woman and a man (both of Northern European heritage) can have a meaningful heterosexual romantic relationship! How old-fashioned is that!

This game is one of the benchmarks for a video game portrayal of Northern European heritage that is worth playing even though it's not quite as amazing as all the over-hyped reviews made it out to be. Pretty much everything after this one gets much worse.\t\t\t\t\t
