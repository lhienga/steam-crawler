\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhile I can't deny the influence of this game and the fact that it's way more polished than most AAA games nowadays, Hellblade: Senua's Sacrifice still left a mixed impression on me.

It surely seems like a nice experiment on how expansive of a title only a handful of people can create. However, it fails to catch you as a player, and I'll fill y'all on this later.

When creating Hellblade: Senua's Sacrifice, the developers relied on people suffering from mental disorders. In the story, you go into the world inspired by Norse mythology. The game follows Senua, a warrior who must make her way to Helheim by defeating otherworldly entities and facing their challenges to rescue the soul of her dead lover. You will be plunged into the melancholy rage and frustrated consciousness of Senua, reliving her life, hearing everything with her ears, including the crazy voices that you wish would just shut up, and seeing everything the way she sees. The most powerful thing about the game world is the atmosphere of complete, genuine madness. Delicately created locations with absolutely astounding graphics powered by Unreal Engine 4 draw you deeper and deeper into this journey until the credits start rolling.

And this is where the "game" part falls apart—in its core gameplay. The entire game consists of two things: walking around searching for runes or combat where your main enemy would actually be the camera controls instead of the dark monsters of Norse mythology, since you can't look around and are forced to be locked onto a single enemy at a time. The combat isn't too engaging; it only features two basic attacks, block/parry, and dodge. It gets really button-mashy towards the end of the game as you're getting tired of pressing a single button non-stop for minutes on end. If you're not in combat, then it's all about walking and searching for damn runes for the 100th time to unlock some door and see more story. None of this is really engaging, but I can't blame a handful of devs for at least adding and polishing this small amount of mechanics into the game. So, I guess I'll let it slide because everything else feels amazing.

This game is a great deal if you want to kick back and play some short story-based game. For 85% off on Steam, I think it's a must, and it also comes with a free VR mode, which I haven't tried, but it's cool that it's there. Everything from visuals, story, acting, sound design, PC optimization (this game would fly on any potato PC or Steam Deck), game world, and characters get an absolute S+ rank on the scale of badassitude. Sadly, the gameplay itself really drags the whole game down, and it feels more like an interactive movie, if you'd please. This game is surely a nice experiment, but sadly it barely passes a yes than no from me.
