\r\n\t\t\t\t\t"As boring as shoveling dirt..." - Me

Skip this game. You're better off buying the newer God of War game or Horizon Zero Dawn.

This takes a spot in the collection of one of the worst games I've ever played in my entire life. I actually tried to give it a chance after already disliking the mechanics but the story is boring in and of itself. It's a movie trying to be a video game and it doesnt work for the following reasons:

Walking is slow. Sprint = a Sunday morning jog

Combat and dodge mechanics are clunky, voices lose their charm very quickly, story is vague, annoying, and boring. Puzzles are gimmicky and stupid. The narration is unhelpful and borderline brain dead; monotonous.

The linear path is no problem for me, just to put that out the

This feels like someone wanted to make a movie and figured why not make it a game with zero thought process on what makes a narrative story game actually fun to play. I probably wouldnt even consider this a good movie if it was made into one due to the lack of substance within the "story telling" if you can call it that.

Playing this game feels as boring as shoveling dirt, but with the added factor of people breathing in your ears the entire time with no reason besides "oh, she's insane. Isn't that quirky?" The voices arent even helpful. They might call out enemies and then they won't. Not to mention the stupid and unnecessary feature of having to start over if you die after dying so many times.

I never worried about dying in this game and I STILL found this feature stupid. If you die in this game a lot, you shouldn't be exposed to this BS. It actively loses my interest as a guy who revels in well-written solo player story games.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is an outstanding example of why we need AA games. You play as a young Celtic woman whose lover was killed and sacrificed by the Norse. She then goes on a quest to revive him by travelling to their lands and confronting their gods. The gameplay is a combination of visual puzzles, and hack n slash combat, with a strong emphasis on narrative cohesion. It has an extraordinary degree of ludonarrative consonance. You feel very immersed in what you're doing, and in how what you're doing progresses the story.

The visual direction in this game is absolutely incredible. The sound design too, is amazing. I'm not talking about the voices in your head and so on, but rather the sound design of e.g. Valravn, the god of illusion. I could sit and listen to his theme all day. I also loved the visual puzzles. For me they felt unique as a gameplay mechanic, and solving them was satisfying. The combat too was excellent. It was fast, but heavy. And very precise. Even if it does drag a bit, especially toward the end. Sometimes diegetically you are supposed to die, and the game doesn't do the best job of communicating this.

I also found the narrative compelling. I loved all of the locations, and the journey the hero goes on. \r\n\t\t\t\t\tHellblade: Senua's Sacrifice : By Triple F

"Hellblade: Senua's Sacrifice", developed by Ninja Theory and released in 2017, stands as a testament to the creative potential of the gaming medium. This psychological action-adventure game is a masterpiece that seamlessly blends storytelling, gameplay mechanics, and audiovisual elements to create an experience that is both emotionally intense and intellectually stimulating.

From the outset, Hellblade captivates with its breathtaking visuals and meticulous attention to detail. The dark and atmospheric landscapes of Norse mythology are rendered with stunning realism. The game's dynamic weather system enhances the immersion, with rain-soaked environments and ethereal lighting contributing to the haunting atmosphere. The character models, particularly the protagonist Senua, are intricately designed, showcasing the dedication to visual fidelity.

The art direction is a standout feature, weaving a narrative through environmental storytelling. Each location feels unique, from the hauntingly beautiful Helheim to the mystical, otherworldly realm of Surt's Fire. The game's commitment to visual storytelling adds depth to the lore, creating a world that feels both tangible and surreal.

Hellblade's narrative is a psychological journey through the shattered mind of Senua, a Pict warrior haunted by psychosis. The plot revolves around Senua's quest to rescue the soul of her deceased lover, Dillion, from the Norse underworld. What sets Hellblade apart though is its meticulous portrayal of mental illness. The developers collaborated with neuroscientists and individuals experiencing psychosis, resulting in a nuanced depiction that avoids sensationalism.

The storytelling is nonlinear, with the player piecing together Senua's fragmented memories and unraveling the mystery of her past. The use of a Celtic warrior's personal struggle as the backdrop to Norse mythology adds a unique flavor to the narrative, making it both personal and epic in scale. The inclusion of Norse mythology isn't just cosmetic; it's interwoven into the core of the story, creating a compelling fusion of historical fiction and fantasy.

Hellblade successfully balances narrative-driven exploration and intense combat sequences. The combat system, though not overly complex, is fluid and weighty. Senua's movements are deliberate, emphasizing the brutality of her battles. The incorporation of permadeath, where failure to overcome challenges results in the gradual corruption of Senua's soul, heightens the stakes and amplifies the sense of tension.

Puzzles and environmental challenges are seamlessly integrated into the gameplay, requiring players to engage with the environment and use Senua's perception to progress. These elements serve as extensions of the narrative, providing insight into Senua's mental state and the challenges she faces in navigating a reality warped by psychosis.

Hellblade's audio design is nothing short of exceptional. The game utilizes binaural audio technology to simulate 3D sound, creating an immersive experience that is both haunting and visceral. The whispers, voices, and hallucinatory sounds that accompany Senua throughout her journey contribute to the disorienting and unsettling nature of the game.

The score, composed by David García Díaz and Andy LaPlegua, complements the atmosphere perfectly. From melancholic melodies to thunderous orchestral pieces, the music enhances the emotional impact of key moments. The use of diegetic sound, where the voices in Senua's head guide the player, adds a layer of interactivity to the auditory experience.

In the end, "Hellblade: Senua's Sacrifice" transcends the boundaries of conventional gaming, offering an experience that is as emotionally profound as it is intellectually stimulating. The game's commitment to portraying mental illness with sensitivity and authenticity sets it apart, creating a narrative that is not only gripping but also socially relevant. With its stunning visuals, innovative audio design, and a narrative that lingers long after the credits roll, Hellblade stands as a testament to the potential of video games as a powerful storytelling medium.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFirst off, beautiful. Amazing sound. Clearly a lot of thought was put into this experience.

That said, the actual game aspects are lacking. The game is mostly a walking simulator (nothing wrong with that), with interspersed combat and light puzzles; sometimes you interact with a door or lever or something. The combat is pretty dull, enemies don't seem to show damage really so you're just kind of fighting against an invisible health bar; and you get slow-mo boosts pretty frequently, so the best strategy seems to be spam attacks and roll or dodge intermittently as you prefer. The puzzle aspects vary but include such highlights as: walk around until a visual becomes less blurry, walk around until things line up, walk around until you are behind a gateway that shifts something you might not have been paying attention to.

The world is designed very "realistically", which means it's all kinda brown and dark and I lose myself in the maps fairly frequently. Combined with the slow movement speed this means a lot of backtracking between content. Sometimes they're nice and open up shortcuts after a boss, but areas are so samey and forgettable I can't tell which is the new shortcut.

The game has amazing narration and voice acting (cool binaural effects to simulate schizophrenia, I believe, if you're into that), including some "audio logs" you unlock from rune stones you encounter. However, the rune stones only talk to you at an audible level while you're nearby, and they're usually along the path of travel... and even if you walk, you'll move too far away to keep listening unless you stand there and listen to it. The sounds also overlap with your own voices if you encounter something plot related, so be aware that the game cares as little about understand the plot as you will come to.

I didn't even try to finish, got bored before fighting the goddess of Hel, which is quite an achievement. Next time just make a movie, folks.\t\t\t\t\t
