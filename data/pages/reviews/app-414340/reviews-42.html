\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPlaying Hellblade was an experience that gave me much, much more than I expected from it and served me as a reminder about just how wrong it is to judge something basing only on a first impression. Admittedly, I was among those who quickly dismissed this title as "just another cinematic game" when I saw the trailer, albeit promising myself I'd play it sooner or later due to the nature of its subject. So let me clear about this: whoever criticised this game by focusing exclusively on its gameplay understood nothing about making something that tries to go beyond its medium, and should avoid any attempt to approach art in general because it's clear that they lack the necessary basis and sensitivity.

Games like Hellblade are rare examples of the clear divide between the purely ludic nature of the medium and its artistic components being filled with content to the point of cementing an experience that goes way beyond its technical and designing limitations. The gameplay is essentially linear, almost on rails, with a combat so simplistic that one might think it could be avoided altogether - perfect parries are so exploitable that they can even be used against heavy attacks that couldn't normally be guarded - as well as puzzles that have interesting mechanics but are nothing spectacular. But guess what? Nobody cares. Because the game puts you in the shoes of a person whose very limitations in skill and perceptions are reflected by an oppressive and limiting game structure, structure that becomes the frame in which the player is forced to move with a feeling of distress directly proportional to the distress and pain of the protagonist. This frame contextualises and makes incredibly deep and immersive one of the most vivid, realistic and awfully painful representations of psychosis I've ever seen in my whole life.

Ninja Theory's team worked a lot with medical and psychiatric personnel, as well as people afflicted by this kind of mental issues, in order to make the proposed narrative as much believable as possible and the result is something absolutely chilling. The use of binaural sound, to be strictly experienced with headphones, is by far the most incredible aspect of the experience: the constant, obsessive voices, the noises that "guide" the exploration, the silences and the ambience compose an alienating and sometimes excruciating soundscape that can suck the player in and let them feel "there", right in the middle of it all. The way visual perceptions are constantly distorted and deconstructed is exploited in the puzzles to give the sense of "vision" of Senua, her ability to see "beyond the veil", both a symptom of her illness and the only gift that can let her face the darkness tormenting her. Blackouts, flashbacks, visions, everything is perfectly constructed to let anyone experience what "normality" is for people like Senua. A journey to Hell, both literal and figurative, not tainted by pietism nor stereotypical or stigmatising representations, that touches with great empathy and awareness universal themes like grief, abuse, segregation and fear of the "different". Its duration, 10 hours tops, is a strong point that allows for an experience as dense as possible, without diluting it with meaningless digressions.

Due to its powerful delivery and immersivity, Hellblade is an experience that I strongly recommend to anyone who feels strong enough to bear it, while at the same time I passionately advise against playing it to anyone who doesn't quite feel like it or lacks the necessary stability. You might think I'm exaggerating, but I assure you: if you're prone to certain issues or if you're very susceptible, Hellblade is something that might scar you for life. But if you do feel like you can take it, it will enrich you in a way you will not believe.\t\t\t\t\t
