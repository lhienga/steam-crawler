\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is very 'Full On' at the beginning!!

From the start, Senua is seen paddling a canoe towards this island, and she is accompanied by quite intense voices relentlessly uttering sentiments of encouragement or discouragement. To begin with the voices sound over-shouty and they also sounded over produced with this echo effect. Why am I hearing this echo, because she is not in an empty room nor is she traversing a canyon. The voices are supposed to be in her head, I know, but still the echo effect sounded rather outlandish, so to begin with, I didn't feel that it really created an immersive effect for me. Anyway, I persevered with the game, hoping to get something out of it. (Later, two or three chapters into the game the voices did seem to cool down a bit and blend in with the unfolding story better.)

Anyway, I liked the way the opening credits were buried in the landscape which surrounded Senua as she canoed deeper into the island. Just after the opening credits finish, Senua eventually arrives at a beach where she disembarks and kicks her canoe back into the sea.

At this point the game begins and there is no tutorial so you just have to try a few things out to get going, or pop into the Pause Menu and check the key configurations. This game carries an 18 PEGI certificate, so perhaps the game makers reckoned that by that age you would have already been familiar with at least a few game controls - I don't really know, I just think that they may have as well have done because no time is spent getting acquainted with them.

The landscape is a rather bleak and dystopian mass of stone steps and embankments as well as wooden-staked fencing and trenches, all tinted in a bluish-grey light, except when the fires and torches appear. Every now and again piles of corpses are depicted in copious amounts, or are seen hanging from wooden posts. You can't interact with the corpses and loot them, they just have nothing left.

To begin with I did not know why I wanted to explore this kind of environment. While it is beautifully rendered, there was nothing that I wanted to look at or get nearer to; it was just so dammed bleak and repulsive.

At some point I realised that the voices are not just an audio effect. They also serve as hints encouraging Senua to take her next steps or discourage her from seeing the way forward. Sometimes, as you move around the voices change with your actions. Sometimes the voices were exactly what I was thinking at the time, and that made me laugh.

Together with the voices there are Senua's hallucinations. Senua starts hallucinating from time to time, and with all these hallucinations, Senua can start seeing things in the environment that nobody else would notice. So it kind of becomes her ability to find her way around. The hallucinations blend in with the natural environment so it is quite easy to walk past a gateway opened up by the illusion effect, and not notice it. So you have to pace around trying to find the right angle where the illusion will open up. Sometimes the hallucinations only open up when you are in focus mode, which is both a lovely and well executed dramatic effect.

Every now and again, these renegade-like beasts appear out of thin air, start attacking her, and thereby guarding the exit to the next section. Combat is relatively easy as there are only a few moves available to you. Senua seems to be skilled in sword fighting but I don't know why any more that I knew why she is skilled in canoeing. There just seems to be a game in there somewhere.

She can do either a basic or a heavy sword attack and a melee attack. When attacking, she really seems to dive into her opponents and make a go of it, quite fearlessly. I wondered why, as she seemed quite vulnerable with her own inner anguish from the beginning, I would have thought that made her too unwell to muster an attack. Anyway I persevere as there is a game to be had here, and I noticed that the game tries to fuse together two elements of her story: the pervasive incumbent psychosis and her visit to this hell land.

The game can be played with either keyboard and mouse combination or a gamepad. I must say that I found the combat more intuitive when using a gamepad, but either way, I am still struggling to make her evade enemy attacks. She can parry quite well, and that is quite useful when you find the pace a bit too fast.

I failed dismally on my earlier combat attempts, particularly when more than one opponent arrives on the battle scene. After only a few blows Senua was knocked to the ground, and I thought my game ended there, and it will actually end quite brutally if you do nothing. However, I also learned by trial and error that she could also get up off the ground after being knocked down. I am currently unsure what causes her to stand up and jump attack her opponent, I just keep pressing some buttons randomly and if I am quick she gets up and pounces on her opponent. This is quite a relief, as she now can last longer in combat and fight more aggressively while doing so, all at the touch of a button.

As well as voices, visual hallucinations and combat, this game features lore stones hidden in the environment which are activated by Senua standing near them and focusing. The lore stones tell the story and provide the objectives, so you need to listen to them carefully enough, or do as I did, and switch on the subtitles to follow them.

The voice behind each lore stone sounded over acted to me. They sounded like an actor projecting themselves at a theatre in a Samuel Beckett play, even though I am just sitting there listening with my headphones on, inches away from the screen.

Quibbles aside, I think there is a game in here somewhere. I have been at it for 5 hours and I am at the point where Senua is tracking down Valravn. I am not sure if that is good progress, or what. I just like stopping off and trying to solve some of these environmental illusions that unlock the way to the next section. I keep going, wanting to know what will happen to the anguish stricken Senua, and looking for the possibility that this dank and putrid environment is going to give me my sanity back before the game is over.

Recommended if you are so inclined!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a difficult one to review. I like the atmosphere, the lore, and the setting used here. The backdrop of Norse mythology is always interesting if handled right. Hellblade takes a less interpretive approach in contrast to something like God of War, and, to my knowledge, actually tells the accurate stories of several characters from the Norse pantheon while progressing Senua’s personal story.
The so-claimed authentic portrayal of psychosis is tough to experience, but it’s an experience nonetheless and certainly made me feel like having a better understanding of the psychological hell a person with this condition has to go through. I actually think it mirrors the literal hell Senua traverses in a very interesting way and teaches a great lesson of endurance, self-acceptance, and loss. The story is well-told, well-acted, and well-directed… but I can’t recommend this game. Frankly, because I don’t think it works as one.

It’s a very linear game, which should be no problem, as some of my favorite games ever (like, for example, Uncharted) are linear through and through. However, this takes away a certain type of freedom given to the player, which I think can only be compensated for by making a gameplay loop that inspires player choice–whether this is in moment-to-moment action, or inventive puzzle-solving. Games like Uncharted encourage you to make decisions that decide how you progress through a given section, leading up to a big set piece. Which enemy will you take down first? Do you go action or stealth? How will you solve this puzzle?
In my opinion, Hellblade asks none of these questions.

Combat consists of dodge, parry, strike, slow time, and repeat. The small amount of enemy variety only changes the order in which you will do this sequence. Every battle is set in the same empty combat arena, only with a new backdrop each time. You will have to pay attention not to be pinned between two enemies, but other than that, movement or positioning doesn’t matter. This is just not fun to me; it’s one-dimensional and boring.

Puzzles are intriguing at first, but two hours in, you’ve pretty much seen all the game has to offer in that regard. Sure, the symbols, paths, and objects are cleverly hidden in the environment, but no matter how cunningly placed they are, eventually, they will fall flat with repetition. It’s like being shown a magic trick and then being shown the same magic trick over and over. By the end, you’re still being shown the same trick, but the magic is gone.

I understand that a video game can provide a certain and different kind of immersion and insight through the eyes of a person with mental illness, but I truly believe this story would work better as a psychological drama/thriller rather than a game. That is, unless they massively improve on the gameplay...
I usually discern whether I like a game or not by whether I’m longing to play it or to finish it. I wanted to see Hellblade: Senua’s Sacrifice to its end. Unfortunately, I just didn’t want to play it.\t\t\t\t\t
