\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEasily among the most captivating, atmospheric, phenomenal, visually impressive AA storytelling of last gen (good graphics is in the art direction, people). This title will have you sit there in silence when the credits roll and it will be on your mind for a long time, even more so after a second playthrough.

You will be engaging in melee combat and puzzle-solving a lot in this game, but the intricacies of those aspects aren't the real focus here, you shouldn't go into this title expecting a mechanically advanced gameplay loop, all aspects of "gameplay" in 'Hellblade: Senua's Sacrifce' is there to serve the story and to give you a peek in the mind of a grieving, mentally ill Senua. Allow yourself to be immersed and in the shoes of the character.

The playthrough is quite linear if that's something you care about, the story is short and you can likely complete it in around 8 hours, my hour count is simply me replaying the game because I remembered it so fondly, and I hope it will do the same for you. If you've read my review on 'A Plague Tale: Innocence', you can probably point out some similarities here, that is because as I've stated in that review, if you enjoy one of these two titles, you are near guaranteed to also like the other.

However, there will be significantly more moments in Hellblade where you will feel frustrated compared to Innocence, but in a rather... good way, depends how you see it. The puzzles are the main source of frustration throughout my playthroughs of this title, "they make no sense" or "there is no rhyme or reason" is how I would describe them, but as I got accustomed to the method of storytelling in this game, the "nonsensical" puzzles start to feel as though they were intentional (they probably are) and true to what would be Senua's perspective, the puzzles don't make sense because nothing makes sense to Senua, you are not to play a game and control a character, you ARE Senua, you are to think like Senua and see the world the way Senua would. It is "unfun" *to me*, but in a good way, strange, I know. The frustration only made me more immersed and more empathetic for the character.

Should you get it?

I'm not going to act like I know *exactly* what people with mental illnesses as depicted in the game go through, but this title strikes me as among the most effective attempts as depicting mental illness in media (mental health professionals, scientists, and those with various mental illnesses were consulted for the development of this game), and I commend Ninja Theory for taking the risks to create this masterpiece and spread awareness in the process. As cheesy as it sounds, Senua's Sacrifice is the epitome of "video games as a form of art". This title is a must-play.
