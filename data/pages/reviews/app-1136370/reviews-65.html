\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBear and Breakfast is an aesthetically charming but somehow deeply cynical and unpleasant game. If you hoped to actually run a sweet bed and breakfast you can customize to your liking, I regret to inform you that you're instead building a slate of sad AirBnBs to exacting specifications delivered through a speaker embedded inside an inflatable shark mascot (yes, really). I suppose you could ignore those instructions and do whatever you want instead, but seeing how unlocks and story progression are tied to obeying Fin's directions, you'd certainly have to work your way up to it.

I don't know. I wanted to like this game and tried to be generous with it, but it didn't reward me much for doing so. The controls are poorly communicated and the interface is cluttered. The environments are beautiful, but to what end? You wander around mashing the button to pick up resources so you can build the next tier of furniture to accept the next tier of guests, constantly smashing into walls because the Venn diagram of "shrubbery I can walk through" and "shrubbery I cannot walk through" is a circle. Characters exist. Sometimes they talk. Five hours in, they don't have much to say. There's a story, presumably, but getting to it involves constructing yet another AirBnB, and I can't think of anything I want to do less.

[Edit 6/14/2023: I decided to see this game through to its end with a couple friends. I think ultimately the game's fundamental failure is that the management sim feels like an afterthought, despite how many hours you're obligated to spend on it and despite a bear running a bed and breakfast being the entire conceit of the game.

Normally, the challenge and motivation in management sims comes from strategizing about your upgrade path. You want to make money so you can unlock the cool new thing that lets you make more money. Progression is baked into the act of playing the sim.

Bear and Breakfast gets it backwards. An NPC will go, "Here's your cool new thing. Go install it." And then when you install it and come back, they'll go, "Great! Now host five more guests." And then you host five more guests and come back and they go, "Great! Here's an upgraded version of the thing. Go install two of them." This quickly warps managing your locations from an activity that is rewarding in itself to an obstacle you have to overcome to progress. By the time the game starts to open up, you've unlocked everything there is to unlock and are dying to be done.

I've seen other negative reviews suggest that the dull, repetitive gameplay could be forgiven if the visual novel components were stronger or better paced. I disagree. I think overvaluing the (admittedly weak) narrative is what made the game like this in the first place. Why else would it end by shunting the management sim off to the side so four brand new characters can loredump at you for the final hour? It's easy to look at how all the plot suddenly happens in the back third and think the narrative could have used another revision pass. It's more useful to look at how basically everything up to Pinefall is an extended tutorial and realize that the game's pacing problems are much, much more ingrained than that.

This is a game that doesn't really understand what it's trying to be, and it shows. Spend your time and money elsewhere.]\t\t\t\t\t

Pros:
- the bear is simply adorable!
- good build-up of difficulty in the hotel management aspect
- after a while, you'll be able to manage all your B&Bs remotely

Cons:
