\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf Steam's ratings system were a 1-5 scheme like most normal platforms, then I would rate this a 3. So, more "on the fence" than "do not recommend." So, with that out of the way...

This game is enjoyable for about 15-20 hours of whimsical characters, an interesting coming-of-age story line and great sound design (with the help of fmod). It's a genuine joy to wander around the folksy, rustic countryside interacting with all these oddball anthropomorphic animals.

However, there are a few things that are still a bit screwy, at the moment. For instance, once you unlock staff for your resorts, when you have to move the associated workstation to re-do your layout, you have to re-specify everything that the staff member does. There is also a pretty sharp knee in the curve of quest goals. It increases in difficulty more or less linearly at first, then you suddenly have to grind up 50k coins for this old lady called Barbara. Which is fairly difficult and takes quite a while.

So, by the time I got to the last hotel/resort, I straight up didn't feel like completing quests anymore, or even laying out more bedrooms, on account of the screwy staff workstations issue. It's a good game, but it's very difficult to see it through to completion (and I am down to the achievements that have been unlocked by less than 10% of players.)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn its better moments, Bear and Breakfast is a pleasant, lite management game that will easily engross players in its combination of foraging, fetch questing and interior design. Many of the characters are charming and their interactions will likely get quite a few laughs from those who give this game a chance. There is enough variety in the rooms you can build and the objects you can place in them that there will be vastly different ways that players will choose to design their resorts, especially the latter two, Winterberry and Pinefall.

Unfortunately, with as many hours as I've put into the game, I cannot say that it is a flawless experience.

While playing, I've experienced a multitude of annoying bugs. Bugs like the guests being unable to get to their beds despite the player character, Hank the bear, being able to reach them without issue, or the bathrooms being permanently unavailable without anybody using them, which I would have to fix by detaching and reattaching the doors to them. There were times when the trash service would fail to actually clean up messes, requiring the player to either do it themselves or reactivate the service. The guests would never use the movie theater despite the room being considered functional by the game. Worst of all, towards the end of the game I lost the ability to cook food in Pinefall. Any attempt to do so would display a bugged version of the cooking menu that couldn't be closed, softlocking the game.

Speaking of cooking, I felt that the game's story could've used some more time in the oven. The developer clearly recognized the value of having some sort of overarching plot to keep the player interested as they progressed throughout the game, and it does do that well enough. However, I felt like the story was unsure of what it wanted to be and that made some of the events feel tonally inconsistent. Like it didn't really know if it wanted to be commit to being family friendly, or lean more into adult humor, or have a serious storyline with tragedies and stakes. Towards the end of the game there is a point where the player is supposed to be emotionally invested in something that happens to Hank. This scene was hurt by the characters using the same goofy, comedic expressions they always do and it creates a disconnect between the text and the imagery.

Also of note is the ending itself, which turns out to be much more of a whimper than a bang. The game builds up a backstory that involves the aftermath of a war, dead siblings and the machinations of the godly beings that are responsible for it and all of it just culminates in knocking down one last progress barrier, talking to a character and... nothing. No epilogue for the characters, no credits, not even a "thank you for playing!" message. The ostensible villain of the game is never seen, heard from or confronted. If it weren't for the achievements popping I would've struggled to know if I had accomplished something at all. It's like some game developers want to have their cake and eat it too. They want to have an engrossing story with loss and struggle and something to fight for, but if the game isn't about fighting or competition there's not much the story can do but fizzle out.

All that said, I would still recommend this game for people who want to interact with funny animals while building up a small hospitality empire. Just, maybe wait for some updates and bug fixes if you want a smoother experience.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI am indifferent about this game. The graphics are cute, the story's meh (the NPC are almost like forgettable decorations, you interact with them for a quest and that's it), the soundtrack is fine, and the gameplay was fine early game but towards the end is quite questionable...

final verdict: Neutral. Bear and Breakfast doesn't give me enough incentive to complete the game. the early game was quite fun, but toward the end, it really feels like a chore. buy on sale.

this game is a strange combination of a resort management game and a life sim. early on you are given 1 resort to manage. in my imagination, as a mgmt game don't you want to make the most efficient and profitable resort there is? well, no. bc there's no reward for doing so. managing resorts gets you gold, and the only use of gold is to upgrade your resort. after a certain threshold, gold will be pointless. you can't even decorate with gold!

so what do you do in this game? what is ur biggest motivation to progress? well, the story. the main quest. i'm not saying that this is bad but relying on the main quest as a reward discourages players to upgrade previous resorts that are no longer related to the main quest, because there's not much reward to it!

we need more things to do with gold. what about this: tie the main quest progression to gold instead. for ex, you need to buy the property first, and then upgrade it. in this way, players would be motivated to make every resort efficient and THEN they will purchase new resorts. and they will find it more rewarding to think & work on the management aspects of the game and not just work on fetch quests over and over again...

atm i really cheesed this game. i upgraded my resorts to meet a certain threshold that allows me to progress in the main quest and that's it. i never take care of previous resorts anymore. which is sad. i had fun but i feel like most parts of this game just don't cllick with me.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAesthetically this game is lovely, as is the music. Over all it's a fun game, but it's not without it's problems. The first is that honestly, the gameplay loop gets a bit boring by the end. From start to finish you basically just do the same thing over and over again, and I can't say that mechanically it changes enough to really feel any different. That said, this didn't really set in too hard till just before the final area.

The big thing is the games ending, or honestly, lack there of. What I've seen dubbed as the "Epilogue Quests" were, first and foremost, a tremendously tedious slog of running back and forth between areas with no fast travel and the nearest kitchen. That in and of itself sucked. The other problem was that...well firstly this portion of the story came out of almost nowhere. There was VERY little to no foreshadowing on these events. It didn't really make any sense, and ultimately raised more questions than it did answers. Over all that last stretch just kinda felt like I had stumbled into a completely different game that had nothing to do with what I'd just spent several hours playing. Which left me feeling....dissatisfied. The game up to that point is plenty enjoyable, it just didn't stick the landing.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game is really cute and relaxing, the characters are fun and it has so many puns and even a few meme references.

The couple of downsides are you cannot sell or delete your old furniture and end up having to cram it random trash cans etc as your inventory fills up very quickly. Also some in game days felt like I couldn't do anything but wander between locations until I had enough resources to craft items or wait for visitors to finish their stays.

Overall, it's super cute and I do recommend if you like cozy management/decorating style games

Update Oct 2022: So I recently found that you can sell items but only one at a time in a not very obvious place. I'm not sure if it was always there or was just added with an update. I unlocked all properties but the game has now become very grindy especially having to save 50000 coins for Barbara and the main story with the Gods just seems like the creators felt they needed to add a plot near the end. It was so rushed and anti-climatic I didn't see thje point of including it in the game. Some characters just do not make a reappearance or talk to you once you have done certain missions which just seems a waste. (Anni, Will, your mum?)


It's still a cute game but it's very grindy and I can understand why not many people reach the end content. 5/10\t\t\t\t\t
