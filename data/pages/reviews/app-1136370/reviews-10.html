\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn an era where it seems many cozy management sim is trying to replicate the energy of a Harvest Moon or a Stardew Valley, Bear and Breakfast stands out thanks to it's unique camping premise, inviting art direction, and charming story beats. Hank the bear makes for a lovable protagonist. He's effortlessly charming both in conversation and in his simple walking animations. He's upbeat, optimistic, and caring; helping to carry and balance the game's charming forest landscapes and it's witty characters.

Aesthetically it's top notch thank to the art direction of Ioana Șopov. It mixes saturated color palletes with rounded shapes and rendering that feels simultaneously storybook and cartoon. Likewise the music by Andrew Landry is never obtrusive, but strums along perfectly as you feel immersed in the lush backdrops.

For story, the game follows Hank in his attempts to revive tourism in the valley he calls home. Along the way he meets a colorful cast of characters all eager to help to some degree as well as faint echoes of the tumultuous past of the land that drove out all tourism years ago. It's a story about leading with your heart, helping others, and finding ways to heal by both remembering the past and embracing the present. As it goes you'll find the lore to get surprisingly deeper, though it never pushes too far so as to get convoluted. Most of your journey will be playful and brisk, but there will be soft emotional beats that will surprise you by the time you're invested.

The gameplay revolves around a mix of collecting, resource management, and the sort of adventuring you might expect from some of the best cozy sim games. If you enjoyed foraging, talking with other characters, and managing the intricacies of the farms in other sim games, then I think you'll enjoy managing the many resorts and cabins to be found in BaB. The game does a good job of easing you into new mechanics, the quest system is pretty simple and has no time limits, and there's plenty of rewards and things to do once you get a handle on things. The UI has been updated recently and feels pretty smooth, with plenty of visual indicators that are unobtrusive.

I think my only complaint is that there are parts of the game where even with the upgrades available, there can be some long waits. If you're diligent with gathering, cooking, etc... then you'll find days where the only thing there is to do is waist for guests to generate income/valuables, and eventually leave. There's little you can do to speed this up since you can only skip the nights once the in game clock hits 9 pm which seems a bit of a step back from other sims that let you sleep whenever. Likewise, while you can upgrade Hanks speed a smidgen, he's still noticeably slow in the late game when you're running errands across the world map and running through locations that don't use the fast travel system.

Still those are minor complaints and shouldn't really affect you once you're wrapped up in the game. Bear and Breakfast is simply put a fun game with a LOT of heart to it. The gameplay is solid, the aesthetics polished, and the world and its characters lovable. If you want a fun game you can relax to while feeling like your efforts are being rewarded, give Bear and Breakfast a play. It's wonderful. (and a big thank you to the team behind the game. I had a lot of fun playing, and I look forward to any future updates. Wishing you good health and success!)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game is very cute but in my opinion its on the fence leaning towards not very good. I've only got 2 hours into the game and it still feels like it hasn't really started to let me roam free and do my own thing. The start of the game is abysmally slow and going through the beginning of the game is just as slow. At first it's just slow text that cant be skipped as it gives you a tutorial on movement in an empty void. Following that is more tutorials on everything like every 10 minutes. You're given quests to act as tutorial quests but whenever you see something new that you want to investigate you usually cant interact with it until the game tells you that you can. See a shop front or something? Sorry, you can't use this yet. This game does one of my biggest pet peeves when it comes to design and that's to make really painfully slow tutorials that are absolutely everywhere. They could just teach you everything about building and designing a house but instead we're gonna make you build a bed and a bedroom first and then send you to like 3 different maps to collect items for a different quest so you then can actually do the bed and breakfast thing afterwards. But even then after you complete that tutorial we're gonna send you to other places to do other things that have nothing to do with the bed and breakfast because we're gonna give you a second one almost immediately after getting the 1st one set up. The first like 30 minutes of the game alone is just mashing through text to get to tutorials so you can get to the game that has tutorials.

It's a cute game and it has a solid idea but overall I feel like, especially for full price, you can find yourself on the fence of deciding whether or not it was a worthy purchase. Heck even at 35% off I feel like I should have camped it out for maybe a 50% or higher. The art style is cute, the gameplay is straight forward as a concept, but the controls feel clunky and it just feels like it takes way too long to get started up. If you're someone who likes to get down and dirty immediately like how Stardew Valley just throws you into its world and tells you to have at it, you're not gonna enjoy the first couple hours of the game where you're basically fetch questing while being taught the core gameplay mechanics in between the fetch quests.\t\t\t\t\t
