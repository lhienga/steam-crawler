\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really enjoyed this game, it is relaxing and cute resource management game where you are a bear named Hank. Your job is to build hotels to attract humans and make money while learning about the stories of the people/animals of the valley. My favorite parts were seeing the cute art when learning more about characters and organizing the rooms in my hotels.

If you are like me and heightened expectations give you the heebie jeebies, I encourage you to actually do the management quests and not leave them until the end because the rewards for them give you so many quality of life changing tools.

I would not recommend this game if you do not like resource management games or games where you have to walk around to do things because this game requires that. I personally like those things so I had lots of fun.

There are only two things I would change about this game if I could. 1) For quests that require a certain amount of things to happen I wish there was a counter keeping track of how many more you had to do (eg: 10/50). The second thing I would change is cooking, the recipes are not in alphabetical order or by food points, or by anything else I can tell. I wish there was a way to organize them or search within them, it would make cooking in the game a lot more fun and less stressful.

Overall, it is one of my favorite games and I hope to see more from these devs in the future.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWaste of time. Both the gameplay and the story are half-baked, distracting from one another, leading to an experience that's just very clearly an attempt to get you playing as long as possible by any means.

In regards to the gameplay- it's fun initially. However, you aren't actually running anything; you set up the resort and just sit as you wait for valuables and cash to accumulate to do anything else, and occasionally when you get upgrades you wait for cash to be able to buy those upgrades and run around collecting craft material to make those upgrades. The upgrades waste space and by endgame it's difficult to set up your resorts in a way that the quests want while also doing what you want with the resorts.
By the third resort you are also focusing on getting employees unlocked because it's then that you start to "run" your resorts. By run, it means that you waste your time collecting food to cook and then put in the buffet to get eaten. There is no fun minigame for this cooking. You just pick a recipe, click "auto fill" or just manually put each object into the slots required for the dish, and click 'cook,' where you then have to literally just wait like a microtransaction-filled app while the thing cooks with no input from you. You have to just watch a seconds-long timer, longer depending on the amount of dishes you make. There is no fun behind it and it's a giant waste of time. Once you move part that resort, not only do you have to start doing that for multiple resorts (until you get the employee that cooks for you), you also need to buy a heater and constantly collect new wood to put in the heater. Again, a waste of time.
There is no "running your own bed and breakfast" here. It's just sitting around or collecting ♥♥♥♥. I was hoping to play a cute bear and be able to run an inn and fix beds and cook food for people staying at the inn. That is not what was given.

PS: The rat that collects valuables will be necessary, as the furniture set-up that is required for quests ensures that you won't be able to navigate into the rooms to collect all the trash. Big oversight and just a giant pain in the ass.

Story:
Lackluster. The characters are interesting and yet all they do is send you on fetch quests across the map to artificially elongate the game time, especially near the end of the game. There is a quest that requires you raise 50,000 coins. This will mean literally just letting the game run while you do absolutely nothing unless you want to waste your time on the non-gameplay of the cooking game and wood collecting I mentioned above. There are quests that require collecting things that don't spawn the requested amount of at one time, so you have to sit and wait at spawn for an in-game day or longer. The story ends abruptly, clearly trying to set up for a sequel after paragraphs of exposition.

Literally by the end of the game I was either just letting the game run in the background while I did other things to wait for coins or valuables to add up or to wait for quests about the number of reviews or customers to fulfill, or I was torturously having to just hold down the A button as I made my way across large swaths of empty map with no quick travel option because the end of the game requires you to continue going into this dark wood back and forth for ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥.

Great concept. Terrible execution. I believe people either did not play this game more than a couple hours or were so enamored with the style that they overlooked the terrible gameplay, because I can't imagine why else this game is not at least "mixed."\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tShould rename the game:
"Backwoods Bear 4 Beginners: Build Beautiful Buggy Bedrooms, Bake Bussin' Breakfast, Bag Bountiful Bundles... But Becomes Boring"
Here's why:

PROS:
● Effortless Entry: Beginner-friendly with well-paced quests and accessible gameplay, for the most part.
● Relaxing Routine: Simple repetitive tasks, long days, optional sleep, and a lack of time pressure make for a laid-back and easy-to-comprehend experience.
● Convenient Commutes: Although walking between locations and collecting resources is essential, the convenience of easily unlocking fast travel through repaired bus stops is a plus.
● Adorable Aesthetics: The cute visuals contribute to an inviting and enjoyable environment. It's hard not to enjoy the aesthetic, and I look forward to them expanding on it more.

CONS:
● Glitches Galore: Unfortunately, the game is not without its fair share of technical issues. Players may encounter occasional freezes, map-breaking during cutscenes, malfunctioning rooms in inns, and illogical interactions by NPCs and guests, including peculiar pathing issues.
● Quality of Life Quandaries: Inns requiring daily visits, challenging building/decorating mechanics, and illogical interactions among guests and NPCs start to weigh more on you as you play. I found myself yearning for the ability to use only my mouse while playing, which is entirely possible due to the small amount of keybinds needed.
● Control Conundrums: Unintuitive controls and keybinds, partially functional key remapping for KnM, a lack of quick loot options despite promises, frustrating cooking mechanics, the absence of a manual save option, etc. Much can be done to make the game's mechanics more rewarding and fun without losing challenge.
● Grindy Gameplay: Post-farming lacks variety, offering little change in activities and no tangible rewards. The game progressively becomes a material collecting simulator without meaningful NPC interactions or surprises.
● Stale Storyline: Minimal NPC interactions, lack of depth, akin to Stardew Valley but with much less substance. Repetitive quests offer a hollow narrative due to their focus on mundane objectives already accomplished, providing no additional challenge.
● Decorative Dilemma: Cute decorations but complicated cost, storage challenges, and some impractical choices make them less rewarding, especially for players aiming to min/max their gameplay. Players who find joy in bedroom aesthetics may hinder meeting guest requirements. You can't have a good-looking room and please all guests.

As you can see, there are more cons than pros. It's challenging because Bear and Breakfast's charm in the simplicity of the mundane is also what holds it back from being a truly enjoyable game. Provided the development team listens to these reviews, it can eventually become a big hit. As for now, while it offers a visually pleasing and fun experience for newcomers, the overall story/gameplay shortcomings and lack of Quality of Life will likely leave more experienced players craving more substance and polish.

I do recommend it *for those easily pleased* but only get it on a sale.\t\t\t\t\t
