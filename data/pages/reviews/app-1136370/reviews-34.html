\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBear and Breakfast is one of those games that I really, really didn't want to admit to myself that I'm not enjoying. I mean - look at it! You play as a fluffy, bouncy, derpy bear! And you build inns and stuff! And there's some mysterious darkness going on in the background! What's not to love? How can this be anything but amazing?

And as always, it's prudent to remember that concept doesn't mean absolutely anything. One of the most popular game franchises is about a plumber who's tripping balls on shrooms. And the cute bear... well, yes, he's cute, but you're not going to spend the entire game just starting at him and dressing him up, will you? I mean, you could, but you need to progress to unlock new outfits, so you still gotta play the game... so let's get to it.

What do you do here?
Two things: Walk around, talk to people, collect stuff for construction or to exchange for furniture... and build rooms in your facilities, decorate them, and admit customers. So, adventure and management.

And here we have the first problem: it feels like the game cannot make up its mind whether its an adventure game with a management element, or a management game with an adventure element. It tries to give equal attention to both; but both of them feel shallow and... I don't know, incomplete? An afterthought?

Adventure
Let's take a look at the adventure part first. At first it seems like your typical RPG style game without combat. You walk around, collect items, talk to NPCs, do quests, explore, find new places. Experience the plot.

After my 7 hours of gameplay I can comfortably say that I haven't seen a plot this forced for quite some time. The reason as to why you're doing all of this is pretty much a hand wave. All the characters I've seen so far are static and single-use. The writing feels like high school RPG Maker project. Perhaps the most jarring is a certain quirk of Hank, the main character. Usually, he talks in a "I'm an idiot and well aware of this!" kind of way. But then sometimes he switches over to being a snarky smartass. What? Those two things aren't exactly compatible, I think. Oh, and if there's anything like a narrative arc then I've yet to see it, apart from some minor foreshadowing at the very start of the game.

Otherwise, exploration is just... uninteresting. The area is revealed to you right away, quests always have a pointer to where you need to go (you can untrack them at least), and the only interesting things to find are materials, NPC questgivers and an occasional monument which you can repair for more popularity.

Management
Considering that the adventure part is so flat, you'd think that management is what the game is really about. But... well, I also found it flat and a chore more than anything. For the record, I have 200 hours in Rimworld and 108 hours in Prison Architect, so I'd say I'm familiar with this kind of management game. So how does this compare?

First, you have to collect materials in adventure mode. That's fine by me. Then you have to craft/buy whatever furniture is needed for the rooms that you want to build, and then you build the rooms themselves, and put as many decorations as you can fit. Then you go to the front desk and accept guests...

And that's pretty much it.

Later on you also have to cook for the guests, but the cooking system is unwieldy and seems clever on paper, but in practice it's a chore more than anything else. By the time I got to that part I realised I simply can't play this anymore. So it's very possible that the game gets better later on - I hear something about being able to hire staff to manage stuff for you instead, so you don't even need to admit guests and cook - but I don't feel like slogging through whatever there is to get to the good part.

Miscellaneous
I found some other minor issues that normally I wouldn't bother complaining about, but here they made the already unfun experience even worse.

- I managed to get stuck on the stairs in such a way that leaving them would make me go down the stairs instead. Hard to explain.
- getting stuck on furniture was rather annoying, but that's on me for packing the rooms
- when you encounter a new character you get a little picture with some text explaining who they are - but when the picture disappears you see some old leftover dialogue from before
- the music. Oooof, was it bad. I don't think I heard a single piece that I liked
- you can untrack quests, but whenever you progress or get a new quest the tracking for that particular quest comes on anyway. So I had to spend quite some time deselecting quests
- the shark mascot that's your main questgiver is such an unpleasant guy, I think he'd get an award for the most unlikeable NPC of at least two years

There's more, but I think that's enough to give you the idea.

Conclusion
Look at my profile pic. I'm a freaking bear. I want to see more bear representation in games. Which is to say: I really wanted to like this game. And believe me, I tried. But, according to one of my Steam friends, "I turned on the game, played for some 15 minutes, and then turned it off". And sadly, that's how it was - and not because I was busy, but because 15 minutes were usually enough to make me bored.

But hey. Lots of people seem to like this game. Maybe if you think that the writing isn't so important, the general outline of a plot is enough, and shallow management is good actually because it makes things pleasantly simple... well, I could see you enjoying this. But if you're looking for a well-crafted game, I think you should look elsewhere.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is perfect.

Not technically perfect, mind you - it's got a couple of bugs, a few hinky things that you get used to pretty fast. But that's not what I mean.

To me, this game is simply perfect. The art style is ADORABLE and so consistent. The story is lighthearted and funny but also has plenty of depth to it if you want to let it take you there.

It's got some minor time pressure so it's not like you're going to idly wander around staring into space, but neither is it OMG WHAT TIME IS IT? IS THE STORE STILL OPEN? WHAT IF I DON'T MAKE IT BACK TO MY FARMHOUSE AND PASS OUT??? levels of anxiety.

There are enough decorations and pieces of furniture to choose from without it being completely overwhelming. The design aspect is challenging enough to keep me engaged but never so challenging that I get frustrated.

If you've got a couple of days to a week where you're sick, this game will keep you perfect company until you feel better.\t\t\t\t\t
