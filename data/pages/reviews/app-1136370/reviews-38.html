\r\n\t\t\t\t\tShort version if you're on the fence:
If a low stakes, no pressure pseudo-management pseudo-interior-designer simulation game with chill music, cute graphics and (sometimes) funny dialogue sounds like your thing, and you don't mind back-tracking, sitting around waiting for things to happen, and a lack of a cohesive and satisfying story, then Bear & Breakfast is a nice little wholesome time sink that provides a decent amount of content for its price.

Long version, because after 100%'ing this game with my own enjoyment ranging from absolute captivation to wishing I was working instead, I feel compelled to:
I have never seen a game as sloppy and disrespectful of your time as this one. I had to check the store page to verify that this game wasn't still in early access, and to my disbelief, it's not. This game is ROUGH. There's a skeleton of a fantastic game with neat ideas somewhere in there, but it's buried under the sheer mediocrity of every half-ass unpolished system you slog through in the vain hope of the game recovering from its initial dip.

The gist of the game is you set up B&B's in several areas, with each new B&B introducing new mechanics. In each area, you cycle through the process of renovating the space, creating bedrooms/bathrooms/etc, collecting materials to craft or purchase furniture, decorating the rooms, renting them out to humans for gold to purchase more upgrades, finish side quests for more money & richer guests, repeat until the next area. This gameplay loop is amazing for the first few areas, but as the holes in the game design reveal themselves and the intrinsic reward of keeping things looking nice and maintaining a 5-star rating rapidly becomes outweighed by the desire to make progress, the game plummets in quality and never manages to save its fall.

Rooms that you rent out have ratings for comfort, decoration, hygiene, food, and heat, introduced in each new area, and accompanied by their own problems. Customers you rent out to have their own minimums for each value, sometimes more picky about certain values than others.
-Comfort/decoration are determined by the furniture placed in the room, but the decoration value of furniture is so arbitrary and always absurdly low, regardless of its cost, that you are forced to cram whatever crap the floor and wall space allow for in order to satisfy the needier customers while still having enough rooms to rent out and make a reasonable profit per night.
- Hygiene points are determined by the quality of bathroom fixtures. Problem is, some customers have hygiene requirements that are not currently possible in the game ("Hygienic" guests require 45 points when the highest possible is 36). How this is still an issue 6 months after release is beyond me.
- Food introduces the hands down WORST cooking system I've seen in any game. It's perplexing how the devs put in so many different ingredients and recipes, just to opt for such a UI-unfriendly cooking minigame that makes you wait 15-20 seconds for each dish batch to cook. But hey, maybe some people find enjoyment in spending all in-game day getting ingredients and cooking dishes, just for all of it to disappear overnight thanks to the guests larping as garbage disposals
- Heat is a shameless gold sink to slow your progress by making you decide between wasting time foraging for fuel or wasting money buying it, along with lowering your overall building decoration score a considerable amount and, thus, forcing you even more to cram whatever junk you can afford into each room to make up for the lost decoration score. The heaters are also extremely volatile, requiring you to use multiple per building and creating situations where the temps can exceed 110F. It's probably for the best that guest requirements only have a minimum for heat, but it's still laughable that 114F is perfectly fine, but god forbid the thermostat reads 74.

Every corner reeks of wanting to waste your time for no reason other than pad out the game's runtime.
