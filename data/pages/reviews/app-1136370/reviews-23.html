\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGame is alright, but not great.

If you're looking for a Sim similar to stardew valley, this is not it. The game doesn't really even scratch that particular itch even remotely. It's a half a fetch quest game and half a very limited resort builder. You get access to several properties overtime and each resort has a very limited amount of space to build in. Which at first isn't a big deal, but as time goes on your higher grade furniture gets bigger. So you're left to either try to cram furniture and decorations into your pre-made rooms, or rebuild everything from scratch and have less rooms in general. The property never expands or improves, just the furniture you need to use to get higher pay and achieve quest goals. that part of the game is.. unfortunately really unsatisfying.

The quest part of the game also is sort of unsatisfying. Several quests just dead end. the two biggest main quests end in such abrupt and lame ways. Barbara just ditches you and we never hear from her again. Alright i guess. but the final quest with the gods ends even more vapidly. You find out about two gods having a battle over the area, the bad god is literally never seen. it's alluded to that they're still out there. what happened to him? Who knows. I don't. They mention a bear god but we never meet them. Not only that but we also never meet the frog god, unless it's supposed to be the alligator witch, and we just never do anything more with any of them. We just off and never think about it again. Vlad the bird never leaves his cave of delusions, and Cian just rots in his cabin. These quests all felt so empty and unfinished it makes me believe they were left that way for a future DLC. Which fine, but it just adds to the very unsatisfying soup they're serving. They also have some moon logic, they frequently tell you to ' find someone' and there's no hint or markers as to who 'someone' might be. I also finished the main plot before I finished any side stuff because I was stuck on Julia's greenhouse quest, I didn't realize the plants she wanted could be bought in the trash menu.

Cooking also isn't great. You have very limited inventory space, even with the upgrade you get later on. You are expected to have at least 4 items for most early/intermediate items and 6+ something for later ones. Then you make a generous serving of food or drinks that can only stack up to 10. You'll quickly run out of space and fridges can't hold more then I think item stacks at the highest level. For higher level customer satisfaction you need those ingredient heavy dishes. You'll also need items you can't find in the wild. The game never explains that you need to buy these items from the desert merchant. You just have to know I guess. He previously only sells fuel so there's not a lot of reason to keep checking his shop when you don't even need fuel until close to the end of the game, and another npc in the area you need it in sells it too!

inventory space is sort of abysmal. Even with the upgrade I found myself constantly running out of space and my bear stash was always full. The bear stash is even smaller then your regular inventory too. You'll quickly end up with excess wood, lower level decorations and food ingredients without recourse. You can't sell these items either. You have to throw them away, one stack at a time. This again was confusing and very poorly explained. You need to drag a stack directly from your inventory, Then just keep dragging stack by stack into it. It's weirdly hidden and monotonous.

Trash is not balanced as a currency. You'll run out of it so fast and it's the only way to buy decorations. Also decorations are kinda ludicrously priced until you've reached end game and gotten the npc who collects it automatically. But by then you don't need it. The game is over and there's really no reason to keep playing. unless you just want to muck around in making perfect lvl 5 rooms for some reason.

The npc staff is very helpful, but if you get tripped up like I did you just play a frustrating mid mid game without them, and only get them after the game is over.

Overall, it's ok, but I wouldn't buy this unless it's on sale, or they fix alot of stuff. It's just kinda.. empty. Not one part of this game is particularly satisfying or enjoyable. The story is cute but also very basic and bare bones. They just leave every interesting thing about this place as surface level as possible. Great idea, really bad execution.

PS. There was one good thing, Sabine.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI 100%ed this game in a week I loved it so much.

Pros: The dialogue is fun and witty, surprising me enough at times to try and voice act each of the characters while also making me look forward to talking to them again. By the end, I knew them all by name (except Wade who I would call Gabe lol) and it made the end game guessing quests pretty fun! The mechanics are easy to get a handle on, the art style is cute, and the character designs are very fitting to their personalities. I never felt like I ran out of space for the required special areas in my buildings, and felt like I optimized my bedroom space really well, which made me feel smart as a player! I liked how you could just afk or work on other missions while you earned passive income to put back into your residences. As someone with anxiety, seemingly missing opportunities to do better can be a huge turn off for me playing a game. I don't feel that with Bear and Breakfast! I also was constantly and pleasantly surprised with the way the lore unfolded!

Mids: I'm calling this between pros and cons because these were things I was only a little bothered by in my play through. Setting up dining halls was a little annoying as the AI humans will sometimes get stuck on the tables's seats but they won't get stuck anywhere else with a similar small amount of space. This might be because of the animation for them to leave the seat, but I don't know. The kitchen furniture in the end game does not really fit in the minimum required space, but it's understandable since, well, you're in large mansions by that point. The last area in the game (I will not spoil this wonderful experience) feels pretty darn far away from the people you need to talk to, as it doesn't have a bus stop. I mean, it makes sense lore wise, but the walk is a little tedious. Also, some decorations feel smaller than the grid area it takes to put them down, but that's just semantics.

Cons: Iron plates in the late game are a nightmare to get! I feel like they should be added to the end-game shop, which already has mahogany plates and silk thread. When trying to grind for iron plates, it feels like it takes (approximately?) 2 in game days for them to spawn, or if you sleep in a different area then come back. By the end game feels really bothersome when I can already buy the rest of the resources I need from the shop.

TLDR; I recommend this game to people who like to progress at their own pace, decorate everywhere, cute and memorable animal characters, and surprisingly mysterious storytelling. It has a great lesson to tell that is both heartwarming and striking, and I feel somehow needs to include Smokey the Bear. I am pretty sure only people who played can understand what that means, and if not, this game will still surprise you!
