\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhen a game’s title is a pun, you know it’s going to be good. Bear and Breakfast is an adventure into a lovable community, one that is slightly reminiscent of another found in Stardew Valley. But the people of Bear and Breakfast have their own message they’d like to share, though it may take a while to be delivered. In the meantime, you can find yourself in love with the area and its residents.

Game World
The setting of Bear and Breakfast finds itself in a long-deserted valley, ravaged by the aftermath of a fire. This valley holds the usual plethora of biomes that is only available in a fictionalized world, ranging from a snowy wasteland to a dry desert and all within walking distance for the loveable bear named Hank, the player character. None of these biomes suffer from a lack of ambiance, however, each is filled with rattles from snakes, elk bugles and howling harmonies from wolves and wind. They are simultaneously full of replenishable resources, including a concerning amount of fuel for the arsonist’s paradise, as well as being populated by a person or two.

Characters
A cartoonish one-dimensional cast with comical dialogue that emoldens their personality, that is the inhabitants of the valley in a nutshell. Despite each resident having just a few overly-emphasized traits, they do not fail to garner empathy and emotion. It’s easy to laugh along with Jax, the “hardened and comical” mastiff— his words. It’s equally as easy to feel pity for the dim-witted player character, Hank, who is in possession of a heart of gold despite his short-comings. These traits are also explained in a very direct and concise description shortly after meeting the character in question, just in case they weren’t obvious enough. But while the characters may seem simple, the dialogue is anything but that and it very easily caught me off guard. Most notable of these lines of dialogue were the thought-provoking, almost philosophical ones, though it also includes movie references, adult jokes and wise-cracks. But although every character may seem wholesome and honest, there is one exception and it comes off as a total shock— possibly because every character is so straight-forward that it becomes hard to imagine that anyone would have ulterior motives, even if the warning signs were there for me to see. Such is the beauty in this, everyone is so innocent and welcoming that it becomes so disheartening when the one bad apple starts to show its rot and by that time, you’ve already grown attached to them.

Resort Management
The business side of things is slightly less enjoyable, though not by much. It all starts with a several bedroom one outhouse cabin. You could reasonably assume that guests would use this outhouse to alleviate themselves, but they instead seem to hold it in for the duration of their one or two day stay; but it does serve as some nice foreshadowing. Soon, you’re providing guests with bedrooms, bathrooms, dining areas, heating and entertainment out of your own expenses and, possibly, your own labor. Some labor, such as booking the guests, is easy-enough to perform. Cleaning, on the other hand, will have you working constantly, picking up litter after your guests because NOBODY knows how to throw away their trash and EVERYONE seems to be a never-ending stream of rubbish and then THEY get upset when THEIR GARBAGE IS ON THE FLOOR, BUT MY MORAL CRUSADE IS COMPLICATED BY A RACCOON SELLING NECESSARY FURNITURE IN GARBAGE, MAKING IT A TYPE OF CURRENCY! sigh I have a problem. These rage-inducing trash-centered moral dilemmas are alleviated with helpful staff hired through the power of a well-cooked meal (how cute) but this does ruin the pacing of Bear and Breakfast, turning it into quite a dull waiting game. At that point, a Spotify playlist would be highly recommended to waste away the in-game days so that you may sleep away the night and repeat the process. But my monkey-brain was still quite satisfied to say “OOOooo, money number go up!”

Quests
The missions in Bear and Breakfast can be broken down into three categories, each with a distinct purpose and point at which they’re available:
Meal quests - Mostly transactional-based missions that add a little more charm in the game due to their requirements. A character will offer to work at your resorts, give an item or information in exchange for some food in their belly. Oftentimes it gives some character exposition.
“Never-ending cabin quests” - These are only never-ending in the sense that there’s just an excessive amount of them. They will reward you with higher-paying customers in exchange for improving your cabins or booking more guests. Though they may be redundant, they’re also entirely optional so I never felt too negatively about them. I would liken them to the side quests in an RPG that you never get around to finishing, but they do make life significantly easier if you choose to do them and then automate the cabin to the best of your abilities.
Main quests - Rebuilding the valley’s resorts and bringing tourists to the area is a commonality throughout nearly all the main missions, culminating in the symbolic repair of the lighthouse. The biggest criticism I could levy against the main questline is the lack of a through point connecting it to the central theme from beginning to end. For much of the game, I was unaware if there was any point to what I was doing, outside of just making successful businesses.

Environmentalism
This theme wasn’t apparent to me until mid-to-late game, which can arguably be a source of huge criticism. Even so, I was absolutely astonished when the ultimate message of this entire game was delivered. Most talk about the environment devolves into hopelessness and despair, into talk of doom and gloom. It’s hard to talk about the environment and not lose your faith in humanity when topics such as climate change, species extinction or habitat destruction come up. What’s unique in the theme of Bear and Breakfast is a restoration of your hope and faith when it presents the same pessimistic, accusatory and perhaps realistic view, yet challenges it with an optimistic and reassuring perspective from an unexpected source.

In a way, I would say that the game’s conclusion made my time playing feel unusually productive. Both because there was a message to all of it that I can learn from and because I can now go on and recommend it to people.

Conclusion
Bear and Breakfast has an arguable rough start. Initially speaking, decent gameplay is the only thing holding the game together along with its hilarious dialogue, but it did successfully carry me on through to become interested in the disastrous fire and eventually the closure to its tale. Though this point of interest occurs later in the story than it should. Thus, there is a fair amount of criticism that can be levied against this and the pacing issue, however it is only truthful for me to say that Bear and Breakfast has been one of my favorite games to play this year and certainly my favorite to review.\t\t\t\t\t
