\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe great thing about roguelikes/lites is that you play the same challenging game over and over again in quick successions with each being slightly different than the last until you finally FINALLY beat the game with a great sense of accomplishment. This game is receiving a ton of love (and for good reason) but my personal experience was a fairly boring grind that was over in two loooong attempts. It is very clear that this game has a good hook and a great addictive foundation....the problem is that it just isn't very hard or that much fun. The game warns you from the onset that it has a brutal difficulty so I played on the vanilla setting thinking I was playing something along the lines of Frostpunk. Sure enough, I did die in fairly short order but then on my second attempt, I breezed through the game without much difficulty other than nearly killing myself a couple of times (accidentally creating heat or cold, when I thought it was the Domains doing it - shame there isn't a warning). Once you understand the mechanics, its fairly easy to be hundreds of points ahead of any dangerous outcomes. Also, the game warns you not to populate your town with too many Pips at around 80, but I felt this was perhaps for poor game balancing purposes or maybe graphical performance more than anything. I grew my village to 120 and the more I had, the more I was able to accomplish - it just took longer to complete every turn (I think I was at 30 hours total gameplay when I finished). I might consider playing this game again in a years time after some finessing but for now, I can't say that I recommend it. Having said that, I actually still think you should buy it and play at a harder difficulty setting. It is my hope this negative review will have an impact with the developer to hopefully make some changes (and don't worry, its a blip on the radar against your Overwhelmingly Positive review score). Congrats on the successful release - I look forward to trying it again someday.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOverall I recommend the game. I really liked the core worker and building placement mechanics, as well as the escalating prophecy goals. It's really fun to figure out the mechanics and see your village grow, and you always have some impending doom to prepare for.

On the negative side: I'm a huge fan of rogue-likes, but I didn't enjoy this games take on the genre. The games are far too long for a roguelike (Seems to average around 10 hours, maybe more? I haven't played enough to be sure, but it's definitely *long*.) It's not very fun to lose a game after 5+ hours, and similarly not fun to high-roll into a good position early on and grind out the next 5+ hours being sure of a win. Managing the workers is also a bit fiddly especially towards the end game, it's got some automated smarts, but not enough, or maybe they're bugged.

Ultimately I don't think it's a game I'll play much more of, but I really enjoyed my time with it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is going to be an uncensored spoiler review of the game! Read at your own peril!

It's hard for me to say whether or not this game has great replayability. As you proceed to unlock more memories, you gain access to new Elders that seem to have different methods of play and buildable structures that's unique to each of them, but it's hard to tell exactly how much each character differs from each other. And that is to say, if it's even worth going through the effort of unlocking all of the characters and playing an entire game with each to see.

DotAGE demands a lot of your time. It's a turn-based roguelike city-builder that can take upwards of 8-10+ hours to reach the end, and that's if you don't end up losing beforehand.

Considering that a lot of buildings are locked behind memories and prior research paths, it's likely to happen, too: Different buildings require being around certain buildings or tiles of terrain, require specific paths to lead to dwellings, and produce more if around certain buildings or tiles.

While it is fun and interesting to find out how these different tiles interact with each other, sometimes you find yourself making a suboptimal mistake without even realizing it, setting yourself up for failure in the future or making you need to adjust it. On the other hand, if you have a good idea of what's required to make the most optimal placements, you're aces.

Reading is absolutely your friend in this game! Read everything that each new blueprint unlocks! It'll tell you what the building is made out of, where it needs to be placed, what path it needs to what sort of building, and what gives it extra production. Playing the game a second time will give you a definite advantage, because you already have an understanding for how each tile best interacts with each other.

I'm not sure if I would have beaten the game on normal on my first try if it weren't for me briefly watching someone else play a few episodes first. I already had a basic understanding of the domains and how the tile placement mechanic worked, and I walked in knowing the basics of what to expect. I figured out that you can actually prepare for the omen beforehand by increasing your affinity with the domain before the omen arrives, which definitely simplifies matters quite a bit.

The domains themselves are the main challenge that the game provides. It challenges your ability to multi-task, prioritize and properly prepare for when they arrive, yet it also gives you a chance to win against the domain depending on how well you've prepared against it. Even if you were only able to match the domain, you're given a chance to win: I only lost against a domain two or three times at most in my playthrough, and it was mostly through my own incompetence.

Even so, the punishments for failing an omen check doesn't put you in an unwinnable position for the most part. The Cold Domain check I failed only had a pip steal some of my food over the winter, while the big Cataclysm event that absolutely SHOOK me killed five of my pips in one go. Two of them were long-lost twins who only found each other a month ago!!! How awful! I was able to pick myself up after that, but it was honestly an eye-opener and certainly a fantastic experience.

What I find very interesting about this mechanic is that the game rewards you with the possibility of defeating an omen even if you weren't able to fully beat it, which validates the effort that you made to do so. Even if you can't beat it entirely, it serves as a massive motivator to try anyway.

A lot of the ways that you defeat omens is obtainable through the Research tree through various means--if you know what omens you need to defeat and you know where to upgrade from there, you can work your way up from there. I struggled in my second summer because I somehow forgot to upgrade my puddles into ponds, and I almost failed the last winter because I couldn't figure out how to make advanced tools for the clothes mechanic.

The same could be said for my Hope generation during the Apocalypse--everything else, I was absolutely aces with, but as I proceeded through the game, it seemed that the Fear domain was the most intense of them all. By the end of it, I was losing pace with with the fear generation and was frantically trying to build a cottage for bourgeois pips to learn how to become actors, and then built a second book generation place with a thinkers post nearby, and then I realized that forest tiles get in the way of the tile vision for the book maker and had to cut down the forest... it was a lot.

But I was able to eventually outpace the Fear generation with my Hope! As the days passed and all of my pips were focused on Hope generation, I felt victorious! Despite all of my scrambling about to compensate for my oversights, I was somehow able to pull out 100% on all Domains in the apocalypse and secure a victory! And it tasted sweet. I doubt I'd have been able to manage it if a LOT of boar tiles hadn't spawned.

Aside from that, the big struggle is food. But once you have a few fields, barley, a windmill and a bakery nearby, you're more or less set on food for a good while. Considering how much work and organization it takes to get that kind of setup, it feels good and rewarding to be able to worry about 25 units of food less every day! It's especially helpful if you have other food sources and are able to build warehouses and upgrade your storage.

When all's said and done, though, how was the pacing of the game? My playthrough was about 10-12 hours, if I remember correctly. While it was fun for the first 6 or 7, it became difficult for me to come back to when things started to look undesirable. It's difficult for me to call it worthwhile if I spend multiple hours on a game only for it to be destroyed and have to start from scratch, especially in a game like this.

After I got through that minor roadblock, the struggle became less about the fear of losing everything and more about conflating it with how much of a repetition each turn seemed to be. I don't know how to describe it, but at some point, it seemed exhausting, and between breaks, I started to dread coming back to the game.

I think it was probably the fatigue of how long the game had been going on at that point: I probably would've set it down for a much longer period of time if I didn't realize that there WAS an end to the game while checking the Omen scroll.

I was able to unlock the Shaman after the first playthrough, and I did start a game as her, but I'm honestly not sure if I'm going to play a whole new game. It's not a bad game, but despite all of the replayability elements incorporated in it--the memories, the blueprints, the new characters, and the largest domain threat being different each time--I don't really see much replayability value in it... personally.

I'm sure that replaying the game as different characters appeals to other people! But it doesn't appeal to me. And that's okay. Because I still had fun playing through the game as Matius. :)

As an aside, some people have said that chickens are an early-game trap. It is kind of true! But I ended up harvesting their eggs until it reached my storage capacity and then deleted the pens so I'd stop wasting seeds on them. Also, one of the pips that died ended up getting raised from the dead and later became one of my bourgeois actors.\t\t\t\t\t
