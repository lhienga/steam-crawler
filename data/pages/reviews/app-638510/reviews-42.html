\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHere's the rub; it's a neat game, solid premise, and pretty easy to understand. It's got good art direction, it MOSTLY makes sense, and hell it's got a demo.

But. It's also pretty slow, mostly on account of having little animations to go through between turns - even when sped up. It's also extremely easy, to the point of it getting boring - and completing a run is like 6-8~ hours, mostly because of the animations. Early on it's rough, the challenge being to produce enough Numbers to satisfy a challenge quota, but you can beat every single bit of difficulty in this just by having more people. The penalty for having people is needing house/space and food, but the maps are huge so housing is kind of irrelevant and it turns out having more people also solves food, too. Bizarrely, the end game techs all resolve around using fancy people to use less people, with their own requirements, but you can just put down two of whatever lesser building instead and be just fine. It's not entirely an issue of the chosen difficulty, either, as I was besting challenges by margins of hundreds of points and the difficulty doesn't scale that far. That's ignoring that winning the challenge quotas also gets you boons and lets you snowball.

In my first game I haven't been under any threat about forty minutes into a seven hour run. It isn't a particularly compelling game for something that paints itself as a challenging builder. Still cute tho\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA medieval city builder "board game" very similar to Banished, but with turns, and a tech tree that becomes randomized as you unlock more and more techs.

This means that your runs feel fresh. For example, you need to produce fresh water for your citizens ("pips").
However, in one run your water tech will be underground wells, whereas in another it will be mountain springs. This forces you to change your strategy and layout depending on which techs are available.

In addition, the "prophecy" system is EXCELLENT. You are warned ahead of time of which disasters lie ahead, and must build (and staff!) the requisite buildings. However, this pulls your citizens off other important tasks. So maybe, you decide to just "take the hit" and endure the disaster. The disaster "tug-of-war meters" reset to zero after each event, so even if you get hit with one bad event, you won't fall behind if you have a building ready for the next one.

Another long-term goal is to save food for winter. Thankfully, winter is roughly half the length of the other three seasons, so it feels attainable (excellent design choice!).

But you still need a lot of food. And warehouses. And extra food to LITERALLY sacrifice to the gods of nature to prevent earthquakes.

....and then when your pips starve, they fill the "disease" bar, usually culminating in a giant starvation death event that wipes out half your village. You laugh, you cry, you hit the "give up" button, unlock more stuff, and try again...

Support your indie devs. This one is worth your time.

One last note: You can try the demo, but know that it doesn't contain some options for fast animations. I almost didn't pick up the game because the turns were slow in the demo (not too surprising since a dev isn't always going to keep the demo up-to-date). The dev seems pretty responsive to feedback too, but give them time.\t\t\t\t\t
