\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is very fun and approachable.
I really like how simple managing a town is in this game.
The turn based nature of the game really allows it to be less stressful and more about minimizing issues and addressing problems thoughtfully.
I really like how as the game progresses you are introduced to new mechanics.
I found placement of buildings to really help maximize your labor.
The music is really good, but sometimes repetitive.
One frustration I had, during my first play through I ran up against several barriers that were unable to be addressed since I have not recovered enough memories yet. I couldn't make a graveyard for quiet a while in my first play through, so I got some rotten events and before I knew it I had 20 bodies in a mass grave constantly draining my pips' overall health. But losing is also suppose to be fun, and now this barrier will not be present in my play through, but I did find it to be a frustration that does present itself in a couple different ways until you unlock some of the general buildings.
Notifications are excessive during your early play through, since you will be unlocking a lot of basics.
My biggest issues so far really come from some of the early unlocking of mechanics.

I still have lots to unlock, this game surprises me each time I play, and I've really enjoyed it.\t\t\t\t\t

If I was ever to promote any game in the world, it would be this one. So much love, time, and pure artistic talent has been poured into this game over 9 years of development.

First off, I do not usually play games like this, maybe except Age of Empires is the closest relative, so please take my review with a grain of "stupidity salt."

The large-scale random events absolutely make or break your run-through are perfect. They require skill, knowledge of where to place your pips for future events, and a bit of random fun. "THAT WAS THE ONE THING I DIDN'T PREPARE FOR!" Type-esc.

The smaller random events keep things interesting between the large-scale events. Things such as randomly finding basic tools (thank you for this), to your most important bourgeoisie randomly jumping into a lake, which is hilarious and fun.

The stunning visual changes between Spring, Summer, Fall, Winter, and ApOcAlYpSe is an amazing colorful, and utterly vivid experience one could only describe as Vincent Van Gogh's most famous paintings, I mean seriously, it keeps you wanting to progress into the next season, even if your fruit trees which you just planted lose all their fruit.

Before my last one, I would like to say, fire events go crazy. You thought you were safe, but don't forget about the heat events...

Last but not least, complexity. You think everything is all fun and games until you're stopping the world from ending because some gods want to smite an old guy (or all elder types, I haven't even unlocked them all yet!). There's tons of different jobs, societal levels like the poor and rich, events where pips become crippled by different sicknesses (like explosive diarrhea), stages of production to each material and food, farming, animals, herding, etc. It's amazing and has CRAZY replayability.

The only negative I COULD have said about the game is the weird bug where random events would choose baby pips to become love interests to my original 4 pips which are now years older than the baby pip, but, it was fixed within the first update! I haven't yet to run into a game-breaking or ending bug over my 20 hours, which tells you how much time and love has been poured into this cute, dangerous, fun game. It's not common anymore to have a game like this come out in the condition it's in, so props to the creator.

Anyways, buy this game!

p.s. I forgot to add, but the Omen system is amazing. It forces you to have a negative event, no matter how prepared you are for normal events. It makes you get backup plans, like getting medical centers to cure pips.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDotage is outstanding. It's a roguelite city builder, not something I've seen before. (well, more accurately, a village-builder, the playfield isn't huge.)

The central conceit is that your character is elderly, with minor powers of prophecy, and you have led some of your people to a new homestead. There are two main problems: your dotard's memory is poor, so he's forgotten most of how to make a village, and the Apocalypse is coming. No pressure.

The economy is intricately balanced; you're constantly pulling against one or more limitations. It's a very impressive example of good game design; you can tell the author worked on it for ages. You can never do everything you want, something is always limiting you, so you're balancing between fixing bottlenecks, generating food and construction resources, generating new labor by creating children, and creating resources to fight off bad events.

As you progress, your Elder will warn you about incoming events; he never knows what they are, but he knows their type. (at least heat, cold, fear, and disease; there may be more.) Your village needs to create resources of the opposite type to try to turn the event from bad to good; the more your total of good resources exceeds the game-generated total of bad resources, the better your chance of success. Good events give you minor advantages; bad events give you problems.

Once a season or so, a Doom comes upon you, which is much stronger; fight that off, and you'll get a village Boon, which is a powerful permanent buff. (at least to the current village, I don't think it applies to later ones.) So far, I haven't failed at a Doom, so I don't know what kind of debuff you get if you blow it.

And finally, after maybe twenty-ish seasons, you come to the Apocalypse. I have no idea how that works, I haven't gotten that far yet. I may update this review later with more info if I think it's important, but I doubt it'll change my opinion.

At 6.6 hours, I am *thoroughly* hooked. Dotage is a prime example of the One More Turn genre. It is *hard* to put this thing down, even though it's turn-based, and you can quit and save at any time.

I haven't been this gamelocked in quite a long time. This thing is well worth full price.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDotage is a turn-based village builder with a lot of depth and charm. The premise is that the apocalypse is coming, and you play the village elder that has prophesied this turn of events. There are no direct enemies to fight, but you'll be working against different elements (sickness, fear, cataclysm, temperature) that produce bad events for your village every few days.

Luckily, the visions the elder has means you'll know ahead of time when these bad evens will occur. And they can be avoided (and even turned into *good* events) by producing a certain amount of resources as a counter to their specific element; medicine against sickness, heat against cold, etc. But creating these resources in sufficient amounts will take clever placement of production buildings. Some of these work better when placed next to (or close by) other particular buildings, and since every building takes up a tile on the grid there's a strategic element to placement that often requires thinking ahead. Furthermore, you'll need too make sure you have enough villagers to keep all your buildings occupied and functioning, but not so many that you'll starve during winter.

There are a lot of different events that will influence the life and death of your villagers (or 'pips' as the game calls them), and failing too many events by not producing enough of a certain resource can doom your run; causing a situation that is hard to bounce back from as everything spirals into doom. The game even actively recommends restarting in such a case.

Bad events can range from minor inconvenience (a sick pip, locusts, etc.) to absolutely devastating your village or economy (murders, earthquake, famine and many more), depending how far along in the game you are and how many events you failed prior. But produce enough resources to counter the event and you might instead see a *good* event; like new animals migrate into your territory or one of your pips finding some crucial resources.

There's a surprising depth into the game, with an enormous amount of resources and buildings and different production branches. Luckily the game eases you into it very well; foremost by having a long run-specific research tree to unlock - but also because during each run you score 'memory' points that you can use to unlock entirely new game features. And there are loads of them; new events, new maladies, traits for your pips, new buildings, crops, animals and characters, Every time you fail a run and restart you can be confident that your next run will contain entirely new content. Pips will become more interesting too; able to gain traits like falling in love or becoming clumsy - that further distinguish them from each other, and may even attract particular future events.

The interface is also worth mentioning; very minimal, yet the game conveys information surprisingly well. No extended menus or complicated interfaces, almost everything can be done in one or two clicks. Still, a single run can last for many hours, especially when there are so many decisions to make each turn. This isn't a game about 'waiting it out', especially in the early stages - you'll often be fighting against the clock (or turn-counter); there's an apocalypse coming, after all.

All in all, I highly recommend this game. It's a captivating turn-based city builder that oozes charm. The cute aesthetic makes it a joy to watch your little pips run around interacting with each other and doing their jobs (or occasionally getting into trouble after you end a turn).

But be warned, it definitely has that 'one more turn'-vibe going on. The one that will cause a realization that it's far past your bedtime all of a sudden.\t\t\t\t\t
