\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt takes a little while to get comfortable, but once you're rolling, this game is addictive.

I recommend breezing through the tutorial, and then heading over to "Practice" to start working your way up from Easy to the most difficult setting, after which you can jump into multiplayer without getting obliterated.

And obliterated you will be if you jump in too soon, because Mechabellum isn't just about countering units, it's about positioning, knowing how fast your different units move, knowing which units are worth upgrading at each stage of the battle, which upgrades to choose, how to mix your units and more.

You might find yourself winning the first two rounds only to get zerged in the third because your enemy realized you're light on area dominance and too heavily invested in single-target damage. You might have the right mix of units, but you're not positioning them correctly to take advantage of their synergy.

Or you might end up in a tight battle where you and your opponent are trading rounds and just barely edging each other out, stretching into late rounds when you've both got the resources to field the largest and most powerful units in significant numbers, so your last round is a clash between huge, lumbering mechs.

The game forces you to think and adapt. Although you and your opponent are offered the same purchasable upgrades and one-time effects between rounds, your options are determined randomly. That means you can't play the exact same type of "class" every game, and you can't always use the same tricks.

Mechabellum is in great shape for an Early Access game. There are some rough spots: The devs should contract an editor who is a native English speaker to smooth over and rewrite tooltips, hints and tutorial prompts. There are also complaints about dominant combinations and balance, which I hope will be addressed as the devs fine-tune the game. The game could use some cosmetic enhancements as well, making it easier to tell at a glance who owns a particular unit, and perhaps adding some customization options so players can pick things like unit color, laser color, markings/livery on their mechs, etc.

Overall it's worth checking out for the pure fun of its gameplay, and I'm hopeful it just gets better from here.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWorth playing if you enjoy the genre, that being said would not recommend this to a casual audience. I had my fun with it, reaching about top 1500 in the leaderboard which felt like enough time to see how the game would play long term. Also, most of my issues could be remedied with patches, so take the thumbs down with a grain of salt.

Main issue is that game's are often determined at the outset as leveling units comes with kills, which results in a snowballing advantage from the outset. While clever play can theoretically get you back in, any experienced player will typically build in a way that doesn't lose this advantage. Given games can take 30+ minutes, it can be extremely tedious to both slowly whittle down an opponent or yourself without any hope of a comeback. An easy fix to this would be to significantly reduce the amount of stats gained by leveling units, as it is far and away the most cost effective action in game.

Second main problem is unit balance is somewhat nonexistent. While theoretically each unit has niche applications, some are basically unusable while others are so centralizing you will see them every game, and use them yourself half as much. While all games are imbalanced by nature, the extremely limited number of units means this results in an extremely stale play experience.\t\t\t\t\t
