\r\n\t\t\t\t\tMechabellum is an exceptional title that harkens back to a very old DOS game called "Robot Crusaders." The premise of that ancient title was fairly simple: use strategic placement of a variety of different robot types, and let the bots duke it out when you hit play. Given that it was, well, built in DOS, the concept was fairly limited and rudimentary, but I always have carried a special fondness for it just because of the unique and cool premise of how that type of game works.

Enter Mechabellum some 30 years later with modern technology and the internet. Suddenly, that simplistic premise is fleshed out in a much more complex fashion. The game is ultimately very simple to play: you gain a certain amount of "supply" each round, and get more each round that the last lasts. You have a large pallet of units you can place each round, each costing a certain amount of supply, which range from squads of mechanical infantry to gigantic factories churning out other machines on the regular. When you're done playing units, you hit play and they fight it out with a fairly rudimentary -- but subsequently very easy to understand and plan around -- AI and the player who wins damages the enemy health bar based on the supply cost of units remaining. Between each round both players get to select from 4 random choices that may or may not cost supply, varying from tactical orbital missiles to unit attachments to special deployment bonuses for units.

And that's really it. There are a few more layers, such as limits to deployment counts every round and unit levels and upgrades, but the overall gist is really that simple. And this is where Mechabellum really just shines -- within that framework is an elaborate, intricate interplay of well-thought out units that do anything but fall into simplistic paradigms. Your simple infantry unit has just as much tactical relevance as your gigantic flying battleship, as each unit fills a wide spectrum of rolls depending on what you want it to do. Your simple mechanical infantry can be equipped with shields to eat one instance of a big shot from a big unit, making it great frontline fodder, or you can choose to have them act as high-dps ranged gunners who can chew through enemy air squads. Want to get a bit more tactical? Use them as deep-strike fodder to pull the enemy away from where they'd rather be, giving you the precious seconds needed for the rest of your army to deliver a devastating blow. All this can vary, curious enough, on whether you place the squad two tiles forward and backward, and can substantially change when you start putting different combinations together.

What ultimately erupts from this framework is a deep, layered tactical strategy game that doesn't require RSI-inducing mouse clicking or splitting your attention over 15 different places to achieve "optimal" play. Instead, you have a fairly limited -- but certainly not imposing or overly restrictive -- set of time on each round to make choice decisions that heavily impact how the following round will pan out. It's really quite brilliant, and no two games ever feel the same even if they may have similar starting premises from time to time.

Where Mechabellum ultimately shines, however, is that it has fully functional real-time streaming of other games and a ladder system which seems quite fair as to how it places you against other players. I've seen some people complain that is has microtransactions, but these are strictly purely cosmetic and certainly do not feel gougey in terms of pricing compared to other titles. Not that I really care, mind you, as I don't really pay for that kind of thing in the vast majority of circumstances, but the game's an absolute steal for $10 on sale and is well worth the investment and the developers are very open on this very steam page about how they want their pricing structure to go.

This is definitely a hidden gem that doesn't get a lot of traction but is well worth your money if tactical strategy is your thing. Recommended.\t\t\t\t\t
