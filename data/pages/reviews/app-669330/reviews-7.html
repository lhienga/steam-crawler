\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI gave the chaotic fun patch a chance, I really did, but it's just not fun any more.

The stated intent of the free unit drops was to provide an opportunity to explore different strategies at different stages of the game, but the unpredictability of what can drop and how much those drops are worth strongly encourages a super conservative style of play.

Normally you can look at your opponent's board and determine what holes you might leave open on your own to emphasize your own strengths, but now you _have_ to account for the likely possibility that they will be able to drop one or more high-levelled units out of nowhere to exploit that, so the only remaining option (if you don't want to lose) is to play as safely as possible, sell the free units to build up your safe board, until you eventually get a set of free high-level units offered that allow you to one-shot your opponent.

The devs have been clear that this is the intended direction they want to go.\t\t\t\t\t

Mechabellum is a weird beast.

TL;DR:
So here we have a paradox: a great game with a great gameplay, which actually ends up being frustrating because it seemingly gatekeeps itself so hard it won't let-in the larger player base it needs to be more enjoyable.
If you have a friend on which you can take revenge in real life, get the game on sales. If you don't, and are not generally a masochist, don't bother.

If you're like me and are into RTS but have a tendency to bleed from your ears...

...when multitasking, which stops you from being both at the table and in the kitchen, you'll be happy to know Paramecha has fired every cook and replaced them with über eats, leaving all the focus for this half of your brain that can actually coordinate things at the tablefield. Battetable. FappleTield. F-you, I'm trying.

This game is an auto battler, you place your units on a board while keeping in mind their power, speed, range and possible upgrades, validate your orders and watch the fight unravel before you, and become the observer, back to you childlike wonder at the flaming, exploding carnage happening down below.

I'm a bit of a stickler when it comes to games that do everything for you...

...and usually I'd prefer watching a drunk streamer playing a slot machine than have my gameplay taken over by automatic pathing and attack. But Mechapacem is very, very good at balancing units and, in the end, it feel like playing a board game with a friend, complete with the feeling of utter hatred and injustice.

And that's where the game shines:

When you have a friend. Just like in real life you get to try together all the borderline experiments you can think about, without having to experience too much post fight clarity. Most importantly, you don't care whether or or not you end up on top, and you can do it while catching up on life, the universe and how Netflix really got worse now.

If you DON'T have friends...

...or if they're busy watching slot machine streamers, then be ready to suffer because your initial love for Parapachem Mechabellum will be reciprocated like your dad did in the 70's: it will put you in extreme emotional distress thinking that the repeated trauma will make a responsible adult out of you.

Yes, there is a tutorial. "Here, it's a plane, it flies. Look, this big thing is big but there's a bigger one, now go be an hero, the game's on and I don't have time for this nonsense". There is also a single player campaign that sets you against decreasingly dumb bots until you're bored and decide to try playing with strangers.

And that's when it hits you: you suck...

...and unless you're accustomed to strategy games, you will suck forever. The player base if large enough to find a fight after 15-ish seconds, which are the only part of the game that are safe for you, on the account of this same player base being mainly composed of game veterans, or game veterans painted blue and wearing white beanies.

Every victory will feel like a major achievement, which will get trampled by the next 70 losses by way of players having an hour count an order of magnitude above yours, with absolutely no options to learn by playing other than exploring the 1001 strategies effective to get your bottom booted until it grows enough calluses to absorb the kicks.

To add insult to injury...

...every few rounds introduces bonus units for you to tinker with. It would be a welcome addition as an extra mode, but as a beginner it segments every match into usually 3 definite phases "I think I got my strategy right", "Those units don't fit the match's meta at all" and, "Oh I'm thoroughly buggered".
That's the result of the developers trying to be creative and spice up the mix, but it's become so spicy that the community is now fighting itself over who's too lame to appreciate an extra drop of bear mace.

There is another genre where getting-in as a n00b is extremely punishing: fighting games. But those games have evolved as a different type of dad, who doesn't force you into competition when you don't have the skill yet and let's you practice on your own, that's to say, they have a casual cue and an actual single player campaign.

So here we have a paradox:

A great game with a great gameplay, which actually ends up being frustrating because it seemingly gatekeeps itself so hard it won't let-in the larger player base it needs to be more enjoyable.

If you have a friend on which you can take revenge in real life, get the game on sales. If you don't, and are not generally a masochist, don't bother. \t\t\t\t\t
