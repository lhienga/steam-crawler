\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOk, when I first saw this I put it on a wishlist because the idea seemed mildly interesting, and because I hadn't seen a good mech strategy game since Supreme Commander.
When it went on a 20% off sale my initial gut reaction was "that's hardly a sale at all.... ugh I guess I've got $10 to blow on this game. Lets try it out."
This game is consuming my life. I love grand strategy, I hate micro managing, I'm fine with macro managing. This game removes all managing. Its just what I love best about strategy: Build formations, counter your opponents, try to catch them off guard.
The battles are beautiful, the units are straightforward.

One problem that I have with most strategy games is that they usually aren't great at creating a natural feeling "rock-paper-scissors" effectiveness circle. Either their units are too gimmicky to really have a specific hard counter, or they create it with arbitrary "it deals xx% less damage against this type of unit" which feels fake to me.
Mechabellum has the most natural and straightforward rock-paper-scissors I've ever run into. If the enemy has a mech with a powerful sniper rifle, you counter with a swarm that can't be taken out with a single hit. If they have a swarm, you counter with a unit that does blast effects to blow away a swarm of weak units. If they are blasting your swarms of weak units, get a powerful sniper to target their mech that's doing the blasting.
None of that was forced or has arbitrary percentage boosts you need to memorize. Its just a natural, logical, "what do I need to do to counter that attack." It almost completely removes a need to know the detailed ins and outs of the game, and replaces it with the common sense, logical planning that strategy games SHOULD focus on IMO. Watching for things like "Oh, they don't have any air defense, lets take advantage of that and deploy some aircraft" works, because they haven't made every single unit a jack-of-all trades. Noticing that you don't have any air defense and anticipating your opponent deploying some also ads some fun strategizing.

If I'd have known that this would be everything I love about strategy games bundled together I would have bought it months ago. Now I just wish there was a 40k Apocalypse version of this.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA broken, unbalanced idea. The idea is nice, but I was annoyed by the end of the tutorial. There really is no explanation as to why one unit is better than another. You have multiple units, some with the same description but clearly different designs. By why? What makes one better at a thing than another? Why does this "Anti Unit bot" which has a description claiming it's the "Bane of high HP bots" do more damage to enemy A but this OTHER "Anti Unit Bot" which ALSO has a description claiming to be "the bane of high HP bots" does less to enemy A but more to enemy B? Who knows. Units have expansive stats as well, making comparisons straight forward but laborious at best. I can see they were aiming to ensure the game was so delicately balanced that you couldn't just go with a 'regular' formation and unit composition and win consistently. But that has proven to be too delicate with how many units they've decided to provide. I'd say half as many units would probably make for a more rounded game at this point.\t\t\t\t\t
