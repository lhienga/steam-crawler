\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI enjoy that this game is a different take on RTS. I've played other RTS games like Starcraft, Age of Empires, etc, and I've enjoyed the single player but never gotten into the multiplayer as it involved way too much memorization of build orders and micro managing. This game doesn't have the base building element and much less in the way of economy, so the focus is all on unit placement and choosing good units to counter the enemy strategy. I really enjoy that as it's a refreshing break from other RTS that really just tinker around with the same fundamental formula of base building, economy, and mouse clicking really fast. So if you've enjoyed other RTS but want to try something different, this is definitely worth a look. It has solo vs AI and multiplayer. I have to say, until I learned the fundamentals even the easy AI was kicking my butt but I've now improved to the point that I can handle harder levels of AI. I've messed around a bit in the multiplayer and have enjoyed playing to a reasonable degree there.

Note to the devs (if you happen to read this): I'm not sure calling this game an "auto-battler" does it justice. I get the tag but I think of an auto-battler, at least the ones I've played on my phone, as way more passive (and easy) than this game actually is. While it's true you're not directly controlling your units you absolutely will get creamed if you're not paying careful attention and using good tactics in choosing units, placement, special abilities, etc.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTLDR: The game's reliance on gimmicks and chance undermines its strategic depth, making outcomes feel more random than skill-based. I hope the devs will consider rebalancing the game to reward strategic play more consistently, aligning with the game's original vision.

Long winded rambling details follow, you have been warned.

This game can be fun, it used to be about strategic skill, and once you learn all of the mechanics you can do better than people that don't know the mechanics. You can easily call that part a skill but it's just basic recall based on experience - so protocol and procedure more than skill - but if you want to call it skill, hey by all means you do you, not gonna argue. A less subjective view could be "the game heavily relies on memorization of mechanics, which may diminish the strategic depth for some players." It's not the skill of unit composition, combination, placement, timing or future planning the game's tutorials teach you, no. Sure, those such things have an impact per individual round and are not unimportant, but not as much as initial circumstances in the full duration of a typical match, provided you don't grossly violate obvious placement taboo that results in AoE casualty or inability to target, utilize unit recovery, redeployment and movement mechanics. That gets you up to basic equivalence with everyone else playing.

Learning the game's mechanics shifts focus from broad strategy to exploiting specific gimmicks. Your initial card choice is extremely important for not just getting shut out - you start making better choices once you get the hang of it. The per-round card choices will eventually start introducing gimmicks into your game; these are extremely situational, and one could mistake them for "strategic choices" but simply choosing a card strategically is not enough. You have to choose, commit and invest into a particular gimmick. If you generalize or play passive, your path forward becomes extremely limited - aggressive play is heavily rewarded as you can simply wait for another gimmick to cover your inadequacy with a high enough probability.

That all sort of sounds like high strategy right? It could be and in fact it used to be. Now there are spell cards, mass unit cards, free unit cards, etc. that let either you or your opponent overpower the other for a round, despite any strategic over-match achieved - or conversely, they can prevent one from making any strategic underdog or comeback plays. Ahem, I mean "the introduction of these types cards can disrupt well-planned strategies, leading to outcomes that feel more dependent on luck than skillful planning." When you see those cards in the deck, you should defend yourself preemptively and pick the one that gives you the best chance at winning, but that forward thinking might just cost you the game as much as just forgoing it, or picking some other at random. Balanced right? Coin tosses are balanced, that is true. But that's... not what you want in a skill-based strategy game. You want there to be a correct choice. (I touch on this later irt. higher level players playing players instead of playing the game.) These sorts of things weren't always in the game, but they're interesting and 'fun' mechanics, right? Shaking things up?

The devs seem to be unable to tell the difference in what makes a game 'fun' and what satisfies an addiction hit, or unwilling to lean toward legitimate fun. That seems to be curious distinction, right? What is fun if not a good time?

Well, a dopamine hit you can get from winning a simple coin toss, and as long as the win-rate is around 50%, you are pretty much guaranteed a dopamine hit every other game, and that's good enough to hook most people. 50% win-rate is to be expected of most competitive games, sure. That's how gambling is so effective, but you expect strategic play in a strategy game to be rewarded more than luck. When it is biased toward chance, it's less strategy game and more gamble. Ask a gambler if they're having fun, they might say "yes!" - but when you really watch a gambler, you can see what's happening. They're not having fun gambling, it's addicting. Karaoke is "fun" (entertaining might be more a apt word, obv. not for everyone, not the best example, but making a point) but doesn't give people that predatory "just one more song" chemical brain response (maybe for some; Karaoke addicts I feel bad for you) because it doesn't have predatory gambling psychology baked into that process. There's no "intermittent reinforcement" or "conditioning" or social pressures that push you to go 2 out of 3, etc.

Casino owners figured this out a long time ago. This game is gambling mechanics and psychology without gambling (yet) - they are monetizing it currently with the same practices that every other freemium game turns to at first; cosmetics. I got nothing against cosmetics. The psychology employed by the game mechanics here is predatory, and cosmetics is an easy way to sidestep the connection between gaming and gambling while still keeping the components of addiction well in place.

If you are in control of yourself and your time, you will enjoy this game to a up to a point. It is, however, supposed to be a competitive game, and it will try to pull you in. With the latest set of changes, at that particular point in your understanding of the game, you will begin to realize that strategy breaks down; high level players are afraid of playing against low level players because it will cost them their rank if they lose, and the win rate is 50% - but how can that be? High level players are more skilled right? Sure, but skill stops mattering beyond a certain level of play, save for executing 'perfect meta' strategies when imbalances are found, and it just devolves... In chess, as in Mechabellum, higher level players tend to play their opponent instead of playing the game; the slightest misread can spell their doom, regardless of strategy... and that can be fun to watch... but so will a bad initial hand, composition or placement... or just absolutely random bullcrap that neither player expected to happen. Normal strategies becomes fickle or meaningless. Both sides feel disgusted. One side laughs. Dopamine hit all the same. It is "fun" when it makes sense. It is "funny" and bad when it doesn't. Dopamine hit either way, right?

War Factories have two gimmick meta. Overlords have two gimmick meta. Those are the two biggest gimmicks at the moment. You can discover them, learn them on YouTube. Play the meta absolutely brain-dead and you will do just fine up to a point. They are basically "scholar's mate" level of brain-dead. They are imbalanced, but the randomness of the game prevents any analytics from weighting them specifically as being more or less powerful than any other units or meta of similar class. You can deduce this statistically. Just enough RNG in the mix to put everything nice and close to 50% so that strategic play stops mattering.

Maybe it will get back to it's roots in the future, drop all the crazy wacky randomness and reward high strategy, but that doesn't appear to be the direction the devs are going. That way probably turns too many people off, doesn't get them hooked if they can't 'randomly' win often enough if they're playing poorly, they can't get their share of dopamine from the basic game mechanics, because they're carefully trimming out the 'fun' of that stuff so that you look toward cosmetics and social interaction so that their social competitive pressures are more effective.

I keep playing this game for the dopamine hits. I can't recommend it for someone looking for a good, fun game. I'm actively seeking something else to fill this void and as soon as I find it, I'm not looking back.
Too harsh? Ahem. "I am on the lookout for an alternative that better fulfills my desire for a strategic and enjoyable game."\t\t\t\t\t
