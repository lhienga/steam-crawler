\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI’m never quite sure how to judge downloadable content, in part because I’m never sure what’s fair to expect from it. Complete overhauls with entirely new mechanics can be fun and valuable, but if every additional release had these, the game would never feel stable enough to bother mastering. Likewise, I’m happy to see updated graphics, but if a release were to include only a few new menu screens, no matter how attractive, I’d be disappointed. Reviewing Stellaris: Astral Planes is one of those times I wish Steam ratings offered more granularity than simply “recommended” or not. At the moment, this DLC is a 7.5/10 for me, which is passing.

Let me start with the price: the DLC was released at $19.99 and is currently $17.99. I truthfully don’t know how to think about game value vs. monetary value. I’m in the fortunate position to be able to buy games as I want them. I don’t have to make hard calls about how to spend my money. Do I know what else I could get for $20? Of course. But I also know that I’ve been okay with spending well more than that on a night out. At least this downloadable content is available to me permanently, not enjoyed and then flushed away.

In a recent patch note, Stellaris’s devs referred to Astral Planes as a “narrative expansion.” As I’ll show below, that phrasing is apt: the DLC is more story than mechanics, more exploration than challenge. Could the developers have included content? Of course. Could this release have been cheaper? Sure. But I haven’t experienced any bugs, and I’m having fun. That’s basically all I want from the games I buy. I recognize that other players have other, perhaps more demanding, desires from their purchases.

I also realize that my way of playing Stellaris isn’t the same as everyone else’s. To be clear: I’m not someone who plays Stellaris for the blobbing. I enjoy building up my borders and seeing my naval cap increase, but I find the game’s warfare unremarkable. Sure, it’s a delight to watch sparkly lasers and exploding ships, but the military side is tedious. Astral Planes doesn’t add much to the game’s combat. There are new active and passive buffs and maybe some novel technology. Fine, whatever, ho-hum, shrug.

What I love in Stellaris is the exploration. At each game’s start, there could be just about anything out there, only a little beyond sensor range. My science ships head out, undoing my ignorance with every scan, and I’m rewarded with a host of planets, resources, and pop-up events and stories. These mysteries, tales, and discoveries bring seemingly empty space to life, and though I’ve seen most of them before, I still read each one in full—usually aloud. I imagine the settings, events, and characters. And the hours of play tick by.

This DLC is clearly meant for someone like me. It includes are new events, a story-driven origin, and a new exploration system. The last of these is modeled on the earlier archaeology mechanic, which allows players to discover the history of their universe. The new system, accessed through attractively bright tears in space, instead gives insight to the history and present of other universes. One advance on the earlier archaeological system is how the DLC’s astral delving stories present choices. Archaeological events happened mostly on-rails, step-wise through, with occasional hidden rolls to determine outcomes. Astral Planes' events lets the player decide: Will you help the other worldly beings? Will you run at the first sign of danger? Each of these choices comes with an explicit difficulty marker, similar to the current espionage system. They can fail, sometimes disastrously. But these decision prompts remind me of my favorite childhood books: Choose Your Own Adventure.

I’m hopeful this release heralds future DLCs with substantial narrative content. Even if they’re not everyone’s favorites, they’re mine.

Recommended.
