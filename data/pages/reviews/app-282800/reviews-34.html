Don't Trust The Dice.

Summary:
Part board game, part card game, part dice game; all classic.

Review:
100% Orange Juice is a board game like any other, except it isn't really its own game as much as it is a hybrid of other game's features.

The objective is simple: four players on a board try getting their 'norma' to level 6 first. Think of 'norma' as a kind of goal, which is either to win a certain number of battles, or collect a certain number of stars. They are cumulative, so if you choose battles and find that harder to get (it tends to be depending on your character), you can switch to stars, and the stars you've collected (or lost) will count toward your goal. Same with battles; what you've already earned are applied toward your goal, and while harder to get, battle victory count does not go down, where a lost battle can cost you all of your stars... which you can then get back in another battle or via cards.

It sounds complicated, but you'll get it after a few plays.

The complexity comes in once you realize the scope of this game's ensemble of quirky characters, wide array of boards to play on, and absolute landslide of cards to collect and use.

Every character has their pluses and minuses, special cards they bring (but you or others can steal through the course of a game), health stats, and even personalities should you play story mode to experience their adventure. It's a lot harder than it sounds: the story mode for QP, one of the most neutral ability/status characters in the game, took a lot of tries to win for me, which may depend as much on dice rolls as it does on strategy.

Boards seem fairly straightforward, but can become absolute mazes as 'hazards' come into play. They follow a formulae: square you move to depending on your dice rolls, starting spots for every player that regenerate health and allow each player to check in on and choose goals, and tiles that initiate battles, give or take stars, give cards, teleport, give a second dice roll, and so on: there is a HUGE amount of possibilities. Those aforementioned hazards can cause rolls to reverse direction, drop bombs on tiles, make all tiles double rewards or penalties, and so on; really mixing things up in for a few rounds (a round is over when everyone has taken their turn).

As complex as this may appear by now, things get even deeper with the cards. Battle cards, seasonal cards, 'Hyper' cards (character specific cards), trap cards, boost cards: these serve as your abilities during a game as well as one of many collectibles you get to keep in a binder as you play, allowing you to select these cards as your deck for future games.

There are modes, events, characters, cards, and boards being added all the time, the developers have stuck with this game for over a decade and their efforts have not been wasted. Seasonal events regularly mix things up, as do the new additions to the ever expanding roster, all of which are optional. You can play with whomever you want with whatever you like, be it the base game, or one or more character from an expansion pack, as board games go this is as customizable as you wish.

Sound and music are pretty good, albeit at times somehwhat generic. Some of the music tracks sound as dated as the game is, while others are just okay. As of writing there are several voices to choose from for the announcer, which is also a nice touch, but can become grating or repetitive. These can all, of course, be adjusted, so if you aren't into the tracks or voices you can turn them down or off. Same with sound effects, which again, are okay, but sound like everything else that came from the early-mid 2000s.

The visual style will either win you over or not. It has a ho-hum presentation marred in some cases, like the music, by just how dated this game actually is. This is to be expected from a game that was originally released in mid 2009. That said, it holds up very well and really, it keeps to a style all its own. Monopoly came out in 1935 and its style has become downright iconic; who's to say the same won't be said of this game in 70 years? Given Fruitbat Factory's track record, this will be an absolute monstrosity of modes, options, characters and cards by then.

The stories are largely nonsense, I finished only one but have played at least 3. This is a good way to ease players into simpler boards before graduating into more complex boards, but other than that they serve little purpose to the game as a whole. The best games are online matches, and I found the community to be small but dedicated, and players themselves tend to have their preferences as far as whether or not they are willing to battle for victory, or in most cases just be content to collect stars. Occasionally you will get people that have no qualms about using everything at their disposal to win, which is at the heart of a game like this, but the politeness I've experienced is refreshing.

This game was played with all settings maxed out using an Intel i7-8700k, 32gb DRAM, Samsung Evo SSD, 1080ti, running in a window since my monitor is 3440x1440 and the game is locked at a 4:3 ratio, which I won't dock the game for considering its age and type. I played using a mouse, which is really all you need.

Pros:
  • Solid balance of skill and luck
  • Casual friendly game
  • Loaded with modes and features
  • Deeply customizable experience
  • Good voice acting
  • New boards, characters, cards and events added regularly
  • Continued support from developers
  • Unique visual style
  • Varied ways to win
  • Collectibles galore
  • Approachable once you grasp the concepts

Cons:
  • Rough learning curve
  • Repetitive music and voice overs
  • Some characters not well balanced
  • Dated sounds, music, and design not for everyone
  • Collectibles can feel overwhelming

Verdict: 8/10

Rare are the board games that feel like classics, rarer are those that deserve such recognition. Though many try, most fail. 100% Orange Juice isn't trying to be a classic. If anything it's more of a hybrid of classics, which in itself makes it not just a classic, but a classic of classics that stands alone in quality, weirdness, fun, and brutality.\t\t\t\t\t
