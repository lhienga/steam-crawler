\r\n\t\t\t\t\t***UPDATE 4/16/23***
The problems with lack of communication have only gotten worse, and it has now been over two months since we have had any contact of any kind from Brilliant. No new content, no updated road map, no response to mounting frustration on the community forum. Since January we've had only a *YAWN* post about LOS processing on the units. That's it. As such, I can no longer recommend its purchase if, like 99.99% of us, you think modding is critical to the viability of this game. It may actually happen one day. Then again, it might not, and nothing Brilliant is doing (or not doing) is inspiring any particular confidence in the former. It's looking like Black Masses, part deux.

***EDIT 11/6/22***
Since my original review, the developers have shown a willingness to be more involved as it pertains to communicating with their customers. This has included an updated roadmap providing an updated timeline on game progress and upcoming features. This has renewed my faith in the potential surrounding this title, and I look forward to witnessing their vision being realized.

***ORIGINAL REVIEW***

I currently have a major love-hate relationship going on with this game, which prevents me from whole-heartedly recommending it. At the end of the day I still do, thus the thumbs up. Read on for a more detailed explanation of why I struggle with this game as much as I do.

The developers, Brilliant Studios, do some really great and interesting work within this genre. The technology they have developed, allowing at first hundreds, then thousands, then tens of thousands, then hundreds of thousands of units to clash in battle is amazing and can truly result in some breathtaking skirmishes being played out across some pretty nice and varied maps. The AI, while far from perfect, is sound for the most part, and allows you to set up a fairly wide variety of scenarios. It would be nice to have a few more options when setting the default behavior of specific units, and hopefully we'll see at least one or two more before the game reaches final release. The included RTS element does allow the player to overcome these limitations to some degree.

The graphics are also decent, and are reasonably impressive when you're sending the camera flying through a formation of two hunded thousand Roman soldiers, the individual units moving as such with varying animations. This really helps give the impression of a mass of individuals, and not just a lump of figures. The sound is ok (I get tickled listening to the modern soldiers with their grumbling "-Merica"s) and the roar of massive bodies of warriors as they charge into battle is done well and adds to the atmosphere of epic clashes.

Of course this game is heavily dependent on VRAM and dedicated graphics cards, so you have to have the hardware to truly enjoy it as intended. The game seems to be coded well, although (as mentioned in the discussions) it doesn't seem to free up resources when you exit battles, potentially causing degraded performance as you play. This of course is fixed by restarting the game, but again is something you would hope is fixed prior to final release.

Everything I've mentioned is pretty positive in nature, so you may be wondering and waiting as to why I'm conflicted in my feelings for this game. Simple - the developers have not done a good job thus far in delivering on their own published Early Access roadmap. To add insult to injury, they have been largely incommunicado regarding these failures as well, providing only the very infrequent update or response to posts asking for updated information. Unfortunately, in researching Brilliant Studios, it would appear that they have a history of such behavior, which is truly regrettable seeing as how they obviously have a talent for making games that people want to play, but for some reason don't seem to value their fans enough to communicate with them openly and effectively. I like the Early Access format under Steam, and believe it provides a way for developers to work on games that find an audience while generating the often times crucial revenue to allow them to continue that work. However, by accepting money for a product that is admittedly under construction, I also believe that the developer takes on a responsibility to engage openly and honestly with those that have contributed monetarily to their project. While we all know the risks of participating in the Early Access model, when the developer fails to do so, it will inevitably lead to fewer people taking that risk, and that in turn will result in fewer games making it to market under this format. I understand that Brilliant is a four man team, but at the end of the day that just means there are four separate people who won't take a moment of their day to drop a quick update.

The failure to abide by their timeline along with the accompanying radio silence makes this even more detrimental when you take into account that this game, like its predecessor, depends on player created mods to truly reach its potential. Unforunately mod support can not effectively be implemented until final release. And as developer updates are slow to be released, and they get more and more behind their roadmap, this capability is pushed further down the road.

So to summarize:

-The game is playable, and enjoyable, as it stands now, thus my overall recommendation.

-It is a successful entry into a genre that is otherwise underrepresented, and improves on several aspects of the original.

-Without developer updates providing new units, maps, and more importantly, mod support, it can become repetitive, and eventually wear out its welcome.

I still believe in the potential of UEBS2, and hope the developers take to heart the importance, not only to this project but to the Early Access model overall, of frequent and open communication with those that have posted a stake, however small, in your work.\t\t\t\t\t
