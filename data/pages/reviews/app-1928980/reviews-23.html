\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is an early access review.

Nightengale is not a bad game. Currently, I find the game mildly enjoyable, and I want this to be only a slightly negative review. But in its current early access state, I cannot recommend it. The ideas are great. The mechanics are clunky.

The game has a solid Victorian-Gaslamp-Fae aesthetic. From your home base, you conjure up small, procedurally-generated maps with the biome and difficulty type you specify. You can further modify these realms or your home realm with a single set of modifiers. These modifier, biome and difficulty options come in the form of "cards" you unlock. Quests and a desire for gaining better equipment or more options drives you to explore ever more dangerous realms, all theoretically in a quest to reach the fabled Nightingale city after a disaster obliterated stable fae-realm transportation.

All of that is very good. The developers nail the aesthetic. But the elements beyond that feel shallow or clunky.

There are famous figures from history and mythology, like Doctor Frankenstein, to meet and get basic fetch quests from. These characters are not voice acted; their models are barely more then mannequins. And they won't offer more than a paint-coat thin level of lore. Your titular guide, Puck, is a far more interesting character, delightfully voice-acted, and with obfuscated intentions. Puck is intriguing. So far as I have played, he is the only one.

I have played for a considerable number of hours and have not reached the end of the current content. At the same time, I somewhat feel like I did long ago. There are three distinct biomes which the difficulty cards modify with largely cosmetic changes. I thrilled at seeing airships in the sky for the first time, or industrial towers, until I realized they were just stage dressing with no baring on gameplay. I'm still looking for the same landmarks, still completing the same three-note musical puzzles, still fighting many of the same enemy types. As difficulty increases, I will occasionally slightly more complex musical puzzles, or a new monster to pickaxe to death. But these changes are too incremental.

The building is clunky but serviceable. After playing games with glorious building systems like Valheim and Enshrouded, it feels like a step backwards. The same goes for crafting. I craft what I can with what I have, but feel no drive to push up a technology ladder. There's no dangling carrot. Neither building nor crafting are bad, but they fail to inspire.

A few survival elements are beyond superb. The penalties for over-encumbrance in Nightingale scale. And you are always able to teleport back to your base regardless of encumbrance. This a phenominal bit of game design! Yet other survival elements are mediocre at best. The hunger rate in the game is obnoxious. Nobody needs to eat that many animals worth of meat a day to avoid starvation. Hunger does not work that way.

The largest problem, however, is that Nightingale is plagued with poor mechanical choices, tragic complexity, and one of the worst documentation systems I have encountered.

Inventory can be a nightmare. Things only stack with things that are exactly like it, and minor variations abound. You will find your storage containers clogged with single-digit stacks of small variations of the same item. Even the same meat from the same type of animal killed in a different realm or under different conditions might craft with a post-decimal difference in one stat, making it require its own slot in your backpack. This problem extends into crafting, where you cannot craft an item that requires two pieces of leather if your two pieces come from different animals. To an extent understandable as some items gain bonuses based on which animal's hide went into its crafting. But most don't, and this issue applies unilaterally.

Your crafting tables receive bonuses by building augmentations nearby and/or putting them in suitable positions. This is a wonderful idea implemented exceptionally well in Valheim and echoed in many other games. Nightingale takes it a step farther by having these augmentations also give extra plans. However, crafting tables only accept limited augmentations and you cannot chose which ones they are drawing upon. Plus many of the benefits listed for augmentations are locked until unclear conditions are met.

The answers to these and many other questions might be found in the byzantine labyrinth of in-game documentation, but just as likely doesn't exist outside asking people on reddit. In some cases, different information about the same game element are available on different menus with no explanation. Some information is obfuscated unnecessarily. For example, when sorting by weight, you lose the ability to see how many items are in a stack. At the same time, the game has features that would be very welcome if it was clear they existed. I am by far not the first player to rack up multiple digits of play hours before learning you can break down higher tier essence for the lower tier needed for item repairs.

To exacerbate matters, there is a lack of complexity where it is sometimes needed. For example, one quest requires you to betray a trust in order acquire further vital quests. That is not fun, especially since you feel railroaded into it. There should be another option. Similarly, building in general needs a lot more room to be creative.

I could list other examples, but the point is already clear: the developers need to give many systems another pass, tightening them up with a focus on fun over tedium and a wariness for non-beneficial complexity. They need to completely overhaul the in-game documentation.

I believe this game has a lot of potential. I find the idea of it fun. I find the aesthetics of it delightful. But right now it has too many problems to be more than mildly enjoyable. I look forward to the day when it becomes so, and I hope to change this review at that time.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat I write for this review is the experience I had with the game, everybody will experience things differently. This is my honest review if you don't like it well tough. Also no game is safe from a review whether its early access or full release, now onto the review.

Nightingale a an open world, crafting game that lets you go to different realms using cards that can alter things in your realm. This game is fun to play & is more medium to hardcore gamer's. The game isn't hard at all but its desgined to help you learn the basics than lets you off on your own doing quests. What your main objective/quest from my understanding is you are trying to get to Nightingale City, not sure what for though.

Graphics: the graphics of this game is amazing even on its lowest setting, the game looks amazing. There are some spots where textures do look ugly or not rendered in but its very low encounters.

Crafting: the crafting in this game is cool but its very difficult to progress when the game blocks it by a specific card you need to advance your crafting stations & needing tier 2 & tier 3 essence which the game doesn't tell you how to get.

Combat: the combat in this game is so far basic. From what I know is you can get melee weapons & guns. The melee combat is basic swings & blocking. I do not know about gun weapon mechanics but a friend who got a few of the guns claimed that they were weaker than their melee weapons.

Enemies: there are a decent enemies in the game, I forget what the main enemies are called but the game does have quit a few from basic hand to hand to magic (I dont know beyond my experience). I fought a few of them & were not that hard to defeat.

Magic: I do not know if there is magic in the game but I did see a trailer that showed something with magic.

Essence: essence is the currency & upgrade in the game & you have 3 types. Tier 1 to tier 3. Tier 1 is everywhere & is easy to get but tiers 2 & 3 are in certain realms from what I read on. The game doesn't tell you where to find these, you just have to travel to different realms until you obtain the essence.

Leveling: so each realm has a level difficulty & if you are to low, you will have a hard time fighting higher enemies & even cant access certain quest or dungeon locations. The realm level is the what the quest or dungeon level requirement is to do them. I read & it seems that there is level 20, 30, 40 & 50 I think, again read up on this.

Internet: this game is heavily internet required to play. I have 150 package with 50U/50D & I can't have anything else going/using my internet or else I will lose connection.

So with all that Nightingale is pretty fun to play & is cool but its not for the relaxed player base. This game requires you to figure everything out on your own with no hints or guidance on what to do. I do not like that is internet required to play this game basically & I do not like how the game doesn't help you even on the easiest difficulty. Nightingale gets a 6/10 score but do recommend this game & would suggest playing with your friends.

That's the Pony review for Nightingale\t\t\t\t\t

Everything it does, others do better
This game has some pretty difficult if not impossible to overcome problems, as they're essentially baked into the core of the game. I'm going to break this review down into some sections, hopefully the leadership team at inflexion takes the feedback well.

Areas
The portals idea is unique. It makes sure all biomes scale with you, rather than sending you to different biomes for different levels with different areas. But the loading screens this makes you need are frankly atrocious. Why not a single world with teleporters to specific areas in the same world as you, that you can also walk to? As well as this feeling like a cop out instead of simply making the world scale with you. As the only reason to go back to an area is because of the arbitrary NPC's needed from specific areas to do quests or buy certain items, which is an arbitrary restriction entirely of the games making, they did not need to have NPC's stuck in specific portals. Another issue is that every biome at different levels feels pretty much the exact same, some have a mega boss, which is cool, but not enough to actually be unique. The only real thing to change between levels of biomes is the stuff you're mining/lumbering/gathering, which again is simply an arbitrary restriction that the leadership team at inflexion has added. I would've rather seen a big world with multiple different biomes that either scale with you or have sub leveled areas within those biomes in a well thought out manner. This could've also taken away from development time that was needed for their portaling to different biomes with different levels.

Crafting
This is a convoluted mess that seems like a conspiracy theorist smoked too much meth connecting dots and thought "complexity means fun! That's one of the things people like about Path of Exile!" Complexity adds fun when it's rewarding to understand, not baseline needed to continue. Needing augments, 15 different crafting stations, turning raw materials into 5 different stages [Porter Pistol needs 4 different components, which each need 1-3 different components, which each require different amount of ingots or may require different sub components to craft, and 3 different crafting benches] to craft a single item isn't fun. Other games have had this refined for a significant amount of time in this same genre, and other games that came out within a month or two of this game also have their crafting systems refined (Palworld, Enshrouded). This was a situation where they should've stopped cooking, and simply looked at what people enjoyed from crafting in other games from the genre.

Combat
There's nothing game breaking about combat. This game does some portions of combat with a nice little twist for the genre, such as some enemies being weak to different tools/weapons you have. Some enemies will be weak to the maul, others to the axe, others to the pickaxe, and if you're pretty good at hitting the heart of animals the guns will do very well too. Generally melee combat swings feel like you're swinging a feather, but that your opponents have Hemophilia. This is a general problem in the genre, not specific to nightingale. Gun play is frequently janky, where shooting the same spot on a target doesn't necessarily yield that same result in terms of crits.

Story and Writing
Failure to choose if you want a story or not, and simply dipping your toes in provides an awful feeling. Make the choice. Any dialogue with Puck is either an instant skip, or as if I'm trying to swim through Molasses listening to the 2 words per minute voice actor (why in the did you want the voice actor to speak this slowly???) do his best to make it not awful. Puck specifically says 500 words for a point that can be made in 10. You can have non-succinct writing without dragging your feet. The tutorial is also absolutely god awful and you'll contemplate if you'd rather just go play Ark, Palworld, Enshrouded, or innumerable other games.

Art
Frankly, this is the only point the game shines. The content it's got has some pretty great art direction. Arachnophobia mode is a nice addition.

Conclusion
I don't think this game can be salvaged, at least not with budgetary and time constraints existing. This isn't a no mans sky situation where it doesn't have enough content, it's a situation wherein the content it does have just isn't great. I think they chose this genre because it's pretty cheap to make in comparison to RPG's, which is what you'd figure the ex-bioware staff would've had more skill in making, though with the writing from this game I think they did themselves a favor avoiding RPG's.\t\t\t\t\t
