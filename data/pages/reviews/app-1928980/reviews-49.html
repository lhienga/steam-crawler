\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI don't like reviewing early access games, because due to their changing nature it can easily mean that by the time someone reads this review the game could be different than when I wrote it. But I feel like I have enough to say about this game as to warrant putting it down somewhere.

There's been a lot said about this game, and I'm willing to bet dollars to donuts that regardless of when this review is being read, the complaints about Nightingale will go mostly in the same direction: the crafting system is too complex, the game is too grindy, the AI is too stupid, the combat is too basic, performance is not great, loading times are way too long; and there are some points I absolutely agree on, but others that kind of baffle me.

I'm not even going to pretend this game doesn't have problems, but like, having complex production chains and gear level progression is not one of them imo. I guess we're at a point where these kinds of games can be split between crafting heavy or exploration heavy, but like, who the plays a survival and crafting game and then complain about there being too much crafting? I mean yeah, you'll have to keep several crafting benches, and use them incrementally to build stuff, but I have absolutely no problem with this, and I absolutely prefer it than having timers, or your base constantly requiring maintenance and attention, or gating difficulty through combat (though let's put a pin on this, I'll talk about it later). I have much more of a problem on how the game seems to want to funnel me down progression than the fact that there's always more and better to craft with complex production chains, and while we're at it, for all the complaints I've read about the UI, I have found crafting to be super quick and intuitive. Most of the crafting can be done with just a few double clicks, there's an autofill option, and the only annoyance that comes to mind with it is the fact that you need to have the reagents in your inventory , meaning there's a lot of back and forth from your containers when you're trying to build things. Well, that, and the realm hopping you're going to have to do to collect materials.

The game is postulated in the idea of using cards to basically dial in some Stargate-like portals, essentially allowing the player to choose where they want to go next. This is in theory a great idea, and I think Nightingale handily trumps Icarus at its own gimmick, forcing variety and requiring the player to set up several bases on each new world, but doing so at their own pace, being able to always return to their previous base if they need to and always keeping exploration new an fresh. Which is all the more puzzling when it shoots itself on the foot by forcing the player to follow a very, *VERY* narrow progression path through its main quest.

See, from the start Nightingale will put you through a series of objectives in order to familiarize you with the game's world and progression: go here, craft this, reach this gear level, find these cards. It's very railroady, and also very tedious. Let me put a clear example, and also talk about my other major gripe with the game right now, which is combat.

Right now I'm on my second world. I'm being told I need to reach a new realm, for which I need to find some cards in POIs located in the first world. So I go back (after a lengthy loading transition), head to the POI, and immediately get my pushed in, because... okay, look: I don't necessarily hate NG's combat, in theory. Most combat encounters consist of a bunch of hostile mobs bumrushing you , with a very limited degree of locational damage which honestly, can sometimes be fun when you and your AI follower start going on them. It's nothing to write home about, but it's serviceable and it mostly works. The problem is when the game decides to throw a bunch of elite enemies that will relentlessly chase you down while your AI follower gets stuck on something or is downed by another of those spammy . Click detection while in combat is pretty bad, and a lot of your blocks will not trigger, and even if they did, the attacks will go through your guard, and you'll eventually get killed. Yeah, your follower can revive you, but up to a sliver of health, just enough for you to get downed again with just one attack. So you'll get killed, I'm assuming due to not having enough gear level, but you don't have the blueprints to make better gear because you don't have the cards to advance through the "quest" that you can't get 'cause you don't have enough gear level.

I'm assuming you can just dispense with the entire questline and doing your thing, but I'm unsure how much you could advance before having to pass through that funnel anyway, but my point here is that while I have nothing against having a main quest or the combat itself in principle, the situation above makes me feel like I either have to either cheese the game or team up with other players to get past the extended tutorial, and while I guess I can't complain too much since the game *is* multiplayer, it feels cheap and bad. You can have mindless combat, and I don't care if it's spammy, but locking you into stupid hard fights with your only recourse being cheesing them or dying over and over and over again is not how the game should go.

All in all, Nightingale is a very worthy entry to the survival crafting genre, and it goes for a very craft-centric experience that puts crafting and expeditions on the forefront, but at the same time it feels like it's not giving you the tools that it should to let you towards the goals it itself provides. This combined with the long ass loading times, the constant busywork of gathering hundreds of resources to keep your gear in good condition, having to constantly micro manage your storage, the cheapness of its combat and the dumb as rocks AI turn it into an experience that is much more frustrating than it ever should. And while I think that the core of this game is perhaps the strongest step in the right direction for a single-player focused crafting and survival game I have seen in a long time (that includes Valheim and its constant mob rushes and boss mechanics whose AI isn't much better than this game's), it definitely introduces a ton of frustration that work against what itself tries to do.

If anything, you'll get a good 10 hours of gameplay until reaching these limitations, and while I don't expect it to do any radical changes during its Early Access period, I do hope that, through improvements to especially its combat and AI, it can at least secure a decent progression through its content that allows players to actually engage with its complex crafting and realm-diving mechanics.

5/10.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWell, 31 hours into the game and I am pretty disappointed. I would refund it if I could. In my opinion, it is not even Early Access ready.

I got it for the base building but the crafting system is very clunky and not intuitive at all. I just do not understand why everything is so complicated. Like, to the point where I found myself searching the web in order to figure out how to do the simplest of things.

Even as a seasoned gamer of this genre, I just could not figure out what I was supposed to do next because nothing is explained in the tool tips. It is even missing explanations for materials and in a lot of cases there is not even an icon built for the material, yet. For example, coal is the same icon as wire which is the same icon as a lot of other things.

Gonna recommend you pass on this one, at this time. I will check it out again in a few months but right now this is just a shell of a game and nothing more.\t\t\t\t\t
