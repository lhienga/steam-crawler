\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDO NOT buy the game in its current state. I'm sure the game will be fantastic when it's sorted out, but for now, avoid it.

The gameplay is fun, crafting is in depth and interesting (a lot of people complain because its more complicated than just 2 sticks and a rock makes a sword). Overall the game is very good IF it didn't have catastrophic glitches and bugs that basically ruin the entire game.

My first character, I got to near the end game and travelled to a place called "The Watch" and spawned underneath the map and fell forever. No way to teleport out, no way to sort it out except submit a support ticket and wait three days for a dev to respawn my character at my house.

In those three days, I started a new character and rushed to the end of the game to essentially carry on from where my (at the time) stuck character was supposed to be. After my first character got spawned back, I was already further with my second character, so I carried on with my second character.

Two days later, I am gathering the highest grade materials to make the best gear I can, teleport back to my home realm, only it's not my home realm. It's gone. My house, all my supplies, all my crafting materials and crafting machines... Just gone. So, all in all, the game gave me a big middle finger and basically said "Start the hours of resource gathering and building and crafting all again."

tl;dr - game essentially rendered one character useless and deleted the progress of the second. DO NOT BUY IN ITS CURRENT STATE.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAfter trying my best to get into Nightingale multiple times. I'd like to share my experience as someone who really wants to like this game. Before sharing though, this does not help the current "mixed" status and I do want to emphasize that I think the game has great potential and like a lot of other reviewers mentioned, a great bunch of unique ideas.

Introduction:
Despite the first few reviews I read before buying the game, I actually think the introduction is great. You receive both appropriate gameplay and lore progression alike. It is clear you get through a couple of portals in the sake of progression but I think Puck (your guide) is a great character that really reminds me of Fromsoftware characters in its own unique way. I'm a huge Survival game fan and the mechanics were all there. Enough to sink my teeth in, or so I thought.

Gameplay:
This is basically where everything collapsed for me. For all the rough unpolished systems I can get through, what I can't look past is gameplay and progression systems. In my 15 hours or so of gameplay I have been on an never ending linear quest hunt. Clear this dungeon, get this Gearscore, go to a realm which uses this card. The sad thing is, I actually am a sucker for grinding and questing like this. But combined with a Survival game, this rips all the player agency and exploration right out of it. I don't get to explore the game my way, I have to follow a questline.

Progression:
The progression comes down to a few things. Hope, Gearscore and Cards. Now I like this idea, but I just loath the implementation.
Hope: is a horizontal progression which is gained by a combination of what you wear, how much you have built, and I think what you have accomplished.
Gearscore: Pretty straightforward, quality of your gear
Cards: How difficult the realm is you can create.
The big problem with these: Hope makes me want to build a bunch of random stone to improve it. Gearscore comes down to spamming re-used puzzles and fights, Cards gets down to clearing a re-used dungeon. Everything is intertwined so it might as well have been one system instead.

Examples:
I want to emphasize a few examples of why this game does not work in its current state. I do have to add I have not reached end-game or probably even mid-game, but to be fair I don't think this game can surprise me looking at how stale the systems got so quickly.

- Valheim: One of my favorite survival games. It brings mystery and player agency. You as a player are there to explore the world. If you don't get into the Black Forest for 20 hours it doesn't matter. No one or nothing is urging you otherwise. This feeling is great and really should have been focussed on in Nightingale as well.
* In Nightingale: You are doing a linear boring quest. The world has nothing to explore besides chests around sites of interest. For a game with such an interesting theme it is really sad to see that the world feels so lifeless.

- Grounded: A much more linear story with a similar fetch quest type of progression. Grounded, as all Survival games should focus on, has a lot of mystery. I can't stress enough how important this is as a player. I don't want to know what an enemy is going to do, whether it is passive or aggressive, if I can tame it or not, etc. I am new to a world, and it should surprise me. Grounded does this incredibly with bugs fighting each other, unique sets of behaviors and fighting styles, unique weaknesses and unique rewards.
* In Nightingale: everything I've seen felt like a different model with the same behavior and move set as the guy before.

- Enshrouded: A game which to me has the same flaws as Nightingale and I'm no fan that the Steam reviews deem that game very positive and this one mixed. Enshrouded knows what it is and you can tell very quickly. A voxel survival game. The voxel part is used for an amazing building system, it has fun class/character progression and skills. The combat and survival aspect however I found to be very bad.
In Nightingale: Its linear quest progression takes away player agency. Building is alright. Combat is pretty bad, but it works I guess. No character progression and skills besides the items you craft.

- Palworld: Very flawed but the focus is the pocket monster catching and the monsters helping in the base. Amazing feeling.
* In Nightingale: Your companions feel as unique and interesting as the enemy swines and wolfs are predictable. There is just nothing there. No interaction, no dialogue, no unique behavior, no customization except what you give them to wear. If you recruit someone else, the previous companion dissapears and leaves a chest with its items.

Verdict:
Nightingale feels like a game with so many awesome ideas that I wish I could support, but a game that does not know what it wants to be, and thus does everything either mediocre or underwhelming. If you've done one mandatory tower, you've done all the mandatory towers. If you've talked to one NPC you've experienced them all, if you've recruited one companion, you've recruited them all.

My Player Feedback:
If the developers were to look for a list of issues from a player's POV to improve on, mine would be this:
- The progression is too repetitive. I felt like I knew what was going on within the first 2 hours. A survival game's power is in the player experiencing the world for the first time and slowly working themselves up towards the next progression, which could be anything from a different ore resulting in different materials to a different card resulting in different materials. It would have been so satisfying to make a portal on my own and finding the next quest giver NPC. But since there is a questline I'm following finding an NPC in a new realm did nothing for me.
- Drop the hand holding questline. The magic and beauty is in players just trying out cards they found or crafted without knowing what to do or where to go. It really should not come down to following a quest for progression. If I can't experiment, I don't want to play. Same goes with basic storytelling, if everything is being explained but a listener/reader is not invested in the overall story, then why tell the details in the first place. It just doesn't work like this.
- The reused assets and AI are very obvious for me as a player, and it takes away from any urge I feel to explore. If you're a procedural survival game, please adjust the way the assets work. Instead of a whole premade building and puzzels, try to go for biomes/areas with smaller premade assets. Minecraft's biomes/villages/towns are a good example of how every part of the map feels unique. Nightingale totally misses the mark and instead feels predictable and boring.
- As a player I noticed very quickly that everything in the wild came in packs of 3. Another way for me to feel like I totally knew what was going on in the game's systems without even trying to figure it out.
- Combat is just not there, but it feels like the base is solid.
- Character uniqueness. I really liked the ideas of the family trees but nothing was done with it afterwards. Why not use a system like that and implement it partly into the story. Why can't I go for a certain theme. I didn't get far enough but it seems like I was just going to get a gun and a melee weapon. Terrraria is a great example of being able to wield and use everything but still building towards a specific theme or build.

Positives/Conclusion:
As you might be able to tell from the length of this review, I'm very invested into Nightingale as a concept. I think it has a lot of potential and could be a great game. I definitely see that there is a large chance the studio will look at the mixed Steam score as a chronical illness and stake development. I root for the IP and I hope the team will continue improving on the game. I think Inflexion has some great ideas and I'd love to see a finished product\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThere are a lot of reviews that seem to be written before even getting to the end game (The Watch).

This game is a lot of fun and one of the few survival games that has a promising end game system. There is plenty of content currently to get your money's worth and they've recently published a roadmap. I am excited to see what the future holds for this game. The story concept is very interested and I'd like to see it fleshed out more.

In regards to some reviews talking about the grind. This game has FAR less of a grind than most survival games out there. The crafting system is complex and offers a huge amount of customization in terms of your preference in stats on gear. You can have HP boosting gear, stealth boosting gear, stam boosting gear, increased movement speed, etc. You gear is based on the materials you build it with and it's a very captivating system in which I've made multiple gear sets for different scenarios.

My few wishes for the future:
A clothing dying system (currently colors are determined by the materials you use to craft the gear)
Larger storage bins, the amount of materials you can collect means you need a lot of storage. Thankfully they are adding autostacking and craft from storage in future updates.
Storage bin search functionality.
Ability to change T3 essence into T2 & T1.
Transmog.
Better offensive magic.
Swords - Why are these not already in the game?

I am looking forward to increased biomes for the endgame as well as the different materials and stats that come with it.\t\t\t\t\t
