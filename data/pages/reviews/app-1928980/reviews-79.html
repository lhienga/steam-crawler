\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA stylish and beautifully methodical game. While many of the mechanics are tried and true, the procedural realm generation offers limitless permutations of environments. With a well done dynamic weather system and distinct biomes, it's easy to get lost wandering about just enjoying the relaxing ambient audio and vistas.

My main gripe is that the server network that supports this game is very finicky. I ran into several issues with disconnect during my time playing so far. I highly recommend that you hard wire your connection via ethernet, this game seems to sense when you are on wifi and will cause problems.

All in all a great early access release for people who like a survival game with complex crafting and a plethora of materials to sort through. Though I have not tried the multiplayer I think once you advance to a certain point it becomes the end game.

I hope the devs pull this one out of hat and make a game as deep as it is beautiful to play. Totally worth the early access price and your time to learn the game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTLDR; Decent EA but too early to be able to recommend. For 30 bucks if you have disposable income it's alright for killing some time but not currently a long-term game for the library till further optimizations are made.

Pros:
Nice graphics
Unique concept
Many build materials for EA albeit tedious to farm up.
Smooth combat
Decent amount of weapon/spell types for varying play styles. Could use some rebalance tweaks though once the infinite stat exploit is addressed.
Decent performance on a 1080ti on medium settings.

Cons:
-Ingot dupe exploit and infinite stats not addressed or fixed since launch.
-Building system is lacking many tiles for non-cube bases (no wedge foundations, no inverted triangles wall, no inverted corner roofs)
-Public multi-player experience questionable and could be a pro or con for you with exploited players 1shotting all bosses and mobs with 1bil crits and 1mil hp. You could either view it as a pro that you can leech resources fast or a con that you won't get to actually experience the game or any challenges in a public setting.
-Realm instances have insanely long creation/load times (5-10min+)
-Inactivity timer logic does not work and you will be continually disconnected in your base while building, crafting, organizing requiring you to wait for a long realm relog time again.
-Game advertised as building and cohabitation with friends in a realm however current building caps could be a hindrance for you if you or friends prefer creatively building large structures. (300 tile per snap grid structure, 350 realm wide deco/craft table cap, 100 snap grid per realm)
-No public roadmap or much dev interaction on their social platforms for addressing concerns outside of a few posts regarding offline mode.
-No craft from storage (yet, stated it's coming)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI just crossed 40 hours with Nightingale, probably half solo and half with a friend or two. I'll start off by saying I desperately want to like this game and genuinely hope the feedback and early access cash infusion happening right now translates into more fruitful development. There are a lot of great ideas and potential I am sincerely excited about. But Nightingale needs a LOT more time in the oven before before I expect I'll be able to feel optimistic or pessimistic about its ability to rise to that potential. This isn't 'early access' as we've come to experience it in the last ~4 years. It feels like ***EARLY*** access.

Spoilers to follow:

I'd like to focus more on what the initial experience was like, what worked and what didn't, and then what I would hope to see added or changed in future versions of the game, and less on the aspects that are inherent to the 'Early Access' status, like janky animations, or the immature crafting and progression systems.

The tutorial process was fine, pretty smooth. Character creation is currently meaningless. The family history aspect of creating your character either needs to be streamlined and made more impactful down the line (like on your stats, or maybe some story beats?), or just cut in favor of something more straightforward that can achieve the same visual results.

The Fae dimension-Stargate system with the Realm Cards has so much potential. Each realm you open a portal to is finite in size, but still decently large, and has a small degree of procedural generation. But currently there is not enough variety, of anything, to take full advantage of this setup. Realm traits, like the topography, numbers of creatures and enemies, types of sites and puzzles... are all going to need a great deal more diversity. No Man's Sky in 2016 suffered from this, but eventually iterated its way up to something that is way more fun to explore. I see no reason Nightingale can't get there, but it's going to take iterating on the bones. Everything currently looks and feels too much the same, regardless of what cards you use to spice things up.

The best example I have is when I first jumped into the Desert Herbarium level realm, and realized I could not access the next story character without adequate climbing picks and stamina to make the climb. Everything before that point had just been "find icon on map, walk there" level of challenging. That made work I had done previously feel meaningful, instead of just filler. Nightingale needs more progression obstacles and solutions like this. It could really fill out how much exploration and play time you get per realm you visit, instead of just popping in, raiding it for every resource you can, buying all the recipes from a vendor or two randomly dropped in the map, and then never going there again.

A handful of quick ideas on this topic before moving on: Maybe vendors don't show up in the Realm until you've done some objectives and built a base of operations. Reconnect some portals so they can find you, and have a safe place to show up, THEN you can have their recipes/knowledge as part of the reward loop. The instanced 'dungeons' that are found in some realms could be expanded and diversified, and maybe opening those portals to do that content is gated by some realm-specific crafting which isn't difficult, but encourages exploration. Gating things behind tool upgrades for resources you can't harvest until your tool is a certain level feels very outdated, and not adequate alone. It could take a Zelda:TotK approach and let crafting certain equipment be part of the exploration progression. Generated ravines that you need the right equipment to get across, creatures you need materials from that need a special lure, a gatekeeper who needs three very specific items before letting you pass, which you can read about in the lore drops found around the world... just more. More variety, more obstacles to solve, more ways to solve them.

The building system shines because of the 3d blueprint feature. This should be supported down the line with more cosmetic building component options, and less grueling crafting queues/times to get higher level building materials. Just like with Enshrouded, there is currently no real threats to your bases, so materials are essentially just cosmetic. A roof made of sticks keeps the rain off just as well as stone, or fancier materials. Creating reasons to build forward bases and benefits for going deeper into the build trees will be beneficial.

The boss fights are currently a pretty low point to the experience. These need work to even bring them up to Valheim's level of boss encounter enjoyability, though it would be amazing if they ended up feeling closer to Remnant II's boss encounters.

The crafting system gets pretty complex, and for many, very tedious. I don't personally mind a complex crafting system, especially if I have sight of some great goals it will eventually get me to, like access to higher level realms, more challenging combats, or really amazing cosmetic build options. But using No Man's Sky as an example again, there is a balance to be struck with a complex crafting system that has many different items. It can feel rich and full of options, or just a tedious drag. Getting this right is probably going to take a lot of testing and iteration.

I'm never going to want to reach the end game as the trailers depict right now if the game is repetitive and works against engagement. Nightingale needs to do its best to not re-tread tired mistakes that the Survival genre has been making since inception, and seize on the "what's over the horizon?" feeling, along with "I want to live there!", or better yet "I want to live there with my friends."
