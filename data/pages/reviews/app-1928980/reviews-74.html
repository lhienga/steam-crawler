\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFrom the outset, Nightingale greets you with a grind that could rival a blacksmith’s anvil. Want to level up? Grind. Need better gear? Grind. Aspire to unlock the next area? You guessed it—more grind. It’s like the developers handed you a pickaxe and said, “Dig your way to greatness, but don’t forget to pay the toll in sweat and tears.”

But here’s where things get murky. You’re mining for minerals, right? So why does defeating a pixie yield more experience points than unearthing a vein of precious ore? And why does battling a dragon unlock the ability to craft a wooden spoon? It’s as if the game’s economy was designed by a sleep-deprived alchemist who accidentally mixed up the recipe.

Now, let’s talk minerals. There are more minerals in Nightingale than there are stars in the sky. But when you venture out to farm them, you’ll need a compass, a sturdy pair of boots, and a lifetime supply of patience. Travel distances rival those of a migrating dragon, yet the yield? A handful of rocks. It’s like the game’s saying, “Congratulations, you found a pebble! Now build an empire.”

Nightingale dances on the precipice of greatness and absurdity. Its gaslamp fantasy world and intriguing ideas beckon, but the grind, the senselessness, and the paywalls threaten to snuff out the magic. If you enjoy masochistic mining marathons and have a penchant for pixelated pebbles, this game might be your jam. For the rest of us, there are sunnier realms to explore.

Remember, dear adventurers, life is too short to spend it chasing minerals across foggy landscapes. Perhaps it’s time to find a game that doesn’t require a cartographer’s degree to navigate.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEverything is more clunky than a 2024 game should be. Esp. coming from Enshrouded, which is also in Early Access, where progression feels fluent and systems just make sense, every system in this game feels forces. As if the devs planned it on a whiteboard and then never playtested.

Tons of little things like for instance why is the inventory for moving things from my backpack to e.g. a chest a different one than the one I use when just looking into my backpack. One shows your current inventory weight the other is the exact same window graphic but doesn't show the weight. So if you're overloaded and want to move things from your backpack to a chest, you won't know how much you've moved until you close the chest, re-open your backpack and check the number. It's idiotic.
Crafting is not so much about the joy of crafting and more about inventory management. Guessing which of these 10 baskets - that can't be given names - I put a resource into. Dude they are all in the same room, why doesn't the workbench just use any resource that's in a 10 foot radius around it. Also why is the first backpack that I craft like 5x the size of a storage basket that I put in my house? Can I please just put backpacks into my house instead of storage baskets?
The world also feels sterile and lifeless. Early on you get a quest to grind your gear level up to 20. It's trivial but again a time waster. You just run around the area, look for the same group of wolf or the same group of deer, kill them, get 2 hide, turn that into leather, build a gear piece, rinse and repeat. If the wolf aren't there, wait a while for them to respawn in the exact same place every single time.

This game doesn't respect your time and feels like it was built by 500 people that didn't talk to each other. Obvious things that they should notice after 1h of playing. Idk. they should have waited another year with early access.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI love this game. It's everything I've ever wanted to be completely absorbed in a survival fantasy adventure. As a fantasy fiction reader/nerd this surpassed my expectations with the world/realm building, crafting, and attention to detail. The fact that this is just the early access (which many players/refunders seem to forget) is so exciting since that means even more realms, content, and fixes will be made. You will need to get past the tutorial and into the "higher difficulty" (legit just past your home base realm) realms to really be fully immersed and realize the vastness and depth this game has to offer. Actually walk the realms and you'll find why that is the cornerstone of the game and your character.

I think many players forget that early access is a time where we can leave feedback and find bugs for the devs. This allows the player base to work in tandem with developers to truly shape the game together and Nightingale is already fantastic but will become absolutely extraordinary.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEarly access, so it still has some flaws: Grindy mats, unclear progressions, missing voice actors, criminally low object count for buildings, a few OP exploits. I expect the final product will gear more toward "building" than "survival", but that's my preference anyway. And I haven't yet played anything above Medium difficulty, so maybe the hard stuff is hard.

The art is simply beautiful. Building mechanics are quite pleasant: You can lay everything out even without all the mats in hand, for example, and deconstruction yields 100% return. Gear crafting is layered and nuanced. Character appearance is infinitely variable (within a fairly limited band of styles). Biomes, difficulty, and environmental effects can be customized. I haven't tried multi-player yet, but the architecture sounds like MC Realms on demand, engineered to be more flexible and robust, built in.

The potential for this game is huge: More biomes, more architectural styles, more fashions, more minor realm effects, some faces without weird chins. There's some farming available, but I haven't seen automation yet. I'll get my $30 out of it if it never gets better. If it turns out as well as I hope, I'll probably play for a few hundred hours.\t\t\t\t\t
