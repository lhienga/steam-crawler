\r\n\t\t\t\t\t52 hours in, think I'm ready to spill the beans... Nightingale is a faithful attempt to drag the crafting/survival genre back to a more serious, adult, and "crunchy" vibe. But, it is very bare-bones (IE. Absolutely early access) in its current state. Just for context, 7 Days and The Long Dark are my two gold standards of the genre - both games that involve a good deal of searching/improving your recipes, but ALSO makes room for an actual game while you climb the crafting ladder. Unfortunately, I feel No Man's Sky really broke the genre by turning the ladder into the game itself, but I digress.

Nightingale runs on Unreal Engine 5, which is absolutely gorgeous. The atmospherics are top-notch, which is a large part of why I love these games, but some of the models are a bit iffy. NPCs are stiff and awkward, lots of potato faces to go around, and the fur on those wolves looks like it never properly rendered. The core loop is (yet another) ladder climb, unfortunately, with little actual "game"... You jump through (very slow loading) portals into various procedurally generated realms in order to buy recipes to get you into the next tier of realms, etc.

The procedural generation, like NMS, really just noodles around the edges making most realms of a given type feel very same-y, and a single pass through all of the POIs in a realm is a fairly simple affair. Like Enshrouded, these POIs dot the map, and involve relatively little more than a simple puzzle or to kill a few mobs. Even the few-and-far-between dungeon delves seem mostly empty and hollow. However, the combat feels really great.

Crafting is where this game shines, with an endless level of detail paid to material stats, levels of materials (tiers), enchantments, charms, etc. It's almost too much crunchy, to be honest, once you realize that there's a bazillion "augments" you need to keep around just to craft one or two items. Augments are similar to what we had in Valheim, where you put down an anvil within range of your smelter to buff the stats; except in Nightingale, you use augments to grant the ability to craft items you've unlocked (that wouldn't normally be available in the workbench without it). I personally LOVE the crunchiness of the crafting system, but I hope they streamline the management of all of it... because it's a lot.

My final comment here is the building system, which is awful. They clearly left this system for last. Whereas Enshrouded has a beautiful, deep building system that not only allows you to place prefabs that "stitch" neatly at the seams but also grants the ability to add/remove bits to your liking... Enshrouded is prefabs only. And not very many. There's also no way to upgrade/replace prefabs, sending everything attached and above the object you're removing crashing down to the ground. It's a crazy amount of unnecessary rework whenever you want to replace a foundation block, for example, as all attached walls, roofs, and crafting stations need to be manually moved first. Total pain.

What's worse is, relocating to a new realm involves breaking everything down into components, shuttling those components through a portal (capped by weight limit) over the span of a dozen trips, rebuilding at the new location, and then cleaning up after yourself. Awful.

That all being said, the game in its current state sets a solid foundation for tons of content and improvements. I hope they stick with it and make the actual "game" part of the game more interesting. As it stands right now, I'm still enjoying the crafting and upgrading, but it's getting stale fast.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEvery review I've seen on this game, mentions issues that have already been addressed, or will be addressed.
The game is a neat little loop of going through portals to fight bigger challenges and upgrading your gear to accommodate. But it's simply much more than that. You get a realm called an abeyance realm. A little slice of heaven all for yourself, or whomever you invite to your island. On this island you can construct castles and homes within a very generous build limit. From here, you can launch expeditions into the relative unknown Fae wilds. Each of these expeditions can be as difficult as you need. Very soon, you will notice the amazing crafting system this game provides. Each material regardless of where and how you get it, provide a stat bonus to what you craft with it. Want more range damage? Look for the pursuit ingots, or collect the Bombardier ingots the bomb lobbers can drop. Want more magical proficiency, collect the ore that has higher magic strength. On top of this, each realm has specific formulas and recipes you can collect, effectively making you a master craftsman by the time you reach the endgame. The endgame which I will explain in the spoiler section. My point is, if you enjoy exploring, this game is for you. The realms are beautiful, the combat is satisfying to master, and the progression loop keeps you engaged. If I didn't have to go to work, I'd have way more hours. The game keeps you coming back for more. There's always something to be improving around the base, and if things are moving too slow, you can use magical cards that you collect called "Minor Realm Cards" to change properties in the world. This includes crafting output of ingots, lumber, and even higher durability crafts. There seems to be a solution for any problem you may have while in the crafting section of the loop.

SPOILERS AHEAD



The game has a hub area named The Watch. This is where you take the character you have been taking so long to perfectly cultivate, and throw them into the fires of the public Raid dungeons. This game not only has public hub to buy high level recipes and items, but it also has a matchmade raid dungeon that can be farmed for endgame tools and item materials. This is not required, as all of these items can be found finding these raid bosses in your own personal realms, however the option is there. This is also where you unlock the ascended realms. An extremely difficult version of each of the previous realms, which provide new recipes and even more rewards.

This game has extreme play-ability at the moment, don't deny yourself the pleasure of a fun self contained experience because everyone is jumping on their favorite streamers band wagon.

I highly recommend.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI cannot, right now, recommend this game. I have played it, walked away from it, come back to it, and left it just as fast.

Pros:
* Artwork is nice, the world is pretty.
* Followers seem to follow you without too many problems (pathing-wise), they fight, and are easy to resurrect and get back to battle.

Cons:
* World travel is briefly explained, but you definitely feel like you have been thrown in the middle of a book without much knowledge of anything before hand.

* Without going online and searching how the cards work, how to get them, or what you need to get them, you are basically running around a farming treadmill until you figure out what you need to do. By that, I mean that the quest in the top right corner tells you what you have to do (most of the time) to progress the game, but it doesn't really tell you specifically you need a gear score of 30 to be able to enter this building. The building just has a force-field and no way to get into it, while your active quest says "Get Gear Score 30" - the link between the two is missing. Some people do not like it spelled out, but if you are trying to play the game without spoilers, it doesn't hurt to connect the dots in the beginning of the game.

* Difficulty of cards - most of them are so non-descript in what they do, you don't really understand what you are doing. They have base descriptions, but you don't know the fine details. Which is worse, the Antiquarian cards versus the Astrolabe card? Middling danger or Moderate? Probably Moderate, but you have to guess which one it is by engaging a monster and seeing the level. if you die, it is a corpse run, and you better hope you did not end up in the path of a roaming group of monsters.

*Small cards are realm cards that can change how the world works, and it is a cool take on changing your environment to suit your needs. A lot of them have a negative, or too negative of a, consequence to deal with it regularly, so you really only use it when you are farming something specific.

* As has been said, the crafting is terrible. They need to overhaul the entire system and give the player more information about what each unit does, how to upgrade it, and why certain upgrades you build for the machines do not actually upgrade them. LIke adding bellows to the starter forge does not do anything. When you get the upgraded forge, it unlocks one recipe. The description says it adds about 10 items, but it doesn't. You have to unlock those recipes and then they can be used at the forge. It is the same for all equipment you have to build, and there is a lot. The game never tells you this though, so it looks like it should unlock everything.

* The constant need to farm pelts gets old after the 3rd Fae Tower. No way to store wood or grass, no way to store ores or rock in their own pallets. So you have to build an enormous amount of bags and place them all over your house, with limited inventory space, just to hold the extra starter items you are always going to need going forward. Always. You never stop needing animal hide, wood, grass, and stone. When I stopped playing I had over 15 bags in my house, that was crammed full of begging and upgraded machines, complete with the one-off items you build to enhance them.

* repetitive monsters - - in the beginning world, it was wolves and pigs. In the next world it was hippos and something else. In the desert it was scarabs and another monster. Then you had the regular monsters in the Fae towers for each world. All of them feel the same no matter the zones you go into, just different skins. Hippos, pigs, and scarabs will charge you and a simple side-step negates all advantage they had and you can kill them easily. Fae die fairly quickly, but in numbers can overwhelm you.

* Clothes - you stay in your starter clothes WAY TOO LONG. There is no progression until you upgrade like 3 or 4 machines to the advanced level, and farm all the materials for that, which is fairly far, play-time-wise, in the game. Then you have to go farm another load of materials just to get new pants and a shirt. You keep the turban for the entire time (at least I did, I am sure there are other hats out there you can get), but I didn't find any unless I stole them from a follower.

Summary: The game right now is just not very good. It has some great pieces to it, but it needs a lot more clarity in the tool-tips, more ways to store items, better clothing system, and new monsters with varying attacks/skills.

Maybe give it another 6 months to a year and see if they can salvage it.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOK, I've got 50 hours in Nightingale now. My initial obsession began to dwindle around hour 30.

The environments go from breathtaking to meh. The first time you visit each of the 3 basic realm types, your mesmerized, and seeing how these are Fae realms, excited and nervous of what creatures, aggressive and passive, lurk just beyond the view on the portal.

Creature designs are fantastic, no pun intended, and each new species is unique. We go from boars, wolves and deer, just normal animals, to Desert Elephants like something out of Lord of the Rings, terrifying Harpies, and what resemble Falmor, if they were armed by Mordor, The Bound.

Combat isn't bad, but it's not very complex either. It reminds me very much of Oblivion or Fallout 3, for better or worse. Actions depend on weapon type: large weapons like picks and axes are slow and wide sweeping, with blocking as alt-fire. Knives and climbing picks much faster, offering a dash I used in traversal more than in combat. Firearms are clunky, with long reloads, which is accurate to the time in which this game takes place. Hits on weak points are where firearms gain their utility, with much larger critical bonus damage.

The crafting system is intense if you're not used to material systems like Nightingales. The shear number of material types and tiers, each offering different bonuses to the gear they are used to craft, is daunting, but exciting if you're into that sort of thing, allowing you to really take your time and pick the specific material bonuses you want for your play style, or go hunt for new and rare materials. Collectors will have tons to do.

The equipment system needs work, as does the system for crafting equipment and improving gear score. While it can be fun to try lots of different material combinations to find just the axe or firearm you want, the results are lackluster. I noticed very little difference in the materials used on the same tool type. An axe of tin or copper feels identical to an axe of iron or even something as rare (and cool sounding) as Pursuit metal. Some material usage will effect how parts of your gear might look, which is a great touch, but ended with most of my character the first 40 hours just covered in leopard print and looking rediculous.

The "leveling" system is not great. Gear score doesn't slowly ciimb, allowing you to survive stronger realms and dungeons, as much as it jumps every 10-15 hours. The idea is trying new materials you find as you explore to make your equipment, but score is effected so little, you just use what you first craft upon obtaining the next tier of crafting benches. From 20s, to 70s, then upgrading rarity of your equipment jumps to 120ish and 200s. The different in material types for equipment needs to have more and less effect on gear score. Easier prey and ores with less addition to gear score, and new/rarer materials and tougher mods giving mats with more effect. The difference currently has very little effect.

As crafting is what you'll be spending a lot of time doing, the system needs a lot of work and balancing/buffing.

I have a lot of criticism for this game, but I actually really enjoyed the first 30 hours, maybe even loved them. It's early access so it has a lot of work to do, but the foundation here is phenomenal, and the potential to be up with the greats.

I recommend this game to those who love survival crafting games, and understand it isn't a finished product. Enjoy it for 20 or so hours, then check back in after a large update, I know I will be\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe most damning thing I can say about Nightingale is that it's just another survival crafting game with a randomly generated world. The levels don't feel as well assembled or interesting as the hand-crafted world of Enshrouded, and the portal traversal isn't as compelling as Pacific Drive. The game is gorgeous but it's only skin deep, underneath everything is the same mechanics as every other game of this genre but not done particularly well. This is not to say they're done especially poorly, but we've long since been at a point in this genre specifically and the industry as a whole where just being okay doesn't cut it. For $29.99 it's not overpriced, but it also doesn't feel worth the cost.

For me personally, Nightingale should have been an easy win. I like so many elements that it presents but to see them all be done in such a middling fashion is disappointing, and worst of all it's not even like I was sitting there pointing at things and going "oh it'd be perfect if not for x," I was just bored. And while not every game needs a rip roaring opening to keep people involved, in a world where you need to grab people within two hours Nightingale didn't. Hell, I've only put in 76 minutes, and while I could give it another 44 minutes to try and grab me, I realized I don't care enough to play.

Perhaps the game will improve in the coming months and years but I'm going to be watching from a distance.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really wanted to like this game. The artwork is nice but this is a game that seems to have taken the wrong messages from previous survival games. What results is a very tedious game that is credibly slow with very long winded backstory expressed very slowly that I never found myself caring about.

You grind through the collecting and building, and you end up buying most of your upgrades rather than finding them through gameplay. Once you build up very uninteresting bases and gear, you are ported to a new realm where you basically have to do it all again. I think you're supposed to go back and forth between realms but the load time in this is the worst I've ever seen. Also, for some reason you have to be online all the time which causes a lot of warping and artifacts.

It's possible this may improve later, but my feeling is the core gameplay is the problem. They would have to redesign the entire game to remove the tedium and slow/clunking action.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game is good, feels good, looks great. It is grindy and my biggest complaint is having to wait on a server for every map change I do.. super annoying. Still recommend.
Wanted to update this, my new biggest complaint is having 30 crafting stations, and having to refine my materials like 10-15 times to get the material I need to build something to unlock the next portal.. to presumably start this process all over again. Just spent 2 hours doing this.. my god.
Maybe have the follower, who ultimately only seems good as fodder when in combat, to craft for me. Like, for example, I need to built Coating. Have him go to my chest, get the fiber, refine it into refined fibre, refine that into Fabric, refine that into Coating, etc. That would probably fix this massive issue.
Please also stop requiring the use of the Escape key to exit out of every menu, just use the same key (E) that we're using to open the menu to exit it - much more smooth from a gameplay perspective.
The red wax stamp icon placeholders really need to be resolved.. please get them replaced asap, it's very frustrating.
Also with the massive amount of different items required for crafting, it is absolutely necessary that when you're crafting in your base that the game pulls from the chests automatically. Manually looking through several chests with no signs in game, no search function and place-holder item icons is a terribly frustrating system.
The game guides you through some elements in good detail and pacing, but other (honestly super important) things aren't touched on at all and left to the player to figure out (and by that.. I mean Google, endlessly). If a player has to Google something about the game more than once in a play session because there is no indication on how to "do the next thing" (ex. crafting, missing a reagent, no idea where to get it) that is a problem. The game should be intuitive enough to guide players, even proactively (for example, if you're crafting an item and you hover over the materials needed.. a useful bit of information would be what crafting station makes that. Heck bonus points if you indicate whether it's built, because with the amount of crafting stations and augments.. things get very very hard to keep track of).
Another update: some bugs, logged in and half my base was missing.. luckily I was in the process of building a new base and the base that went missing didn't contain anything super valuable.\t\t\t\t\t
