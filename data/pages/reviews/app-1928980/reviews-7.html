\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI am having a lot of fun with this game, tickles every part of my brain that likes survival craft games. I'm really excited to see how the game grows.

The view is beautiful. It puts my powerful PC to work.

The building is so nicely done, plan out what you want to make and fill in the required materials from anywhere.

Fast travel home whenever you want.

BEST PART! You do not need to pay some third party vendor to host a dedicated server - yea so the loading times are pretty long but the graphics make it worth it to me. Also the convenience of being able to play with other people by just inviting them... If you log off they can keep playing, if they log off too when they start playing again they will be in the same realm even if it was "yours." Never seen this in an open world survival craft, I love it and I am impressed.

The crafting isn't all that intuitive I find myself needing to look things up. But aside from that I've thoroughly enjoyed what I've played so far.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI wanted to let the developers have some time from launch before doing a review and not review the mess that many early access games are at launch (not to mention many AAA MMORPGs are at launch).

So first let's go over the good:
- The land is beautiful and the environment well made.
- Creatures look good for the most part.
- There are a lot of blueprints and they don't always directly upgrade (IE you can have equipment based more on Melee than magic and another set of blue prints for similar items that are more magic vs melee focused to help you individualize your character more).
- Many types of building styles for houses that you can make.
- This is more opinion than necessarily a good thing: There are no levels, your gear is everything. You don't spend hours skilling and leveling up.

So now the bad:
- So there are really only 3 realms at the moment: Forest, Desert and Swamp. You pair one of those with a card that changes the level of the realm, but really you're just doing the same 3 realms over and over as you build up your gear score.
- Housing has a lot of types of light, but they all produce a similar level of light. There is NO way to increase the level of light something puts out, you just have to build more lights.
- Similar to above, you can build a lantern to walk around in the dark with. But really unless it's night out, you may as well be walking around with your umbrella out for all the good the lantern will do for you. This is especially noticeable in caves where the time of day should have no bearing on how bright your light is...

Overall I like the game. It needs work, but it's a far cry from release day. and for the better.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tStill early access so it's a bit buggy but so far I'm really loving the game! I am a fan of survival games but very few hooked me in long enough to get to the "end" of most of them. Nightingale is a unique twist on the survival genre and uses its features to tell a story that couldn't really be told in another genre.

Nightingale takes features from some similar games in the genre but nearly perfects them. The environments are detailed and feel supernatural but still following some magical version of scientific laws (and the in-game universe runs with that). There are neat puzzles at POIs that aren't hard but don't overstay their welcome. Exploration is rewarding, unlocking a new recipe each time. "Cosmetic" building pieces have always intrigued me in survival games but I've never really had a strong enough aesthetic sense to really use them. Fortunately, there is a real in-game consequence (reward?) to using decorations, in an intuitive and simple mechanic. The maps remind me a lot of Icarus blended with . The story-telling isn't as compelling as Subnautica or Grounded but it's told better than in games like Ark or Last Oasis which rely almost entirely on emergent stories. Combat is simple but interesting in that weapons have inherent damage types and enemies will have fairly obvious weaknesses to particular types (constructs take less slashing but more bludgeoning, for example). What really sets Nightingale a part and plays to the strengths of the genre is the rich crafting system which rewards attention to detail as there are dozens of types of ingredients that each add unique properties and boosts as you make items. It really is a crafting dream! You get to truly feel like a victorian-era artisan where your choice of wood, leather, and metal can create unique weapons. The only game that came close to that was Last Oasis but Nightingale is much more open and transparent with the player as to how the system works and what information is given.

It's still early but if you are a fan of survival crafting games, you will likely find something to love in Nightingale.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is an enjoyable game if you like exploration and complex crafting. I appreciate how crafting takes more than one step and actually requires you to have the items. There's nice amount of complexity that mirrors real crafting more than most games, but not so complex that I want to give up half way through.
As for game play, I have encountered far less bugs and issues than any finished game of this type I have ever played. In 220 hours of playing; my death chest dropped through the world once, I've had difficulty logging in 3 times, and I've seen mobs awkwardly stuck in an odd spot 2 times, and that's it so far.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI want to love this game. I've been following it since it was announced and was super excited for it. Now that I've played it, it feels clunky and repetitive. The crafting is cluttered and confusing, the world generation is monotone and the quests are tiresome. I haven't played it for long but at this point, I'd rather wait for the full release and give it another try. The idea behind the game is still fascinating and the whole feel is incredible. Once there are a few more biomes, quests and enemies and the crafting system has been cleaned up this game could still be amazing.\t\t\t\t\t
