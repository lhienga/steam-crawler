\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe amount of negative reviews on this game is a genuine tragedy when derivative and broken games like Palworld manage to top charts.

This game is not what most people think it is. Yes it is an open world survival craft game and it does a lot in that department to sell that idea in media and gameplay alike. However once you start playing and allow the game to explore itself along side you, you realize there is a lot more going on than your typical OWSC game. The Crafting systems are pretty easy to learn but also have a high skill ceiling if you can believe that. And a lot of the depth in this game comes from that intricate system. Creating realms and exploring them doesn't really ever start to feel monotonous because of all the modifiers and differences between all the mutators.

SPOILER WARNING for the "endgame" because its genuinely surprising how well Inflexion kept it a secret and what it does to the experience. So if you want to dodge any spoilers and experience the game organically then take this review at face value and try the game.

SPOILERS START:
The end game flips things around a bit. You spend how ever many hours it takes for you to grind up your item level and explore increasingly more and more dangerous realms to complete the story quests for what one would likely imagine to be all the game has to offer for early access. Once you do reach this point after crafting the best gear, hunting or trading with apex creatures you are granted access to the end. Except once you reach it things are different. The game puts you in a third person and you now find yourself in a sort of 'hub' world, bustling with other players of varying item levels and gear. And the game essentially says, "Yeah all that before now? That was the warm up, welcome to the end game ♥♥♥♥♥♥ time to get that i lv up." And introduces raids and a plethora of new very powerful weapons and item sets as well as unlocking ascended realms, effectively doubling the amount of content.

SPOILERS END.

I don't know if it was just me or not but I noticed my friends also had a pretty profound reaction to reaching the 'end game'. What was a game I was already enjoying significantly more than any other open world survival game I have played/ has come out in quite some time I became almost obsessed once I reached the beginning of the end game.

I think if the developers take all the reviews, positive and negative, in stride and really work on and flesh out everything post 'The Watch' and build on the extremely strong foundation The Watch puts out, this game could genuinely be a topper for great OWSC games and even go beyond that genre.

TLDR
To water everything down as much as possible. If you like Destiny's gameplay loop and/ or survival craft games you will like this if you give it the chance it deserves.

Support creative and fresh ideas done well, not amalgamations of stolen ideas done 'good enough'.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNightingale isn't a terrible game, but there are some really bad decisions that the devs have made that significantly lowers the enjoyment value as a whole. I would hold off until these issues have been resolved. I think you can boil down the problems to two main categories: networking and crafting.

This is an online-only experience, and one of the worst I've ever played in that regard. I have never seen loading screens this long in any generation of gaming. Sometimes when I initially load the game, it takes upwards of three minutes to actually load me into the realm. There is either something seriously wrong with how the game loads assets, the path my computer takes to communicate with their servers, or the servers themselves. Perhaps all of the above.

Network-related issues don't stop once you've loaded into a realm, either. You will frequently encounter significant freeze-ups or stuttering when mining or chopping lots of trees down; for whatever reason, the server doesn't seem to handle this very well. During these times my framerate is not dipping whatsoever. Enemies often just straight-up miss you as long as you are walking backwards and diagonally due to network lag. It's a real shame because I don't think the combat is nearly as bad as the server sync issues portray, but when you run into fast charging hippos that are rubber-banding all over the place and nearly impossible to hit as a result, it makes you want to just ignore them and do something else.

You are going to repeatedly be jumping between realms to complete quests, gather realm-specific materials, or head back to your main base to drop off loot and craft better gear. You can only have one main base and thus only one spot where you can fast travel at a time, so it's pretty much required that you build your base near the NPCs of that realm if you don't want to walk for several minutes just to buy items or do quests. And going back to the comment about loading screens, since you only have one base, you'll have to sit through twice as many loading screens every time you need to pick up or drop something off (these are usually shorter than the initial loading screen, but still take thirty seconds to two minutes each time). To a small credit, I have thus far not been disconnected from the server even once in 40hrs. Whether or not just being disconnected would've ameliorated some of the weird issues is another story.

The other major issue with the game is the crafting system. It is a convoluted mess. There are more different materials and items here than I've seen in most other survival games. It almost reminds me of an overly-modded Terraria, minus the ability to have any quality-of-life storage to mitigate the bulk of the confusion. You have different composite materials, material types, material tiers, even different version of materials such as those with ingots. There is no visual differentiation between any of these items at a glance from within your storage. Even worse, many of the materials have no unique icon to begin with, instead sharing a placeholder 'N' stamp symbol, meaning you need to hover over each individual item with these placeholders until the tooltip pops up to inform you what the item actually is.

Most of the recipes involve crafting some obscure material from another bench, which as it turns out requires some other obscure material obtained from another bench to craft said bench, and the recipe utilizes some incredibly vague wording to not-quite clue you in as to what it means you need to acquire, so then you have to give up and tab out to look it up online instead of checking the in-game guidebook or search functions (which absolutely suck btw, because networking hiccups often cause the search bar to become just briefly unresponsive long enough to make the game thing you're trying to change menus instead of type in the search box)

You will receive tons of augments, which are basically furniture/decorations that grant certain recipes to your crafting stations. As it turns out, the game gives you more of these than you can use per station, meaning you'll have to remove some to utilize others. This is a pretty stupid and meaningless limitation that just adds to the fu­cking-you-around element that crafting is.

There are other issues, such as the bizarre flickering on every single surface when you move. I don't know what causes it, but it's probably my runner-up for third worst issue that occurs way too often and doesn't seem to resolve itself until you reboot the game entirely and sit through another several minutes of loading screens.

Your NPC survivor/recruit follower is impossible to control and does whatever it wants, when it wants, sometimes to great detriment. Giving it an axe before clearing the entire area around your base of trees is a miserable experience, giving it a hammer seems to make it actually damage your base, giving it any type of precious wood that happens to double as a fuel source (because you weren't being screwed with enough) causes the stupid fu­cker to beeline to any campfire you come within proximity of (and don't think avoiding building a campfire will prevent this, because there are prebuilt campfires littered throughout every realm) and promptly chuck all your burnables into the furnace. It is incapable of using ranged weapons. As useless and annoying as this all sounds, your personal idiot is unfortunately indispensable for its lack of weight limit, meaning it can carry full stacks of heavy materials for you without issues.

Overall, Nightingale is not worth it at the moment. Check it out when the major grievances have been ironed out and the game is more than just beautiful vistas and skyboxes.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNightingale opens a portal to an alluring world that's as vast as it is mysterious, beckoning adventurers with the promise of untold stories and hidden dangers.

Pros:
Rich World-Building: The game excels in immersing players in a beautifully crafted, fantastical universe filled with lore and mystery, encouraging exploration.
Innovative Crafting System: Nightingale offers a deep and satisfying crafting experience, allowing for creativity and personalization in building and equipment that is easy to learn hard to master.
Dynamic Environments and Encounters: The game features a variety of biomes and dynamic weather systems that affect gameplay, along with engaging combat and encounters with mystical creatures that increase in diversity the further in you go.

Cons:
Early Access Feelings: The game does still feel very early access, with some mechanics not fleshed out, limited items from what you can tell is their end goal, and some items having default logos as place holders for their final version
Steep Learning Curve: Newcomers to the survival genre might find the game's systems and mechanics overwhelming at first.
Resource Grind: The necessity for resource gathering can sometimes feel tedious, especially in the early stages of the game.

While it might test your patience with its demands and occasional technical hiccups, the reward is a richly woven tapestry of survival and exploration. So, if you're prepared to weather the storm and delve into the unknown, Nightingale offers a journey that's nearly pitch-perfect, earning it a 9 out of 10 portals to wonderful worlds.\t\t\t\t\t

So here's the deal... I really like this game, but I think you should hold off on playing it until it has a little more polish if you aren't used to Early Access titles.

On Questing & Environments:

I applaud Nightingale's dev team for its art and environmental design. The game has the consistent "gaslamp fantasy" vibe that they were going for and I really love it. It's also rare to see a survival crafting game - and make no mistake, that's what Nightingale is - put together coherent lore that makes me feel as though I want to continue learning it. I like questing in my open worlds and I also like crafting/building; It's why I like Fallout 76 in its current iteration (yeah, yeah, go ahead and do your memeing).

I want to feel as though the world is alive, and the presence of NPCs who actually engage you in conversation and provide information about the world helps bring this game that small facsimile of life, yet the game stops short of actual substantive questing and story, which I find a little disappointing. I really would love to see more like the early game questing in the future.

All that aside, the realm system and card matching is a great, fresh idea and I love it. I have yet to see a realm (even using same/similar card combinations) that looks the same. Each forest, despite being just called a forest, is truly unique. Each desert is an unforgiving sandscape, but one where you'd be hard-pressed to find anything in a similar location to where it was in the last. I just wish the Early Access version released with more biomes, but like many of the flaws I'm pointing out here, the devs say they have the intention to resolve it. The generation system here is cool and I can't wait for them to add new cards.

On Gameplay:

The crafting system is great and the variety of stylized aesthetics you can choose for your estate is AWESOME, but it's hampered by a couple of really, really painful points.

First, the build limit per structure is minuscule. I don't mean "Oh, you can only build a giant castle and not a massive realm-spanning town? That sucks..." No, I mean you can get away with a few modestly-sized buildings. Yeah. The devs have pointed out that they plan to fix this in the coming months and that the initial limit is so low to ensure server stability in the meantime. That makes sense, but their choice for the initial build limit is truly heartbreaking in a game that advertises the amazing estates you can built. At the risk of sounding entitled, it sort of feels like a slap in the face. Getting the game to a point where it's stable enough to handle larger build limits should be an absolute priority for the dev team. Like, above anything else except the mentioned offline mode.

Second, I have no idea why in the world Inflexion would make it so that I can't craft from storage in my estate. Yes, I can blueprint my entire building and everything in it ahead of time and then just go grab things from storage, but it's an annoying extra step that shouldn't be there. Unlike most of the complaints I have (with the exception of the UI/UX design that I'll talk about later on), this hasn't been mentioned as a feature that might be coming but is instead a deliberate design decision, and it's not a good one. I hope they change it later on.

The companion system is pretty barebones at the moment and, consequently, terrible. Sorry, Inflexion, but this bit does bug me. They grab random items and break down trees/rocks, which can be helpful, but they can't be directly controlled and do very little damage with their default equipment.

Of course I could forgive the inability to command them if this next part weren't the case: Of everything in this world, the companions have the least actual "life" to them, and I think a part of me is really sad about that because I want them to add to the living feel the rest of the game gives me. Yes, the companions run around and kill things like you expect them to, but they also sometimes stand uncomfortably close and stare at you wordlessly with the soulless gaze of one who has seen Cthulhu himself (praise be His name). It's unfortunate that the enemies and creatures in this game seem more alive than the companions I spend time adventuring with. Yet again, this is another Early Access pain point that will be addressed, but I hope it will be prioritized.

On Stability:

I've only had one disconnect in my time playing, and that was because my internet connection went out in a storm. That being said, there are issues with the "always online" model. Maybe two or three times, I've experienced a small "rubber banding" effect. It's jarring, but only happens once and then resolves. Sometimes I'll craft something and lose the resources, but not get the product in return. It occurred about three times in separate cases. These things are annoying and frustrating, given that so much of the early game requires you to painstakingly gather resources to get anywhere, but not particularly game-breaking as of right now.

I don't get stutter or framerate drops with everything set on quality or ultra. I have an i7-13900KF CPU set to factory clock speed, 48 GB of RAM, and a 3080 Ti GPU. My computer runs it fine and gets consistent framerates, but I might not reflect the median profile of the person playing the game.

On UI/UX Design & Controller Support:

I think this is my real sticking point with the game, and one I just.. I really don't understand. I get it, I'm one of the few people who plays PC games with a controller but, by the Gods, using a controller can be frustrating in this game. I want to move one stack of something to a container, so I press X. Cool. I want to view the options for something in that container or in my inventory while that menu is open, so I press Y... aaaaand it just moved everything from my inventory or the container to the other thing. What am I even doing here? I realize that it also says Y is for moving all items in a container, but the tooltip on the item I'm currently viewing IN that container says to press Y to view options still. If I want to view the available blueprints at the essence trader, I actually can't use my controller to even do so and am forced to use my mouse to hover over and click the dropdowns and click something in them to be able to use my controller again in the menu.

The UI itself is clunky, with menus that require inordinate amounts of clicking through to get to options. I know this bit is subjective, but the style of the UI just seems offensively ugly to my eyes. There should be a more intuitive, less text-heavy way to get to something I want to craft than clicking through two wordy dropdowns in separate tabs. These sorts of old style, counterintuitive design choices are everywhere. Many of these things could have been more cleanly conveyed with icons and in tooltip menus like in the inventory, but they're not. Everything takes up a huge portion of the screen and it doesn't make for an attractive layout.

This kind of thing happens consistently. It isn't a bug, or a glitch, or an Early Access issue; this is bad UI/UX design. Why? Why would you so carelessly make almost every aspect of the menus you make me stare at frequently? Please, devs, get someone on your team to completely rework the UI/UX. Your art and environment teams knock it out of the park, but this might be the single most important aesthetic choice you make, and it shouldn't be ignored.

Overall, Nightingale is fun. I like it, it's creative, and I'm enjoying my time with it.

But the little things can make or break an Early Access title, and Inflexion's decision to see through this release in such an unpolished state has marred the reputation of an awesome-looking game that should've been a slam dunk.

And I can't help but find that really saddening.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLet me start this review with some frank honesty: this is a true early access game and, unless I miss my guess, built by a fairly small team. If you are hoping to play something polished, you will want to come back to this later.

The survival-crafting genre is fairly large and contains a great deal of variants, so here are some more subjective aspects of the game:
-Crafting and building is far and away the biggest driver of the game--exploration and combat feel more secondary to building and making upgrades. If you enjoy a crafting ladder, then you will probably enjoy this game! If you are more into exploring and hunting, it's a little iffier.
-Granular Systems: the crafting and combat systems both have a bunch of fiddly pieces, from damage types to hit locations in combat to a truly nitty gritty crafting system. Again, if you like things to be crunchy, you may enjoy this, but if you don't want to spend time with this, you might want to pass.

Pros:
+The game is stunningly beautiful, even on my panini press of a computer, and while the facial models are a little potato-esque, I see a lot of room for potential improvement, and the bones are promising.
+the lore is promising, and Puck is an appropriately obnoxious jackass of a goat. 10/10 did their homework.
+the concept is a fresh take on a post-apocalyptic genre, with a time period I don't often see in survival games. Fans of Failbetter Games may have a great time with this one.

Cons:
--the game does not currently do a very good job at explaining it's very granular systems, and is quite unforgiving about figuring them out at any difficulty above easy.
--I would prefer a little more freedom in the building controls, particularly ways to swap out wall styles without dismantling an entire building.
--combat is VERY swingy, and goes from so easy the granularity doesn't matter or punishingly difficult with little guidance
--this is less a con and more of a personal note, but I would prefer more camera controls in third person mode or an ability to adjust the FOV in first person to help mitigate motion sickness.

Overall, the game is very obviously unfinished and has some of that classic early access Jank we all know and feel some kind of way about, but it has the bones of something spectacular, and if you don't mind the rough entry, it's well worth giving a shot. If you don't quite love spending hours building unnecessarily elaborate home bases, give this 3-6 months and check back on their progress.\t\t\t\t\t
