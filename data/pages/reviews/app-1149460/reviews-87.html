\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo I stopped playing this game around week 40ish, mostly due to stability issues. (30 or less fps with a 3070 and even worse during weather events and also while hosting) however I decided to come back and see how much the game has improved and I'm Incredibly pleased to see me getting 144+ fps with the same setup I had back then but with a much better resolution to boot. optimization has improved immensely and I'm glad they actually put effort into doing so. the newly added updates every week between now and then has made the game far more enjoyable and fun for me as well with the game itself being a nice mix of chill relaxing moments and tense sudden ones.

The good: There Is a lot that Icarus brings to the table

Base building is very aesthetically pleasing, especially once you get to stone and refined wood structures, bases begin to look so comfortable and nice looking. a lot of this game is nice to look at, with lush forests, harsh deserts and oasis and barren tundras.

Crafting and progressing is a chore sometimes depending on where you are, but feels rewarding at least to me. I've always found it nice to finally get away from early game tools and weapons and then upgrade to steel or higher, being able to bring items from the workshop also aids in early advancement and is nice because workshop items aren't OP but they are far better than stone and some iron.

The world of Icarus feels brutal as everything feels against you, animals both familiar and unfamiliar are always out to get you, and the weather events are there to level everything you've built with only the toughest of bases brushing off these storms. Lightning storms will rage and cause fires that consume anything burnable nearby unless countered, strong winds can level wooden bases before you realize It, sandstorms can burn you out of your home and snowstorms can quite literally crush your base.

Audio Isn't too bad either, animals, player actions and overall noises are great (axe and pickaxe sounds could be lowered a bit) The storms also sound amazing with the game having some of the most Intense sounding weather In nearly any game I've played.

The bad: The game Isn't all sunshine and rainbows, there are still quite a few issues that need addressing.

Combat Isn't entirely bad but with the game having "bosses" that can really ruin your day they need to be made to feel like bosses instead of just stronger variants of an animal. Having attacks that you could telegraph and dodge to avoid damage Is needed heavily instead of every encounter having said creature just rushing you and attacking non stop, this isn't combat its a damage check at that point between you and the enemy.

Spawning Is also a bit of an issue, especially in the desert with random cougars and scorpions just poofing into existence right in front of you and Immediately aggroing you is just bad spawn design, spawns need to be set out of player sights and a distance away so they can perceive the threat and act accordingly on whether to fight or run.

AI isn't terrible but I don't believe all carnivores should immediately be out to kill you, bears are a good example of this, being territorial is one thing but watching a bear traverse a mountain just to come near my base, see me in my window and start breaking my walls to get to me is a bit much. there should be warning calls or ways for animals to basically say "back off" and warn the player. Herbivores shouldn't also turn tail and run when the spot you a mile away, they should just notice you're there and keep an eye on you, If you start acting like you're about to attack them or move towards them then yes, run for the hills.

Last but not least the UI needs an overhaul, I hate having to open every fireplace or furnace and physically click a button to turn on, or having to click a button to craft, there should just be a hot key for that. as for the HUD, I feel that needs a rework as well. While It isn't horrid to look at, it feels very bulky in terms of screen space, theres so much unnecessary info being displayed at all times and at times i just end up playing with the HUD turned off.

Overall Icarus Is by no means a bad game, sure It isn't for everyone. but if you enjoy a game that can bring both a challenge and be relaxing at the same time then It may be for you. The devs release new updates nearly every week, they listen quite a lot to community suggestions and are actively working to fix issues that players find. The game from this point will only get better.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt is not a bad game, but I cannot really recommend it for player interesting in the genre, because there are better ones. Like a fully modded Minecraft or The Forest or Valheim.

Icarus has a general idea: Play in short missions and progress through the game by learning the world and mechanics. Good idea, but badly executed. You spawn in a totally generic world with some advanced technology (dropshop, air space suit) and your first steps are "craft a stone axe".

You have the typical progression. Stone, metal, better metal, basic firearms and advanced electronic, but nothing over the top "fusion reactor". But you are already a space nation?

You also encounter generic enemies on your "alien" planet. Bears, deer, boards. The most exotic one was a giant scorpion and some cave worms. These animals can breath. You cannot. YOu need to collect oxygen. Why? Because it is a third survival aspect you have to manage. You are a space exploring species. And need oxygen tanks?

The missions are an interesting idea, but most of them that I tried were basic "run to point A and do something". Running was the major part of the missions and I abondend them. I played a bit "free survival" and it was nice, but the progression system didn't have any unique stuff that differs from other games of the genre.

The most unique stuff is the weather system. Sometimes you encounter storms, winds or heavy rains effecting you and your structures. Visually pleasing and interesting to handle. Gives you a reason to build stone walls.

So, generic world, generic enemies, generic technology progression. Weather system. And it costs 35$. And there are two major DLCs each costing the same. Introducing some other worlds, which could be really interesting, but I don't want to pay 30$ more to maybe see interesting worlds and creatures with the same generic tech tree.

All in All I had some fun with the game. Played it with a buddy. But most of the time was running, grinding and hoping for some uniqueness. There are good ideas but badly executed.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhile there are so many things that could've been enjoyable, and the art for the game is quite appreciated, the game is so broken it ruins the experience.

The game has a soft requirement of multiplayer, yet there are no official servers or matchmaking system in place -- so it forces you to convince a friend to buy this game. This wouldn't be so bad if the game wasn't so broken.

I started the tutorial mission and no objectives loaded. I spent ~4 hours before deciding to leave the planet and it said mission failed. I redid it and suddenly the objectives popped up. Waste of 5 hours. I played a few more missions and with one mission the objective glitched after 2+ hours of building up (couldn't pick up radar station delta and couldn't request a resupply). What happens when I play one of these missions that takes 40+ hours? Just start over when/if it glitches? No thank you.

The lore in this game makes no sense, and was haphazardly thrown together. You need oxygen, yet there are deer and bears running around. It's just an excuse for them to give you another food/water timer to manage to distract you from the fact that the game has so little content. The in-game character over the radio said it's "toxins" or whatever, but that's complete nonsense. The animals have genetically modified magic that makes them immune to said toxins, including cyanide (https://surviveicarus.com/lore/), yet my character drinks water from outside. How do these unspecified toxins supposedly in the air not wind up diffusing into the water? Does Henry's law not apply in this universe? Also how does my character eat meat from these genetically modified magic animals without getting cyanide (or other unspecified toxin) poisoning? Does bio-accumulation also not exist in this universe? The vegetables my character eats from outside don't accumulate it? The game had so much potential, and I feel they didn't even invest the time to consult with someone who passed college-level introduction to chemistry. Could've been the O2 partial pressure was just not enough to sustain humans, but they genetically modified the animals to have hemoglobin with low-affinity to O2 or something, to survive and bridge the ecosystem until the atmosphere reaches full pressure -- but they went with something that makes no sense. Also how does my character eat food from the outside through their space helmet without inhaling all this cyanide? And isn't his spacesuit continuous with the helmet? He gets mauled by bears and gets deep wounds, yet it doesn't ever puncture his space suit? Won't the cyanide diffuse through his skin? Why doesn't the helmet ever break?

Okay, if you turn your brain off and pretend science and logic don't apply, we can just appreciate the scenery because it's a game: you have fun for a few hours, take in the gorgeous view, but then you realize that you exhaust majority of the content early on. It's just the same wolves/boars/jaguars/bears that you fight on these defense missions with their janky AI. One bear got glitched on a mission and kept resetting aggro (and his health) over and over so I could never kill him. Eventually I just ran around it to finish the quest objective. So many game-breaking problems that kill the enjoyment.

If you aren't satisfied with the base game, you can pay three times as much in DLCs to get more limited content which should've just been included in the original game.\t\t\t\t\t
