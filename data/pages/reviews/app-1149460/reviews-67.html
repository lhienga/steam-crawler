\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI like Icharus.
Well, I *want* to like Icharus.

Lots of the game is just fine, following much of the expectations of the genre. The visuals are very pretty, the lighting lovely.
Some of the game is really quite interesting subverting expectations by introducing missions, events and most interestingly Tarkov style single missions.

However, strikingly large portions of the game are just amazingly badly judged and or/lazy.

You get a taste of the worst part of the game very early on, giving a very poor impression as leans heavily on the least well designed and implemented portion of the game Combat.

Combat has hit-point sacks of enemies seemingly thrown at you by the thousand, regardless of your technological proficiency (tech dictating access to equipment and tools, which defines how well geared you can be).

Defence missions tend to devolve into "build a lot of walls and stand on top of them" whilst you get swarmed with around 10 enemies at a time, often with up to 60 in a wave, with three waves of increasing difficulty.
This is made worse by very poorly implemented spawning systems, which may require you to go and hunt enemies that have somehow gotten stuck on cliffs/rocks in the surrounding area. This is irritating, but not a major issue. However, finally clearing the last of the stuck enemies, only to return to find whatever you were defending is now in ruins because the next wave of more difficult enemies spawned in behind you and has had free reign to smash everything down in your absence.

You'd think that this was just because the developers have made no distinction between a solo player and groups but no, apparently the numbers of enemies ramp up with the numbers of players present.

This is just lazy design. Using quantity to get over the lack of quality. The enemies are dull and basically all the same at least in the first zone, there is some small variation in other zones, but nothing to be impressed by "Challenge" is provided by throwing numbers at you and by making every enemy have spongy amounts of health.

This might not be so bad if you had many different ways of dealing with enemies, but no, not really. There seems to be very few ways of setting up traps and very few ways of setting up defences. Your weaponry is uninspiring and slow not necessarily a bad thing if this was a slower, tenser survival experience but, as mentioned, the game throws cartoonishly large numbers of enemies at you, so this is obviously not the case. Irritatingly, you cannot aim straight down and the wall's hit boxes play poorly with your weaponry. A problem when you spend so much of your time standing on top of walls, aiming downwards.

In the end, you find yourself having to vastly over tech yourself to overcome these problems, which means you have to throw comically large amounts of time at every mission, as the later techs require the player to gather lots of resources, make equipment and deployables with those resources and then use those deployables and equipment to make further and more specialised resources which will require even more stuff to be gathered that takes ages to process, which might be turned into something you need... only for all of that to basically be for naught if you get caught out by the buggy mess that is combat/mission design.
Do it all again. Because you just failed.

It's not like you can afford to experiment, either, as your access to variety of gameplay is locked behind metagame levelling and, through that, the Tech Tree. Levels are slow which I have some sympathy for, as it is maintained between play sessions but you need them to unlock all of the things you will need. Multiplayer can allow you to specialise, somewhat, but you ultimately need to spend a lot of points on the core items/equipment and experimentation means levelling some more if you made a poor choice or, more likely, the thing you chose turns out to need a bunch of other things before you can make/use the thing you chose in the first place. Again, that ends up meaning you need to level even more, which eventually forces you to focus on the core tech path to exclusion of all else.

In short, the game doesn't seem to know what it wants to be. It has elements of lots of genres, but it doesn't do any of them well enough to stand out. These elements fall to pieces as soon as they interact with each other.
Each of these elements seem to provide challenge solely though mindless volume and so, as a result, you end up having to throw incredible amounts of time at the game only to look back at it and wonder, did I actually enjoy any one part of that? Was there any part of that time that wouldn't have been more enjoyable if I had played several games each doing one aspect of Icarus, but actually done well?

Barring the few twists Icarus has to offer... no.

I could break down all the different things that feed into this, from animations and atmosphere to gameplay loop decisions and UX, but I have spent way more time rambling about this than I had expected when I sat down to do this review. Long story short, this game has been out for some time and I quite sure that many of these things have been deliberately implemented, rather than teething troubles, and I cannot fathom why.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLove hate relationship, much work still needed to be done. Its pretty relaxing (nicely saying its boring) and its a great low brain wave activity, but that's a problem because its advertised to be a little more...exciting. I wouldn't say this is a big deal because adding some boss animals in different parts of the map could easily fix this. I love the excitement of running into a bear but it's kinda ruined by the fact that you can just run away from it and it won't attack anyways unless you want it to. The animal AI needs lots of work. There's not much incentive to explore or much to discover, they need to add some more stuff to the map. The Tech and Talent trees are fairly extensive, which is the main thing that drew me to this game, if you stay focused you will stay busy so this kinda handles the "lack of excitement" bit. Yet, those both need work as well, they don't always make much sense, especially the talent tree. Open world is obviously the main mode attracting people to the game, and it has a ridiculous amount of potential, which is mostly what this game is, un-used potential. That is to say, I don't know what the developers situation is or what limitations they are working under. If they listen to the community, I'm sure this game will be a show stopper in 2-3 years. They need to stop with the paid DLC's for a bit and beef up the base game, I can understand being strapped for money makes developing difficult, but they are taking time away from what they should be spending on the base game with the DLC's. I hope they can find a way to handle this, either hire more people to work entirely on DLC's to free up another team to work on the base game, I think this is how they should handle it but i'm not a dev so I cant say much. Overall I do love the game and I'll get my money's worth out of it so I would recommend.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIcarus is just one of those games where you can tell the devs put love into it. I truly love this game. I play it solo half the time and the rest of the time friends can pop in and we can get missions done. The nerds that want to explore and fight can go mine the caves and the nerds like me who want to base build and manage can! I still don't quite get the hydroponics and automated farming systems yet... and I have 250+ hours in the game. So there are little advanced things that could be improved and expanded on. There are bugs. There are little wonky things. And that's just software. Not a deal breaker. The over all experience is fun and tense and the heuristics are great. Other than normal things that will get fixed or built out as things progress... this game is highly recommended for the survival game enjoy-er. Final score: 11/10.\t\t\t\t\t
