\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLet's start with how this game was developed.

Apparently a bunch of content that was promised in Early Access is now sold as DLC. I didn't realise this when I bought the game, but I also bought it on sale. So for now this hasn't really affected me much, but if you are worried about what the devs might do in future, here is something to know.

Having said that, the game is being actively developed, new things are added all the time, and perhaps having funding through DLCs is helping this aspect.

The premise: terraforming gone wrong, you get sent down to do things for various factions. But why on Earth (excuse the pun), would you be sent to a new planet with not even a bedroll? I understand that you can earn some currency that allows you to go down with some equipment. But we can build rockets and space stations. You should be able to get basic supplies with you. I am pretty sure that's what NASA plans to do with Mars. Also let me make my bedroll out of fibre or something. I don't need all that leather.

So you kind of have to go, "Okay so the premise is kind of cool, but there are contradictions. We can terraform planets, we can build rockets, and space stations, but we must be in an hyper-capitalist world, where you have to earn ABSOLUTELY everything yourself, even a bedroll. And someone must have gone cheap on the suits, because we can get pneumonia. In caves. Only." And even though the thought was amusing for a short 2 seconds, it still didn't make much sense. So I just shrugged and moved on.

I found the beginning of the game on your first character is very grindy. So grindy that I wound up modding some of that. I still want a challenge, but I feel that maybe having some sliders like Valheim and V-Rising might do this game some good. The game takes off at around level 20ish. But it takes a long time to get there.

This game has some decent mods though, and the fact that it is moddable is certainly a plus. I think most people mod survival games at some point.

The crafting progression in this is rather good. It is what is keeping me engaged. I really like how No Man's Sky lets you automate certain things and how you can create extractors, how you can power bases, etc. And this game does the same thing. It has several tiers of things to craft, etc. It isn't something new, but its implementation is just satisfying.

I rather like the weather events too. They add jeopardy. You do learn easy tricks to trivialise them, but a thunderstorm can set the forest on fire. And that you have to deal with. I managed to set a forest on fire by being on fire myself and running towards a lake. So... I burned all bushes between my cabin and the lake. Yeah. I do like how environment hazards work here. I would have rolled on clear ground. But you can't do that here.

Or you are exploring somewhere, wind up in a snowy mountain range, a hail storm hits, you hide under a rock but four wolves and one snow leopard come to greet you, so you can't stay put, into your rocky shelter anymore. I think the weather is a strong selling point for sure.

The game looks pretty good in general.

The combat is a bit of a downer, and it's rather binary. You hit the face with a ranged weapon, you kill stuff really quick (and can often one shot). If you do not, you have to strafe in a circle with a knife and spam your left click until the thing is dead. That's it. No reason to craft any other weapon other than your ranged weapon of choice and a knife. You might like to build something in case what you are trying to hunt is a bit too hard, so it can't reach you straight away. And then you try to hit it in the face. It's almost like you are playing Fortnite but this is not as smooth.

Animations in third person are not very good. And the aiming is a bit wonky. All the game does is to zoom out a little when you are in third person, but when you hit something, you are still trying to hit things with the dot in the middle of your screen, which is slightly removed from your character. You are not actually trying to make contact with your character. So I just stay in first person, as third person seems to be an after-thought. I wonder if it was added some time in Early Access as a player request, but it was never well implemented.

Icarus has an open world and a mission system. (It also has outposts, which basically is a non-aggressive version of the game where you mostly just build.) The mission system works like a rogue-lite game. You do the missions, extract, lose anything you might have built on the surface. But you earn currency to unlock new equipment and your character still levels and earns talent points. Yet, not all missions involve creating bases. Some are just hunting. Some are just gathering ores. You get the gist. It actually does not feel like you are wasting your time (at least I don't). I like having missions to do. It's almost like Deep Rock Galactic in that respect.

But if you like to build persistent bases, you go in the open world, and then eventually craft the mission board, and then you can do most missions in the open world too. That makes it so you can access the equipment from both progression systems - the equipment you get with currency, and the equipment you get via levelling tech trees.

The game also has talent trees, which is something I really like. You can become a better hunter, a better builder, a better forager, etc.

CONCLUSION
Once you get past the initial logic issues with the premise, and if combat isn't a make or break proposition for you, this is a very good survival game.\t\t\t\t\t
