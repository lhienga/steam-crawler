\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFirst and foremost - this isn't a game. It's an "exploration simulator." But unless you're really into space exploration, astronomy, and star gazing, there's nothing for anyone to do. There are no quests, no goals, no enemies, no economy, or anything that a gamer would associate with a game. While most of the locations are real - there are a lot that aren't. For instance, if you want to orbit the star Rigel in the constellation of Orion, you can. And you can see what the night sky would look like if you were sitting on a planet in the Rigel system. Except ... there are no real planets orbiting Rigel. Everything is procedurally generated.

Don't get me wrong - it's a neat little program. Well ... perhaps not "little" given it represents most of the known universe. It will even take you to different galaxies. Some of the imagery is just downright beautiful. It's the kind of program I like diving into when I'm yakking on the phone or feeling a little under the weather or watching television and need something to do during those annoying, repetitive, and psychologically torturous commercials. Even people without much interest in space would probably rather hunt for comets or find life on another world than to sit there slack-jawed as Big Pharma tries to get as many Americans as possible hooked on their drugs with their repetitive, non-stop, and boring commercials. (Whichever Big Pharma lobbyist who managed to convince the courts - under the public's radar, mind you - to allow prescription drugs to be advertised on television should be imprisoned for life - since it used to be illegal to prevent patients from self-diagnosing.)

But be careful. For as gorgeous as the graphics can be, there are a couple of horrible bugs. For instance, if you zoom in very close to a moon or asteroid in the "solar system chart," the program will seize your mouse -- at which point you won't be able to move your cursor off your task bar. All you can do to fix it is to enter the task manager and end the program. It's annoying as hell.

Also, figuring out how to use the "Find Object" menu is somewhat of a challenge unless you know and understand astronomic nomenclature. Even then, Space Engine uses its own nomenclature. For instance, if you want to "Go to" the star labeled as 21 in the constellation Leo Minor - you have to type in "21 lmi" for "21 Leonis Minoris." If the star has a proper name like Betelgeuse or Arcturus or Regulus then you can type in the name and you're off.

But most stars do not have proper names. They instead use a greek letter. So if you want to visit Sigma Ursa Major - good luck. Typing in "sigma" won't work. It only recognizes three letters. So you have to type in "sig" -- but typing "sig ursa major" won't work either. You have to type in "sig uma" .... yeah. Cryptic as hell. Especially since sometimes even the three letters won't work.

So ... let's say you want to visit the star "Theta Persei" in the constellation Perseus. Good luck. I still don't know how to find it in Space Engine. "Theta Persei" doesn't work. Neither does "The Per" or "Theta Per" or any other combination. The best thing to do is to use another program like Stellarium that has a much easier "Go To" program to find another identifier. Like "Theta Persei" is also known as the star HIP 12777. If you type in HIP 12777 then Space Engine will take you to Theta Persei. Otherwise you'll be floundering indefinitely trying to get the program to do what you want it to. They really need to make the "Go To" more user-friendly without needing to use other astronomy programs to look up obscure astronomical nomenclature in order to get Space Engine to take you where you want to go.

But overall it's a fun program to mess around with. For anyone who loves astronomy -- to put on some space music like Stellardrone or Jon Serrie and literally explore the universe without the stress of enemies or goals or running out of fuel, then Space Engine is the kind of program for you.\t\t\t\t\t
