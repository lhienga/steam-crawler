\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA mildly entertaining software toy. Recommended when on sale.

This is basically a supercharged version of the VistaPro landscape generation software from the early 1990s. It procedurally generates fake planets which all begin to look very same-y after a few hours. Don't get me wrong, it can produce some truly spectacular screenshots. But the rendering engine is limited in that it can't produce anything but empty planets. No flora or fauna of any type. No alien ruins. No cities. Just bare, empty planets. Oh sure, you can find a life-bearing planet. And the extent of the life you'll see is a different color texture on the ground to represent grass or the alien equivalent. Yawn. I got really tired of looking at the same planetscapes with slightly different hills, canyons, etc. The only real variety is in the atmospheric composition and densities, which can produce different color effects of sunsets/rises based upon the star color. That quickly wears thin as well. Yes, you can hop instantly to any of a gazillion different stars in billions of galaxies anywhere in the universe... and see slight variations on the same procedurally generated planets over and over. You can find six-star systems with complex interlocking orbits and watch them go around each other for a few minutes. Then yawn. (By definition, the tidal forces of such a star system would almost certainly preclude planets and the stars are very far apart at any rate, so no cool screenshot of six suns rising in the morning.)

What there is of it is pretty well done, it's just that there isn't much here to hold the interest. I suppose if you're a chemistry nerd you could get all excited about atmospheric compositions... "Oh wow! That planet has acetone seas and a high-pressure methane and carbon monoxide atmosphere along with heavy concentrations of three different noble gases! Cool! I must land there!" For us normal people, however, that's another yawn. The reviewers claiming to suffer an "existential crisis" from this software must have very shallow imaginations indeed. The universe, as the saying goes, is not only stranger than we imagine, it's stranger than we CAN imagine. This software is proof of that, because it is limited by what very little we know about the universe from observing the planets in our own system. Everything that we think we know about those recently discovered exoplanets is still just inferences on a spectrometer; nobody's actually sent a probe to one or even taken a useful photograph of one yet, so we have no idea what they are actually like. We're similar to a guy who has been stuck in a single house his whole life with only a globe of the Earth for entertainment and who is speculating wildly on what the culture in India is like. We simply don't have enough information to even make an educated guess -- which the James Webb Telescope is now proving as it systematically dismantles modern cosmological theory. (Accompanied by much snickering from those of us who have been saying for decades that the currently accepted gravity-based model of the universe is fundamentally flawed and that dark matter is just the modern version of phlogiston.)

Anyway, get this on sale and make yourself some pretty screenshots that you can cycle across your desktop.\t\t\t\t\t
