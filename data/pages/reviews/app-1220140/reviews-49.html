\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLet me be clear up front: if you can't tolerate Early Access titles that have a lot of problems, don't bother picking this up yet. The "rough edges" on this thing are like sandpaper. I'm not going to go through an exhaustive list of every awkward UI element or frustrating oversight in the game's mechanics, but buyer beware: there are many, and they need to be fixed.

That being said, I had a ton of fun playing this and I'll certainly be back for more. This game is more than just another tycoon/management game with a pallet swap and a new aesthetic, this thing turns many of your tycoon-citybuilder-management-sim-whatever expectations on their heads to give you a genuinely new experience.

For starters, unlike most tycoon games, where the key to success is maximizing the production of every building and pushing as much product out as possible while stocking all of your storage facilities to max, you can't do that here. You'll lose, and fast. Overproducing not only causes resources to be wasted, it attracts the attention of law enforcement. Any building that stocks an illegal substance to its max value has a risk of being raided, and your distribution points (seaports, airports, and border checkpoints) have limits on how much product you can safely move through them. You need to carefully control production, pushing it as high as you can [/i]safely[/i] get it, while making sure your warehouses never accumulate a surplus. Sometimes you may even need to burn a building down and run, so the less you have in there, the better. The key is to build light and safe, instead of big, and that's an interesting difference from most games in this genre.

Secondly, you cannot (and should not) over-expand. To gain territory, you have to fight rival gangs for it, and this will quickly get the attention of higher tiers of law enforcement (think: GTA). To get around this, you have to think about what you can get with the least amount of work and what you can do with it; consider the tradeoffs of bribing officials to use a city without taking control of it, or hide contraband inside legal goods to pass them through rival territory unnoticed.

And finally, the dirty-money/clean-money dynamic is pretty cool. You can pay for anything with either clean money (in the bank, works electronically, and doesn't require an infrastructure to use) or dirty money, which you hide on your private property and, because it is cash-only, has to be physically moved from location to location by gang members. Dirty money is easy to get (you get it from all drug sales), but cant be moved through rival gang territory or through government checkpoints without paying a portion in bribes.

To get clean money, you have to launder your dirty money, either by funneling it into service industries (taxicabs, churches, amusement parks) to create legal cover for its existence, or by calling in favors from politicians and paying them under the table at a premium. You can then use the clean cash to pay for legal services or support buildings without plugging them into your network. But you can avoid doing this if you are able to establish efficient delivery lanes from your sell-points to the locations where you want to pay your employees in cash, but remember that storing too much means it could get lost or trigger a police raid.

Everything in this game is a balancing act, and that's what sets it apart from other tycoon games. The trademark of a lazy management game is the ability of the player to simply make more of everything and brute force their way through the story. Here, subtlety is a large part of the strategy and it gives the game that extra edge that it needs to really shine.\t\t\t\t\t

If you get this game thinking you are going to get a simulator of gang warfare you will be disappointed. Combat basically comes down to who has the higher number.
