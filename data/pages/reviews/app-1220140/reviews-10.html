\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRating: ★★★☆☆

Cartel Tycoon, an indie strategy game that puts players in the shoes of a drug cartel leader, presents an intriguing concept with the potential for an engaging and immersive experience. While the game has its strengths, it falls short in certain areas, leaving room for improvement.

One of the standout features of Cartel Tycoon is its unique setting and theme. The game dives into the dark and dangerous world of drug cartels, offering a different perspective and a departure from typical strategy game themes. Managing resources, expanding territory, and making strategic decisions to maintain control of the drug trade provide a sense of intrigue and challenge.

The game's visual style and atmosphere contribute to its immersion. The hand-drawn graphics and vibrant colors create a distinctive aesthetic that captures the essence of the game's setting. The soundtrack adds to the overall ambiance, enhancing the sense of being a powerful cartel leader.

Cartel Tycoon's gameplay mechanics provide a decent level of depth and strategy. Balancing production, distribution, and security while navigating the challenges of rival cartels and law enforcement can be engaging. The progression system, with its unlocks and upgrades, offers a sense of growth and advancement as players build their criminal empire.

However, Cartel Tycoon is not without its flaws. The game's tutorial and learning curve can be overwhelming for newcomers to the genre or those unfamiliar with similar management-style games. The lack of clarity in explaining mechanics and objectives can lead to confusion and frustration early on.

Furthermore, Cartel Tycoon suffers from repetitive gameplay and a lack of variety. Once players grasp the core mechanics, the gameplay loop becomes predictable and monotonous. The lack of diversity in mission types, events, and challenges hampers long-term engagement and can result in a feeling of stagnation.

The AI in Cartel Tycoon is also lacking in depth and intelligence. The rival cartels and law enforcement agencies often exhibit predictable behavior, making encounters and interactions less dynamic and strategic than they could be. Additionally, the pathfinding and AI behavior for workers and vehicles can be inconsistent and frustrating, affecting the overall flow and efficiency of gameplay.

In summary, Cartel Tycoon offers an intriguing concept and visual style that immerses players in the world of drug cartels. The gameplay mechanics and progression system provide moments of strategic satisfaction. However, the game's steep learning curve, repetitive gameplay, lack of variety, and AI shortcomings detract from its potential. I really want to like this game. I love the setting and play style. But after everything I try I cannot continue a game for more than a few hours until I lose all my money or a cartel wipes me. I slowly expand my cartel and seem to run out of money. Im talking restarting over and over again. Your wanted level if you will is the biggest thing that holds me back from expanding. If you choose to wait other capos try to raid your properties causing you to defend them and then guess what...your terror goes back up.

I get bombarded by other capos for favors too often and quests from my cartel members. I usually don't have the resources to complete them so I lose more loyalty.

I've played before the official release, shortly after the release and current day. I have yet to find a system where I can see the end game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've played cartel tycoon since the beginning. The game is in a much better state than it was on release and therefore I am very proud and grateful, as the game definitely has something to it. I find true enjoyment in the challenge that the game provides with certain mode and starting point combinations. When the numbers just keep creeping higher and higher and you've nailed the entire production to launder ration without anything overflowing, it is truly satisfying.

My strategy before the fight and stash update mainly focused on getting my lieutenants as powerful as possible as early as possible and going to war on absolutely everyone, relentlessly fighting to erase all enemy gangs. With the addition of weapons and lieutenant stamina, taking a city by force has become much less attractive. My strategy totally changed. I only use weapons and lieutenants for defence and possibly for taking a building or two with valuable resources in it, otherwise it is much simpler to just burn the enemy buildings. I now make friends with rival gang leaders and then recruit them. I finished survival mode without any real trouble without having a single war. I find this ironic, as the fight and stash update was aimed at improving combat, it achieved the exact opposite. Attacking rival cities with the intent to eliminate the gang is a very bad decision. 30+ Power difference, 80+ weapons and still my lieutenants run out of stamina before the end of the war.... Seems stupid.

All in all I am happy with the fight and stash update, as it opened a completely different strategy. I now see enemy gang leaders as recruit-able lieutenants and use this as a strategic element to gain an advantage or a needed lieutenant ability. I enjoy Cartel Tycoon immensely and I will continue to do so, as I wish to 100% the game.\t\t\t\t\t
