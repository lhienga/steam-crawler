\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDragon Age: Origins blew me away when I first played it back in the day and while it didn't age very well in certain aspects, its core RPG parts more than hold up.
Combat and character building are not incredibly complex, but they have more than enough depth and options to them. Being real time with pause and with several difficulties, you can pretty much adjust the pace of combat to your desire. It definitely requires more pausing for strategizing and micro-management of each party member on higher difficulty, but if that's not something you're into you can get by just fine by controlling mainly one character in real time and relying on the amazing tactics system for the rest of your party on lower difficulty. Each class has plenty of abilities and a few sub-classes and there are various interesting synergies between them. So you can tinker with builds and party compositions a fair amount, although mages obviously have a greater number of spells which are also more distinct and they generally feel more fleshed out and engaging to play than the other classes. Amount of gear is adequate, but there is not a ton of unique sets and items.
I am just a huge fan of BioWare's writing and presentation style and this is also one of the highlights of DAO - the world building and lore are strong, companions and important characters are well written, the dialogues are very good and so is the pacing of main story. Different player character origins are a nice touch, both due to giving you different start gameplay-wise and having consequences later on. There is a lot of freedom for dealing with each situation as well as the order in which you can tackle different story arcs or areas. Choices are numerous, genuinely impactful and intertwined - there are many possible outcomes for everything even remotely important. Conversations with companions are quite interesting and sometimes funny, as your party members become more intriguing and start showing different sides of their personalities as you become friendlier with them. It is clear more time and effort went into making specific characters, but everyone seems realistic enough or is entertaining at the very least. I strongly recommend keeping everyone around, trying to get on their good side and finishing the personal quest lines in order to get more insight into their personalities and better understanding of their motivation. Banter between them can be top-notch too - Alistair, Morrigan and Leliana are probably my favourite party and companions in any game. Everything being voice acted and animated also helps immersing the player into the conversations and feeling more connected to the ongoing events. The tone and look are quite dark and serious, but there are also occasional light-hearted moments. Neither the grim nor playful parts are uncomfortable though. While not too unusual today, all this is pretty impressive for a game that came out in 2009.
Now for the bad - the gameplay and controls can be a bit clunky compared to newer releases (even Inquisition for example, while not nearly as ground breaking, feels much smoother and satisfying to play). The graphics were already mediocre for its time and they absolutely show their age now. Some of this can be slightly remedied by modding though. It really isn't awe-inspiring or visually pleasing by any means, but I don't find it too bad even today if you mainly stick to the top-down view (and that's coming from someone who's sucker for quality graphics and visuals). Enemy variety is low - you're fighting the same warrior, rogue and mage hurlocks and genlocks the whole game with maybe a demon or ogre here and there plus a few area specific enemies and Dragons. And now the biggest issue - it's far from unplayable, but it seems to be prone to crashing on modern systems. Save often.
I reckon the pros easily outweigh the cons and consider this a genre classic that's still worth checking out, especially for people who have never played cRPGs as this is just about the best introduction to the genre you can get.\t\t\t\t\t
