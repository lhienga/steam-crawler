The peak of the franchise

The second Black Ops is a game not needing to be introduced to anyone who even remotely heard about the Call of Duty franchise. You could say it is something of a legend. It is the entry in the long running FPS franchise that, although undeniably popular, has by this point been getting a bit stale and repetitive to the point where CoD became bud of a joke for its never-changing template for campaigns and although these were often stellar in both their design and storytelling (by this point we got the Modern Warfare sub-series along with the first Black Ops). However, it was clear that a subtle, yet intense change was needed - and Treyarch delivered.

"Aside from the fact that I'm still alive, none of this surprises me. Technology got stronger, but we got weaker. We built computers, robots, whole unmanned armies, but no one ever asks: "What happens when the enemy steals the keys?" When the things they built to keep us safe are turned against us, that's when they figured it out: They'll always need men like us, those who are willing to do... what others cannot."

The implementation of choices & consequences system into a (up until now) completely linear campaign was a bold move, but it definitely paid off. It took a campaign which unveiled more like an on-rails interactive movie and showed that this time, you are in charge of how the story develops and your victories as well as f*ckups both have the potential to influence the progression and the endings. I can't stress enough how great this decision was as it not only keep player more engaged and drawn into the story, it also adds another layer of replay value to an already great game.

Another step outside of bounds was a radical change of setting. Prior to Black Ops II, we saw CoD games cover many interesting historical periods (from WW2 all the way to Cold War) along with a alternative present/very near future scenarios displayed in Modern Warfare(s). Black Ops II takes us into (at the time) slightly more distant future of mid 2020s, which allowed the developers to be more adventurous with ideas of the technological evolution, which brings with it a host of new, interesting weapons which play perfectly into newly improved gunplay. However, the best part which shows the true genius of Treyarch was the additional inclusion of a Cold War campaign serving as a direct sequel to the first BO. The juxtaposition of both the distant future and the times long past keeps fresh not only the gameplay but also the narrative as both parts are directly intertwined and as you progress through the historical part, you uncover pieces of the puzzle crucial for the future. It is precisely this which keeps getting this particular entry in the franchise continous and well-deserved praise even over decade after its original release.

Positives & Negatives

\t
Positives
\t
Negatives
\t
+ New graphical effects that, once again, push the franchise's visuals to the next level (the mission "Celerium" is a great example) - combine it with Treyarch's signature level design and you have one of the best looking CoD games to this day
\t
- The pre-rendered cutscenes look outdated at times as their quality suffers
\t
+ Gameplay improvements - gunplay now feels more impactful, animations smoother and I personally enjoyed the inclusion of a hit marker even in singleplayer
\t
- The game's technological state suffers from similar issues present in the first Black Ops - sometimes, objects disappear before they should, enemies bug out and controls do not react to prompts
\t
+ Stellar story writing and presentation with game's dual-setting pushing it amongst the finest of CoD's campaigns
\t
- Sometimes, the graphical quality of close-up textures is not up to par with the tech at the time
\t
+ Character writing is, once again, perfect with both returning characters as well as new ones amplifying the undertone of the story
\t
\t
+ New Choices & Consequences system leading to some of the best and most satisfying endings in the series
\t
\t
+ Voice acting and mo-cap is perfect
\t
- Although, it is fair to say that Hudson's changed VA did not do as great of a job as Ed Harris in the original
\t
+ Soundtrack, although having less licensed and more original songs did not disappoint
