\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCampaign: 9/10
The perfect sequel to black ops 1 and with more fleshed out characters, gameplay and choices that affect the story. Only a 9 because the story can be cliched at times.

Multiplayer: 7/10
Great gunplay with a wide variety of interesting maps, better than the previous multiplayer but not by much. With a few annoyances like the riot shield. (Ruined by hackers though unfortunately)

Zombies: 5/10
Mediocre compared to Black Ops 1. Only Mob of the dead and Origins make it worth playing, the rest are ♥♥♥♥♥. The worst maps in Black Ops 1 look like masterpieces compared to this game.

In short, worth it for the campaign alone. Get it on sale though. Don't bother with the zombies DLC unless you are a fan and even then just get Origins and Mob of the dead.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI can confidently say that objectively, this game has some weak points or elements that are outright horrendously silly, but it is overall better than the first game (but not better than Cold War, the ideal sequel). I know that is a bit of a sus thing to say, but hear me out. What makes Black Ops II great is that its a sequel to a great game. The downside of this is that this marked a very dark era for the multiplayer elements of these games and the focus on multiplayer and zombies would later become fairly hampering to the series' creative stories.

This game, much like David Mason and his father Alex Mason, come from different eras. Even though they are close and consecutive to one another, they are two very different Cold Wars. Ironic, given that a New Cold War is the back drop of the game's setting. (which takes place in 2025 btw, lol, truly - this near-future setting as far as tech went was far too optimistic, while other elements remain entirely prophetic by merely just extended the truth of our present time)

This game came out when Call of Duty had the bitter rivalry against other shooters of its genre, (a Cold War of shooters, if you will) rather than other genres. Whereas Call of Duty dominates now in every regard, Battlefield was a close contender and the personal favourite of many cultured gamers. For every "navy ship taking on water" scene in these games (there are far too many) there is also a jab at Battlefield.

While Battlefield made dynamic and realistic gameplay (but still far from mil-sim), Call of Duty preferred spectacle. The Black Ops games especially, shoved where Modern Warfare may have just pushed, with the amount of vehicle sections that took place in their games. To show that realism wasn't necessarily the best thing for a game that was not a milsim (this is surely up to debate and perspective) and I have to say, some of these missions are very impressive, not just for their time, but they surpass similar attempts in recent COD games.

Black Ops II has some stumblings, however. Raul Menendez is sometimes so over played and over focused, that he becomes a Marvel Villain at some points. At other times, he actually is a fairly competent antagonist, but his most interesting attribute is what he contributes to the pacing and narrative. Split between the past and the "present", the game actually shows some uglier sides that Black Ops flirted with in comic-like ways but with some more seriousness. Alex Mason being a ♥♥♥♥♥♥ dad, Woods being disillusioned, disabled and miserable...

While this game lacks the impact of some of the in-game gore of the first game and WaW, there is some incredibly gruesome scenes involving knee caps and shotguns in the game. Not to mention, some of the darkest parts of the game is what is merely suggested rather than shown. The game is not-reluctant at all to show the darker side of deniable operations and how their ramifications have seemingly vindicated the mindsets of numerous well-intentioned extremists turned out right terrorists.

It is wild, because parts of this game, especially in Africa and Soviet-Afghanistan, feel like more of a Hideo Kojima game. And then Phantom Pain came out years later, hinting a lot of the same marks. What it chooses to emulate, eventually becomes the toolset of the teacher. This student doesn't necessarily surpass the master, but the game still retains a fairly strong narrative that sticks to you and later, has many similar parts of it bitten off for later (and done better, in both MW2019 and Cold War)

I thought I'd grow to hate the future settings, but as a futuristic setting that would go onto continue this trend for a certain dark era of COD's franchise. After years of subverting expectations, they began to recycle and push hard for a more Sci-Fi oriented IP to compete with other shooters of similar aesthetics and to have far more artistic expression and narrative control. The result - moderate success, lot of good things overlooked and forgotten, but ultimately - it created a vacuum, a want for a return to the type of CODs that Black Ops first subverted initially.

This entire series is an endless tug of war of identities. Different studios, different ideas, the same IP. And the competency varies between each direction. And yet, despite everything, this is a game that is torn right down the middle of two eras in its fiction, two eras in its narrative. And honestly, this COD has aged beautifully in its story. Although I think the first Strikeforce mission is stupendously boring and grotesquely unfun. Other Strikemissions were short, effortless and were worth running through for some variety in the gameplay here and there.

This game even acknowledges Mason's brainwashing and has a hilarious joke final cinematic involving a Reznov gag. The fact this post-credit scene made it in the game showed a lot of confidence in their story. This was the first outing for some of these characters and they were willing to joke about it. This game's narrative is borderline nihilistic, even with the best ending, so this cheerful parody of a music video was a well deserved treat.

The game at times, has those jolly little hints of the American DoD's slight propaganda easter eggs. However, a lot of these remarks are held and spoken by many Americans, so it hardly felt out of place. But it surely will make some adults blink. That being said, the game has a major truth bomb, said by Woods himself. Where he basically says the world is ran by greed rather than ideology, which is the same truth that the antagonist holds as well as his primary inspiration for his actions. Its a good bit of dialogue, mentioning the war of rare earth materials and the concept of opulence as humanity's sin is a present theme throughout, but the game never seems to preach any ideology other than to allow these philosophical points to punctuate the narrative with the motivations of the characters individually. And this is surely a game where the characters, as individuals, stand tall with their convictions and have some compelling scenes and some really, hilariously stupid ones too.

Jonas Savimbi, an Angolan rebel chief who appeared in the game, along with Manuel Noriega, sued the developers for their unflattering depictions in the game. Which reminds me of the controversy around the Castro "assassination" in the first game. Incidents like this were why the developers of subsequent entries preferred dealing with fictional events in a futuristic setting, with narrative lynchpins in the form of characters being fully fictional.

Points for bravery and boldness, but surely, this game touched on aspects that are untouchable to many, but not so much the sensibilities of Westeners. These games and their popularity have the tendency to offend some (the infamous No Russian mission of MW2 was widely condemned in Russian media for one) is often rooted in their ability to emulate reality too. The games are, surprisingly, just as critical of America, the CIA, etc while also painting them as almost being accidentally heroic by circumstance. A lesser evil, of sorts. Propaganda or not, its interesting how deep these stories actually manage to be under all of the explosions, set pieces, zany moments and often, incredible soundtracks. Keeping on with WaW and Black Ops tradition, this game has some stellar tracks and interesting combinations - which reflects the nature of its varied gameplay and narrative.

In a way, there is too much going on and it feels like the story or missions and otherwise simple shooting mechanics can become cluttered with all the choices, (the game makes you work a bit for the true ending at least) callbacks, hints, gadgets, equipment, sci-fi optics, confusing historical timelines and placements (how the hell is Woods even alive at this age after all the ♥♥♥♥ he went through?)

And yet, where it does fall, it always picks itself back up.\t\t\t\t\t
