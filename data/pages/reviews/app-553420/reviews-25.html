\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOn Nov. 22, 2022, TUNIC team would nominate their game for the Steam Awards under the "Most Innovative Gameplay" category. To a limited extent, I understand why TUNIC didn't end up an award winner. TUNIC feels like an unfinished game, and what's left is poorly stitched together into a messy patchwork. It's a game that thrives in subtlety past the point of understatedness- its structure is deliberately opaque, yet what's left after its depth has been explored tends to end up vapid and uninteresting. It is because of this that TUNIC often falls short of its lofty ambitions. But without a doubt, TUNIC was robbed of its title last year.

TUNIC is a weird game for these reasons. I struggle to put my thoughts in writing; to reconcile the oxymoronic nature of the feelings this game evokes. TUNIC is not a good game- it's clunky in execution more often than not, and there is surprisingly little to discover in a game that prides itself on mystery. TUNIC is also a game I want to tell everyone to experience, as the messy way in which its component pieces of game design overlap and abruptly collide with each other feels profoundly unique for a game of any genre. I would argue TUNIC is simultaneously interesting and obtuse enough to defy classification as belonging to a genre; a piece of art to be admired and contemplated rather than evaluated and categorized. No matter how many games I've played, both better and worse in whatever 'objective' frame of reference I decide to operate within, TUNIC stays in my mind for this reason- a testament to what can be, a statement written in its eclectic design decisions over words or pictures. I ask, 'should innovation demand conformity to standards of quality and enjoyment to still be considered innovation? Or can an unexpected shot in the dark, a blind leap of faith into the unknown propelled by instinct and emotion drive the most unique experiences in this medium?' If you've read this far, you likely know what my opinion is.


From here on, I'll discuss spoilery stuff for those interested-

Hi i'm gonna stop being so pretentious and just talk for a little :3

Once again, if you're even considering playing this game I would skip this and just... experience the whole thing. I don't even want to give a general overview of what the game does lest I ruin an integral part of the experience for you.

With that being said...

TUNIC breaks up into 2 main gameplay 'modes' - combat and adventure-game-style puzzle solving. It feels a lot like NES Zelda- both in a good and bad way. Nothing is directly explained to you- you either need to experiment until you figure out the solution to puzzles or attempt to interpret the manual and get a direct answer. Also like in NES Zelda, however, these puzzles are generally structured in a 'hidden by obfuscation' manner- most puzzle solutions simply involve you not knowing a specific piece of information, whether that be hidden paths, a specific function of a button combo, or not having a necessary item. Even then, because there are only so many options that can be hidden in plain sight, these early game puzzles tend to just be repeats of previous obfuscation puzzles, all the way through the end of the main game. Despite the fact I would generally call this a puzzle-focused game, the majority of novel gameplay is comprised of traveling through new areas and fighting combat encounters, not of involved puzzles.

It manages to evoke a 'lock-and-key' kind of satisfaction from solving puzzles, like the original Zelda, but in my eyes this isn't substantial or particularly rewarding puzzle solving- it's a prompt & response structure that helps the player feel like they are interacting with their environment in a novel way more than satisfyingly challenging them.

Combat doesn't fare better. The simple components are a sword swing, movement roll, block, and parry, with assorted inventory items having minor combat utility as well. Ultimately, it just boils down to attacking with the sword whenever possible while the other moves help avoid damage, with progressive areas having generally faster attack timings & ranged options. It generally fails to be more than a filler for the world, with two large contributing factors to its staleness being the lack of enemy complexity and the failure of items to be particularly meaningful in combat.

The biggest issue with items is a mix of their limited quantity and limited damage output: the magic rod, for instance, is the first inventory item that can be used as a ranged weapon. It can be used 20-ish times, with ~3-4 shots being needed to kill normal, weak enemies on standard difficulty. It is unfortunately common for inventory options to fall into this low-damage trap, compounded by the fact that mana & consumables are only rarely dropped by dying enemies, which makes them essentially one-and-done damage sources or buffs against boss enemies, and a lot of the complexity that would make later 2d Zelda's combat engaging through item use is lost because of this. This spirals into the general lack of complex enemy behaviors to make combat feel especially unsatisfying to play.

However, continue through the game and you will eventually hit a roadblock that can't be solved by the above methods of play- the story resolves inconclusively, and there's nowhere else to explore to find a solution to this problem. This brings about the revelatory bridge to the last chapter of gameplay: (one more spoiler warning, just in case) TUNIC has been a puzzle game the entire time.

Alongside your whole journey up until this point, there have been more "hidden-in-plain-sight" puzzles in various environmental patterns that you could have solved using the otherwise unused d-pad, if not for the fact the game didn't explain that these puzzles existed until immediately before the regular ending. They're littered throughout the world in a very The Witness-esque fashion, with the basic line-indicates-password puzzle spun into many variations by obfuscating the line in new ways. For being as simple as they are, the puzzles are quite engaging not just from the novelty of what the game has hidden up until this point. It is also a justification, though incomplete, for the quality of gameplay leading up to this shift: this reveal is only as thematically coherent and hence impressive as it is because the rest of the game has been employing simpler obfuscation puzzles the entire time. That the earlier puzzles feel incomplete and unsubstantial complements the feeling of discovery from this in an incredibly satisfying way, helping this new phase of gameplay feel like a genuine moment of understanding in a world that has otherwise refused to explain itself. On the other hand, I still believe that this doesn't exonorate the mediocrity of the earlier bulk of gameplay.

I want to restate- TUNIC is not a 'good' game. It does, however, experiment with evoking emotion through its construction in an entirely unique way from traditional game design. I am intentionally being vague with what the puzzles involve at this point; once you've broken past the first hidden boundary, the quality and scope of puzzles dramatically increases through the end of the game in ways I would feel horrible about spoiling, even at this point in my review. It's the juxtaposition of an incredibly stale shell with the intensely creative and rewarding core that made TUNIC both a chore and joy to play; one I would struggle to recommend someone else play as a purely tactile experience. By the end, though, every step felt as monumental as the first in being pushed to discover what can be understood about the world of TUNIC, in a way I can't help but tell you to experience yourself. \t\t\t\t\t
