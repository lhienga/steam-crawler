\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTUNIC is an action-adventure game that takes place in a visually appealing world filled to the brim with secrets. The art, music and sound design are all top notch. It has everything someone like me says they want in a game. Yet why was the experience so unenjoyable?

This is a very subjective review. I'm posting it with the understanding that Steam users generally read multiple reviews, and then judge based on the aggregate, and I think the majority view that TUNIC is a good game is correct. But I also want to highlight the things that made it unfun specifically for a player like me, and why I was muttering "Cryptic BS: The Game" between clenched teeth by the end.

I was attracted to TUNIC when I heard of the game mechanic of collecting pages of a manual that would gradually reveal secrets. It sounded fun and satisfying, and there are tons of secrets in the manual...but it's written in a different language, and the game doesn't give you any clues to deciphering it until you've collected most of the pages, meaning you're stuck for most of the playthrough with a booklet of frustrating gibberish. The temptation for someone like me is then to look up an alphabet online, taking me out of the immediate gaming experience. I wasted a lot of time painstakingly translating the manual pages, going back and forth with the alphabet key, and not even gaining any hidden information by it, since what's written are mostly vague hints. Eventually I just looked up a guide that translated the pages for me, which further undermined the experience of figuring things out for myself. At that point the whole game is kind of spoiled for me, and I may as well use walkthroughs for everything.

The game has way too many puzzles. and most of them are frustratingly difficult. The difficulty curve of these head-scratchers is out of whack: it sort of starts small, but the puzzles quickly become way too elaborate, and there's not a clear idea of which puzzles are necessary to progress the game, or get stronger item upgrades; and which are just for novelty items that do nothing. There just aren't enough stepping stones for the average player, who is left feeling stupid rather than smart.

With the proliferation of enemies that respawn every time you save, and the abundance of mind-bending puzzles, the world feels cruel and confusing. Major spoilers: at one point, when you finally gather three keys from three difficult bosses, and naturally expect to be rewarded in some way, the game instead throws you into an unwinnable boss battle, kills you, and banishes you to the realm of the dead, where all the upgrades you've thus far acquired are taken away.

There's nothing particularly interesting about the game's story. The fact that everything is wrapped in layers of secrecy may make you think there'll be an "a-ha" moment, when you'll care about the lore, but it's just the usual fantasy fare. Travelling around the world is annoying, for while there are some warps, you have to keep referring to the corresponding manual page, to figure out which of the identical squares in the warp zone lead where. Even with the warps, there are some areas that just aren't easy to get to (or back from!), which if you're roaming round the overworld trying to uncover secrets, adds another layer of frustration.

Clearly this isn't a game for someone like me, and I don't blame it for that, but I know I'm not the only person that responded to TUNIC this way. There's enough puzzles here for three, far less frustrating games. The unglamorous dungeon spelunking aspects are actually pretty enjoyable (though I've grown to hate the old "treasure chests hidden from the camera" bit), and the combat, while very tough, can be overcome with judicious item use and practice. \r\n\t\t\t\t\tInstantly one of my all-time favorites! It's an unforgettable adventure and an experience I wish I could have for the first time again...

This game throws you into a world that you will have to discover on your own. You will be able to explore it, to firgure out what you are seeking and to have a nostalgia orgasm if you ever played old action adventure games.

I enjoyed the challenge of figuring out the manual and the whole meta-game aspect of it. It's based on old gaming experiences, yet it feels very unique in a modern game. I also enjoyed the difficulty curve, moderately clumsy, yet intentional combat, plenty of exploration and secrets (oh, there's so much wall-hugging!) hidden in plain sight, and the deep and mysterious lore with some major plot-twists along the way. All of that is just wonderfully implemented, nailed down to the details and feels like a labour of love from a very professional and passionate indie game studio.

My only gripe with it was that the final boss is way too hard. I spent a lot of time on it, and it felt like a big achievement to beat it, but it was just a bit overpowered in my opinion. Luckily, there is a flexible assist mode in the settings, so anyone can enjoy the game without getting stuck on the combat.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTunic plays like an isometric Zelda with the sense of discovery of Outer Wilds with a bit of the flavor of Hollow Knight or Dark Souls. If your reaction to that statement is that I've just named some of the best games of all time then stop reading this or any other reviews and play Tunic without any other information! (Also, my compliments on your taste in games.)

I wouldn't say that Tunic is better than any of those games I mentioned when compared directly for what they are, but it is unique for what it is, at least in my experience, and was a truly special game. It is clearly made by people who love video games, and also feels like it was made with a very strong vision, unapologetically and with no compromises.

Mechanically, Tunic plays like an isometric Zelda. If you have played a Zelda game then I imagine you would feel right at home. The combat gameplay is fun and competent (except for the lock-on implementation, which frustrated me), but I wouldn't say great. There are also some Hollow Knight influences. When only considering the action gameplay I would say that Tunic is generally easy, although there are some boss battles and a few combat sections that were challenging for me.

The heart of Tunic, however, and what sets it apart and makes it great is its knowledge-based discovery, and when it comes to the mental challenges it is anything but easy (in the best way possible). The best comparison I can think of is Outer Wilds, although the two are quite different. In both games you are tasked with figuring things out, and once you do it opens up new things that you can do (and which you will never unlearn, even if you started a new game from the beginning). Tunic is amazing in that it seems like it tells you nothing, but then when you figure something out you realize that it was actually telling you all along. If you loved the feeling of "ah ha!" in Outer Wilds then you really owe it to yourself to play Tunic as well. If, on the other hand, you felt frustrated and lost in Outer Wilds then Tunic is probably not for you, since the main goal of the game is to figure things out.

I don't know if I have ever played a game that had as many "wait, what? No way, haha" realization moments as Tunic, and it is immensely satisfying. I am being intentionally vague because you would really be doing a disservice to yourself to learn about any of the hidden discoveries without making them yourself. If what I've described sounds like something you would enjoy then I wouldn't hesitate to recommend it.\t\t\t\t\t
