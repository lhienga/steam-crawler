\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA good top-down action-adventure game to scratch anybody's "2D Zelda" itch. The inspiration in the legendary franchise is quite noticeable, not just through art design but through the overall game design as well. Puzzles, the magical sense of discovery, rewarding the player for exhaustively exploring every nook and cranny. And all this without a single line of dialogue, or cutscene. Quite a piece of work.

If exploration and wracking your brain a bit seems like fun to you, this one I can recommend. Moreso if you're a 2D Zelda fan. Do keep in mind that this game is best played completely blind, since most of the magic is about exploring and discovering every little thing the game has to offer.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a cute game that falls victim to bad map design and frustrating gameplay.

Tunic is, in many ways, a competently made implementation of its genre. It's got a nice art style, and a few new neat ideas like being able to find manual pages.

Unfortunately the actual experience of playing it is frequently turned into a frustrating slog primarily through poor map design. Tunic map is very, very complicated, largely in service of Tunic's favorite trick of showing you places you can't get to until later and hiding shortcuts behind geometry. That's not a bad thing in itself, but it does mean that you'll frequently find yourself very close as the crow flies to areas you've already cleared and want to get back to that are, through wrinkle of map layout, in fact a long and convoluted trek away.

A trek made more difficult by a guiding map that prioritizes aesthetics over actually showing you how to get where you want. In a simpler map design, not a problem, but with Tunic's map the lack of detail means it's often not clear how to get where you're going.

It's save points are also poorly laid out. Some are quite close together, but other new areas lock their save point behind multiple encounters of difficult enemies, and the closest outside save point is very far away. That means that to learn how to play around this difficulty spike, you'll need to spend around a full minute treking through already-cleared map to get back to the neat area every time you die. Which will happen a lot, especially for difficulty spikes like the quarry where there are sniper that one or two shot you. Each time something goes wrong, back to two areas away you go, to hike run through empty areas for 60 seconds.

It technically has a fast travel system, but it's laid out in such a way that it doesn't mitigate these problems.

It also has a lock-on 'focus' system that's fairly vital for combat sometimes, but is clunky to use. The default keybinds for switching focus targets require you to move your entire hand away from your attacks and items, and because it uses directional inputs instead of cycling through all available targets, it's hard to rebind, and difficult to get to focus on who you want.

There are lots of small problems like the interact prompt both taking a moment to show up and sharing a key with your dodge roll, meaning if you run up to a ladder and press interact, you'll lock yourself into a dodge animation for a second instead of grabbing the ladder. Not a huge problem, but they pile up.

I'm just going to keep listing them as I run into them. Was this game not QA tested or something?
- Some bosses can be pulled with the grapple hook, except when they're in some attacks, at which point instead of it failing *you* will be pulled to them and murderized
- The grappling hook can latch onto dead bodies in the seconds before they disappear, even if you're locked onto someone else. It locks you into the animation, doesn't do anything, and just got me killed.

Tunic has a lot going for it, as a mentioned before, but the omnipresent frustration of dealing with the map design sours the experience. I'm probably going to finish it once, and I don't regret putting time into it, but I'm not sure I'd recommend it to anyone that doesn't feel called to it.

More than anything, the setting and music made me wish I was playing Hyper Light Drifter instead, which exceeds Tunic in just about every category and doesn't have most of its flaws. Better combat, similar setting but more interesting, similar music but better, more engaging story, similar themes... I'd point anyone in that direction in a heartbeat.

Also, if you do play tunic you're going to want to customize the controls early on. The default control layout is pretty poorly optimized, feeling cramped and not using the mouse at all. The game doesn't tell you you can bind keys to the mouse, but you can, and I highly recommend putting your most-used few buttons on the mouse and rerouting the rest around your WASD keys.

EDIT: Now that I've finished the game, I can say with confidence that I don't recommend it. The second half of the game solves some of the map design problems (it turns out the entire map is designed to be super obtuse to navigate until you get a special dash when you're 75% finished), which is... a choice. But the omnipresent problems never really let up.

Small places where you get stuck and have to reset, map design that remains obtuse even when you get the magic dash.

The boss design in particular is bad throughout the game. It's seldom clear when vulnerability windows are, what attacks are being telegraphed. The final boss is the worst example: its has a stunned state, but even after half an hour beating it I never figured out what triggered it. Not damage, not health loss, no clue. Some runs it got stunned three times, others none.

It has several wildly different attacks that all share identical or near-identical windups, so the same telegraph sometimes is a short jab and sometimes is a two-part combo that will take your entire health pool if you don't get more out of the way. Those weren't the only radically different attack with an identical windup.

It seems to be able to cancel its own animations at random: attacks that usually have a two-second windup will sometimes come out instantly followed immediately by a second attack. These can even chain together, meaning at sometimes it will do a janky 6-attack flurry over the course of four or five seconds. As time went on I became less convinced this was intentional and not bad coding.

The effect is the same as with all the other bosses. I never once felt like I was learning a boss, growing familiar with its behaviors and beating it with finesse. You just run at it repeatedly until you get lucky and it chooses not to do its heavy attack six times in a row.

Overall, I just can't recommend it. Tunic has good points, certainly, but the actual experience of playing it is frustrating and tedious. The story is Hollow Knight to a T but less interesting, the everything is Hyper Light Drifter but worse.

A solid attempt was made, but the finished product just doesn't measure up.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t"Tunic" is an adorable, indie, souls like game which, without being innovative at all, uses few fundamental gaming elements with so much care and perfection that it feels unique. Addictive battles with fluid animations and responsive controls are mixing magically with exploration and clever puzzle inside a colorful, eerie world full of mysteries and unexpected discoveries. "Tunic" shows how going back to the basics in a creative way can gives us gems.


Manometer: 8/10


The good
- Adorable aesthetics. Nice, minimalistic 3d graphics with vivid colors and atmospheric lighting. The cool day/night system gives it some variation too.
- Great world design with multiple height levels, hidden paths and alternative routes that get unlocked gradually as you play. Each region guides you masterfully in order to always be at the right path, but also not limit you from exploring the surroundings.
- Awesome core gameplay logic. The way that you unlock secrets and gain small pieces of knowledge through exploration and observation is amazing. Even after a lot of hours you keep discovering things that may related to basic control mechanics. The game is full of well hidden secrets and clever puzzles and always gives you enough hints so it never feels impossible or unfair. It even has its own real language that you can try to translate. So imaginative and well balanced.
- Fantastic animations. Soft bodies, particles, physics after each blow, everything seems smooth and natural. Protagonist's walking is so funny.
- Fast battles with nice controls and addictive kills. Everything feels fluid and responsive.
- It offers a decent variation of abilities, upgrades and tools that introduce new interesting mechanics when you acquire them, so the game never gets boring or repetitive.
- Well designed enemies. Each region has its own tribes. Bosses' design seems a little bit generic, but their battles are great and have really good pace.
- The story is cool but pretty abstract. The most interesting part though is trying to understand what's going on by collecting all these scattered pieces of information and putting them gradually together.
- All those illustrations at the pages you find are simply gorgeous.

The bad
- It would be an entirely different game if you change this characteristic, but the fact that there are side invisible walls everywhere so you cannot jump down to the lower levels seemed to me odd and I could get used of it till the very end.
- Poor sound design with only few environmental sounds. The background music keeps playing almost always, which can be tiring after a while.
- Sometimes interacting with some objects like chests and stairs can be unresponsive.
- The story is pretty abstract and fragmented which isn't bad at all, but initially it doesn't give you enough clues to understand your purpose, what you are fighting for. So you play half of the game wandering and killing foes without knowing exactly why.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe first 3/4 of the game is excellent, the last 1/4 is lame. However, I don't think the last 1/4 ruins the game enough to not recommend the game. Note the in-game "manual" plays and extremely important role in all stages of the game, so pay close attention to it.

For the first 3/4, it is fun. The game lifts from Dark Souls mechanics and motifs in a positive way, and is less so Zelda-based than you'd initially think. The game has in common with Dark Souls:
- sprinting, parrying, dodging with i-frames, lock-on targeting, stamina
- mana for spells and abilities
- not-bonfires which refill your not-estus, and which respawn enemies when you rest at them
- great interconnected non-linear map design, whereby you unlock many shortcuts
- many secrets; the game is chalk full of secrets, which range in difficulty to find from mildly clever to needing a galaxy-brain
- stat levelling up
- difficult bosses whereby you have to learn their moveset and weaknesses; at least for me they were difficult
- an interesting, fallen, and mysterious world, whereby the narrative is the opposite of spoon-fed to you
- you ring two not-Bells-of-Awakening and collect the Hexforce and collect the not-Lord Souls

One of my biggest criticisms with the art direction is the fox protagonist has bead-eyes. He is still a cute fox, but he would've been far more cute and relatable if he had "normal" emotive eyes with an iris an such. Hopefully if the devs make another game, they'll mix up the eyes to be better. Otherwise, the lofi aesthetic is very cool, and the lofi soundtrack is amazingly good. The art in the manual is also great.

The game keeps you engaged by providing you interesting items and abilities as you progress. It also gets you to ask deep questions like:
- why tf am I praying to mecca
- why is my (incomplete) game manual scribbled over and slopped all over with coffee stains
- who is writing in pen on my manual
- in fact, why are parts of the map geography straight-up wrong
- why does it feel as if someone has been though so many places already before
- who made all these robots
- why and there so many meccas
- why does the purple stuff irradiate me like I'm at Chernobyl
- do I get to wield the cool sword
So yea that is all cool.

The last 1/4 of the game sucks cuz:
- it takes away all of your upgrades, but makes you play a long and difficult section whereby the game mechanics are pretty much the same. I don't think this was necessary from a story or gameplay point of view. They could've just made this section hard without taking away your upgrades, and thus not removing your feeling of progression. This choice would be like if Dark Souls yeeted your levels and weapon upgrades partway through until you defeated a couple of Lord Soul bosses.
- to not get what I regard as a bad unsatisfying ending, you need to get a bunch of difficult collectables. Getting these collectables requires you to press your d-pad/arrow-keys a lot, with no indication if you made a mistake. I think the default ending is too unsatisfying given the amount of manual-decoding, careful exploring, and d-pad effort needed to get the okay ending. For most of the "golden path" stuff, I used online guides.

This game is a great demonstration on why games don't come with manuals anymore. They've been rid-of for good reason. Having to decode and rely on a game manual for important gameplay purposes is an immersion-breaking anti-feature. Games whereby you must solve their secrets and challenges by immersing yourself in the game world are far more compelling.

Anyways, if you don't think the last 1/4 of the game will bother you, go for buying it.
