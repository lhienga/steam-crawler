\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI didn't expect to enjoy this game. I'm not a fan of isometric games, nor do I like the general artstyle of the game. On top of that, I've currently got a bit of Soulslike fatigue. So, I didn't expect to enjoy a game that looks like a mesh together of Legend of Zelda and Dark Souls. Time and time again Tunic would be recommended by someone I respect. I'd consider it, then decide against it. But something about the overwhelmingly positive reviews eventually swayed me to get it.

And I don't regret my decision one bit.

Exploration
The exploration in this game is fantastic. The game is in part a metroidvania - with traversal abilities you can unlock that allow you to explore the world. The game is also "metroidbrainia", with abilities you can do from the start, you just don't know how. The world is nicely interconnected with many satisfying shortcuts you can unlock along the way.

The Manual
The game drip feeds you pages of a manual, but unlike pretty much every manual in existence, this one doesn't bore me to death. In fact, it's fantastic! Most of it is encoded in a strange different language, with only key bits of information in English or told via images. The game slowly lets you in on little tips and tricks, but you don't NEED the manual, you can figure these abilities out on your own (and when that happens it is very satisfying).

Visuals
I mentioned earlier that the artstyle was not my favourite. Despite this, the animations, colour scheme, and the sound design are masterfully put together. The environments are far more varied than I would have expected. While I loved the atmosphere of the rustic forest temple-y areas, there were more impressive areas later into the game.

Progression
The progression in abilities feels great. I love it when games make loot that feels valuable and most loot either contributes directly to a valuable upgrade, gives you an upgrade, or rewards you with information.

Combat
The combat was good. The final boss was fantastic. The bosses in the runup ranged from decent to great. I had a little trouble with the lock-on system, but all of this could have been mitigated if I'd used the arrow keys to switch lock on targets. Even despite my Soulslike fatigue I still enjoyed the combat.

Puzzles
You don't need to solve any real puzzles to get an ending, but if you want to get the good ending there are a lot to solve. The final puzzle required for the good ending is incredible. There are hints hidden right under your nose throughout the game; when you figure out the solution, it feels absolutely amazing. One downside, however, is when executing the puzzle solution, it is very easy to make a tiny error interpreting parts of it - which then unfolds into an unfortunately frustrating experience diagnosing the small error you made. At the end of the day though, I am still very satisfied with my experience figuring it out.

Conclusion
This game had me hooked. From ringing the bells to now (having just completed the good ending) I had a burning desire to uncover the secrets around every corner; it takes masterful game design to create that feeling. I was able to recapture similar feelings in this game as when solving puzzles in Outer Wilds. I highly recommend playing this, and I highly recommend playing it blind.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo the first 5-7 hours or so were fantastic for sure. Atmosphere is great, combat is not bad ("not bad" being the best it'll ever get ) and you figure out game mechanics through clever use of finding manual pages and part of the game is puzzling out how things work. All of this, when it works comes together well and feels great.
However when it fails it fails also really hard and all the fun goes out the window pretty fast. There's multiple ways the game can do that.
One is to simply be stuck on not figuring out where to go. Thankfully this didn't happen a lot to me and usually I could find my way to looking over the "map" again and deciding that "there is probably something here, so let's check that section out again". But generally almost all access points are hidden by the perspective of the camera. Be that hidden ladders or corridors or whatnot. I get the throwback to older games where some secrets where hidden that way, but it's way, wayyy overdone here by being basically the default way of going from section to section at times. This also makes it more annoying to remember which way you needed to go when going through an area later.
Another really bad aspect is the combat. At first it's not too bad, since both you and the enemies seem equally clunky. But in the second half of the game enemies are mostly a keep away AI that runs away from you and then attacks from long range. And for some enemies it takes you longer to swing and hit with your weapon after they just attacked you than it takes the enemies to run away from you again. Some also get invulnerability frames just after their attack so you're guaranteed not able to hit them. Basically, most basic enemies become a huge pain in the ass while adding nothing interesting to combat.
Similar for bosses. except bosses have the extra problem that for some the camera moves too fast (as it's always on the boss) so aside from that one boss where during a specific sequence all it does is circle around you (and thus move the camera, which made me dizzy as heck after a while) you often get problems of the boss attacking while moving fast that leads to not quite seeing what's going on or inputs not working, or blocking not working cause your character turns too fast (or not fast enough, idk). which means sometimes you block sometimes you don't which is also quite frustrating. Aside from the same keep away/i-frame stuff on top, which just drags out the already not so fun battles too much.
Then the secret puzzles. I'd say I can see how some people can get really into those. And good for you, I hope you had a lot of fun with hose, but unless you enjoy staring at cryptic messages and just trying stuff out a lot and never getting clear feedback it's seems a bit too much trial and error. I even had it happened that I actually correctly figured out what to do, but then it didn't work anyway, so I assumed I needed maybe a certain item first, but as it turns out I did everything correctly already, it just didn't work as intended (for whatever reason), so that kinda sucked big time.
Some puzzles/clues are incredibly vague at times and some seemingly require you to translate that whole fake language to even get the clues. So if you enjoy that kinda stuff, that's probably a plus, but I just thought the game had its head up wayy too far it's own arse at that point tbh.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI can see why some folks don't like Tunic. It's pretty punishing at some parts and it suffers from the same problem a lot of puzzle games have; making one/some of the late game puzzles hard but not fun. The Witness did the same crap.

Also, do the devs seriously expect me to try and decipher a made up language for a game I was able to beat in just a couple days? They should have had unlockable "letters" to translate works as you went along. Maybe in NG+ if they need to keep the first play through a pure experience. I am not going to waste my time cracking that code and from the translations I read online I really didn't miss much.

That said Tunic wins out over other games with it's amazing visual style and interesting, unfolding story told through progressing and not through many words. Combat isn't perfect (parrying is terrible) but is overall snappy and fun. Finding secrets everywhere kept me going. Not sure why they went all in on a specific kind of puzzle at the end (very repetitive and dull for me) but solving puzzles using the in-game manual was a treat. I love that manual so much it looks amazing and is full of crazy details.\t\t\t\t\t
