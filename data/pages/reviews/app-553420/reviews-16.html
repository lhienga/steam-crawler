\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAlthough I enjoyed Tunic overall, if someone who normally loves action-adventure games told me that they hated it, I’d be completely unsurprised. This game prides itself in being cryptic. You’re playing a game where the manual is in gibberish, the isometric viewpoint purposefully obscures your next destination, and your character’s most basic goals are shrouded in mystery. Tunic tries to make everything difficult, from its item descriptions to its environmental design.

That’s also the fun of it. Everything about Tunic is a mystery for you to solve.

I had many moments where I finally pieced something together in Tunic that completely changed how I interacted with the world. Those moments of satisfaction where some new mechanic clicked into place were the highlight of the game, and Tunic was full of them. The best part of the puzzles in the first part of the game were how they would interact with and progress all the other pillars of gameplay. Correctly interpreting manual pages would teach you core combat mechanics. Learning Tunic’s style of cryptic breadcrumbs would reveal hidden paths to explore. Engaging with the game’s riddles would feed you pieces of lore.

While I adored this portion of the game–where I kept learning new secrets about the world–I will admit that my interest started to wane by the end. By the last third of the game, you’ve exhausted all of those other pillars of gameplay. There’s far less combat, far less exploration, and far more puzzles. All that’s left is puzzle after puzzle–and I don’t mean Zelda shrine puzzles, I mean the type of puzzles where you’re pulling out a paper and pencil to frantically scribble down solutions. These puzzles were also distinctly more meta than most of the ones sprinkled earlier throughout the game. I wasn’t naturally stumbling across mysteries that were cleverly integrated into an existing world and narrative; instead, I was just walking from Riddle A to Riddle B to Riddle C. If you love mind-boggling puzzles, then this section of the game will feel like getting served free dessert after a full-course meal. If you picked up Tunic because you were told it was a cute adventure game with Zelda vibes, then you may feel somewhat tricked by its transformation into a pure puzzle game.

I loved Tunic’s purposefully obtuse way of presenting its combat, environment, and lore. It was rewarding to have to piece everything together myself, and if you like games that don’t hold your hand, then you’ll enjoy that aspect too. That’s truly the appeal of Tunic, and the continual sense of wonder and discovery that permeated the first half of the game was more than enough to push me through the somewhat repetitive final sprint.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you're looking for a game that gives old-school NES vibes, then this is definitely the game for you. It won't hold your hand, it won't tell you what to do, and it will throw you for a loop looking for your next task. Despite it all, it feels amazing when you do figure it out on your own. The last puzzle of the game especially deserves a shout-out for being incredibly creative and engaging.

However, Tunic's greatest strength will likely be frustrating to a certain degree for most people. To keep the wonder of discover intact Tunic is purposefully obtuse at times. When you start walking in circles to try and find an undiscovered area because you didn't find the very specific instructions/clue, you may feel pretty tired of the lack of guidance. The payoff is worth it, but it does feel as if the pace of the game drags due to constant wandering.

Other than the above, the combat of the game is just good enough. If you've played most of the Souls games then the bosses and general enemies may feel a bit simplistic. There aren't any real difficult sections, and most combat encounters can be dealt with by simply pacing backwards out of reach or in a circle followed by spamming the attack button afterwards. Not bad in any sense, but not amazing either.

Overall Tunic is one of the most creative games I've played in a long while, and I would highly recommend it. However be warned that it is definitely not for everybody, especially those who don't feel like looking over every nook and cranny of the game world for the next clue on where to go.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTunic is a great game that reminds me of older games, like zelda 1, where the game doesn't tell you much and you have to figure it out yourself. The game does help and guide you, so it's not as obtuse as zelda 1, but even most of the text in the game is unreadable so you have to interpret what the game is saying instead of being told.
For the gameplay Tunic flows well with its movement and combat, the level design is expertly designed and you can see a lot of thought was put into it. It's shocking to me just how many secrets are in this game, your desire to explore is always rewarded with something, and I most likely missed at least half of the secrets.\t\t\t\t\t

Overview
Tunic is a game that is a blend of classic 2D Zelda mixed with some Dark Souls in the form of minimalist story telling, having bonfire-like checkpoints that you respawn at upon death, having healing flasks that you refill at said checkpoints, and having an interconnected world full of shortcuts that loop back upon itself. The game evokes a feeling of trying to play the original Legend of Zelda game for the NES for the first time and your only guidance is a copy of the Japanese version of the game's manual. This is the game's selling point that makes it stand out from other games in its genre. Throughout the game you collect pages of the game manual that will include game mechanics, controls, uses for items, hints for secrets, maps, and story elements.

What Makes Tunic Stand Out
However, the challenge comes from the manual being written in the game's fictional language. Furthermore, the pages are often collected out of order, so there may be a 2-page spread where you only have half of the information available. This requires making deductions and inferences in order to "solve" the puzzle with the missing information. This incomplete information is often sufficient enough to progress, but often times when you get the rest of the puzzle the information will help recontextualize the information in some new way that helps further the player's understanding of the world. The game has a wonderful pacing with giving you just enough information to keep you on your toes to solve the puzzle, but not too much to keep you feeling overwhelmed. You will often feel clever by scouring the pages to notice little details for hidden secrets and puzzle solutions. This is how about 90% of the game is, keeping a good pace at drip-feeding you new clues in order to keep you moving forward without keeping you stumped for too long. However, the last 10% or so of the game really falls off, which I will go into more detail later.

Criticisms of the Late Game
While you can translate the in-game language, you can certainly complete the game without doing so. Unfortunately, you do not get the crucial clue used to decipher the language until the very end of the game, and even then it seems like translating the individual characters of the language felt like it required making a lot of assumptions and using trial and error to finally get the right answer instead of having a more deductive approach. I looked up a spoiler-free hint guide to try to translate the language on my own with some success, but once I realized how the language was supposed to be translated, it felt like it was going to be more of a chore to not only decipher the symbols, then then also translate the 50+ pages of the manual. I ended up just reading a translated version online. None of the hints/clues were things I couldn't already decipher on my own by using the pictures/context clues in the game's manual. The only real benefit of reading the manual was getting elaboration on the game's story, as well as clearer item descriptions. Had I tried to continue to decipher the the language on my own and try to translate the entire manual, it probably would have taken another 5 hours or so to do this. Considering my total playtime is under 20 hours, spending another 5 hours to solve essentially "one puzzle" the wasn't even necessary to complete the game, did not feel worth my time.

I got through the first ending without a guide, but in order to get the "true" ending you have to complete a series a time-consuming, tedious puzzles (e.g. having to input a string of button inputs in a specific order at certain locations). I was able to figure out how to do it on my own and did a few of them on my own, but some of them I knew what I was supposed to do to complete them - but I felt the game did not communicate clearly enough at times what is the "start" point of inputing the commands. And some of these commands could be 10-20+ inputs. So sometimes I could not tell if maybe I double-pressed accidentally or if I made an error in my notes. After a certain point, it became unfun due to it felt it was just wasting my time. At that point, I just looked up a guide to get through those string of puzzles. The "final" puzzle requires you to complete 25 puzzles/clues in order to find 4-5 input commands each, put them all together, and then input those combined total of 110 inputs - in order with no mistakes - in order to unlock the item you need for the true ending. I had the right idea when beginning to solve this puzzle, but looked at a hint guide just to check if my answers were right. Once I verified I did the first 4-5 puzzles/clues correctly by checking a guide, I just found myself looking up the guide for the written command. Because it would probably take another 1 or so hours to get through the rest of the clues to come up with that 110-command solution. But if I tried it on my own without a guide, and - even if I got it all right on the first try - if I found myself making one wrong press in that 110-input code, then I would find myself having to second guess all myself on all those 25 other puzzles to see where I went wrong.

Final Thoughts
Despite my criticisms of the last 10% of the game (really just those last two "end-game" puzzles, I thoroughly enjoyed my time playing Tunic. It was a little slow to start, but once it got its momentum going, I found myself have a hard time putting it down. I was highly engaged and immersed by pulling up the in game map while exploring the game's world. It was always exciting to find a new page of the in game manual in order to learn more about the game, world, puzzles, and where to go next. I loved how the game had tons of puzzles and shortcuts that hid in plan sight. I would definitely recommend this game, even with my reservations with those tedious final puzzles.\t\t\t\t\t
