\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI played this after Tears of the Kingdom, several 2D Zelda games, and Sable (clearly BotW-inspired but chill) because I was still in the mood for something else vaguely Zelda-like.

Pros:
-Based on the preview images, I came into this game expecting an isometric puzzle platformer rather than a strict homage to Zelda and was not disappointed. I really hope more developers bring this isometric style back now that we all have full analog controllers, which was the main hurdle in the 8- and 16-bit console eras. There's a certain satisfaction from making all parts of an area slowly click into place.
-The art styles, both in the main game world and the NES Zelda-style in-game booklet, are wonderful. I'd sometimes stop walking around just to take in the airbrushed geometric art of the game world.
-The regular sword/shield/etc. item mechanics are straightforward and give you a lot of freedom in how you map them.
-The orb/whip is super fun once you get that. You can get into some hilarity by whip-snapping enemies over to you.
-Difficulty toggles.

Cons:
-The shadows in dark areas are a bit hard to see subtle details in even after bumping the brightness.
-It worked with a PS5 controller, my preferred Steam controller, but kept showing XBox button names. The choices for how the menu controls were mapped were also strange.
-Targeting is a bit dodgy. You need it not just for attacks, but snapping yourself around using the orb.
-After the first bell, the difficulty ramped up ridiculously. I toggled invincibility on after getting repeatedly brutalized by the crocodile enemies and could tell I would not have passed the quarry or ziggurat. It has its own equivalent to the Gloom from Tears of the Kingdom, but more frustrating.
-The Souls-like leveling mechanic doesn't intuitively fit in with the retro aesthetic. I had the manual page for it but it didn't click what it was telling me to do until I looked up a guide online.
-Several other mechanics, like teleporting, don't have any subtle UI cues beyond anything shown in the manual.
-There's too many collectibles and they're not fun like the regular platform puzzles. After a point, I pulled up a guide and powered through them to get the Steam achievements rather than for their own sake. I wouldn't have bothered getting them at all if I'd bought this game for the Switch instead. The gold skulls one was my least favorite because the physics were wonky.
-The devs are way too in love with the Wind Fish Egg puzzle from Link's Awakening. It's repeatedly rehashed here, such as for a good chunk of the hidden collectibles, but in longer and even more annoying ways.
-I still didn't entirely get what was going on plot-wise even after collecting the whole manual.

Neutral:
-I was back and forth on how I felt about the gimmick of the fake foreign language in the in-game UI and the manual pages. It's a novel idea, but it sometimes got in the way of getting my head wrapped around the game mechanics. I think I would have preferred something where the fake language translates itself bit-by-bit as you collect more pages, with 100% manual pages = 100% translated.

TLDR; fun premise and artwork let down by a quasi-attempt to make it a Souls-like and the devs forgetting that some 8-bit era game mechanics were just cheap rather than a fun challenge.\t\t\t\t\t
