\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTunic is captivating from start to almost finish. The game is structured like a classic Zelda game, where items are required to progress in the various puzzles that make up the game‚Äôs challenge. It borrows Dark Souls drop currency at death mechanic and the stamina-based dodging and blocking. Combat is fine; it won‚Äôt blow your socks off. You have a wide selection of items and weapons that let you be as creative as you want in combat, but in the end, I stuck to the trusty roll and stab them in the bum, only whipping out the more destructive items and weapons for large groups or boss battles.
It‚Äôs the world design and puzzles that were the driving motivation for me. The story is told by finding and piecing together parts of an in-game manual. However, the manual is in a runic language and guess my fox character was asleep during his runic language lessons in school. You have to carefully examine the small handwritten notes and illustrations on each page to help you solve puzzles and progress. There is a very satisfying sense of accomplishment in figuring out these obtuse hints that lead to greater discoveries. Some are a little too obtuse at times.
Towards the end of the game, the backtracking becomes a little tedious and drawn out as you solve the remaining puzzles, which you will want to solve if you are going for the best ending. Overall, I would strongly recommend Tunic.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs others have said, TUNIC is a Zelda-like, Souls-like, isometric/top-down action adventure, with secrets inspired by both games, as well as The Witness ‚Äî in both the "lines" sense (directions, actually), and the sense of discovery and revelation. Once you see one, you'll seek to see them all! Some of the secrets are references to other games too (FEZ, Celeste, et al.), like any good indie title's secrets. Also the character design is cute and the sound design is gorgeous!

But had it not been for the language (or rather the "encryption" of an already existing, natural language) seen in the game's old-school "manual" and elsewhere, I would've brushed this title aside as "just another one of those types of games with cuteness and combat." It's not a mere one-to-one English letter cipher or the like either, like in numerous other games, but something that showcases a deeper linguistic understanding. This language is everywhere, adding a sense of mysticism to the world until its "Rosetta Stone" is revealed to you. Say what you will (and many already have :P), but this sort of thing is criminally underrated in all forms of media.

Even if you don't understand, the best thing is to try. Never be afraid to experiment nor to fail, even a little; and if you are afraid, there are 100% shameless accessibility options in the menu to make things easier. TUNIC is a harmless singleplayer game after all.

Oh, and as you may have surmised, this game is surprisingly spoiler sensitive, so I'mma stop my review here before it becomes a wall of ...this.


All that said, and all things considered, you may want to buy this game on a small sale (15-33% off), as it's nothing particularly groundbreaking (for me, if an indie game is going to charge $30 or more, it had better be absolute top-notch!).

If you're unsure of whether to buy the game, the first episode (20-30 min.) of any blind LP video is generally spoiler safe, so maybe watch one before deciding whether the game is for you.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\ttl;dr: It's very meh. Overrated, overpriced.
SPOILERS BELOW
People talk about this game like it is so fresh and unique, OOOH, MANUAL AND EVERYTHING IS WRITTEN IN JIBBERISH! OOH THERE IS SECRET KNOWLEDGE TO BE HAD! OOOH YOU GET MANUAL AS A SECRET REWWAAARD! GAME REWARDS YOU WITH KNOWLEDGE. SO COOL
Well, jibberish is a silly gimmick. It wears thin really fast, and doesn't add anything to the game.
Secret knowledge is in almost all (except for one, basically) cases is either a input combination to open a door, or a shortcut to skip some stuff.
Manual pages are either useless (most of them), have those input combinations I mentioned (a couple), or have information on game mechanics which are usually hit or miss, depending on whether you were able to deduce yourself that being out of stamina or health is bad for you.
I remember in a first couple of hours walking around, seeing unreachable stuff and thinking to myself - wow, cool hooks. I wonder what they do. Ooh, what about camertones? Maybe there is a simple melody you can play. Oh, those are teleport waypoints. I bet if I knew how they work I could skip like half of the game.
Well, no. Hooks and camertones are literally just a grapple point.
99% of all unreachable stuff just requires tools. Tools no knowldge in the game will be able to replace.
And first two teleport points, which you could have gotten to before getting the info on how to use them, are useless without another tool you will get later. Nice.
You know what this game really is? Legend of Zelda. One of the worse ones, where every tool has a designated use points. Oh, it also has 2 endings, and Zelda is evil and you have to kill her. Don't worry. she is just cranky cause people use her brethren as battery fluid.
Music is really good, The highlight of the game for me.
Combat is as basic as it gets (roll. block. single(usefull) attack, single usefull weapon, several spells), and doesn't feel good. Your character is slow. Attack feedback is boring. Enemies looove to block and dash back, and both your chasedown and guard breaking ability is basically non existant without spells.
I don't know whether it is a deliberate choice or a bug, but holding sprint when you a locked on forces you character to run to an enemy in a straight line. Yep, you heard it right, you can't sprint around while you are locked on. Weird.
Block is almost useless with how much stamina and hitstun it takes, and how easy it is to get dead angled. Parry is such an afterthought that some enemies with shields don't even drop their guard when they are parried. Also it is so slow that most attacks in the game can't be parried on reaction even with good reaction and moveset knowledge. Just sad.
Also there is a LOT of combat. With how much there is, I expected it to be mor fun.
Well, thanks, Fromsoft, I guess we all now get to live in a world where every game has stamina meter and other soulslike trappings without understanding of what makes them fun and compelling.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tVery good game for a single playthrough. Not many videogames made me want to use physical notepad and note down all the clues and combinations trying to figure out all the puzzles, but this one managed to succeed in exactly that, and it made the process really fun to boot. Puzzles are very reminiscent of games like "Fez" or - if you want to go REALLY oldschool - Atari "Swordquest".
So while on surface level it seems like just a Zelda clone, it's actually a puzzle game deep down in it's core. Despite that it still has some interesting Zelda-like mechanics such as ability to grab most items in any order depending on how good you are with exploring - like in "Ittle Dew" - and there are some devious ways you can combine these items for additional effects.
The game biggest flaw would be probably that it's magic is gone after single playthrough, no matter what you do you cannot unlearn the mechanics that are used to solve the puzzles so most likely single playthrough is going to be all of the enjoyment you are going to get out of this game.
However that single run is still decently long, the puzzles are top-notch quality and at the end of the day this game is well worth it's asking price.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTunic is a really fun but challenging puzzle-based Indie game. You have to navigate through a series of areas and find the right path on your own. The game pretty much gives you free rein to explore on your own which is great but it does make finding the main objective difficult. I think the game could have been better served with a tutorial system to explain the various actions you can do and how to fight. There are manual pages scattered throughout the game but it's written in a coded language that I couldn't make sense of. As a result, I had to consult a walkthrough to get through most of the game. The game has a really cute art style and the areas are all different/unique. I really loved exploring all the different areas and finding the secrets/chests scattered across the world. The combat is pretty challenging at first because you have a stamina bar like in Souls games. Every action consumes stamina so you have to be careful with how you fight. You also have magic too which is really useful for tough enemies. Didn't really use them much in boss fights. On the whole, Tunic is a great puzzle-based Indie title that I had a lot of fun with.\t\t\t\t\t
