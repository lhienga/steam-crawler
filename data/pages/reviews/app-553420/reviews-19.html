\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTunic took me aback. The game-play is surprisingly challenging and cryptic mixture of zelda and souls-like, but that crypic-ness is just the point!

This took me back to a time where I bought all my games and the inserts they came with were downright essential to not only beating the game, but making it feel like a complete experience. The artwork and the teasing made me nostalgic for the time where I used to go on holidays in the UK with my family, with 20 pounds in my pocket from my Grandma, and I got the chance to buy my only games for the year (we were quite broke as a family) - I used to go to GAME and get 3 PS2 games with that money and play them to death until the next trip, but before I could do that, I'd sit in the caravan, or in the car on the way home, reading the manuals and inserts over and over and over, pouring over the art, the controls, the hints for the content. Tunic made me think of all of that.

The gimmick in this game is not knowing what you're doing, how to do it, or really where the game will end up, but what you have in the form of your incomplete manual is enough, should you read it carefully, to understand how to get to the next stage of your adventure, get stronger, and get more pages. Whatever you do, much like other games where the mystery is half the fun, like Obra Dinn or Outer Wilds, don't look anything up about this game. Play it blind and complete it without guides or the internet if you can, I think the experience benefits from it.

Also, this ran great on my Steam Deck and was my preferred way to play.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA nearly flawless adventure game that goes far deeper than you might expect. Tunic is a masterclass in air-tight game design, with a particularly keen sense for how and when to influence the player. It manages to nail down a particular kind of old-school childlike wonder that I didn't think a modern title could really faithfully emulate. If you're a fan of exploring and secret hunting, and you enjoy the look of the combat and aesthetic, I cannot recommend this game enough.

I recommend playing Tunic completely blind, from start to finish, with no guides. It's tough, in a lot of ways, but it's extremely gratifying to unravel the game's web of secrets as the developers intended.

If you're well and truly stuck on something to the point of giving up, the "Spoiler Safe Guide to Tunic" on the Steam guides page is a great way of giving yourself a nudge in the right direction. I needed this a couple times - though frankly, every time I looked at it, I felt pretty dumb afterwards.\t\t\t\t\t
