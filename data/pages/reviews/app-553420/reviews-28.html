\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA gorgeous-looking, zelda-inspired exploration game that drinks a lot from souls-like mechanics (for good or bad).
I was in constant awe and just addicted the keep exploring and seeing what else I could find.
When I think ADVENTURE game, this is what comes to mind. This immersive sense of exploring a world and actually paying attention to it, uncovering its lands and drinking the views, music and every little detail.

The combat can feel excessively punitive and most deaths will feel a bit cheap instead of fair, but he game apparently has options to tweak difficulty.

Just beware. If you are not interested in puzzle solving this game ISN'T FOR YOU. The combat isn't deep enough to hold your interest by itself.

My only complain is that the devs might have gone a bit overkill with a certain late game puzzle. One of the most charming things about this game is the feeling of realizing something was under your nose without you noticing all this time. But with one particular mechanic at the end (HC), even when I know what I must do, I still find myself staring at the screen for minutes or hours without seeing what the game wants me to see, despite me already figuring out what the mechanic is. That doesn't happen for any other mechanic in the game, so it feels really out of place.

It would not be too bad, but this particular mechanic is REQUIRED if you want the full experience. I get what they were trying to do, a modern take on what Swordquest did on the atari is really cool, but that one had more reason to be more obnoxious to solve since it had prize money involved.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBefore getting into the review, I'll say the game has an "Infinite Stamina" mode and "No Fail" mode which is just invincibility if you just want to enjoy the exploration without the challenge from the combat. I played with these modes off but, of course, you should enjoy the game how you want to.

Tunic is a top-down Zelda style game with some Dark Souls elements that has a big emphasis on exploration and acquired knowledge through that exploration and inferring what things do.

In additional to the exploration elements, the game also has challenging bosses that can be approached a few different ways since there is no one progression sequence in the game and is open-ended shy for a few ability-gated parts that give it a bit of a Metroidvania flavor at times. I wouldn't define it as a Metroidvania but there are hints of that flavoring. I would say the strongest focus and feel is Zelda and Soulslike with probably more Zelda than Souls and a lot of the normal combat can be addressed more with a mind toward Zelda's top-down combat rather than just relying heavily on the dodge roll i-frames. You will want those dodge rolls for the bosses, though. And reflecting a bit more on the bosses, there's also at least a little hint of Gungeon in there, too.

It's an amazing game that has an interesting visual style and a lot going for it in the gameplay and design departments. Also the soundtrack is great if you're down for a chill electronic vibe and, if not, mute it and play whatever music you like! :D\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA game that blends "tapestry" puzzle-solving and "organic" puzzling.

Typically, with any puzzle (let's say sudoku) you approach a mile-wide ocean with very little sense of direction. You start to "corner-off" a few items until you eventually find a hit. This gives you some sense of direction of what to do next. This phase of puzzle solving is very taxing. Many will give up.

Eventually, the solution begins to become more clear and the decision space becomes narrower. There is a certain phase that is generally the "sweet spot" or high we get during a puzzle that feels like we have attained a certain mastery over it.
The next phase is the usual problematic one for most puzzles, tedium.

During the "tedium" phase we know what we have to do - it is simply a matter of mechanical execution. This is where even great puzzles like sudoku begin to fall flat, and people will give up out of boredom.

Tunic is a game that avoids this by delivering continuously innovative puzzling on all-fronts. I used the word tapestry because it really feels that way, a complex mesh of different ideas that all somehow work together.

Tunic is also a game that relishes in "organic" puzzles. There are very few games that do this effectively. I look back to classic point and click adventures like the golden era of LucasArts games (Grim Fandango) or what is, one of my personal favorites and I believe of the apex of this game direction, Riven.

Tunics puzzles are not only part of the world, but also integral to the story it is telling. Very very few games deliver in this way. It is not easy to do and the execution here is awesome. For me there is only a few things that do it as well, an old book called Puzzle Island, Riven and now this.

The other elements of Tunic have been discussed a lot, the route finding, combat, visual aesthetic and retro-throwbacks. I figured it would be interested to think about this game from the context of it's puzzling. As the solution narrowed, I never felt inclined to put it down. To the contrary I was pressed ever forward until the final page.\t\t\t\t\t
