\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAt first glance Tunic is just a very nice and cozy looking top-down action RPG with metroidvania and souls-like elements that doesn't want to tell you much, is not very hard to play and it reminds you of isometric iterations of "The Legend of Zelda" and of "Bastion". But the further you proceed, the more curious you may become about what that game really is. And it turns out to be an excelent example of how videogame may provide its narratives with a lore instead of a plot.
At start of this journey you wake up trying to comprehend what's going on, what does the game want from you and what do you want from the game, gradually finding pages of a manual-book in which not much is clear right away. You finish (no spoilers) either being too lazy to solve all the riddles of the game and watching an ending that would not have been canonical OR you're trying to get the hang of everything, trying to translate made up language, searching for visual patterns like in the game "The Witness" (which is fully consists only of puzzles and exploration), disclose a lot of other secrets that are very-very far from obvious (or using guides) and getting the true ending.
I guess that people who played on a release had an exciting time trying to figure out all that stuff, there's really big amount of what to discover and some things are left uncertain or unrevealed even after a year since the release. So that game turned out to be a real hardcore, but not as a souls-like, but as a puzzle-game.
P.S. MacOS-version is brilliant. There were 2 crashes, but other from that the port is awesome - at 4K resolution M1 Pro chip managed to provide an average of 70 FPS.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHonestly, tremendous. I've been following the development of this game since it was announced, and it has been well worth the wait. This game somehow captures the cryptic nature of games from the NES era, and that theme is continued as the game guides you almost entirely through a 90s-esque gaming manual in-game full of impeccable artwork, charts, and maps. I showed up for a cute, Zelday game with a fox, but I will compelled forward by gorgeous graphics, an immersive soundtrack, enchanting sound-design, and perfectly-paced "story." The game play overall is accessible but still spicy. I found myself having to reevaluate my strategy each time I faced defeat, but didn't spend any longer than half an hour on any one boss. This game is truly excellent.

I do have two areas of critique, however. The first is that the controller support for Steam failed to support the targeting system. I started with a controller, and had to switch to keyboard because there were certain enemies I could not get past, as the 'locking on' in fact, did not lock on, and chose instead to switch targets as it pleased. My second area of critique is that this game has a LOT of hidden pathways, and relies heavily on them to move you through levels. I found myself getting stuck for far too long simply because I didn't find a specific, hidden path. If hidden paths would've been introduced earlier on, or even in the manual, I think that would've prepared me better and alerted me to their usage. But even after I knew about the out-of-sight passages, I still struggled to find them; imagine my frustration when I couldn't move forward without finding a path forward that I could not see, when I did not know that they were ever on the table. Those are truly my only complaints, and overall they were very minor. Play this game. It's a good time.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA good game, although not without its flaws. The puzzle design is almost perfectly balanced, there is always just enough of a hint available to nudge you in the right direction and nothing felt particularly obtuse or unfair. You're going to want to have a pen and paper handy. The "main story quest" is not too difficult to figure and there are some side mysteries as well which will require more thought.

The action combat is challenging and fair, but the difficulty of the combat encounters is all over the place. Locking on can sometimes do crazy and unpredictable things to your movement as well. I had fun with it but it is definitely less of a draw than the puzzle side of the game. If you find yourself getting frustrated with it then there is an invincibility toggle available in the game's accessibility menu.

As for my complaints, the soundtrack is ... not that great, but that is a matter of personal opinion. I ended up muting it about two thirds of the way through my playthrough because it is a little bit too grating and repetitive. Since this is a puzzle game you are going to be doing a lot of backtracking, so the option to return to the last rested-at checkpoint voluntarily without having to get killed in combat would have been nice (maybe this option does exist but I wasn't able to find it; the interface itself is part of the game's puzzles). A minor nitpick is that the game over-uses vibration and I ended up turning that off as well for the sake of my controller battery.\t\t\t\t\t
