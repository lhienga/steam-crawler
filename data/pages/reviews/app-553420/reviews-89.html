\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is not what it seems.

I enjoyed everything about it at first. The art is fantastic. The in-game manual is incredibly well put together, and piecing together information both about the world and game mechanics by studying the images on it was incredibly fun. The combat is very satisfying, and the excitement of finding out what each power-up I obtained does after getting the funky text 'item obtained' screen filled me with wonder.

Then, the end game drained all the joy out of me. Spoilers from here on out, obviously.

The game stops being about exploration and combat, to instead focus on cryptography, solving puzzles that sometimes can become obtuse and following patterns hidden in the world that can require you to do up to 100 button inputs, and missing one of them forces you to restart the whole chain.

If you're into that sort of thing, then you'll love this game. But if you're anything like me, the sudden shift in gameplay will leave a very sour taste in your mouth.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTunic is really two games in one.

The first game is obvious - it's the Zelda-like, souls-inspired, dungeon crawler that you probably bought the game for. Exploring distinct, colorful areas, fighting enemies with basic hack-and-slash combat, finding upgrades and new progression items, solving engaging but simple puzzles, using maps to chart complex dungeons, unearthing secrets, and fighting demanding but satisfying bosses. All your Zelda staples are covered, and the game does a pretty competent job of copying the nostalgic bits from ALTTP and other Zelda games while also adding it's own twists. It also borrows the corpse run mechanic, demanding boss encounters, and parry timing from Dark Souls. The graphics are amazing, the music is nice and atmospheric, and the story is simple at first glance but becomes steadily more engaging and surreal as you progress.

I'd give this main, Zelda-like portion of the game a solid 8/10. It does most everything extremely well with some fresh ideas, but it isn't groundbreaking (with the exception of one aspect I mention below). It will definitely hit the spot for any fans of Zelda or Souls games, though. I certainly thoroughly enjoyed the adventure, though be aware the difficulty is closer to Dark Souls than Zelda.

The second game is the in-game user manual that you will piece together throughout the game. This is Tunic's gimmick, the thing that makes it unique from other retro Zelda-likes. Throughout the course of the "main" game, you will find pages of this cute, Zelda 1 style manual that will give you small pieces of information and clues to push you in the right direction pr explain game mechanics. Crucially, the manual has very limited readable text, as most of the text is in an ancient runic language that is indecipherable. So you have to take the limited hints you get and read between the lines to really understand what is going on.

The manual also acts as the game's map. You will pick up detailed map pages (complete with compass showing the player's position) of the overworld and all the main dungeon areas. You can miss pages that will help you progress, but generally the important ones are along the main path and difficult to overlook.

But that's just the beginning of the manual. Hidden deep within it's pages is a much larger puzzle, one that will consume countless hours of time beyond the main story of the game as you slowly piece together dozens of hints and clues. To complete this huge meta-puzzle, you will find yourself flipping back and forth between the manual and the game world, completing smaller puzzles scattered around the world, writing down information in a physical notebook (yes, this is one of only a few games I have played, along with La Mulana and a couple of the Myst games, that really requires you to use a notebook outside of the game to track info), and testing out hypotheses to figure out what hints are trying to tell you. Honestly, this puzzle alone is better than many puzzles from standalone puzzle games, because the answer is the culmination of everything you have accomplished and come to understand in the game since the very beginning, and it feels immensely satisfying to finally put it all together.

Even this meta-puzzle is not the end of Tunic's endless depth, though. Remember that "indecipherable" runic language that most of the manual is written in? Turns out it's not indecipherable, and you are actually able to translate the runes, which can help with a few puzzles and is absolutely mandatory for two puzzles at the very end of the game. Ultimately, deciphering the runic language and solving the huge meta-puzzle is not required to finish the "main" game (a wise decision considering most people interested in a simple Zelda clone won't have the patience to complete these mind-boggling puzzles), but it goes to show the sheer amount of depth and dedication put into a portion of the game that many players won't ever see.

The puzzle part of the game involving completing the manual was by far the more interesting and engaging part of the game for me, and I'd give this aspect an enthusiastic 10/10. It includes one of my favorite puzzles in any game ever, and I love the aspect of writing down information from across the game world and manual in a notebook to solve an overarching meta-puzzle. This portion of the game is not for everyone, but if you appreciate the ideas behind old-school adventure games like Myst or Riven or puzzle games like the Witness, this part of the game is for you. Not enough games explore this type of intense puzzle-solving that transcends the limits of the game itself, and Tunic does it very well.

Overall Score: 9/10 \t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI have no idea where I am, what on Earth is happening, or even what to do, and all I have to guide me is a 85% un-understandable instruction manual.
Truly one of the games of all time.








Ok, I guess you want "real" infos, since you clicked on "see more".
Here they are:

This game is good because it DOESN'T explain you anything clearly. You're left to figure everything out on your own, and that's a very interesting experience.
For the gameplay I would say that it's the fusion of Hollow knight and the old top-down perspective zelda games, with some souls-like elements. But the arstyle is very cute. Cute Fox makes me go happy.

Pros: -That constant feeling of discovering something new, making playing that game a unique experience.
-The instruction booklet. I love this idea, super creative. Sometimes you go "I could do that the whole time?!" upon discovering a page describing gameplay elements, or you try to apply something you read to a different but similar situation and it works and you go "I'm a genius!".
-Most of what you can expect from a souls-like games: go back to your last death location or you'll loose a (very small) amount of stuff, healing flasks and stamina bar management, pattern-based bosses...
-Dunno if it matters that much, but the game is beautiful.
-Got a ton of awards/nominations since release so that's probably why you're here.
-Is published by Finji so that means the game is good (they published Chicory and I Was a Teenage Exocolonist, as a matter of fact).

C ons: -The nature of the game means that even the most mundane thing can be a spoiler, so it's pretty hard to ask or search what to do if you end up stuck.
-Again because of the nature of the game, replay value is pretty low.
-Gameplay is quite rigid sometimes, but that's probably just skill issue from me.

At the moment I'm writing this, I just beat the final boss, but I know there's more to that game than that, and I'm eager to discover it.
Hoping that what I wrote made you interested in the game a little more

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTunic managed to capture the essence of playing games like the Legend of Zelda back in the early 2000s. I am amazed at how well it made me feel like I was 10 years old, without knowing enough English to understand fully what is happening but enjoying the game nonetheless. It encourages players to get stuck at certain points and try everything in the hopes it will help them progress. I have to admit that I after completing maybe 60% of the game I became impatient and went online for some guidance. Aside from that I have to say that the Art is amazing, as well as the Music. My favorite part about it tho is the challenge it offers, as it is something that I am always looking for in an adventure game. It is challenging enough that I know some players will give up after a couple of tries, but yet so rewarding if you manage to keep trying. Highly recommend giving it a try.\t\t\t\t\t
