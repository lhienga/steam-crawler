\r\n\t\t\t\t\t"Flawed Masterpiece" really sums up Tunic for me. I loved the sound track and souls-like combat that wasn't quite as punishing as actual Dark Souls. I had a lot of fun rounding corners, discovering abilities, and going on an adventure with so many twists, but I have to admit I was not expecting the wave of puzzles that is the last few hours of the game. Artistically it's a bold move and one I applaud, but I don't enjoy the feeling of being so lost directionally and circling the map for pages. The puzzles themselves seem interesting and I might come back to them, but I'm feeling pretty frustrated after all the hard work put into the combat. We'll see. It's a 7/10 for me, but (knowing the ending and developer's message) I can see why some people say it's their GOTY.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI went into this game almost completely blind, I had seen only a little bit of the gameplay and heard a lot of good about this game. Coming out on the other side, after finishing the game with *almost* no hints on the internet, I feel like going in blind is the absolute best experience with this game. You can absolutely play through it multiple times--in fact, there are some things you can only do via NG+, albeit not story-related things--but going through the game for the first time, you get this feeling for which there is no better adjective than "mystical." You don't really know what you are doing or where you're going at first, and then the game drip-feeds context and information as you continue through the world. Every small puzzle I figured out on my own, every little mechanic I figured out, it was genuinely fun from start to end, and the world story leaves a lot up to interpretation and diving into the slightest details that the game gives us to examine.

I said *almost* no hints, because there are a handful of puzzles (I think I could actually count them on one hand) where I absolutely could have figured it out on my own time, but at the time I was playing, I didn't have the spare time to do that, and I did look them up. Especially the final secret puzzle, that one likely took the first people who solved it several minutes of piecing together one clue at a time. They're solvable, just very time-consuming.

It's also fair to point out that the game does use "souls-like" combat staples. Locking on to a target, managing stamina, dodging, blocking and parrying, set checkpoints, and losing some of your money on death. That may be a deterrent for some players, but as someone who likes that kind of gameplay, I will say that Tunic's combat is a little less punishing than what is typical for Dark Souls and souls-likes unless you're charging into an area stronger than you are ready to face. It's a trade-off of more manageable difficulty with the mechanics of the game being more obscured until you either find hints or just try something out and realize "oh, that's a mechanic, I should use that."

Absolutely loved my time spent with this game, and I want to come back to it at some point, though I don't know whether I'll fully restart or NG+\t\t\t\t\t

Music, artstyle and atmosphere are all incredible.

Combat is simple and fun but can be janky. Some of the items are vastly superior to others which is a shame, I spent the whole playthrough using just three of the 8 or so items. If you get stuck on a boss just spam bombs or reduce difficulty in options.

There is a very deep puzzle system to explore at end game if that's your thing. I found it so overwhelmingly obscure that I didn't bother. There are also a couple of points in the story where finding where to go next can feel like a waste of time.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFun game. The big thing for me was the combat. It feels a lot like an easier/simplified version of dark souls where you have to learn enemy attacks, how to dodge them, and at what moments you have the opportunity to attack. However, considering that this was my favorite part of the game, I was left a little disappointed as there are only a handful of bosses. That being said, this doesnt seem to be the main selling point of the game.

The world is laid out a bit like a metroidvania where you go around from place to place unlocking new abilities that allow you to access new areas. However, unlike any metroidvania I've played, apart from one exception, I never encountered a moment where I didn't know where to go or what to do and had to look up what to do next. The one exception was due what I consider to be an inconsistency in level design.

Then it is also worth mentioning what I feel like most people consider the main selling point. There are numerous secrets to be discovered. Often times the game's routing has you go down the intended path, and then return via a secret shortcut that was availible to you the whole time had you been aware. There are also numerous puzzles and secrets that entirely part of the game's manual, which is one of the core gameplay elements.\t\t\t\t\t

At the time of writing this review, I haven't finished the game yet (in the second 'phase' of the game currently), but I would still recommend this game to others! \r\n\t\t\t\t\tDo you like very punishing combat and overly cryptic puzzles? If so, give this game a shot.

I'll say some nice things first. The world is very well crafted and fun to explore, progression is (kinda) satisfying, and there's a lot of hidden stuff to find that will make you feel like a genius when you do. The music is an excellent addition too and fits in perfectly with the atmosphere.

My enjoyment with this game was highest at the beginning, but steeply dropped off as I continued further. This game tells you nothing and expects you to figure it out entirely on your own. I gave up playing legit about half way through and resorted to a guide. Even then, with some of the later game puzzles, I found myself asking: how are you even supposed to figure this out? Some of the puzzles seem like they would take *hours* of searching and trial and error. Now, depending on what kind of gamer you are, you may like this, and I'd say go for it if you do, but I did not like this.

On to my major complaints

This. Game. has one of the most unforgiving dodges I've ever played in any game. Period. Dark Souls' dodge even isn't as much bullsh*t as this one is. To make things worse, bosses constantly spam their own dodge abilities too (just to really rub it in), so the whole fight is just you chasing them around the arena trying your damnedest to hit an agile-af monstrosity with your sluggish sword that has *zero* reach. Changing the target focus is infuriatingly unreliable, and other weapons are clunky and have very little ammo to the point you're always using the terrible sword because it's the only thing with infinite durability. Everything but the bossfights and general combat are fine. The world design is great, and the combat *outside* of bossfights is pretty fun, but holy sh*t this game's combat made my blood boil to an unnecessary extent. Call it a skill issue if you want, but I just found it too frustrating at times, especially the cathedral bossfight.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is a wonder in an aspect but a disappointment for me on the other.

I had a blast decoding the entire language - it's the best part of the game (even though the vast majority of the puzzles don't really need you to do that). However, the battle system is wonky at best, it tries to be a souls-like but lacks the clean controls to achieve that. With reduced combat difficulty it's more enjoyable, but there is still a part where I was frustrated enough to open up no fail mode. I do understand the argument that, when approaching the battles with a more puzzle-like mindset it's a lot more fun, but to me it's not very effective. Maybe I'm just bad at action games idk.

Overall I would still recommend this to players that like puzzles, there are some pretty awesome moments throughout. Probably turn on reduced difficulty though if you're not into the combat.
