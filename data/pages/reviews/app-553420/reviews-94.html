\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTunic is a hearty game in filled with brilliant puzzles and an amazingly charming world.

Tunic is an open map adventure game of very few english words that has confidence in itself and it's players. Using natural human curiosity, learning through experience, and an in game manual that serves to nudge players in the right direction rather than spelling out your objectives, this game is a master class of making it's story your own.

The world itself is jammed full of interesting corners to poke your cute little fox nose into, finding new items, neat puzzles, and sometimes just plain fun little ester eggs to have some fun with. It puts the isometric view to good use, letting the world have a strong unique visual style while giving you a lot of elbow room to work with.

The combat is pretty good. Most mooks are dispatched pretty easy but it's not hard to get ran down and killed. If the adventure part of the game is the legend of zelda for NES, then the combat is a FROM SOFTWARE game in no ambiguous terms. You have a set of healing potions that refill at checkpoints, those check points respawn all enemies, and you got a dodge roll with intangibility frames. It can be an uphill struggle to get use to the combat, especially if you're not use to enemies that are as relentless as these ones can be.

There's little more I can say about the game without diminishing parts of the game. An adventure is best told from your own first person experience. Just know this game gets a full heart container from me. A truely amazing little masterpiece of a game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t9.5/10

This game has been one of the most memorable storytelling experiences I've had in my life, rivaled only by Outer Wilds, and some Borges short stories. It is an incredible game, full of mystery and challenge and it's definitely among my top 10 games of all time.

The music is incredible, it looks gorgeous and it just feels good and it's just fun to play. The game is both difficult when it comes to combat and puzzles, although I never found the combat frustrating, or too difficult, but I do like these kind of games. If you like games like Hollow Knight, Outer Wilds, Death's Door, or The Witness, you absolutely need to get this game.

If you are a completionist and want to uncover all of the mysteries of the game, the puzzles are genius. This game truly contains a few of the most memorable and ingenious puzzles I've seen in a video game, but some of them can be frustrating. I did need some internet help a couple of times in order to nudge myself in the right direction without spoiling the solution. But once I understood whether I was in the right path or not, or generally what I was looking for, everything made sense and felt coherent. I do think when it comes to some of the most challenging puzzles specially at the very end, clues could be telegraphed more clearly, there were a couple of instances where I was in the right direction but I felt like I was doing it wrong.

Having said that, I do admire the challenge and how this relentless approach to puzzle design makes the game have an incredible air of mystery and wonder.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is probably the best game I've found in the past year. It accomplishes everything it sets out to and then some with a thoroughly cohesive world practically oozing with secrets. Using the instruction booklet as a means to learn about the world and its mystery, building up to the point in which you are given just enough of a hint to figure out how the mysterious runic script throughout the game works is such a unique and interesting mechanic that kept me questioning and curious through my entire playthrough.

There are lots of cool nods and influences from Dark Souls and the NES Zelda, which veterans of both series will notice in their careful and tasteful placement. What's more, is the game manages to perfectly balance itself between the map problems of DS1 and Zelda. The latter had highly distinct areas with poorly defined paths of progress, while the latter lacked so much in graphical detail that it could be difficult to tell one part of the map from another at times.

This game was an absolute blast that held my attention for its entire duration, and I'm already itching to look for the rest of the secrets I've yet to discover so far...

100/10. Masterpiece.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTunic is one of those rare games where I recommend going in knowing as little as possible beforehand. The short gist is that you wake up as a small fox, and must venture through a world not dissimilar to the original Zelda, wrought with secrets, puzzles, and enemies. Combine this with a souls-style combat system, as seems to be the indie standard, and you have the grounds for an already intriguing game. If this alone interests you, I recommend you stop reading and play now.
If you need more convincing, then know that everything I described above exists as a surface level blanket which obscures what Tunic truly is- a meta game wherein you collect game knowledge as you play, usually in the form of pages of the in-game manual, and use that knowledge to solve the game itself. Everything in Tunic is a puzzle, even things you might not realize matter at first glance. To learn about any of the big secrets hidden in the game is tantamount to completely recontextualizing it, and suddenly you find yourself considering the importance of things you would have never bothered looking at originally. This makes the game, in actuality, more akin to opening a puzzle box, and the joy of the game comes in those moments where the game redefines itself in a way it can only do on a blind first playthrough.
Aside from the games core, its presentation is stunning. The voxel art style is simple and crisp, conveying all necessary information while also looking remarkably cute. The music and sound design fits this theme, and pulled me into the game world quite immersively. The manual itself is really the best part though, beautiful and detailed, highly intricate but simple on the surface, a perfect metaphor for this game.
I encourage anyone who wants to play this game to go in without any kind of guide, and to avoid them at all cost. If I could, I would remove my memories of this game entirely and play it all over again, just for that feeling of discovery that I can never get again.\t\t\t\t\t

Play fox game.



But for real, I really do recommend it if you like exploration, puzzles with little to no explanation and having to figure stuff out yourself. Keep in mind that pretty much everything in this game is a spoiler, however. There's a reason almost all achievements are hidden.
Some reviews I've read complained about combat difficulity, and that's fair - not everyone wants to experience the game as a souls-like (myself included, I'm more about the exploration aspect). If you find combat too difficult, you can always turn it down to Reduced in the settings. With that option, it only took me a couple tries at most to beat bosses. Worst case scenario, you can turn on the included "No Fail" modes. Having to collect pages of the manual was also a very enjoyable experience, and a take I simply have never seen before,
The only real "complaints" I'd have, are how I look a little too deep into something and completely miss the most obvious things, but that's more on me than the game itself.
And don't get me started on the soundtrack. It's absolutely worth buying that alongside the game itself.
Bonus points for having a key sequence to show an adorable animation of the fox hugging the TUNIC Huggable Plush.\t\t\t\t\t
