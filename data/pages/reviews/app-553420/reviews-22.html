\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game makes me wish every game came with a cute manual like they used to. The use of the manual as a guide is incredibly creative and fresh - very rarely did I feel compelled to look anything up, the manual pages I had were always there to provide at least some sort of clue, some hint. I think a lot of games tend to hide valuable information at particular locations in the game world with no way of collecting said information in-game, a problem that Tunic does not suffer from assuming you've found some of the pages (some are easier to find than others). The puzzles were also very engaging: it has been a long time since I felt the need to grab pen and paper to jot down notes on a game, something that I did not know that I missed doing.

If you like solving puzzles, fighting (arguably) tough bosses, sitting on the couch staring at a game manual for a straight hour trying to understand where to go next, or exploring a polygonal world as a cute little fox in green garb, then this game is for you. Even if those things don't sound interesting I would still recommend the game, it is among the best titles I have ever played.\t\t\t\t\t

cons:
1. The map is hard to access and there is no world map that shows the entire map. I'd love to know what connects to what in a single view. As a newb it's so hard to know where I'm going, what am I supposed to do(even though I watched the full stream of 100% true ending).(There's no mods about this)

2. The in-game language is forever untranslated. This may be a pros if you got fully immersed to this world but for most people who I assume don't have the patience to learn the language like myself, it's just a hassle(AND THERES NO TRANSLATION MODS TOO...).

3. The controls sucks.. it's very inconvenient making the game-play boring for me personally. It feels like a game for controllers not a keyboard and mouse, and you don't learn your keys from a tutorial, you have to check it for yourself, in the settings, moreover there isn't any tutorial at all.

pros:
1. The map is very large, a lot of contents are available. A ton of hidden stuff exist.
2. The game has depth, it has it's own language, a long story (hard to understand tho),
3. The main character is cute(especially on the ending credits, IF YOU SURVIVE TILL THEN..

I have intentions on playing this again if more skilled modders start to make mods, but until then I'd rather play other metroidvania games

summary
cons: I'M LOST, GAMEPLAY SUCKS, WTF ARE THEY SAYING

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt deconstructs the game and lays it wide open on your table, allowing you to put your own soul in it.

It also testifies to video games as "wonder machines". All games try to create wonder to the best of their resources (even Starfield, don’t laugh!), but Tunic seems to be more focused on the process than the output, more interested in the "clockwork" behind the magic, the “machinery” that makes the god appear rather than the god himself (shifting the emphasis in the traditional deus ex machina device). It even has the mechanics and the meta-narration for it.

Your main mission will be to piece together and figure out the manual that describes the game, which is also a game guide, a cheat book and your most important resource for puzzle solving. All that because Tunic is essentially a puzzle game, masquerading as a top-down souls-like metroidvania only so that it can push the puzzles into the meta layer of its narrative, hiding them in plan sight, as it were. And they are wonderful, brilliant puzzles! So great they totally overshadow the action mechanics the game relies on in its early stages, to the point that it almost makes them irrelevant by the end game (you can choose a greatly reduced difficulty for enemy encounters and boss fights and even activate a sort of god-mode from the game options, if you want to concentrate on the puzzles). And the game will want you concentrated on those.

That’s not to say its action mechanics are less accomplished, of course, but the gradual shift from action to puzzles as the game progresses is obvious and a great example of versatile and meaningful game design, that I’ve not seen many games able to attempt so successfully. Therefore, if you want to play it as a slick souls-like metroidvania in a colourful Zelda-like setting, you can totally tackle it like one of those, but its truest nature is closer to the ascetic self-awareness of The Witness, and the game manual it has you put together while playing is hands down the most self-referential statement ever made by a video game.\t\t\t\t\t
