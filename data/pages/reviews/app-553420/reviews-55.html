\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAn absolutely amazing game. It's full of mystery and secrets that demand you to use your brain if you want to figure it out. If you want to get the good ending you need to solve the hardest puzzle of your life which I had to look up because I couldn't figure it out after getting all possible clues (that's on me, there was a hint in hindsight). If you don't feel like a mastermind don't worry! Getting to the bad ending just requires you to play through the game and it makes puzzles mostly optional.

I felt a true sense of wonder while wandering around and feeling smart for figuring secrets out, a magical feeling you thought you could only ever experience when you were still a kid.

9/10

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTunic is an ingenious mesh of well-proven inspirations and new ideas. The game is often as much of a tricky puzzle game as it is an enjoyable and familiar-looking action RPG. Tunic undeniably lifts the base of its presentation, top-down camera, tough-yet-fair sword-and-shield combat, and clever interconnected level design from iconic sources (i.e. Link's Awakening and Dark Souls), but the game adds a layer of ‘knowledge-progression’ that’s hidden within a nostalgic, half-translated in-game strategy guide.

In a gripping perspective-expanding process akin to the joy of solving Outer Wilds, from the start, you essentially already have everything you need to go anywhere (or at least do some serious sequence breaking before key items get in the way), but you don’t yet know enough about this new land to harness your own potential. Here, knowledge from the game’s scattered manual has the same door-opening/problem-solving power as keys or ‘super missiles’ might. While you go about the intended adventuring route, so many shortcuts and interactions lay just out of reach, merely hiding in plain sight.

The value Tunic places on information is quite refreshing. As your tools and stats progress, so does your understanding of the world. You have no idea what subtle details could become relevant later, which bestows this unknown depth and mystery to the whole experience. When new pages of lovely art and inconclusive imagery are revealed to you, deciphering them makes you feel like a real detective. I also love the dramatic irony of knowing that nothing was stopping you from your goals besides a trick that seemed obvious in retrospect.

This retro-and-other-inspired RPG that incorporates its manual into the pacing of its gameplay concept works so well. That guidebook/exploration dynamic makes for some unforgettable moments. But it’s also worth briefly complimenting how Tunic’s other components elevate the game into something much greater than the sum of its sources. The story is told indirectly and silently. It brings further intrigue into this seemingly friendly animal world. The music, while an initially unexpected tone, evokes waves of synthy nostalgia. Tunic’s soundtrack could’ve been a lot more predictable and I think Lifeformed and Janice Kwan found a great way to add to Tunic’s unique identity. Combat isn’t a major thrill, but it has good moment-to-moment pacing. While parrying is a little slugging, it’s far from necessary to succeed. Most bosses require ample mastery to overcome without having to pull out any hairs.

And if it ended there, I wouldn’t be hesitant to rank Tunic among its like-minded masterpieces.

Sadly, the game ends on a pretty painful note. It begins with a slew of backtracking. Some paths are new, a lot are now blocked. Some upgrade materials you missed are also blocked off. Combat essentially doesn't exist for this portion. There’s nothing in your way as you run around, looking for the clues from the manual. For the final set of new adventuring material, Tunic asks you to take an extreme dive into the manual for cryptic secrets which demand a lot of brainpower and offer no tactile reward. And, as you’d expect, the final boss isn't anything trivial.

I was so into the first two-thirds of this game. Tunic is still certainly worth experiencing for those first ten-ish hours, so don't let my need to discuss my discontent with the final act stain your impressions of the truly brilliant parts. Seeing as fun as Tunic’s learning process was, perhaps it’s fitting that an end to that growth would entail a decline in my interest in it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI played through the Game and here are my thoughts:
The Game in itself plays fine the controls took some getting used to and combat is heavily inspired by the 2d Zeldas and Dark Souls. The Music is decendly athmospheric and relaxing when running around, the Boss Battle themes are decent aswell and the graphics are minimalistic, but beautiful.
However the Gamedesign and Puzzledesign is where things take a nosedive.
First off, I played games like La Mulana and Hollow Knight (Basically meaning that I am not a stranger to very hidden lore or puzzle hints.) However this Game put some of the most annoying Puzzle hints into the very thing you need to collect. And these Hints are sometimes simple, however most of the Time, you can learn this entire new language, just so you can get D-pad combinations that make the Konami Code look simple.
And all that for one trinket, you just need for a good ending.
The Game also does not tell you that you can upgrade your stats unless you do some digging into the Play Manual.
And with digging, I mean translate some more language or just trying out buttons.
The Bosses can definetly be fun, (if you did upgrade your stats) however if you fail to notice that, you get a rage-inducing slog of a Game.
Another thing, why do you need to Upgrade your Potions? Just give the player 30 to 40% heal per Potion, it is simple Players have always a good amound of health, without a good chucking of potions when hurt and it takes inspitation from Estus flasks.
Can I recomment the Game? I don't really know.
The Gameplay is decent, but the Mindboggeling Puzzledesign that expects some Metagaming knowledge in solving them, clearly doesn't make this into a Game I can recomment to everyone...If you are very patient and you love good old Nintendo Power-escue hints. Be my guest.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA charming LoZ like adventure with greater emphasis on puzzles and exploration!

This game brought me back to the days of playing Link's Awakening on the gameboy in all the best ways! The stylized graphics are beautiful and make for some very pretty locations to explore! The developers did a great job of tapping into some of what makes LoZ such an amazing series, while also taking it their own direction with more emphasis on puzzles and the mysteries of the world.

The world of Tunic is one of mystery, and one of my favorite parts of the game is how some of the mystery is unraveled. You happen upon game manual pages within the world, and they give you meta insights, such as the basic controls, what items might do, or eventually hints at the greater narrative. However they're also obfuscated by being written in runic text, and only receiving a page at a time, so the player has to extrapolate from the info and pages they have and figure out how the info can help them.

I'd definitely recommend if you're a fan of any of the older top down LoZ games, or anyone that's interested in a charming world full of puzzles and mysteries to be explored!

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHaving the manual for the game scattered around the world to be found and pieced together in order to learn new mechanics or different ways to solve puzzles or look at the world was incredibly fun. It was a serious throwback to the original Legend of Zelda and how its manual was crucial to learning how to play and solve puzzles in the game. But in Tunic its like your discovering old sacred text that also reveals more about the story and world itself.
The combat and puzzles are far from easy, but that makes them that much more rewarding when overcoming them.
The art style, atmosphere, and music are great. I felt the progression and exploration were well balanced and never bored me.
Tunic overall is an incredibly well put together and polished game.


If you like puzzle games like legend of zelda or really difficult combat games like hollow knight I would highly recommend Tunic.

Tunic is very clearly inspired by Zelda, Fromsoft titles, Hollow Knight, The Witness. But it doesn't shackle itself to those games success, it provides it's own very strong experience. EVERYTHING, is a VISUAL puzzle. Every aspect of the gameplay, every inch of the map, every objective, every secret.

This is what Zelda NES could have been if it was developed by the Nintendo today with the mindset of the developers back then. Very good job DEVS! And props to your artists for working the puzzles into everything.
