\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI just completed this with the good ending, and if I had to sum Tunic up in one word it would be "tedious". There isn't even a narrative payoff at the end. I bought this because I heard it was like Outer Wilds or The Witness, and it's not. I like the idea of piecing together how the game works from clues, but the clues rarely contain all the information you need, so in practice there's a ton of brute force. The game really likes its "there's an invisible path the fixed camera doesn't let you see" gimmick, and I spent a lot of time running around the edges of zones trying to bump into those to progress (not for secrets, to complete the core game). That style is a good description of the "puzzle" design throughout. It's the epitome of artificial difficulty, and having seen the whole thing, I can confidently say the game is not as clever as it wants you to think it is.

There's an incredibly hard combat game tacked on as well. Combat is pretty clunky to begin with, and about halfway through the game takes away all of your upgrades and ramps up the difficulty significantly. Some people like that I guess, but I think it conflicts with the exploration aspect of the game and just gets in the way. After struggling with my ego for a few hours, I went into accessibility and turned on invulnerability. This really improved the game for me, and that says as much as anything else I might add.

I may have been better off just quitting when the combat got too hard. There's a part at the end where you have to decipher and enter a literally 100 symbol long code. Like this game, the concept is neat, but the execution is tedious.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTUNIC is a game that felt like it promised to be my game, and then said "I lied!" 10 hours later.

What everyone recommends is that you go into TUNIC blind and free of walkthroughs, which I agree with, but from what you can see from trailers and the steam page is that it's a bit of a 2D Zelda clone with a manual that you can't read and secrets everywhere. That's good! I used to love those games and felt like this sort of exploration of old games' level design was a very fun idea.

And the game exectutes this fairly well! The manual pages are fun to find and the secrets can be wonderful to figure out yourself, some many hours after you already had the opportunity to do something with them. A lot of people mention how this makes it so getting lost is way too easy, and yes, it is, but I think that is to be expected if you want the ideal experience with this game. , go play the first Zelda on the NES, are you not lost?

The problem is something else entirely. Because exploration and cryptic puzzles simply isn't enough to make this game entertaining (debatable), TUNIC has a lot of combat too. A sword and a shield, magic items, a dodge roll, and every type of enemy attacking you that you can think of. You heal through flask-like items, and using checkpoints makes enemies respawn. Sound familiar?

Unfortunately, TUNIC's combat is an intrusive mess, a complete " you" to the player with very easy to understand but mechanically unfair fights. Every enemy encounter is potentially fatal, and they're all okay at best and "immediately closing the application" at worst. Out of, what, 5 or 6 boss fights I did, only 1 was all that interesting. , at some point they even give you enemies who make your health bar smaller if you get hit. Are you serious? What is this design?

It is upsetting to me how much a game so unashamed of showing its influences can also feel so afraid of having simple zelda-like combat. This game should not have been a soulslike. Its hostility towards the player can make some sense thematically, but is so frustrating that I've tried three times to give this game another chance and failed. I got to one of the parts people consider to be "when the game gets good" but at that point my patience had wore down so much that I couldn't bear to keep going.

If you want to get this anyway but consider my opinion on this worth a listen, just... Don't be afraid to just use the invincibility. The fact that it's an option right there from the moment you start means something.

I just wish TUNIC was a game about exploring areas and solving puzzles. It isn't.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis has quickly become one of my favourite games ever. It is absolutely outstanding.
This game does not give you a lot to go off. You don't really know anything when you start, but it gives you just enough as you go through the game to piece everything together. It's an interesting idea which a lot of games try to do, but i personally feel like this is one of the only times that it has actually been done well.
By giving you a user manual in a cryptic language, it gives you guidance, but makes you figure it out yourself. It's such a unique approach. It explains everything, absolutely everything, but does so in a way that still requires you to figure it all out for yourself. This is such an innovative way of doing instruction, explanation, and guidance, and it works shockingly well. It is engaging, interesting, and uninvasive.
I do however think that this feature is a massive hit or miss sort of thing, with some serious downsides, and the potential to ruin the game for some people. You will frequently find yourself getting lost while playing, and remaining lost for a while until you find what it is that you missed. You won't know if you're just missing something obvious, or if you're in the wrong part of the world entirely. The frequent confusion followed by aimless wandering definitely gets quite tiresome and frustrating, but overall i think this is an extremely cool part of the game.

The storytelling is done in a similar way. It's engaging, and it's presented in a way that I can only describe by calling it art. It has you experience the story rather than being told it, which i absolutely love.

The visuals are just stunning. A very unique and stylised aesthetic which looks beautiful, has serious love and attention put into it, and doesn't require a supercomputer to run well.

The combat isn't good, but it's okay. This is where the similarities to dark souls hurt the game. The dodging, attacking, blocking, parrying, and healing are all so slow and clunky, and the game could be better with a more fast paced combat system, BUT, it works okay in this. The enemy attacks are very well telegraphed, both visually and aurally, and there are well defined patterns. The mechanics which may not be immediately obvious are understood after checking the manual, the hitboxes are accurate, and the animations are good. The clunkiness is a definite negative, but the combat is built around it better than most games that have the same issue, so unlike those games, it's quite manageable here. Once you get comfortable with it, it feels fine.

Audio is great, the music goes from intense to eerie to beautiful so smoothly, and the sound effects do an outstanding job at making you feel immersed.

The physics give a good feeling of polish to the game, but at the same time, can be a bit detrimental. For example, I got stuck completely at one spot because some debris blocked the path up and I just couldn't push it out of the way for some reason. It's cool seeing pots shatter to pieces that all move independently, and it's cool being able to make use of the physics by doing things such as rolling explosive barrels into a group of enemies, but the fact that these pieces can create roadblocks feels like a bit of an oversight.

The world is remarkable. It is very detailed, absolutely packed with secrets, so interesting and unique, and so well made. Every section fits together so seamlessly, shortcuts are scattered around in the most convenient places, and you're constantly shown things that are just out of reach, filling you with curiosity as to how and when you will be able to reach it. And every time you are given whatever new thing you need for you to get them, it feels absolutely amazing each and every time.
It is so full of life too. There are new enemies everywhere you go, that are all so clearly tailored for that area. They're all unique and interesting, and they are perfectly suited to their environment. Paired with the stunning visuals, this has to be one of my favourite video game worlds ever.

The puzzles are absolutely genius. I thought they were amazing all game, but once i reached the post game, i was blown away. I've always struggled with puzzle games, because they never make sense to me. Rather than trying to figure out the puzzle, i'm stuck trying to figure out what the rules and mechanics of the puzzle are. Yet somehow this game which is intentionally mysterious makes perfect sense to me. I know how to figure out the puzzles, i just need to actually solve them. I'm not wasting much time questioning what I need to do. This game has the single most interesting puzzles I have ever encountered, they are outstanding. Figuring out how to open the mountain door was such a gratifying feeling.
Some of them however, or more specifically, some of the bonus collectables, are way too demanding. They vary from simply needing you to notice little details in the manual, to expecting you to study up on musical notations. I do strongly recommend trying to figure out as much of the game as you can by yourself, but by the end, it felt to me more like you as an individual are not expected to, the playerbase as a whole is. You will definitely need guidance to 100% this.

Overall, i do believe this game is a hit or miss, as the unique approach to guidance could just as easily turn people away from the game as it could attract people, and the complexity of this world and the secrets it contains may be a bit much for some. Though I think if it does hit for you, you're going to have a new top 10 favourite game. It is just so good. I very strongly recommend giving it a chance.\t\t\t\t\t
