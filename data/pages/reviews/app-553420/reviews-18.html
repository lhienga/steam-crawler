\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI can't believe I'm posting a negative review about this gem of a game, but the final half demands it. The core gameplay of traversal and combat is compelling, challenging, and thought-provoking. Equally compelling is their approach to force you to figure the game out yourself, utilizing a game manual mostly written in an unknown language. You piece together how to play, how to progress, and what your objective is, and all of this feels fantastic and polished.

But in my opinion the devs took this mentality of hidden cryptic hints too far, and it causes the entire game to collapse on itself in the crucial final act. If you want the full, "true" ending of the game, you're going to have to either 1) spend hours of real-world time scrutinizing every minute detail of the manual to figure out and write down insanely-long D-pad sequences, or 2) look up the answers online and be appalled by the lengths they actually expected you to go for the answer. The 2nd option is inevitable for most players, and it ruins whatever sense of accomplishment you had from figuring the rest of the game out.

If that sounds enjoyable to you, you'll love this game. I loved 80% of it. But the final bit is dramatically different from the rest of the game, and with no advance warning, I felt cheated.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you're bad at soulslike games play this. I had this game on game pass for a bit. I loved the game so much that I decided to buy it.

The game is a lot like a soulslike game. And it's a perfect way to get someone younger into the swing of the kind of controls they'd be seeing in something more intense like Elden Ring. (I will note that Elden Ring is the only Soulslike I've completed and even so I did it following guides the whole way) Checkpoints that refresh monsters. Potion based healing, stamina based combat, and mana based magic. Heck it's even got a dodge roll. But i would say this and Hyper Light Drifter are closer than a soulslike. The game is shrouded in mystery and has a beautiful soundtrack to accompany it as well. The more I play it, the more it starts to climb the ladder for favorite game of all time. Visually it looks stunning. Clearly taking some inspiration from the Link's Awakening remake.

TL;DR Game is like dark souls for kids. Got a beautiful soundtrack and I can't read anything like in Hyper Light Drifter. 10/10 Definitely playing again and again.\t\t\t\t\t

Tunic

This is such an awesome game. I am really in love with Tunic, a Zelda like action adventure which does a lot on its own. It has a big emphasis on exploration and puzzles. Almost everywhere is some kind of treasure hidden. The game plays with its perspective to hide possible secrets. Later on in the game it gets even more apparent how many secrets you have missed just because you did not know better.

Talking about knowledge, Tunic has a very peculiar style of conveying relevant information. Maybe you know of video game manuals in the far past. Manuals in which actual useful information, where to go first, maps, secrets and the likes were included. Tunic has that and much more. The manual pages need to be actually found in-game. Bit by bit you learn more of the game and find out functions you could do, funnily enough, since the beginning of the game. This is a really neat way of progressing (and you really do progress alongside the manual!) apart of new items which you actually get as well. Not as many but there are a few items to get. It gives this more the feeling of a newly unlocked feature than a rant about why this wasn‚Äôt said earlier.
And talking about saying anything: In-game dialogues, inscriptions and the manual are mainly conveyed with a self-made language mixed with some English words here and there. The language can be actually be translated which is besides for a few secrets not necessary though. To get back to the manual one last time: it really oozes of secrets especially in the late game which I really really loved.

Perhaps you have played Deaths Door, another action adventure reminiscent of Zelda. I did last year and loved it. But Tunic‚Ä¶ Tunic is on another level in comparison, in some parts at least.
You get the similarities: sword fighting, magic, losing retrievable stuff when dying, secrets, stats increases etc. But Tunic does most in a different way. DD has different weapons and magic more accessible incorporated into the gameplay whereas Tunic only has sword&shield, a few magic attacks and a few (expensive) consumables which can be also find in chests spread around the world. The fighting feels different as you have stamina, refillable potions (amount upgradeable via flask pieces akin to heartcontainers in Zelda), a shield (again Zelda! but more) and what I would call slowish input chains (the dodge itself is good but if you are in a sword combo and want to immediately dodge an attack it takes a moment, which makes it harder to perform as fast as required). The fighting is imo not the main selling point in Tunic. It is serviceable and fun but when faced with one of the few bosses you notice it is not as responsive as it could be. I think it is like this on purpose though. The first three bosses I fought were doable. Not easy not hard. But then I got to a boss fight which took me about an hour. I really felt the disadvantage of the battle system. However when I finally conquered that fight I felt amazing. I used most of my repertoire and was so happy when the boss was beaten.

And this feeling kind of translates into the puzzle design. When you feel the sensation of overcoming/understanding a puzzle on your own you truly get Tunic. The combination of perspective, the manual and the world really left an impression on me. I keep this short as I don‚Äôt want to spoil anything puzzle related, but let me say it just once more, the puzzles and exploration for hidden treasures are awesome.

Last but not least the presentation: Tunic looks great, its style is quite charming and who would not like that cute fox? All in all I like the visual design (looks, perspective etc.). It works great with the overall gameplay as well. Funny fact when you switch to the game manual the actual in-game scene transforms into an old 8-bit CRT screen behind the manual, such a neat idea.
Every of the many different areas have a unique look and sound. The tracks fit to the various areas and feel kind of adventurous and magical from time to time and I liked listening to the brilliantly composed score and sound design. The presentation is just another big part where you feel the heart of the developers.

I had so much fun playing Tunic and I wish there are were a lot more games like this to come. But alas there are not. And therefore I‚Äôll remember Tunic as a very special game for a long time.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a game filled with wonderful ideas.

I just wish they were executed better.

Overall I would say this game is less than the sum of its parts. There's not much I can say without spoilers, so I'll just say that this game tries to have exploration like a Zelda game, combat like a Souls game, and complex puzzles. In my opinion this game almost succeeds at having good combat, but is otherwise not worth your time or money.

Spoilers Ahead:
Tunic is a game that wants to be 3 games at once:
- An isometric souls-like
- An exploration heavy action-adventure game.
- A complex observation-based puzzle game

If any (or all) of these seem appealing to you, I have bad news, Tunic doesn't really do any of them particularly well.

Let's break them down in order of appearance.

Tunic the exploration game:
This is Tunic's gameplay roughly 20% of the time.
Tunic's approach to exploration consists of the same trick played over and over again: you can't see something because it's blocked by objects in the foreground. That's it, you explore to find chests, these chests can contain currency, consumable items, upgrades, or other items, but they're basically all hidden the the same manner.
Prepare yourself for the exciting gameplay of moving your character into places you can't see, and praying that a button prompt appears to inform you that there's something there (usually consumable items, sometimes upgrades, and on rare occasions: the critical path.)
The game is also surprisingly narrow in where it will allow you to explore. Access to specific items limits your access to most areas until relatively late, making your journey through Tunic a mostly linear one.
The size of the world relative to your movement speed also makes backtracking take forever. The fast-travel that does exist is placed exclusively in awkward locations that make backtracking with them just as much of a slog as not using them.

Exploration is about wandering and discovering things you didn't expect, Tunic's world never really surprised me, it just sort of annoyed and bored me a bit, so I can't recommend Tunic as an exploration game.

Tunic the Souls-like:
This is Tunic's gameplay roughly 50% of the time.
Tunic's core combat system isn't bad, you have movement, melee attacks, ranged/magic attacks, a dodge roll, and a lock-on. None of these things are particularly problematic, however the roll and lock-on both have notable issues that become more annoying as the game's difficulty increases.
The roll:
- Its distance is too long. (using it to dodge attacks almost always puts you too far away from most enemies to effectively counter or otherwise benefit from having dodged the attack.)
- Its unreliable. (from my testing, it appears that some attacks from specific enemies ignore the roll's iframes, I'm not sure if this is a bug or not, but either way it was still very annoying to deal with.)
The lock-on:
- Bad target prioritization. (the lock-on almost always seems to target the enemy furthest from the player instead of the closest, this makes the lock-on a massive liability in situations where you need to deal with multiple enemies fast.)
- Causes camera problems. (In some arenas and particularly in boss fights, the lock-on will rotate the camera around the stage to keep the player and enemies in frame, this results in the camera almost constantly spinning around during some fights. This adds significant difficulty due to all player inputs being relative to the camera (eg. dodging the wrong way because the camera turned as your were pressing the dodge button).)

These issues aren't deal breakers, but they aren't really good enough for me to be confident recommending Tunic as a souls-like. (as a brief aside, if you want a game that does these exact things but better, play Death's Door)

Tunic the puzzle game:
This is Tunic's gameplay for roughly the last 30% of the game.
This is, in my opinion, where the source of Tunic's problems stem from.
There is, in essence only 2 kinds of puzzles in Tunic:
- Deciphering the manual.
- "Holy Cross" puzzles

The manual:
Easily the better of the game's 2 types of puzzles.
The manual is a collectible in the game, you gather its pages and can learn new tricks about how to play the game and get hints on how to progress by deciphering its contents. Of course, I hope you enjoy cryptography, because it's all written in an admittedly pretty complex symbol substitution cipher. I personally enjoyed this part, but I'm not sure I'd really call it a "puzzle" in the same way that a game like Portal has "puzzles", I'd say it's really more of an activity/worksheet you're going to have to do if you want to finish the game. (almost all tooltips are also written in this language, so if you're just playing casually but you want to know what any of your upgrades do, you'll also need to decipher the symbols.)
(If you want a game that's just this, but an actual game, play Chants of Sennaar.)

The "Holy Cross" puzzles:
Ok, this is where the game really lost my sympathy.
Basically, there are patterns made of arrangements of straight lines hidden throughout the game. If you find these lines, and you input the sequence of lines as a series of directional button presses on either the d-pad (controller) or arrow keys (keyboard) you can open paths or get a little fairy. You'd think it'd be simple, but it's not. There's no feedback, minimal explanation, and the only way to know if you've done it right is to have already done it perfectly (because then the door is already open or the fairy chest spawns).

This would be bad enough on it's own, until you realize what you need to get the game's true ending.

For the game's true ending, you need to input something called "The Golden Path." it's a very specific sequence of inputs, more specifically, its a large arrangement of all of the holy cross puzzles combined together.

The Golden Path is a sequence of 100 consecutive directional inputs, which you must make with no errors, and no information or feedback from the game at all (aside from receiving the reward after completing it perfectly).
Now, I'm not going to say these aren't puzzles, I'd say they are, but I'd also say they're far more tedious and repetitive than fun, so no, I cannot recommend Tunic as a puzzle game.

Overall, I did not enjoy my time with Tunic, and it's higher-than-average price for a game of its size makes it impossible for me to recommend. If you read this and still want to buy it, you do you, but maybe at least wait until it's on sale?\t\t\t\t\t
