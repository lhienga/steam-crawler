\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTUNIC is one of the most awesome games I've ever played, and there's a LOT of stuff I'd like to talk about. However, I wont actually say much in this review at all, because TUNIC is a game that's best experienced without looking up anything about it beforehand. So, I'll be talking very "generally" about the game, rather than getting into specific aspects of it.

Let's get the obvious out of the way: The game, as you could probably tell just by looking at the store page, takes a lot of inspiration from The Legend Of Zelda, and has a similar format (Explore the world, enter dungeons, collect items, etc.), so if you're a fan of that series, you'll probably love TUNIC. However, there are other sources of inspiration that become apparent during gameplay. The combat is much more difficult than in most Legend Of Zelda games, and alongside the savepoint/enemy respawn mechanics, takes more inspiration from Dark Souls. The game is also filled with puzzles and secrets, with puzzle-solving becoming a bigger element the more you progress. I've not played Fez, but I've heard it's similar to it in that regard, and was one of the inspirations for TUNIC. With that being said, despite the heavy inspiration, the game is still very unique, and still has a strong Identity of its own!

The visuals in this game are gorgeous. It has a cartoony aesthetic combined with fantastic lighting. While you might think the artstyle might hinder it from showing detail or making things look all that great due to its simplicity, you'd be wrong! Aesthetic, themes, and style are all things this game does an amazing job with.

And tying into all that, besides visuals, there's also the audio aspect. From the music to the sound effects, this game is great to listen to. While the game does have intense tracks for more intense moments and bossfights in the game (and other equally-fitting music for other moments throughout), they take a slightly different approach than The Legend Of Zelda does for many of it's overworld tracks: Rather than sounding super energetic, a lot of the games tracks sound more relaxing and ethereal, giving the game a bit of a calmer vibe to it despite still being an action-packed and adventure-filled game. You may think this would come across as conflicting, but considering the amount of time you'll spend exploring after defeating the enemies in an overworld area vs. the amount of time the fight takes itself, the beautiful music ends up pairing with the amazing visuals very well. Overall, an incredible soundtrack that makes the game stand out even more than it already does!

There's often been times when writing this review where I wanted to get into more detail, or talk about how amazing certain gameplay elements are or how great the story is, but I really, REALLY think that you should just get the game and see it all for yourself! If you're still on the fence at this point, get off the fence and get this game! And stop reading stuff about it, you don't want to spoil yourself! I mean it!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t9.5/10

Tunic is a masterpiece, plain and simple. For those looking for a TL;DR answer: This game presents as a zelda-like in gamplay and combat, but the puzzles and secrets of the game require a very intuitive and perceptive mind. You have to see patterns that the game will eventually show you, and solve puzzles that you never thought possible.

To start: Tunic is far and wide a puzzle game first, action-adventure second. The puzzles in this game are grand and wide-spread, yet simple at the same time. They can be as tame as discovering a pattern and following it, to having to see said pattern in an enemy's movements and following those directions instead. It is more in depth than anything I have ever seen, and I cannot understate how brilliant the devs were in making this game.

The game presents the mechanics and puzzles in this game through a very unique method involving an in-game Instruction Manual that you piece together with pages you find throughout your journey. This journal is your life. It will teach you core gameplay mechanics, tell you how to do things you didn't know you could do, it will be maps for various areas, it will be reminders of things you missed. It will be the keeper of all the secrets and riddles in the game, and it is absolutely astonishing how well it was implimented. I cannot understate this game's amazing structure. It's just so cool.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game has a specific target audience. That target audience is people who grew up playing NES Legend of Zelda in Japanese who only spoke/read English. They could not understand the words but made sense of things through pictures and context and got through the game anyways. They enjoyed tough battles and hidden secrets, either by stumbling upon them or scouring the game for clues. They liked to reconstruct the story of the game purely from the in-game and game manual imagery.

I am not that target audience. I love The Legend of Zelda dearly, but I love it for the story and character dialogue and other text I can read, and for the approachable combat which can be challenging but not too hard aside from major/endgame bosses. In TUNIC after a while I turned the difficulty down to "easy" and eventually to "no dying" mode. After finishing TUNIC I looked up what the story was, because I knew other people would have translated the writings, but I didn't experience much story until then.

TUNIC is visually beautiful and I really wanted to like it because it's my favorite genre of game, but it just didn't have the things that make me love the genre.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRest assured, Tunic is an excellent game. It touches on so many classic adventure games from the past and builds on those foundations to make a rewarding, fun jaunt that's just long enough to be satisfying, but just short enough to not be a huge time investment. There are a few details that could occasionally get frustrating - combat can at times feel less than graceful, especially against a punishing boss battle, not being able to pause gameplay while accessing the inventory feels just dumb (I virtually never used any of my combat items due to this, really don't understand the creative choice here), not knowing what the items do could be a fun challenge...or a recipe for players to simply not use things due to no indication as to what they do, and the puzzles in the last portion of the game sort of verge from challenging into unfun simply due to feeling like a grind (DO NOT USE D PAD, USE YOUR COMPUTER ARROWS!!!), but through all this the play through was a joy and I would absolutely suggest playing it. A quality game leads you to find it's few flaws, and though I would have enjoyed a few details done differently those come down more to creative choices than things that depreciate gameplay.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOn the surface, this game looks like a well-executed, cutesy take on a Zelda-like action adventure game, and it definitely does have that aspect, and does it well, so if that's all you're looking for, then great, you'll find it in TUNIC! But if you also like the idea of uncovering the secrets of a deep, mysterious world using clues that you slowly put together, then this game has A LOT more to offer you.

Without getting too much into spoiler territory, TUNIC has several levels of puzzles/secrets to discover, and the great thing is that it's completely up to you, the player, how far you dig. For less puzzle-savvy players like myself, there's a major mystery that was *just* challenging enough for me to solve with a nice payoff, but for secret-seekers that like more difficult/esoteric puzzles like the kind you find in Noita, then you're in for a treat.

TL;DR: If you like action adventure games like Zelda, you'll like this game. If you like mystery/puzzle games like Outer Wilds or the Witness, then you'll also like this game.\t\t\t\t\t
