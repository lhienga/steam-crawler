\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOne of my favorite games of all time. TUNIC is super fun and cute with a lot of memorable 'aha' moments. The puzzles are all really well made and properly communicated. The entire time I was playing I wanted to see what would happen next. Over the course of game you'll find pages of the game's instruction manual teaching you how it's played and offering useful information. It's a really cool idea and the manual is so well made with beautiful illustrations and an insane amount of secrets. The game is a perfect blend of exploration, puzzle solving, and combat. I think it would be useful to talk about each of these components in depth.

Exploration
This part is about the exploration. If you’ve done a good enough job to avoid any spoilers, then upon starting the game everything is very mysterious and you’re basically just doing the only things that you’re able to. You don’t have a lot of options about where you want to go or what you want to do, but that’s okay because you’re still figuring out how the game works. Over time, the area that you can explore and the speed at which you can do so improve by exploring, fighting, and solving puzzles. The world is just big enough to feel daunting and exciting, but not too big that it’s tedious to explore.

Puzzles
Now about the puzzles, which are my favorite part. As soon as you start the game, you're constantly finding weird new pieces to puzzles that you're unsure how they work / didn't know existed. Often, you'll slowly piece together how some of the puzzle’s rules might work until you find the solution to how they function in the manual. Not all the puzzles are solved in the same way, so you're constantly finding new information. There's different types of puzzles that all have unique rules. In function it works like this:

There's a new type of puzzle that is present in the world obstructing your progress. Over time you'll find pieces of information in the manual, but not enough to figure out how to solve the puzzle type in entirety. You can guess the general gist, but enough to actually solve anything yet. Then suddenly after a big fight or cool new area you find a manual piece that reveals exactly how the puzzle works! Now you must remember where you found those individual puzzles so that you can attempt to solve them. Each of the individual puzzles will have a cool twist that make them still fun to solve even after understanding how the rules work.

There’s also a LOT to work on with the puzzles. The number of secrets in this game is unbelievable, they’re basically everywhere. There’s also categories of puzzles that range from easy to communal, and necessary to not even in the game. You can just solve the easy ones to beat the game if you’d like or take on the harder ones although they’re not necessary. I recommend that you try to solve as many secrets and puzzles as you can and then just look up the rest. Eventually the difficulty of the puzzles increases beyond the scope of what a single person can achieve.

Combat
Puzzles aren't the only way you make progress, however. Sometimes there's just an elaborate dungeon that you must explore and get to the end for a new weapon or tool. The combat has always taken a backseat to the puzzles in my opinion, but it's never boring. Enemies are basically just an obstacle. Fortunately, they're simple enough to deal with that I didn't mind fighting them, but the strategy was deep enough that I wasn't bored. It's exactly the kind of combat you want from a game that isn't solely focused on combat. You can attack, roll, and block along with some other items and consumables to mix things up. It’s more about your opponent’s attack patterns that makes the combat enjoyable.

Possible Downsides
However, there are some things that you should know if you’re considering buying this game. I feel there are two main things that will turn a lot of people away and it’s best that you know beforehand. Exploring an isometric world might be annoying and this game really isn’t about the combat.

A lot of emphasis has been put on exploring an isometric world, which includes rubbing your face on areas that are obstructed by the players perspective. Often, there are optional treasure chests that are hidden behind your perspective, but sometimes it’s a really important area that is easy to miss. It’s the game’s job to try to communicate to the player that there could be something hiding out of view, and it usually does a good enough job. It’s simply another type of puzzle - trying to find hidden areas. I thought it was a lot of fun, but I can easily see how this would be a turn off to some. If this sounds frustrating, then this game may not be for you.

If you’re a fan of action type games and you’re looking to buy this because it has the tag ‘Souls-like’, that’s kind of deceptive. This game has light combat elements. There are only a handful of challenging fights in the game but they’re simple once you figure out their attack patterns. It’s difficult enough to be fun, but it’s not really the focus of the game. If you like combat more than puzzles in games, just be cautious about playing past two hours for the refund.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tMy favourite game of the past 10 years, honestly. It's so cute and every movement, nudge and effect is so satisfying (like when a banana falls perfectly into a trash can, that kind of thing). That's the exterior though and what's underneath is an incredible puzzle game which really expects you to go above and beyond in deductive reasoning and note taking to unlock everything. I'm always pretty disappointed when puzzle games are too easy, too straightforward and puzzles too narrow in scope. For Tunic, I have a whole folder dedicated to sketches, screenshots, decoding cyphers, tentative possible connections between distant parts of the game, etc...

That might sound like a chore and maybe it would be, if it weren't so well crafted and fair the whole way through. I've put this game on hold for months at a time and come back to it, only to crack yet another puzzle, which opens up more avenues. I suppose you could finish this game in one day if you refer to a guide online, but everything you need to know is there in the game - looking up answers will probably ruin the whole game. Honestly don't want to say any more, because I stumbled upon this game completely blind and I'm positive the best way to experience it is to go in not knowing anything about it.

Whole-heartedly recommend!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA fun adventure of both zelda-like play and deciphering the clues of the world. The more you learn the more interesting it gets. Initially I really didn't like the combat system but after getting used to it I think it's pretty good, so if this frustrates you too in the beginning, at least wait until you get the shield.
Some criticism:
- The puzzles can be very difficult. I had to look up some hints online.
- The game is split up where the first 2/3 is basically what you see in the trailer and the final 1/3 is just staring at the manual figuring out the meaning of it all. Both are fun, but I wish the game were paced such that both types of activities were interspersed.
- The ending (true ending) is a bit unsatisfying, as there is a big part of the story that is still completely unresolved. Sure, you accomplished breaking the cycle of ruin seekers, but I thought the whole corrupted foxes being sealed into sarcophagi thing, which seemed just as important story-wise or at least lore-wise, would be resolved too but it just wasn't.\t\t\t\t\t
