\r\n\t\t\t\t\t5/10
It's hard to recommend the game but it's "probably" worth playing. Most everything about the game is incredible and reminds you of those original Zelda games you played as a kid. The exploration is great when paired with the in game guide manual you piece together over time and the sound track to go along with.

The game falls very short in terms of combat. If combat is your main thing, I would not play this. There was very little to no effort put into the combat to make it a worth while experience. It's very disappointing due to the fact the game excels at every other aspect so well.

The game itself took me about 10 hours to beat, so it's not a very long game either. As with any game, I'm sure there is replay-ability, but I have little to no desire to replay it with how atrocious the combat is. I do look forward to the idea of a sequel but I hope there was a lot learned in regards to fixing the combat.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAn outstanding and excellent 3D Isometric game about a little fox on a grand adventure. Its wonderfully colorful yet simplistically geometrical art style oozes with the essence of early 90's S.N.E.S. titles like The Legend of Zelda: A Link to the Past and Star Fox. This love of classic video games extends to its gameplay being similar to the original Legend of Zelda, with its many secrets and a manual that when pieced together, gives you the knowledge you need to uncover those secrets. The only way to truly play this game is to go in blind as much of what makes this experience so charming is encapsulating the feel that N.E.S. era games had when the internet was barely a conception, in my time playing I only used the internet for if I got stuck on one of the games many challenging puzzles, some of which I have never seen so expertly executed in a game to date. Another great aspect of this game is its challenging bosses, with a death/bonfire system reminiscent to From Softwares Dark Souls franchise. This games soundtrack is otherworldly with a distinct sound I haven't heard in any other game, from relaxing smooth tunes to energetic yet alien battle songs to eerie melodies, this game has it all. If you plan on getting this game be prepared for a challenge and an experience you will never forget, just don't look up anything on the game unless you absolutely positively need the help. I hope this was a helpful review and as one last goodbye let me say this, "To all who seek to follow the golden path, look to the holy cross for guidance".\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTUNIC wasn't as hard as I was led to believe (being a LA-MULANA veteran might have helped here) but it certainly wasn't a walk in the park either and kept me challenged for its whole course.

Basically, you're thrown in a fantasy world you know nothing about and you have to understand how to engage with it, you will be helped by a cryptic but partly translated booklet that you will be recovering page by page with just enough information about how to progress and find a few more pages that may cue you further.
The world is pretty open from the get go but without the needed knowledge, a lot of places will be out of reach, some curiosity might help getting in some place earlier than intended but it probably won't carry you very far without the important information contained in the booklet.

As such, the booklet and its interpretation is a central piece of TUNIC, and I loved how it sometimes conceal important information that may help recontextualise a lot of things you've already seen, there must have been two or three BIG realisations that completely changed how I looked at the game and made me scour many places or the booklet again.
Understanding and deciphering the booklet was a real treat and nostalgic in a way.

Shootout for the soundtrack too, I started noticing how good it is when I got in the first underground area and it didn't let me down once until the end of the game, good enough to get me to stop playing at times and enjoy the ambiances for a while.

I loved TUNIC and I can't recommend it enough\t\t\t\t\t
