\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGorgeously visualized, wonderfully labyrinthine, and a joyously infuriating game to play. There's so much packed into this little game that you can't help but enjoy the challenge. The homage to Zelda cannot be overstated - these devs loved that classic. Secrets, bombs, sword swinging action, overworld maps, freedom to go and die where you will. This is how you take a tried and true game concept and give it modern life.

The nature of the open world - like the Zelda of old - means you can go to places that'll get you killed quickly. It also means that sometimes finding the next step for progress can be a little confusing. And the boss battles aren't just hack-and-slash. Tactics, tricks, and forethought makes this a hard but conquerable challenge.

Pros:
- Animations are smooth and beautiful.
- So. Many. PUZZLES! So much to discover.
- Game mechanics are well thought out.
- Story is layered and deceptively cute.

Cons:
- Sometimes the isometric view makes it difficult to see certain enemies/items/paths.

Is this a long game? No, not particularly. But it's fun, and well worth the price. Here's hoping the devs invite us to return to the world of TUNIC in the future.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game feels so tailor-made for me so I just have to write a gushing review for it! It's a combination of incredibly smart game design which relies on minimal hand-holding and rewards your curiosity; puzzles that give you that 'AHA!' moment and unlock a whole new way of looking at the world once they click; isometric action combat that feels just challenging enough to keep you on your toes but never making you feel like the task is impossible and you aren't going to progress further; beautiful art direction and compelling world-building through visual storytelling and last but not least, a cute af protagonist. If you are like me and all of that sounds exactly up your alley, then I highly recommend TUNIC!

Very quickly into the game, TUNIC teaches you to be observant. You will not be able to progress unless you are curious and are looking for visual clues at all times. The most unique mechanic that TUNIC has is a game manual which can be accessed at any time and contains everything you will need to beat the game and solve even its hardest puzzles. It's very much inspired by actual retro game manuals and the aesthetic of it is *chef's kiss*. The twist is that the manual pages are written in a foreign language with only some english sprinkled around to guide you. You are also able to find only certain pages at a time so you are forced to solve as much as possible with as little as you have. You aren't always sure if you already have the information to engage with what you're finding so that fuels your curiosity even more.

How much CAN i do with what little I know?

One of the smartest parts of TUNIC's design is how both your discoveries in the world and your reading of the manual pages is teaching you to be observant. You will always be rewarded for using your eyes in this game. As you progress the game will also reveal hidden paths & ladders that have been there all along, you just didn't know it but you could've seen had you been looking closer. You will find little scribbles on maps in the manual, only to discover secret chests/paths/etc. in the world. It's an extremely smart loop of both secrets in the manual and smart level design that just constantly fuels your sense of wonder, curiosity and gives you a dopamine rush every time you figure out any of its secrets. I never found myself stuck or lost simply by keeping an eye out.

Fair bit of advice though, go in as blind as possible (don't even watch the trailer/video on steam as it gives a fair bit away that would otherwise feel more rewarding to discover on your own). I went in knowing very little about the game except that it was a game all about discovery, had a cool retro game manual mechanic and that apparently had one big compelling puzzle (which I'll circle back to), that's about it! This is the type of game where you are doing yourself a huge disservice if you spoil yourself so my biggest advice is: DON'T LOOK UP ANYTHING, DON'T LOOK UP SOLUTIONS EITHER! All I'll say is if you're stuck/lost: "When in doubtie, game manual-ie outie". The game is very smart in the sense that at all times you are given everything you need to progress further, the manual pages are very selectively given to you at certain times to give you all the knowledge you need (so a hint is to always look at what page you found last and ask yourself why).

And knowledge really is the biggest "power" you will be given. I was always so excited to receive a new page of the manual and analyze it as much as possible with my current knowledge of the game. Also worth knowing is that you do not need to decipher the manual language to beat the game with the good ending. You also do not need to understand every puzzle/scribbly note in the manual to beat it either. I completed some post-game/side puzzles that I got through using the hints in the manual (that do indeed make you feel like a genius), but there is tons in there just for the pure love of hard af puzzles. There is a whole rabbit hole of secret puzzles and insanity that the community has unearthed online that is worth looking into only AFTER you finish the game. But safe to say, you only need to understand some of the manual puzzles/hints to finish the game and that is always connected to a repeating phrase/word, a whole page spread, etc. The game manual is very clear as to what's important and much more subtle about the extra fun notes/small hints to bonus puzzles.

The game also has some awesome boss fights that have the difficulty fine tuned to challenge you but never make you despair and stop playing. I will say though, some of the boss hit boxes could be finicky and your attacks don't always hit despite not being physically possible to get closer to the boss/weak point. Leading up to each of the boss encounters there is so much visual storytelling and world building as you go from zone to zone, each weirder than the last. I was constantly at awe and curious about why this world is the way it is. There are few moments of "revelations" as well that are purely visual/environment storytelling that had me asking so many questions and made me very invested in the lore of the world. As a huge sucker for cryptic visually driven storytelling that doesn't give you all the answers but forces you to theorize, this game scratched just the right itch!

Lastly, I want to talk about the "last" puzzle and the ending, but do not spoil yourself if you haven't figured it out yet as it's by far the most satisfying part of the game to figure out on your own. The most amazing aspect of the Mountain Door puzzle is that it all comes back down to everything the game has been teaching you so far: be observant, be curious, question everything that sticks out, the answers are always right in front of you. The moment you notice the golden path has been right there the whole time, the feeling is absolutely amazing. It got me busting out the pen and paper and drawing it all out and I kind of love that the game required me to do that! I can't remember the last time I had to do something like that and it felt very rewarding. Inputting the code though felt more difficult than the boss fights lol, but hearing the little ding and finding the first page of the manual with the dev note...what a fantastic feeling you could only ever experience once! The knowledge you end up gifting to the Heir to get the good ending being the game manual you analyzed and learned from in order to complete it is just such a wonderful way to bring things full circle. Definitely a gaming experience that will stick with me for awhile!
I will say though, the puzzles/ world secrets in this game definitely lean towards the visual pattern-breaking observation kind of thinking and if that is not how your brain works this game's puzzle hints may not make a whole lot of sense to you. My brain clicks with that type of thinking and puzzle solving very well so it was all immensely satisfying throughout the game!

TL;DR Amazing game, fantastic world, joyous game manual, smart puzzles that make brain go brrr, brb going to buy the fox plushie now\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhen you're looking at the screenshots on the storefront, Tunic looks like a cute and friendly Zelda clone. When you start playing Tunic, you begin to get the impression that it's actually a fairly punishing Dark Souls clone. And then, once you get into Tunic's late game, it just turns into something else entirely. It's hard to properly review Tunic without wading into spoiler territory, but what I can tell you is that there's people on both sides of the fence who either love or hate Tunic for the exact same reasons. If you ask me, I think it's worth playing if you have the right mindset.

I'll start by saying that Tunic is designed to be played blindly, and you'll ruin the experience the moment you look up any sort of guide whatsoever. There's no tutorial or even any real sense of direction at first, with the game even going as far as to force you to find pages of the manual on your own. Some people drop Tunic within the first few minutes because waaaaah, there's no waypoint for me to follow and no mission objective to tell me what to do and there's no multiplayerrrr and that's perfectly fine because Tunic is made for people that actually enjoy it when a game assumes that they're an intelligent human being. Figuring things out by trial and error is, at least for me, refreshing and rewarding. I like it TO A POINT, a point which Tunic admittedly does reach towards the end of the game, but it's still good overall. I was able to at least beat the game without the use of a guide, but I DID have to go back and use a guide to actually...beat the game.

Nature of the gameplay aside, Tunic has a lot of other things going for it. The colorful and minimalistic graphics get the job done, and the soundtrack is phenomenal. Tunic is certainly unique, and even though it draws inspiration heavily from Zelda and Dark Souls, it's able to use those elements as a springboard to become its own thing rather than being a simple clone. If you're okay with being frustrated by design, Tunic is an ultimately rewarding experience even if it has the occasional brief misstep along the way. I say it's worth a look if ya want something fresh!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTunic is a great isometric action-adventure game, it's a bit like a 2D Zelda and Souls-like game combined. I almost think this is better than any 2D Zelda game I've played and I've played them all.
5/5 for a 2D action-adventure game.

It has more of a a focus on combat and the enemies re-spawn when you rest, sort of like a souls game.
The puzzles are a bit different from the Zelda series, most of them don't require an item, none of them are pushblock and you're only forced to defeat a room of enemies a couple times besides bosses.
Most of the puzzles in the game can be solved right away, but you won't know how till later after you have learned how to do it.

In the game you collect pages to an instruction manual, some pages show you information like: the story, how play the game, maps (with your location), give you hints to secrets, or show where to go.

I would REALLY recommend playing this game blind since figuring out how the game works from the instruction manual is one of the best mechanics in the game and the funnest part of the game, almost every page you pick up gives you helpful information or makes you say "oh wait, I could do that?!".\t\t\t\t\t

For a bit of history, this is the second time I purchase the game. I purchased it at launch and found it very difficult and too punitive for me, and the elusiveness of the world in general made me struggle to know what to do, so I got it refunded at the time.

I decided to give it another try later following some really good reviews from friends. Really got into it at that point. I feel like I just took another route that was way smoother for me (something I should have done in the first place seeing I was having difficulty).

Overall I really really recommend the game, I had such a blast playing it this time and finished it shortly after starting it because I couldn't stop playing, really. The fights are tough but fair, if you don't like that though, there are *amazing* accessibility options to make the game easier for you (which is absolutely valid). Some bosses took me a fair few tries, I didn't think I could do it but I did, and it made me very proud of myself, in a Souls-like fashion. The exploration is amazing. The elusiveness has its charm, I think some of the mysterious puzzles maaaaybe went a bit too far in that sense, but they did an amazing job overall.\t\t\t\t\t
