\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs a kid I often played games in languages other than my native language. It turns regular games into a challenge where you have to figure out every new mechanics yourself because you only understand a few words in the explanation. Tunic is as nostalgic about this as I am. It's immensely cryptic. Sometimes you come across things that seem multiple levels above your understanding. Sometimes it clicks and you see something that you've seen a hundred times in a new light. The constant fighting to understand the game makes it really immersive. At the same time it is still fair the player, rather than asking you to deal with too many questions at the same time.

My one issue was that the bosses are quite difficult. Tunic borrows heavily from Dark Souls, so it seems to tell you to "git gud" when fighting against the bosses. It felt very much like a test of dexterity instead of puzzle solving. This then carried over to how I played the rest of the game to some degree, making me less interested in the puzzles on offer. It seems you're not really intended to learn perfect dodge rolls in the boss battles, rather there are some items or cards that if used the right way make the bosses much easier.

That aside, it's an amazing mix of zelda, puzzle games and a bit of dark souls. One of those rare games that I'll still be thinking about in years.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA very cool game strongly inspired by early zelda, plus some souls-like elements. The main gimmick is very original and had me thinking about puzzles and game elements in ways no other game has ever made me think (plus a healthy dose of nostalgia for flipping through the manuals that used to come with games). Some of the bosses (and even some enemies) can be very challenging before you figure out their timing, so if you're not great at timing dodges, you may struggle at points, but defeating them is *very* satisfying. The game is also phenomenal at keeping you on your toes just when you're starting to get confident, which can result in a spiky difficulty curve at times, but which keeps the game interesting throughout its entire playtime.

Tunic provides a strong feeling of exploration and discovery, and reminded me a lot of Hyper Light Drifter, in that way. Plus, the game is full of subtle mechanics and interactions that you can find through discovery and experimentation, which are very fun to find! There are a some things you have to earn by solving puzzles which you may not recognize as even *being* puzzles at first, and some of the puzzles are *extremely* hard (I ended up filling a few notebook pages trying to solve a some of them!) which is a huge plus for me. Don't feel bad about having to look things up eventually-- I did, once I'd gotten as far as I could without looking things up.

Plus, the game is visually beautiful and the soundtrack is *extremely* good. Overall, a strong recommend.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTUNIC is a charming yet deceptively difficult action adventure game.

At first, it seems too easy. Then the difficulty ramps up like crazy, and as you die over and over again, it seems impossible. But once you unlock some upgrades things start to feel more fair.

Using camera perspective to create hidden pathways is a unique and delightful twist, at first. But the fact that key areas of the game are also hidden, is bad game design! It makes you paranoid and forces you to walk around the edges of every screen.

Boss fights were fun. Navigating the map is confusing. Spent way too much time backtracking. The combat system was OK even with occasional targeting mishaps, and getting stuck on level geometry at the worst times.

I enjoyed the final half of the game, maxing out abilities, and defeating the final boss. However, the ending was severely lackluster. I'm going to ignore the "true" ending, which involves solving a bunch of cryptic puzzles. After spending a few hours solving some of the endgame puzzles, I decided it wasn't worth my time.

The manual was way too cryptic about key elements (bed) which derailed my progress significantly.

While TUNIC was fun for the most part, it's just far too cryptic and not rewarding enough to recommend to others.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDespite the aesthetic, the mechanics are more Souls-like than Zelda. The game wants you lost and confused as much as possible. You wake up on the beach without any context, and it never gets better. A significant component is dedicated to picking up pages of a game manual with hints on how to progress, but most of the manual and in-game text is written in code. Often the path to progress is a secret path obscured by the isometric view, and other times entire legs of the journey are dedicated to revealing that actually, you could have skipped that whole thing if you just happened to notice that one little passageway. Enemies that notice you will chase you forever, so if you don't find the one pickup that allows you a better chance of sneaking, you have to fight the entire gauntlet every time.

I quit after the point where the game turns you into a ghost, removes all your upgrades to where you can only take 2-3 hits and have to use three potions to heal back to full, then you have to fight through a cathedral where every monster that hits you reduces your maximum health. For a "love letter to Legend of Zelda" they seem to refer to their other beau an awful lot.\t\t\t\t\t
