\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRecommended – 9/10

I decided to pick up this game due to the unique graphics and the impression I got that this was some kind of old school Zelda with an updated look. And at first, it was just that, however I then realized that there was much more to this game.

Since there was nothing telling me what the story was, I began coming up with my own narrative on who my character was and what was my goal. As I continued to play, pieces of the story were revealed in different ways that started filling in gaps and kept driving me to continue. This eventually led me to discover many more secrets and puzzles within the game that would further flush out the story and help me beat the game.

The game’s combat is pretty simple and was easy to get used to. I do think there quite a bit of a difficulty curve once you get to a boss. I feel like the bosses almost force you to learn to do certain technique that help you do better in combat. However, I could never get how to perform a parry. I got it to work a few times, but it was highly unreliable and sometimes I felt like not being able to parry was making bosses more difficult than they needed to be. Another thing I found cumbersome about combat was trying to combine powers into new attacks. I would struggle a lot trying to get these combination attacks to work and wish it was simpler as these would probably have made boss fights easier as well.

Some of the puzzles in TUNIC can be tedious to solve and I can’t see this being something everyone would enjoy. I myself didn’t even realize that the in-game manual could be translated until I read about it online…then I saw the work involved and I was like…NOPE, I don’t have that kind of time. I do hope there is some effort to make a sequel though as I found this game to be pretty unique and would like to play more.

This game also has a new game+ mode that lets you go from the beginning of the game with most of your items and abilities from your previous playthrough. However, you can’t complete everything from the first playthrough in new game+.

Overall, I would recommend this game and I would suggest going into it blind as it is more fun that way.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTunic is an exercise into making cryptic nonsense enjoyable. Unlike a lot of games in this "cryptic puzzle" genre, such as La-Mulana and The Witness, Tunic cleverly embeds it's puzzle solving into the game by presenting it in a unique way; make the answers incredibly obvious, but completely obfuscate them.

Tunic is a Zelda-like exploration RPG where the major twist is that you have an in-game manual. Not just a "heres how to play the game" manual, but a full on guide, full of maps, secrets, and important details. The manual is written in a cipher language that you cannot understand, forcing the player to instead rely on visual cues and tiny clues hidden throughout the book to progress. This is, in a single word, enthralling. The game has quite literally every answer for every puzzle stuffed into this guide somewhere, making the act of even picking up a page exciting. Some of the puzzles in this game use the manual in fascinating and engrossing ways, and even outside of the puzzles the manual is used to excellent effect. You get foreshadowing, atmosphere, useful tips and practical advice from this thing, making it a constant companion in your journey. The integration of the manual into the puzzle design is nothing short of genius and it is easily Tunic's most successful idea.

Unfortunately, that's all of Tunic's qualities I can fully praise. Everything else is divisive. To sum it up quickly, Tunic slowly shifts from an exploration-based game to a puzzle-based one. While I personally liked this, quite a few people strongly disliked this shift, feeling as though the qualities they liked about the game were phased out for something else. I wouldn't call this false advertising, more like a shift in priorities. It honestly feels like all the meaningful exploration is just there to set up the puzzles, which can feel disheartening when you realise how repetitive some of the puzzles can be. Using the puzzle as a guidebook while exploring or figuring out where to go next is awesome, using it to do most of the lategame puzzles really isn't. Even though I liked the shift to the puzzle focus, I can't really say I enjoyed it more than the exploration of the first three quarters.

Still, I really freaking loved this game. The manual idea alone is ingenius, and all of the concepts surrounding the game and its cultivated mystique are executed perfectly. Even if Tunic didn't hit all the beats I wanted it to, and ultimately moved away from what I felt was a very strong design focus, I cannot deny that it succeeded in what it wanted to accomplish: inspiring genuine wonder and awe in a guy who's been playing games before he could walk.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOn the surface this looks like a typical Zelda-like, and in some ways it is very reminiscent of older Zelda titles like A Link to the Past. What makes this game stand out however isn't in it's similarities to Zelda games, but rather in it's differences.


Much like Outer Wilds, this is one of those games where I can't give you too much information without spoiling major aspects of the game. In fact, that is the very premise the game is built around. Despite the Zelda-like layout and Souls-like combat, the core of this game revolves around information. Everything in this game is written in an undecipherable wingding language, leaving the player guessing as to what signs mean throughout the world. More importantly though, as you go through the game, you'll find pages of the game's "instruction manual", being very reminiscent of the manuals in older NES/SNES titles. This manual is also mostly in this wingding language, forcing the player to decipher meanings from the pictures, other pages, and context clues. While you may think this sounds annoying upon reading this review, let me assure you that this is excellently pulled off, making you want to find these manual pages and keeping you thinking about what some enigmatic riddle could mean long after you've put the game down. When you do figure something new out, the "Ah-Ha!" moment is unparalleled and you feel like a genius.
The only time these manual puzzles become really over the top to solve is in the post-game for the "good" ending. I did not actually manage to solve all of these, but despite it, I never found it too frustrating to go on, and if anything makes me even more determined to eventually go back and solve that mystery.

Beyond that, I can tell you that while there aren't that many boss fights, they are quite enjoyable and visually pleasing. The exploration in this game is top notch. The items are very fun to use, and combat in general is fun. The skills you get as you go through the game, as well as how you get them, are also a blast. I will say, while the story is pretty decent, it's probably the weakest part of the game overall.

Truly this is a game I would recommend to nearly everyone. One of the best games in every genre it falls into.

EDIT:

Steam shows me playing this game only a few hours. This may be because I played it mostly in offline mode in a foreign country, but if I had to guess I probably put about 40-ish hours into it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTunic - 10/10

I only heard about this game because the OST was in my recommended videos. I thought the music was amazing so I bought the game, and I'm glad I did. This is an exploration based game in the vein of Zelda (main character even looks like a fox Link). The difficulty is perfect, definitely harder than normal, but not too much (except the final boss, who appropriately makes you want to rip your hair out). It has upgrades, collectables, and more secrets than the NSA. Remember when games used to have instruction manuals with neat art? Well this game has one integrated into the gameplay and lore. That was a nice nostalgic touch. It's kind of cryptic though because 95% of it is in a made up language, but I like that it doesn't hold your hand.

As good as the gameplay is, my favorite part of the game is its artistic direction, especially the music.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tReminds me of a cross between a Link to the Past and Fez. A key difference is that, although you still collect items to help you traverse the world, the true primary thing you collect is information about the game, which is learned via pages of the instruction manual scattered across the world. As a result, you should ABSOLUTELY go in blind if you don't want to spoil a lot of the information-based puzzles throughout the game. Puzzles are altogether not terribly difficult, and the game loooves hiding stuff just out of sight of the isometric view, so if strafing walls and shifting camera angles regularly pisses you off, this might sour your experience a bit. One thing to note is that the instruction manual, and nearly all text in game, is in a foreign script, so the primary "puzzle" is critically examining context clues you find in the manual and around you to understand what's going on and what to do. That said, the game still does a very good job directing you along the critical path without relying on you finding obscure secrets, so if you do want to just breeze through without searching for and finding every little hidden thing, there are plenty enough breadcrumbs for that. At worst, if you have slacked on finding pages, you may find yourself in an area without a map, but this still works find so long as you aren't trying to find every secret in the area.

From a mechanical standpoint, the game is a fair bit more challenging than Zelda, and combat has a "weightier" feel akin to Dark Souls that encourages you to precisely use I-frames in dodge-rolls to avoid damage or be heavily punished. Bosses in particular can be very challenging, and force you to really learn their patterns well before you have even a chance to win against them.

Overall, an absolutely fantastic experience and a remarkable example of good, hands-off game design that teaches the player by allowing them to learn themselves via subtle, precise guidance. If you are at all a fan of 2D Zeldas games, or just adventure games in general, this is a must play!\t\t\t\t\t
