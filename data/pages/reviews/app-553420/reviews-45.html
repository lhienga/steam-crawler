\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFantastic game, something I haven't ever really seen tried before, and it succeeds on all accounts.

The design of the earliest console games (NES, yes I know there's older) is basically alien to the modern gaming ecosystem and doesn't really exist anymore. Remakes and rereleases of old games which clean up the gameplay make it less and less likely for people to be remotely familiar with the oldest tropes, but Tunic sets out to remedy that. This game recreates the experience of playing a game in a language you don't really know and whose mechanics are never explained in-game anyway, with nothing but guess-and-check to guide you through and relying on others to fill in the gaps. It's impossible to replicate exactly, of course; many of the most distinct aspects of these early games (secrets scattered around a map haphazardly, dumbass useless NPCs, legitimately inscrutable gameplay mechanics) were due to time constraints or incompetence or both. Here, since they all have intention behind them, they're much gentler; secrets are hard, but deliberate, and there's no major quality of life issues with the gameplay. The NPCs are hilariously useless (and don't type in English), but if you translate them a few of them are actually useful and don't just sit around saying "GO TO THE NEXT ROOM" or whatever. The combat is probably the furthest away from older design out of anything in the game, and feels mostly modern though I think it works very well.

The game shines in its design philosophy; the vast majority of progress in the game rewards you with knowledge, and not tangible checkpoints. When you reach a new area, you find out that there was a ladder to the beginning all along; when you pass a crucial point you find out how to use overworld objects that were available and functional for the entire game. It's not items, but knowledge, that shapes your journey, and if you have the knowledge from the get-go you can travel all over the place in any order you like for the most part.

Gamers of yesteryear were quite fond of speculating about esoteric sequences of button-presses and their real or imagined impact on gameplay. It's obviously been quite popular to actually put these obscure secrets into games for more than a decade now (after all, most game devs grew up when it was popular). Tunic follows a similar philosophy, and while the idea behind the holy cross puzzles isn't new it's very well-executed, much better than most other games that attempt it. Very few of the puzzles are overly obscure and even then the game gives you ample hints.

It's interesting looking at reviews for this, since it seems like the intent of the game flew right over most players' heads at mach five. You know the industry is just straight up ♥♥♥♥♥♥ when the only frame of reference most players have is Dark Souls, but I guess the likelihood of most modern players having touched any of the games this is based off of is pretty low. I'm honestly not even sure most players of this game have played Fez, which goes a long way to explain the general attitude towards it. It's a shame, but for the game's intended audience, it doesn't get much better than this. 8/10

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI wouldn't recommend tunic if you are looking for a one for one legend of zelda experience. It is still at its heart a puzzle game. The entirety of the game is a puzzle including figuring out the manual, the control and the game mechanic itself.

The core combat experience is closer to that of souls borne series. Stamina management is even more important and you are heavily penalized for not properly conserving stamina. This does result in tense boss fight where you must react properly to bosses attack.

End game is all puzzle, one you beat the final boss that is it for combat challenge. A lot of the puzzle will require some form of pen and paper. Otherwise its quite engaging and satisfying to solve.

In summary:
Tunic is a pen and paper puzzle game with souls like combat, not recommended for those looking for a more casual experience.\t\t\t\t\t
