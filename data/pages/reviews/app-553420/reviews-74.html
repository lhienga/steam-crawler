\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOne of the best experiences I have ever played.

Impeccable level design, puzzle design, and simple but to the point combat. I am not usually a person to sing a game's praises (if anything I usually talk about the things I don't like in a game), but this game blew me away so much so that I felt compelled to leave my first (and probably last) review on a game ever.

For anyone on the fence about this game: Yes I know, it looks childish initially and the start is a bit slow since you don't yet have much in the way of equipment, but if you push through that early part it becomes an incredible experience with interesting locations to visit, puzzles to solve, treasure to loot, and bosses to fight. If you are into these things, I can't recommend it enough.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI was really disappointed by Tunic. It has an interesting premise with the manual, but it ends up being used in very underwhelming ways, mostly as a map and as a guide to very simple puzzles. There is basically only one kind of puzzle, so once you find it the first time there's no real thinking required, you just have to find where the solution is written.
The combat is a bit clunky and needlessly punishing, making some bosses annoying to fight.
There is a lot of slow backtracking which just pads the playtime.
The isometric camera makes navigating some parts of the map a bit difficult due to the lack of perspective
The story is very vague and can essentially be ignored.

On the positive side, the artstyle is very cute and the music is quite good.

I don't recommend buying it, especially for the relatively high price.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI do not usually write reviews for games, but this game certainly deserves attention. Tunic seems on the surface as a bright, colorful, indie title taking inspirations from Zelda-like series and a few small Souls mechanics in it. But, it does get quite a bit deeper and involved than most would initially think. It's difficult to tell anyone much about this game, as playing it and uncovering both lore and inner workings is a integral part of it's experience. In order to avoid spoiling anything, it's best to go into it blind.

To anyone who enjoys just getting lost in a game world, it's story, and uncovering secrets, I would fully recommend it. Much of the story you pick up from clues and small snippets in game and you largely construct the story and background yourself as you go, seeing more of that the game has to offer you. The soundtrack and audio design is excellent, as well as the games visual style. And others like myself, may find that this game reminds them of games they played when they were younger, and were able to enjoy with a sense of wonder, and become attached to it long after the game is complete. I still haven't found all it's secrets yet, and will happily continue trying without ever looking up a guide. TUNIC is the first game in a long time that I wish I could forget simply so that I may experience it for the first time all over again, and I cannot recommend it enough to anyone else.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn most games, progress is gated by key items and unlocking new abilities. In TUNIC, there's a roughly 70% chance that you've had said ability the entire time and the game just never bothered to tell you about it.

It's cryptic, arcane, even alien at times, but in a nostalgic sort of way that hearkens back to the old days where the walls were full of hidden passages and instruction booklets taught you more than the games themselves. And yet the game is constantly trickling you information, quietly nudging you forward without the usual neon signposts, and giving you the reins to solve it at your own pace and dig up whatever secrets and shortcuts you can find.

When I finish this game, I look forward to going back to the beginning and seeing how it changes when I know what's in store. I'm sure it'll be a very different game then.\t\t\t\t\t
