\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really enjoyed my time with Tunic. It manages to be a fresh blend of the games that inspired it (Souls, Zelda, etc.) without being defined by any of them. More importantly, it captures the feeling of being dropped into a new world without much to hold your hand and piecing it together as you go along. Combining that with the manual mechanic, the sense of discovery it manages to instill in you calls back to the days of playing classic games.

I didn't go into this game expecting much of a challenge, as I expected the focus would be more around exploration. The game starts out easy enough but it gradually ups the difficulty and manages to include some tough sections that require careful play. The boss roster, while not huge, is interesting enough and I genuinely enjoyed most of them but obviously not all. I don't want to give much away so I'll just leave it at that.

The game is not without its frustrations however, especially with how it handles backtracking, traversal and how it chooses to obscure/present information to you as you progress the game. It does this through its isometric perspective and the manual. Minor spoiler, but the game manual has two languages, one that's alien and plain old English. When this works, it works well, but I found myself frustrated with it at times, especially towards the end. The game's puzzle elements are mostly fun but some of the later puzzles are a bit too obtuse for my taste and I generally enjoy puzzle games. Thankfully though, they're mostly optional and the particularly difficult ones aren't needed to complete your manual (if you want to do that)

Technically, I didn't encounter many issues. Some minor bugs and a couple of framerate dips throughout my playthrough. The camera can be a little annoying because you can't control it during boss fights and it tends to spin sometimes which can be a bit disorienting.

If you're looking to get lost in a new world that rewards exploration and has a reasonable challenge to offer then Tunic might be for you. Just keep in mind that the game does have its flaws but it succeeds at what it's going for. I highly recommend not using guides at all till you get to the endgame, you will manage to figure it all out in time. Can't promise that for all the puzzles though ¯\\_(ツ)_/¯\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe notion of nostalgia is an interesting concept in creative media. The idea of generating appeal based not on the quality of your own work but for the emulation and or restoration of the quality of a different work. This becomes even stranger with the concept of anemoia: nostalgia for a time or a place one has never known. Which is what I felt playing Tunic.

Tunic draws much of its inspiration from earlier Legend of Zelda games such as ‘The Legend of Zelda’, ‘Zelda II, The adventure of link’ and ‘A link between worlds’. These were all games far beyond my time, by several decades even, yet I could still feel a connection and even a longing for such simpler games.

The name of the game for Tunic is simple: discovery. One of the most core aspects of the game is the process of rebuilding the game’s guidebook, allowing you to learn the context and function of many things you may have simply ignored or misunderstood. This results in the highest amount of “Oooooohhhhh thats how that works” situations of probably any game I’ve played.

Game play and direction is deliberately kept to a minimalist taste. combat abilities being three sword slashes and whatever items you have available. Puzzles are often a test of perspective or awareness rather than a say a riddle or contrived three part marathon. Most of the art assets could be recreated in programs like Blender relatively easily. Whilst the game could have come off as unchallenging or unimpressive, Tunic instead achieves a disciplined perfection of style. No hint misplaced, no encounter mistimed. It becomes down to the player to understand the situation as the pieces of the puzzle are commonly already in front of you.

Musically speaking Tunic steps lightly with more focus on ambience and leitmotifs to show continuation and similarity in areas that may not seem related, but later have their true natures revealed as time progresses. But the music does a type of heavy lifting often not mentioned in other reviews, and that is the what I like to call the Professor Layton factor: the music is simultaneously peaceful, distinctive but of a musical quality that it helps you think rather than distract.

Overall, I hold high praises for this game, though there is one factor that is worth mentioning. Tunic is not a game for everyone. There are a number of puzzles that are annoyingly cryptic, requiring either a guide, a true sense of puzzle games or a frustratingly long amount of time to succeed with. And it's with this that I think it's not exclusionary to say that not everyone will be able to beat this game unassisted.

And that's okay.

To me, the most important part of Tunic is enjoying the game for what it is in your way. If that means completely unassisted to you then that's what you should do. But if you have more fun with a guide by your side then that's also valid. Tunic is a game that preaches the sharing of wisdom and understanding what others left behind, and I think that's a fine stance to take for both this game and all puzzle games.

Final score - 80/100\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTunic has to be among the very best games of all times. Not necessarily in my personal opinion, but more so in its artistic quality, much like the greatest books, movies, or classical music of all time.

On the surface it's a cute little Zelda clone that borrows some aspects from Dark Souls, but this is literally just the tip of the iceberg. This game goes so deep, it's mind-boggling.
The gameplay, the structure, the pacing, the story, the world-building, the puzzles, the depth, the visuals, the presentation - every aspect of Tunic is sublime, and everything ties into each other.
The way the game unfolds before your eyes over the hours you spend with it is unreal. It’s evident that the people who created it poured their hearts and brains into this game.

Tunic is a masterpiece.\t\t\t\t\t

with that out of the way: i have never played a game that has stuck with me in the same way that Tunic has, and i'm not sure i will ever find a game like it again.

sure, there have been games i've played that i think about now and then and remember very fondly - Undertale and Ōkami come to mind immediately - but when i think about Tunic... it's an entirely deeper feeling altogether. it's a feeling of enchantment, of being awestruck by a world, of loving something you know that you'll never know fully. the mechanics are tight and the world feels huge and compelling and mysterious. the way it drips more pieces of knowledge with every manual page you discover is ingenious and utterly enamoring.

this game knows what it means to build a mysterious world, and not ruin it immediately by signposting every single answer to you directly. it's not afraid to let you miss things. it won't tell you where to go, because it knows that it is a game about finding your own way to learn what it wants from you. it won't teach you how to read its language unless you dedicate yourself to figuring it out. it revels in giving you the tools and finding your own way to use them to figure out its mysteries. it is the essence of being young and playing a new game for the first time without any guides and barely being able to read, getting lost in the experience and feeling the line between yourself and the game blur slightly.

Tunic will always, ALWAYS be a mystery to me. unanswered questions that were never made to be answered, in a world that was never made to be understood in its entirety. but that is one of its strongest strengths. for every mystery that you can solve, there remains even more that will never yield their answers to you. because Tunic is a game not just about uncovering answers to mysteries, it's about the beauty of those mysteries in the first place. it's a game that you can come back to and dwell in its unsolved mysteries and unexplained curiosities, where you can posit theories and pull meanings from, and it's more than happy to let me do so without telling me of any single correct answer that would squash its meaning down into one single thing.
