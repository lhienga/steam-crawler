\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is one of those games I wish I could forget so that I could experience it all over again, like Portal 2 and Outer Wilds.

Gameplay is Souls-like in a few ways (not as brutal, though), and items and level design are reminiscent of various Legend of Zelda games (especially the top-down ones), but the real hook for me was the puzzle-y nature of everything other than actual combat. Players that enjoyed Outer Wilds will quite likely enjoy how Tunic introduces you to new information and mechanics, as well as how you are encouraged to explore every corner. You will see things you don't understand yet, only to later discover a scrap of info that suddenly brings it all into focus. The "A-HA!" moments were good enough that I literally caught myself with my mouth hanging open at least two separate times.

My sole complaint at this point is that damned 4th trophy that I can't figure out (the Page 1 illustration)... but it's entirely possible the solution is something simple that I overlooked, in which case I will literally facepalm.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is a true gem, and I consider it one of the best I've ever played.

Important: Go in as 'blind' as possible, the less you know, the more fun you'll have.
You won't really be able to play this game the same way a second time, because of yourself. There is a game+ and a second playthrough is also fun, but the first playthrough is where the magic happens. (Much like Outer Wilds or Subnautica)

Tunic is a tribute to the first Zelda, it almost feels like a spiritual succesor in a great way.
But it combines it with games like "Outer Wilds" or other 'knowledge based games'.

Tunic echo's back to the old days of gaming, and introduces a 'new' gameplay mechanic, I didn't know I missed so much. The game developers have really found a great 'new' gaming idea, and I hope we will see more of them, and other developers to incorporate this idea.
Gameplay is king here, although it also looks gorgeous.

This game is basically trying to recreate my childhood games: when I couldn't really read English, and all you had was wat came with the game itself.
And also, great accessibility options! The game can be punishing, but it doesn't punish or shame you.

Some tips:
  • Don't look up guides! (At least not until the end)
  • Don't worry: you are suposed to be 'confused', certainly at the start.
  • Everything will be 'explained', just 'keep playing/move on to the next thing' if you're stuck on something.
  • It's not a linguistics game, it's a puzzle game!
  • If stuck: "study" in-game first, this is part of the fun/game. You won't be able to learn a second time.
  • If you're "not sure" if there is more to find/complete, then there is more to find. If something is completed, you'll know.
  • Beware: there are a couple of optional puzzles that are insanely hard/intensive in my eyes, I think it's cool they are there, but I did look up the answers, and was glad I did. Not every puzzle is for everyone's taste. You won't really 'stumble' into these puzzles, you will know they are there and optional, by the end of the game. And there is one optional "final final" puzzle, that is meant to be solved as a community, and is already solved, this requires some specilised knowledge/skill and software, so for 98% of people, don't even try, I didn't.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe aesthetics of the game are very very good, which is unfortunately all it really has going for it.

There were a few puzzles towards the general ending that were all interesting and a good amount of challenge, and were gratifying to complete.

I can see what the game is going for but it asks far too much on blind faith most of the time. If there were just smallish tweaks, like certain sequences of button presses that had some sort, *any* sort of visual feedback, that'd be wonderful.

But unfortunately this game just goes too far in the other direction from the problem of handholding in modern titles. Despite a very interesting and well put-together diagetic manual gimmick, it simply leaves us with too much guesswork and too much needless time wasting.

And even the amount of effort to get the "true" ending is just...so much compared to what you get. A few extra moments of a very predictable plot line? It's just not enough to justify the time it asks of players. At all.

I know the "story" such as it is, isn't really what most people that play Tunic will want out of it, but honestly, the "true" ending should've been a lot easier to unlock, with the 100% puzzle completion representing something else, unrelated to the story.

All in all, I'd give it a pass.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI want to like this game, I really do.

I really really love the manual pages mechanic, and the exploration/discovery of mechanics is so incredibly satisfying. The soundtrack is amazing. The art style is cute.

But man, the combat. I hate it. Yet another game that suffers as a result of the pursuit of Souls-Like mechanics. Bonfires and shortcuts are cool but if you're going to try to pull the punishing combat part, at least try to replicate some of the depth. Enemies have great variety in attack patterns but the hero has a total of 2 different attack moves and you're stuck with that. No backstabs, no heavy/light attacks, no two handed stances, no perfect parrying. Just dodging, attacking, blocking and "special attacks". Later on you get some magic weapons but they're highly situational and require mana drops to use.

Such a shame, I feel like soulslike mechanics are to indie games what open worlds are to AAA games. Giant mismatch here.\t\t\t\t\t
