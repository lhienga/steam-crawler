Now, for an actual review.

The combat will challenge you. Every enemy respawns on save or death. It can be cumbersome to get to some locations because of this.
Some respawn shrines are spaced quite far from each other. It will be annoying, but it's not really game-breaking.

The game looks very stunning. Every area is beautiful on it's own.
The handbook is truly amazing. Play the game, you will see what I mean.
The combat feels great. You're never too strong, while also never being too weak.
The puzzles are great. There's something everywhere. The handbook will tell you all you need to know, except very few secret treasures,

I can only recommend it. Tunic is genuinely amazing.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTunic is a game which I have enjoyed, although I do think most of its charm resides in the manual, while everything else is okay.

While I think this is definitely better, I can't keep myself from seeing some resemblance to Death's Door, since it also has an isometric point of view and has a similar gameplay (apart from the manual, of course).

As I've already said in that review, I think that these kind of games are mostly "educational", meaning that if you consider them in every way they're made, they get everything right. Absolutely right.

But, same as Death's Door (although Tunic does better), I think that there's not that spark that makes my brain say "this is the one". I think it may have to do with the hype surrounding the game and my expectations being too high, although I'm not one to judge based on that.

The thing that makes Tunic stand out, of course, is its manual and mechanics connected to it, which I'm not going to describe to avoid any spoilers. How you encounter enemies and discover things is definitely another interesting point, even though it's dark souls-y.

The thing with Tunic is that it doesn't really feel new. I'm not saying this in a negative way, since you can enjoy something while also not praising it as the best thing there's is, but it takes many things from different games and tries to tie them together, succeeding in doing so but making you get that "deja-vu" feeling. Again, the only thing that did that is the manual, which fortunately takes a good amount of your time and is the main focus.

I think that Tunic should have been a different kind of game, without its combat elements maybe. I don't know, I wasn't convinced with the overall structure!

I have appreciated the narration, too, but again, it is a simple story told in a non-direct way. I have loved the soundtrack too, it has a foamy and warm atmosphere.

Personal thoughts aside, I definitely think you should play this.\t\t\t\t\t

I'm no stranger to difficult puzzle games, nor to games that don't hold your hand, but even so, I just lost the desire to keep on playing after a few hours. The whole foreign language thing is moderately interesting, but the really frustrating aspect is the mechanics of respawning. I felt like most of my experience playing this game wasn't so much playing it, as re-playing it... having to fight the same enemies just to get a little bit further. As such, I got bored of the repetition after awhile, and the puzzles and mysteries just weren't compelling enough for me to want to keep playing.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTunic is lovely.

I would say that I think my experience of Tunic suffered from having overly high expectations given the glowing reviews of others.

The art is gorgeous. The concept of using the revealing of the pages of the game manual as a progression mechanism really works. The story is intriguing - a seemingly light game gets increasingly dark as you progress and more of the world and its history are revealed.

The combat is ok. It seems like there should be a lot of scope for complexity and varied approaches with its flexible equipment layout and modifiers, but I found a lot of the options not generally helpful, and would (and did) fall back on using and improving the basics.

This was partly because you can not go wrong in having the basic skills of melee/block/roll down, but also because there was a tendency to not spell stuff out. And while that largely works, for some things like the patch/buff items that didn't have obvious effects I think this was unfair and led me to avoid the ones that I was just guessing at what they might do.

One of the issues here bumped in to another of the interesting concepts - the language. I loved the idea of having the language itself be part of the challenge, and the symbols they use here are certainly visually distinct and have an aesthetic that contributes to the world building. BUT when you are playing Tunic blind - and I would definitely recommend that - the writing system gets in the way of some crucial details. And in the end after having a go at 'learning' the language myself - which I wasn't particularly successful at - I looked up online how to go about it, and was ultimately disappointed with both the concept and the execution. To my mind the language being part of the puzzle solving should have been a thing right from the start, but crucial pages for even getting started are gated behind what I consider end game conditions. Having said that, you don't ever NEED to be able to read the symbols to get through the game. Great concept; it could have been done better IMO.

And so to the puzzles. I mostly liked them. Usually in games that heavily feature puzzles, with a spectrum from fairly easy/obvious to incredibly obscure, I'll naturally reach a frustration point. And so I did with Tunic, but to Tunic's credit this was very late in the game - I found the progression of puzzle difficulty reasonable and rewarding for the most part. There's some stuff right at the end that will drive you mad I think, but that's optional stuff anyway so that's fine.

It's a good game, and the fox people are lovely.
