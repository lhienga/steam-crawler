\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTunic is a cute game that initially appears as a standard rpg where you go around the world finding treasure, getting stronger, and killing bosses. The art style is simple yet experssive and the music is soft and atmospheric. While the combat targetting can be finicky against multiple enemies, it works.
What sets the game apart is how you learn mechanics and get hints: by literally collecting pages of the game's manual.
Even if you don't care for the hidden secrets, Tunic still has the basic outline of a story through the manual pages you do collect and even provides a twist, all without any dialog and with most text in a made up runic language. However, going for all the collectables is where the game really shines.
Poring through the manual and understanding certain parts of it literally changes how you view the environment and the world's design. The isometric view and world design is cleverly used to hide secrets; you will miss something the first time around, no matter how thorough you think you were. Rereading manual pages to realize how to get to something you missed or understanding the initially random scribbles within the manual itself is very satisfying. However, not understanding or missing something can lead to you wandering around for a long time not knowing what to do. This is something that applies to the game whether you are going for 100% completion or not, but is a lot more noticeable while searching for every last secret.

Tunic is a game best experienced as blind as you can. Only look up hints or guides as a last resort. The only exceptions I'd say are worth it are the two achievements that will be missed during a vanilla blind playthrough as it requires having the pages for two specific items, translating their descriptions, and inferring the meaning. By the time you have the resources to do so, you will have most likely already failed the conditions to get them. In addition, the items in particular don't really have a reason to be explicitly translated as you've probably inferred the descriptions while using them, meaning you'll be going out of your way to translate them.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIs the game good? Yes, it's fantastic, but it is definitely not for everyone and I can more than understand why.

What's a good way to explain this? Well, this game is more like a game for people who have played a lot of other video games before. That might sound confusing so let me try to break that down a bit.

The whole game, from traversing the map to reading the instructions, is a puzzle. Now, this is a really cool concept and I personally love it, but with no tutorial and absolutely no direction other than a hieroglyphic like "instruction manual", most players will have to rely entirely on their gaming intuition and/or google to figure it out what is going on.

You could honestly argue this is more of a "git gud" issue and I can't really say anything but that that is valid... It brings to mind titles like Terraria where you're kind of just thrown into the water and you either sink or swim, and slowly learn as you go with a trial and error approach. And that is one of the most successful games of all time on steam. But this style can make the game very dislike-able for newer, more casual gamers and children. And ironically, it seems the art and animation is styled more for that type of audience as well.

Overall, I think this game is fantastic. The art style, the music, the combat, the collecting, the souls-like and rpg-like elements, the puzzles and the intuition this game requires would put this on the list as one of my favorites of all time. You kind of just have to be a gamer for lack of a better, less cringe phrase.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSimply astounding game design!

In 2022 I passed Tunic off as a mindless Legend of Zelda clone due to the obviously reminiscent design of the sword, tunic, and shield. This year I actually looked into the game and decided to give it a go. It was probably the best life decision I have made in recent memory.

The graphics are great, the map design is incredibly clever, and the soundtrack is so well fit and insanely beautiful. It has some similar aspects to a 2D Legend of Zelda game which is great, but Tunic goes so far beyond that.

Bosses are really well done. They are equal parts challenging and fair with the various approaches you can take to taking them out. Not to mention there are easier and harder difficulty settings if you desire them.

Game progression is brilliant. Sometimes you make progress because you gained an item, and other times you acquire information via the instruction manual that you piece together throughout the length of the game. Sometimes you just find a hidden shortcut that makes getting back to where you were when you died easier.

Every aspect of this game is deserving of praise. If you enjoy especially well-made games then this is for you.\t\t\t\t\t
