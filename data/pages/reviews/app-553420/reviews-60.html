\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you want a great puzzle and exploration game in the style of a 2D zelda game, Tunic is the game for you. This game world was an absolute blast to explore, and the mystery was thrilling to slowly unravel. Some of the optional secret puzzles I do not regret looking at a guide for as I think they were far too obtuse, but most of the puzzles were wonderfully to try and work out on my own. The combat is good too, but it's certainly not the main focus here.

You'll see a lot of people complaining about difficulty spikes but I experienced nothing like that in my playthrough. My theory is they managed to do something out of the intended order, or they didn't fully explore an area and get all the upgrades, which are both totally reasonable but they would make the difficulty curve seem a bit wonky. All in all this was a wonderful game I highly recommend.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\ttl;dr: A really interesting game that tries to recreate the "no instructions" gameplay style of some retro games. The design choices feel well thought out and intentional, however, for that same reason I can see people loving or hating it. Overall, I enjoyed the game myself but the longer answer to how is nuanced since there were aspects of the design choices I was not enthused about.

First off, going in blind is definitely part of the experience with Tunic. You are given no information starting off and you simply have to meander to figure out what to do and where to go. Even then, the instructions you receive are in the form of "game manual" pages scattered throughout the world which can at times be cryptic themselves. It is a neat concept but also not for me. The lack of descriptions on gameplay mechanics and items (unless you stumble upon a relevant page) was intriguing at first but eventually grew tiresome. I don't blame that on the game since it's clearly very intentional; so it's purely a matter of personal preference. I got to the second boss before looking anything up, at which point I started looking up information. I was one of the people that did not even figure out there was a leveling system until I saw it mentioned online, so I was basically playing on extra-hard difficulty until then... That said, I don't regret both playing blind up to that point or looking up info from then on.

The combat, stat system, and item system are standard RPG stuff. They are pretty good and well implemented. Well, at least once you figure out the details from trial and error, in-game manual pages, or looking them up... I played with a keyboard and you could tell at times some things were clearly designed with controllers first in mind. No big deal since that's somewhat standard for these kinds of games and it did not block me from anything but something to be aware of.

The story was simple but fun. I don't have much to add there. Similarly, I liked the graphics and soundtrack but there wasn't anything extraordinary me to call out about them.

The last thing I will mention is that there are tons of secrets in the game. Some of these are required for the true ending although most are just for fun. These secrets follow the same principle of being hidden behind cryptic instructions or puzzles in the manual pages you find throughout the world. I found some secrets on my own but I also looked up a bunch of them because I was curious to know and also wanted to get the true ending without needing to do sweeps of the map and manual pages to figure out what I was missing. If you enjoy in-game puzzles like that though, you might really enjoy that aspect as well.

All in all, Tunic knows exactly what it wants to be, and I think it does a great job at it. The only thing is whether that game design style appeals to a given player or not since it's very different from most modern games.\t\t\t\t\t
