\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo far the game is fun as hell. When I figured out what it's looking for I realized I can, in fact, do the moves consistently.

And then I tried to do them under pressure in a multiplayer match and kept failing comically. But I have to say, there's some hidden depth and the game is just really fun so far. This is the first VR game that gave me what I could call childlike wonder. There are VR games that made me feel badass, sure, and they were super fun, but this game made me feel like a kid. Like when you mimic the made-up martial arts moves of a cartoon you saw on TV, except in RUMBLE it actually works and you launch a whole-ass boulder at your opponent.

The main complaint I see from players is that the game is very stingy with unlocking your moveset, and I agree. Imagine playing a fighting game where your opponent might have moves you don't have! I still enjoy the game, it's surprisingly fun and tactical even with a limited moveset, and I'm gonna keep playing... but there is a problem with how the game handles unlocks. It's lame that higher ranked players have powerful moves that lower rank players simply can't use. You have to play ranked mode to unlock moves, and there aren't always enough opponents online for this to be viable. I've been lucky with this so far, but some players had a hard enough time unlocking moves that they left bad reviews. It would be great if you could unlock moves in singleplayer, perhaps by somehow proving you've mastered the moves you already have. At least give me a couple for beating the tutorial bot!

Oh yeah and the sprint gesture is way too strict, it shouldn't be this hard to sprint on command. Lifting rocks from the earth and throwing them, sure, I should have to use the correct pose, but sprinting? Come on. Let me run.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI love the concept. It's very... inspired and takes clear cues from its inspiration. When you pull off a move, the feedback is amazing. When the game -works-, it works well. When it doesn't? Well.

It took me several minutes to find the options menu while walking at an incredibly slow speed. IMO, that should be independent of the game world for ease of access. Immersion does not have to get in the way of adjusting the game's settings. Default movement speed in the main camp is also very slow and awkward, most likely due to the way the player model is oriented/positioned. It should be independent of whatever default position the game feels it should be in the playspace. I cannot stand at the very center of my room, and the model stands as if my head was still there, but my body was elsewhere regardless of head position.

Hand positions for poses are incompatible with my body and hurt my wrists. They need to be able to be offset/adjustable. I have to twist my palms just past what I consider "upward" and it hurts after doing it long enough. This game would also be amazing for cardio, but I can't even do that because the default hand positions place my hands practically just inside my body with no offset available. Yeah, I'm a little out of shape, I admit it. If the game can account for that, it'd be perfect.

A small bit of feedback (A subtle jingle? a rumbling of the controller?) when starting a pose would also be very helpful. At least in training. Or make the default palms-up pose slightly more forgiving to get into. I can't really pull any move off as a result. Especially with my physical limitations as mentioned above. Re-calibrating my default T pose does not help very much.\t\t\t\t\t

You have to identify the strengths and weaknesses of your opponent, formulate a strategy that minimizes the opportunities your opponent gets to utilize their strengths, and maximize the times that your opponent is in an uncomfortable situation relative to their skillset. You need to constantly keep track of your position in the arena, adjusting your strategy and attack patterns to work yourself towards the middle as efficiently as possible. You have to do all of that while your opponent is doing the same thing, there's rocks being chucked at you, you're panicking, and you need this strategy as fast as physically possible. Words do not do justice just how intense an actual match is.

That's just ONE of the factors of this game. That's not taking into account the awesome community and the belt system.

The community is amazing, and most times if you're trying to dip your toes into PVP and you just can't get a move right, most players will stop attacking and try to show you how to do it. Everyone is super friendly, it's entirely normal, almost customary, to exchange a fistbump and maybe some thumbs up at the beginning of every match.

The belt system was amazingly constructed. Instead of giving you the entire moveset at the beginning (which would admittedly make the game very confusing) it introduces moves to you bit by bit, so you can practice each one. Every time a new belt gets unlocked, there's always a new move or two that completely revolutionizes the way people of that same belt fight and play. The level of a belt also acts as a great indicator to quickly and easily determine the skill level of that player, which can be used to select if you want to play against people who are of the same level as you, or perhaps a little higher.

Decently large community, match queues don't take long if you're playing from northern america, and you can almost always find a good park with some (extraordinarily friendly) people who want to do nothing but improve and help improve.

I don't think I've ever come across a better use of $20 in my entire life.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFirst of all: Remember that this is not a game where you can jump into a match without practising and still win.

This is a great game and if you don't feel like reading all that you should just buy it and set a two hour timer and just refund it if you don't.

This is by far the best motion based combat out there and it may even be the only true motion based combat out there. You even have to have proper form in order to pull stuff off.

The game starts you off with just three moves, but you are never going to abandon those moves for other ones so the limitation sets you up with a good start as you get to gain experience with the most important moves right form the get-go. You get three more moves each level and they get progressively more fun to play with.

The main thing to remember is to think. You have to get a grasp of your opponent's strategy in order to adapt to it. You also want to keep track of the structures you opponent is able to manipulate and the ones you can manipulate. Almost no one has time to react to a good surprise attack unless you already the there was a chance for it to happen.
You also have to keep calm as panicking will lose you the game since you need to maintain proper form for the moves to work.

You can choose which region you would like to play in so what time it is isn't going to affect you unless you have bad internet, but I can play one two hundred ping with no issues. I do not know how they do it, but the developers know how to handle online play really well.

The game also looks really good and unlike A Township Tale, this actually has a graphics option and decent amount of option for people prone to VR sickness.

It looks great, it plays great, it runs great, and it handles lag perfectly. Just buy it already 9/10 due to there not being an option for left handed move tutorials.\t\t\t\t\t
