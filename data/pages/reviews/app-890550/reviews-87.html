\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've been waiting for this game for about a year now, even having been dedicated enough to find a beta version months before now. I had no clue this game was coming out today (yesterday, technically) and bought it as soon as I saw it had been released. A friend of mine did the same.

The answer's plain and simple. This game is worth it all the way. So worth it, in fact, that II desperately want Rumble to gain traction with anyone with access to a VR headset, because all the love put into this game.

The main appeal with this game is it's similarity to ATLA. The earthbender feel is translated beautifully into it's design. The "magic" casting is all done by body movement, not through button pushing and menu scrolling. You stand in the right position, swing your arms into the air the right way, and you've got a wall blockade summoned in front of you. This is a design mechanic I desperately yearn to be established in more VR fantasy and fantasy-like games. The movement of your arms and limbs feels so much more like I'm casting an actual spell. Skyrim VR and The Mage both fail at these aspects, in my opinion.

The whole point of said movement mechanic is for combat, and the PVP is pretty spot on. Smooth quality severs for both PVP and shared training areas, and never a moment left feeling cheated out. I have seen some complaints about the fact that abilities are locked behind entering through online matchmaking, which only might be a problem if :

1) You're at a newbie level fighting a top-tier player (which I don't think can even happen, the most I experienced was players at least a level or two above me, which was not a huge deal)
or
2) You plan on playing the game strictly offline?? Which is not really how the game is designed, since there's only one simplistic dummy bot you can fight. The main purpose of the game is to specifically enter the 1v1 PVP designed matches and fight.

With the abilities locked behind matchmaking, you're not stuck on the same level unless you win. You get more EXP if you win, but still a pretty substantial amount when losing. Plus, if you're really sore about it, you might be able to play against a friend and grind out points by letting one another win or lose every other round and replaying consistently.

Or, here's a good exert from my friend that summarizes it well:
"Every time I lose, I don't feel like I lost because of the game. It's not like games where people have leveled up so far beyond you that you've fallen behind, it feels like a game of skill no matter what. Me vs. them. No matter what abilities they have unlocked before me."

Anyway, no more rambling, For the interested buyer, there's not a single reason to not buy this game if you've got some cash and a VR system.



Now, before I continue, this part of the review is beyond unimportant to the average buyer. This game is in early access, being officially released as of yesterday while writing this. So when you read, this is never meant to sway your opinion. The game is good. Read, see what I have to say, and understand that I never felt that the game sucked because of what problems still lie ahead for the game's development. I leave this here mostly as a way to offer my own opinion to the developers, opinions that I believe nonessential to the experience, and fully able of being ignored.

Anyway, for the developers I've got a short list of picky notes, if they are interested in reading:

-The running is goofy, I like it, but the speeds for both walking and running are both incredibly slow. For combat, I guess it makes sense, but walking around the main hub or training with friends becomes a chore, especially if you go back and forth between the bot training ground and the move tutorial, or really any one point of the map to the other. Maybe using the unused buttons on the controller as a way to either jump around and/or teleport (maybe even if it's just to main areas?)

-It'd be nice if throwing punches at different angles actually shot it in that direction. You can move your body left and right to punch it in different ways that way and that did feel cool, but you can only punch things at one height. Especially for the Ring Toss, you can feel how stuck you are at certain angles, and are forced to move around to try and get that specific angle where the ring doesn't just arc right over their head. Not being able to punch something flying from above because you can't choose to do a normal punch upwards at it also kinda sucks.

-Some of the moves are a little too tight with their movement. The boulder specifically felt nearly impossible to achieve, which is terrible because the boulder may be one of the most versatile moves out of the whole moves list.

Also, being able to do a plain punch instead of an uppercut to arc the boulder would be nice in general.

-There were many things that I found to be quite hard to learn and find out. There was also nothing that I saw that said moves were learned through matchmaking, and had to go to Google it to find that one out. The lack of a properly in-game menu that allows you to send friend requests to specific people was annoying and a hassle when you couldn't reach the PC easily and had to copy and paste your own friend code into the text-box just to see what it was before telling your friend was yucky. I don't understand why Steam wasn't used to show the people you're already friends with, but I also find it important to note that I am not a developer and have no idea what doing all that entails, and that I do still greatly appreciate the fact that I was able to play with my friend at all.

The notes are lengthy, but they don't detract from being able to experience the absolute pleasure of this game. I'm so dehydrated, and thrilled to play this game again. It feels like the first time I ever bought Beat Saber all over again. I'm beyond excited to see what comes next, and am forever grateful for Buckethead Entertainment for all their work.\t\t\t\t\t
