\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs of writing this review, I have played through over half of both a campaign as a base game evil genius, and 1 DLC evil genius. I own all the DLC and use them in both playthroughs + I have watched videos and tutorials on most things I have yet to do in the game before formulating my opinions on it. To give you a short answer, I do belive that this game is worth trying, however I would only recommend the DLC if you love this game enough to repeat-play it like I have. To give you a steady list of Pros and Cons:

Pros:
+ The Visuals are stunning
+ The characters have interesting personality and well written dialogue
+ Despite being mostly confined to a single island lair the game remains very engaging
+ The story can be interesting in both a serious and comedic way
+ None of the DLC are bad in a way that makes them worthless, all add to the game in some positive way
+ Game fluctuates between hard times and easy going, which allows for a even mixture of challenging and casual game play.
+ Players can choose to 100 percent the game by completing every side story, or skip some if they have no interest in them.

Cons:
- Dialogue is good, but most EG's share the same cut scene dialogue for all side stories, witch can break immersion on repeat playthroughs.
- There is a small array of henchmen to choose from (Even with all DLC it only adds about 3 more), which can make repeat playthroughs boring because you keep using the same henchmen.
- There can be times when the game feels tedious, and your just waiting for something to finish being built, or a minion to finish training, or even more things that result in the player just sitting around waiting for the work to end so they can move on.
-You can only do 1 side story at a time making it hard to tackle all the ones you want to do before the next main story event closes options off.
- Not all EGs and Henchmen are created equal and while some will be highhandedly carrying your campaign, others will be little more than high-health melee muscle minions.
- At one point the cover operation will become virtually useless as all agents being sent your way WILL resist distractions, which leads to massive battles in your cover operation killing many of your staff.
- There are a handful of mechanics that feel pointless, like the ability to release prisoners from their cells when there is no benefit to doing so, and tourists seem to do nothing but stand in the cover operation and sometimes use your distractions for the agents, instead of possibly being a threat to the EG, they are just mindless drones who dont report anything they see even if there were in the middle of a gunfight.

Overall, I think this game is worth playing once. Devote yourself to 1, perfect campaign where it is everything you want, the side quests you want to do, the henchmen you want to recruit, the EG you want to win. Once you finish, the second campaign regardless of EG choice may feel a bit bland and repetitive. If you are able to enjoy your second campaign as much as the first one, then by all means have fun, and maybe get some DLC to spice things up. If you dont have fun on your second campaign and DLC is not enough to make it good, then maybe this game might not be for you.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEvil Genius 2: World Domination is a satirical spy-fi lair builder that lets players embrace their inner supervillain. The game was published by Rebellion Interactive in 2021 and is a successor to the first publication Evil Genius. The game is an intricate mix of strategy, management, and dark humor, improving significantly on its predecessor. Players are tasked with constructing an impregnable lair on a remote island, recruiting and managing minions and henchmen, developing dastardly devices, and advancing a malevolent plot to achieve global domination.

The gameplay mechanics are both deep and accessible. Players can choose from several unique Evil Geniuses, each offering a distinct play style and special abilities. The base-building aspect is immensely satisfying, allowing for creative layouts while managing essential resources like power, money, and minion morale. The game also introduces a World Stage feature, a global map where players can send minions to undertake missions, gather resources, and engage in acts of villainy.

The primary objective in "Evil Genius 2" is to build and manage a secret base while progressing through a series of campaign missions that further the player's nefarious goals. These goals range from stealing famous landmarks to launching doomsday devices. Players must balance base expansion, resource management, and minion training with defending against the Forces of Justice. The dynamic challenge of juggling these objectives without getting overwhelmed is where the game shines.

Side missions and optional objectives add layers of depth and strategy, offering unique rewards and advancing the storyline. The game also encourages replayability with different genius choices, each altering the gameplay experience.

The reception of "Evil Genius 2" among the fanbase was generally positive, especially among those who appreciated the original game. Players lauded the game for its improved graphics, more refined base-building mechanics, and the humorous, tongue-in-cheek approach to supervillainy. The diverse range of options available for base construction and minion management was also a highlight, offering players plenty of opportunities for customization and strategic planning.

However, some players noted that the game could become repetitive, with the need for constant attention to base defenses and resource management. A few also mentioned a desire for more variety in missions and an expanded storyline. The AI behavior, particularly regarding minion management, was another point of critique, though this did not significantly detract from the overall enjoyment of the game.

Evil Genius 2: World Domination successfully builds on its predecessor's legacy, offering an engaging, humorous, and challenging experience for fans of the strategy and management genres. Its appealing aesthetic, combined with deep gameplay mechanics, makes it a standout title. While there is room for improvement in certain areas, the game undoubtedly provides a unique and entertaining take on the world of villainy and base building. For those who ever dreamed of becoming a mastermind of malevolence, "Evil Genius 2" is a lair-builder's paradise.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t4/5 - An overall technical and UX improvement over the original, but slower pacing and some safer, generic choices prevent it from being truly great.

First, I want to focus on what EG2 does better than the original, since it seems like that's what Rebellion primarily focused on (along with the shiny new coat of paint). In no particular order:
* Minion training can now be done without an instructor, just at a slower speed. No longer will you have to capture a high-ranking specialist over and over again due to soldier raids wiping out the ones you have.
* The world map is much safer and no longer needs babysitting. Previously, you'd have to send minions to regions to pull money from them and then continuously check in on them to make sure an enemy agent isn't about to discover and terminate them. Now, passive income is unlocked simply by having a presence in a region, and can only be temporarily locked out by letting too much heat accumulate. Additionally, missions are a one-and-done event; no more placing pieces on the board and hiding or moving them until conditions are right, just send em, wait, and train replacements once successful.
* Bouncing off the above, money is much easier to earn - and keep. In addition to the aforementioned easy passive income, money now appears in your vaults automatically. No more lugging it from the helipad $5000 at a time - and no more sending it to the helipad $5000 at a time when you want to build or buy something! The only way agents can steal your money is if they get into your vaults. Furthermore, your cover operation can now be used as a local money-making operation if you're willing to trade off not being able to lower agent resolve.
* Traps activate themselves, which means you can make much longer and more varied combos. The downside is that it's harder to hit clusters of agents now (traps tend to pop on the first agent they detect) but it's also possible that's just me being mediocre at trap placement.
* No more guess-and-check research! Your nodes are all planned out, no need to have scientists randomly inspect things and then combine machines in the hope it makes a better thing. The research devices needed are also mostly clustered by tier, so it's feasible to replace most (but not all) of your lower-level equipment as you progress.
* Simpler stats. The old vitality/stamina/smarts/morale/loyalty meters have been condensed into health/smarts/loyalty(minion)/resilience(agent). Fewer things to worry about means minions spend less time taking care of themselves and more time working.
* Agents no longer go aggro, attacking any non-civilian thing on site. They're always patient enough to wait for doors to open (and doors always open now, no more making it harder to access an area for an agent than a minion) and will generally prefer to only attack muscle minions (as opposed to anyone who looks at them funny). No more piles of dead valets because they all tried to put out the fire near the genocidal soldiers.
* Bodies are destroyed. Absolutely wild that you used to have to dedicate precious lair space to bulky and incriminating freezer racks, now your minions will just shove the body in a compact incinerator placed in your power plants and poof, body's gone. Tourists also seem to be much more tolerant of body bags in EG2, I've never seen them flee from one.

These and other changes result in EG2 being a much more relaxed game than its predecessor...to the point of slowness. This is an overriding theme in the game's major flaws:
* Missions on the world map that aren't "instant" (10 seconds) will take one or two real-time hours to complete. You will often find yourself interrupting them to run a story mission instead.
* Research takes TIME. Since your minions aren't farting around with guess-and-check anymore, the tradeoff is that they now have to stand in front of a research machine and slowly build up invisible research points until the node is complete. Many story missions will also require a segment of research, which means a lot of the game can just be waiting around for a research item to finish.
* The game is divided into the main narrative and a slew of side stories, wherein you recruit henchmen, steal loot, deal with super agents, and unlock new minion types. You can do only one side story at a time, and they tend to horde one type of resource at a time, which means - you guessed it - you have to do a lot of waiting to restore a capacity of some sort before progressing, while in theory you could be simultaneously doing another side story that would be asking for a completely different resource.
* The voice acting is bizarrely slow. It seems like the one note the VAs were given was "dramatically chew the scenery". I'm playing with Red Ivan right now and I swear I can hear the VA slow himself down to abide by whatever rule Rebellion had. The dialogue is also a lot more bland than the original's humor. I honestly believe Rebellion has a whole thing planned with cutscenes in the lair and longer back-and-forths but just ran out of time so slapped the current exchanges together instead; considering all the flavor text is just as sharp as in the original, I can't help but feel these scenes were an afterthought. [Side note, these scenes are also super generic. Red Ivan knows who Atomic Olga is; Zalika shouldn't be getting headaches talking to science minions; it's clear it was all written for Max and then given to the other geniuses.]

Fortunately, there's a fast-forward button. All it does is 2x speed, which is wack (I could've sworn we'd all agreed on 1x 3x 5x or 1x 2x 4x/5x 8x/10x by now), but it's something, and the background music is much less ambient now which makes it easier to maintain focus.
And again, when there are things to do, it's a truly fun game. There is much more base to build and many more minions to command. While sadly the loss of the Infamy meter means no more going out into the world and being evil for the sake of evil, there's still plenty of hijinx you can pull off to get richer and/or stymy the forces of justice. The game's one real sin was in attempting to be more appealing to everyone, which prevented it from really focusing its tone and being as sharp-yet-loving of a send-up of the genre as EG1. Still worth a buy on sale - and don't worry about getting the DLC, there's plenty in the game without it.\t\t\t\t\t
