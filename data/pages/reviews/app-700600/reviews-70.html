\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEvil Genius 2 isn’t a bad game, but it isn’t really a good game. Overall it was a very ‘meh’ experience and I really can’t recommend this to anyone.

There isn’t anything good to say about the game, but I do have some minor complaints. I am not sure they can be fixed without a major redesign of the game.

Getting gold is way too easy. You have criminal networks you establish on the world map, and they generate passive income. They have 4 tiers, but at tier 2, just a few of them will have you covered. It is more of an issue to build enough storage space to store all your gold. Simple gold storage upgrades are locked behind high tier research. I just ended up creating giant rooms to store a lot of it.

I never found a point or use for henchmen beyond pure combat. You can get science henchmen and social henchmen, but I could never find any use for them other than attacking enemies. I tried assigning a science henchman to a research station hoping it would give some kind of boost, but it was not possible.

Social minions seem to have no use aside from overworld missions. The lowest tier, the valet, did all the work in the base as far as running the casino. The more advanced minions didn’t seem to do anything extra other than collect a paycheck.

The whole idea of a front, which is a casino, to provide a cover operation for your base never really worked out. Despite creating a maze of distractions, with the best items, the mid tier and above agents just walked past everything into my nearly useless corridor of traps.

Traps are almost worthless. There is an improvement from EG1 as in that the traps will not murder your own minions (that is left up to some missions). Beyond the lowest tiers of enemies I found that even the very best of traps you could make either did a trivial amount of damage, didn’t fire off for some reason, or were just simply disabled by invading agents. I created a large winding corridor of traps for enemy agents to pass through and it was mostly pointless for the above reasons. The guard minions were the ones who did the real work.

The missions are simply a list of nonsensical tasks you need to do. The dumbest of these were to murder your own minions. Like an objective to kill 5 technicians or muder 4 guards, then murder 4 mercanaries. There is no reason for this. The game just gives you a checklist of stuff to do. That's all this game is, a bunch of checklists you do. The only creative part of this game is designing your base. If that really turns you on, then maybe you should buy this game when it's on sale.

The overworld map is also annoying as hell and full of mostly useless mission types. One thing that is really bad is that when you have to find 5 of X type missions and you have to hunt through all the regions of the game to find the special missions to do. They could have simply made the super obvious and visible from the zoomed out view or let you click on the objectives which would take you to the mission targets, but no, you have to hunt for it.

The other issue is that overworld missions consume minions. For example, you may need 5 martial artists for some mission and when the mission completes, the minions are gone and you need to train up more martial artists to replace them. This leads you to do massive parallelization in training, but there is still a bottle neck because you need to turn a worker into a guard, who then becomes a merc who then becomes a martial artist. This isn’t fast, even when playing on fast forward mode 100% of the time.

The end result is that you end up spending a bunch of time just waiting around for minions to train to do the various quest missions. It's quite boring.

I have fond memories of the first one, which did have some game-play issues, but this version only fixed the traps murdering your own minions problem and failed to fix any of the other problems. The game design really does need a lot of work and it's too bad because I doubt we will see an evil genius 3 with a chance to really fix the gameplay problems.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEvil Genius 2 is a base building game that provides the ingeniously flawed experience of being bad.

The game itself looks pretty at first. The graphical designs are captivating and purposely cheesy; it runs well and has dozens of graphical options as well as Vulkan support. It's a technically and visually interesting title much like its predecessor which arguably was ahead of its time in both of these departments. The marketing is also the biggest I've ever seen for a singleplayer title.

Unfortunately, everyone in this game is a poet until they start talking. The writing in the game is atrocious, and the gameplay loop shoots itself in the kneecaps when you finally think you can start running with it.

Gameplay

In the game you hire minions, make a base of operations, and take over the world in schemes that run on an overview board. It seems fun at first, until you hit the midgame.

Artificial constraints such as a hard population cap (that you only get to find out once you hit it) absolutely destroy whatever was left of fun in a game that is, at its core, about expansion and scale (growing the base/taking over the world). Here's why:

  • Suddenly in midgame your base building comes to a complete halt: There's no reason to expand, because you simply can't staff it. There's no real reason to get money, because you have nothing to spend it on (either assets or personnel).
  • You can't make it quality over quantity either: you can't get a base full of the super special minions because the minion hierarchy is not retro-compatible; by promoting minions they can't do the lower minion jobs so you always need the lower level minions. Basically with this, that's 2/3rd of the game just gone.

What you're left with is managing the boardgame part of the title. This involves looking at a literal board and "sending" minions outside of your base to give you cash income, or advance "game objective" progress in what are called "schemes".

  • There's no cutscenes, no flavor text, nothing when these are started or completed. Minions just wait for a helicopter to leave your base and then the board game gives you an arbitrary amount of time to wait until they "complete" the scheme. You then get a tally on your objective counter and that's it, the game then effectively kills those minions. An absolutely dripless experience.
  • Waiting; after spending those minions you then need to retrain them, and with the population capacity this means that you can't just amass them and flood the board and end the pain quickly; you'll train them on demand which means more waiting and more artificial constraining so you can't progress the game at your pace.

As if the boardgame by now wasn't brilliantly tedious, it also has a Heat mechanic to it which grows passively in every region you want to do stuff in (read: everywhere).
  • You manage this heat in the boardgame, or you will have strong enemies investigating and finally assaulting your base. The game can then lock the region as well for an arbitrary amount of time (which you then have to wait for it to unlock). To make heat go down, you run deception schemes which also cost minions (that you will need to wait to retrain). You then wait for the heat to slowly go down.
  • You can run faster schemes that cost a resource called intel, and to get intel you guessed it: You wait for some buildings to generate it (or better yet, you wait until the minions operating these buildings are done sleeping/eating/watching TV to generate it).
  • Alternatively, you wait for an enemy agent to get captured, wait for them to get transported to their cell, wait for them to lose their resolve, and wait until they get interrogated. You then get 1-2 intel (the scheme costs like 20). You also have a maximum limit of 99 intel so you can't amass it.

But wait, there's more. The boardgame also presents you with super hero tokens that will travel in real time to regions that are idle (also in real time).
  • Super Heroes stationed in a region increase heat gain and if you start a scheme that isn't deception in the same region, the super hero will notice and teleport into your base. This means you get to use your guards and cool traps to kill them now? No, killing the super hero in your base does nothing for you, they poof away like a ninja once defeated then go back to being a token; so all they do is damage and slow down your training so they make you wait more.
  • The solution to this? Stop your scheme in another region and wait for them to move out. You can guarantee waiting to avoid potential more waiting (which can domino into more problems that just make you wait more to be resolved).

Campaign

Now for the part I was truly waiting for: The missions, cutscenes and dialogue in this game are truly doing their best to not be good.

  • When you start a mission, you'll get a Visual Novel style cutscene with your Genius on the left and whatever character they are talking to on the right. They have idle animations presenting their "mood" and it could not be further from pleasing, except when they start talking.
  • My Genius was "Zalika", what seemed like a quirky character that wanted to create social media as a weapon turned out to be the most absolutely insufferable main character I've had to deal with in my decades of consuming media. She is meant to be an actual science genius but nothing about her suggests this: she says completely ignorant things, makes idiot decisions, gets caught off guard constantly in cutscenes. She also has her head inside of an aquarium and unlimited amounts of voice fry: you get the hellish combination that is her voice going through a tube every single time you start/finish a mission or tag enemies.
  • Defeating/overcoming enemies with this format feels as weaksauce as possible. What's meant to be an incredible feat within the game's atmosphere just becomes a bit of banter exchanged in Visual Novel format. It legitimately makes me sick how much of a missed opportunity this was. Nothing you do feels rewarding or grandiose in the plot because the game doesn't bother presenting it as such.

Okay, but what if you just skip through every optional mission in the game to minimize the agonizing experience of going through terrible writing just for the base building and boardgame mechanics?

    The game will take every "optional" mission you didn't slog through and turn it into a super strong enemy at the end that all bumrush your base at the same time.


Conclusion

Unfortunately this title tried to scoop up the actual Genius behind the original title from 2004 which was a technical marvel at the time.

They hired great artists and great programmers to go along but completely failed at actually designing the game to feel fun to experience and play: I'd take "Good Stupid" over this "Evil Genius" any day.

An expensive mess; it makes you wait in expectation of grandiose then never fulfills it, and when you realize it already took your time and your money. Don't ever let it make you wait for it to be less bad at any capacity.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a fun sequel to the original Evil Genius game. The game itself is a base builder at it's core (so was the first one) and the controls are very similar between the games. Graphically the game is, of course, superior considering the time between both games. Gameplay wise, it's a mixed bag. The world map is more streamlined, you don't have any control of the minions you send on missions like you did in the first game where you can send them back and forth, though it did get a bit micromanag-y. I personally like the scheme mechanic better in EG2 though I know others do not. There are some other mechanics that allow more control of your minions within your base that didn't make it to EG2 either which at times I find unfortunate, though the game seems to manage alright. The base game itself can get a bit long and monotonous at times, though I found the first game did as well...guess it's the nature of this sort of game. I did find the first EG game to be harder though, the enemies don't seem quite as challenging. Playing a genius like Ivan for example, he's a pretty strong fighter himself and can shut down any attack I've seen so far largely by himself. I could see this game probably not having a lot of replay value after awhile, but it's still a fun and largely relaxing game. It's a great game to take your mind off work and relax a bit without being intense. While I haven't finished the game yet, I've been engaged enough to put in 21 hours as of this time and am quite happy to put in more.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBetter than the original, works in Linux with Steam Proton. Not sure if it is just me, but there were some second long silences in the audio in the game.

Things I like. The art in game is amazing, dialogues are good as well as the plot. Traps are fun. Lots of objectives and side stories, yet it is possible to rush the game skipping all the optional content. Achievements are nice - inspire the exploration of the game content. Some fancy animations are really cool. Loot items have perks. These perks are quite fancy and interesting. I really appreciate the amount of content this game has.

Things I didn't like. UI could have been much better. I'd like to have a hotkey to turn the power on/off for structures, a hotkey to remove/sell items, or even select a region to sell. It is just way to many clicks needed for some very common repetitive tasks. Same for taging agents. I have to click on the agent to select it, then using mouse hover to the applicable tag and click it. I would expect having an active tag(activated with a hot key) and then just clicking the agents to assign tag to them. I would expect seeing the agent info by hovering the mouse over it. Way to much clicking and moving the mouse around for the most trivial stuff!!!
I would like to rotate the camera using the hot keys when building items in the rooms. Loosing the keys for rotation when building is counter intuitive and just uncomfortable.
Progression through the game requires some guessing. What are the conditions to get the side objectives for the loot items? I felts like I was doing all the optional stuff, but still didn't get some loot items and missed some henchmens. The order of main and non-main goals isn't clear and is confusing.

Things I am not sure about. Character traits. Every minion has some traits. I guess it adds something extra to the game, it appears there are some bonuses and debuffs. Yet it is a bit confusing, some appear to be purely artistic, others seem to have effect. Minions are expandable mostly, yet there is some micromanagement elements present, which seem to all get messed up as soon as I send minions to a mission.

Overall thoughts. The game is great in terms of content, quality, technical perspective and the most complicated in my opinion things are done well. Yet it lacks some final polishing, tweaking game balance, UI friendliness and other gameplay features. This is a great piece or art to enjoy, and a fun game to play, yet experienced gamers will find it frustrating at times.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhile i LOVE this game and will put in many, many more hours than i already have, most of the DLC should have been part of the base game. If you can get it on sale however, VERY good game with an incredibly charming artstyle and soundtrack.

Below i'll go a bit more in detail but at the very bottom you'll find a TLDR score.

I want to prefix this review by saying that i so far have had an amazing time and that i truly think that the people claiming the first game is better should urgently replay the first game, because they are looking through rose tinted glasses;
Though it would still be hard to compare, as the first game is TOO punishing of mistakes that you have no control over (Rooms being permanent, entire base being whiped for the wrong trap setup and dozens of hours down the drain, etc.)
That said, i will compare everything with the first game for returning players.

The artstyle 10/10:
The artstyle is a straight up 10/10.
The game perfectly rocks a spy-fi aesthetic to the little details, the models are fantastically polished and the character designs are beatifully perfect. The Henchmen,Genius and Minion as well as base designs truly make you feel like somewhere between a bond villain and a saturday morning,over the top delightfully evil villain.

Music 7/10
It's great but could use more variation, honestly.
It's not bad though, it sets the tone pretty well

Basebuilding 9/10

The base is highly customizable and unlike the first game, you can re-place rooms and even fully whipe rooms back to dirt however you'd like. Whenever a room has an empty spot, the game itself adds just the right amount of random details and variation.
Little buttons along the wall, wall lights, bookshelves, posters and paintings, the game's Algorhitm for adding detail to your base is fantastic. You also have a ton of ways to customize and build rooms, have a ton of freedom in doing so and a ton of decorations to choose from ranging from a small chair or lavalamp to a massive coconut palmtree for your casino
The only reason i made it a 9 and not a 10 is because some cool options are locked behind DLC's

Campaign and missions 7.5/10

This is where the game can sometimes get a tiny bit repetitive. The story missions and side missions add a LOT of charm into the mix with little "cutscenes" that seem to be custom per genius that are highly enjoyable to watch. The bad part is that the world map, heat and missions seem unbalanced. You can (and probably should) play the entire campaign without doing any missions on the world stage besides the ones you HAVE to do and the heat-clearing ones. Passive income is all you need and the gold-making missions aren't worth the heat-gain and the micromanaging that comes with it. The game (especially with the DLC) does have a LOT of missions and sidestories, though. The story won't move you into tears obviously, it's mostly a parody of over the top spy-fi movies and pop culture.

Content for price: 5-9/10

This...is a hard one. The gamedev team was hit hard by covid and thus should be cut some slack. But some content should have been part of the base game, not DLC. That said, i love these devs too much to really give them hate for that. Just get the season pass on sale, the DLC are fantastic and add yet more fun content to an already very fun game


Overall: 8/10

Possibly one of the best, if not the best basebuilders i've ever played.
Try to get it on a sale with the season pass included for the full experience.\t\t\t\t\t
