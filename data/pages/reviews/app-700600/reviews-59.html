\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Fundamental gameplay loop of the game is pretty bad and gets worse the more you think about it and play it. As a management game there is way too much micro and deficit without many ways to deal with such things. Lots of unnecessary micro, and too many things that literally cripple you if you touch them with a 50 foot pole. Examples include the main economy mini game that you have to do endlessly throughout the game. Basically doing the most bare bone micro possible well also eating your workers meaning you are spending workers to pay for workers well your lair falls apart. At some point you get so powerful that the difficulty does not really matter. But how you *become* powerful is not through traps or deception or literally any option presented in the game other than combat. Because the enemies *Counter* all of those things so you inevitably just rely on your super minions and bruisers to endlessly kill all of the agents. My trap-hall filled with tons of cool traps? does literately nothing. My cool casino i put tons and tons of effort into to make into a labyrinth money hole? does literally nothing but make a minor amount of cash. And then the super agents will just skip all of those things and just break into your lair. 2/3rds of the entire game is ignored by the endgame and people think that's not a problem?

And then there is deficit, there is about 6-7 things that you have to do for maintenance, and i hear that the DLC AdDdS A ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ NOtHeR OnE!?!? The tutorial slowly unlocks each deficit and the game literally breaks and is almost unplayable without cheese if you don't turn the tutorial on. If you rush the tutorial, you will die, you will get murdered, you may even have a Aneurysm. Because every single time a deficit gets added you will need to spend 3-4-5 times what your vault currently holds in order to handle the situation. AND YOU BETTER, the game has a tantrum spiral and if you aren't on the ball when it comes to dealing with such things you will die. And as the game gets more deficits the efficiency of your lair starts to crawl to a halt as your dudes now need to maintain themselves in 3-4 different ways. In the endgame you have enough dudes where this does not matter. But you must understand how difficult the early game is. Every time you *unlock* something in the tutorial it cuts your efficiency in half so if your on the wire your dead. I can't even think of just how much of a slog it would be if you had all of these things unlocked right away. This may all just come off as a man complaining about difficulty but do understand that this is the type of dull difficulty that is hated in all management games. "You know how we make a management game difficult? How about we add 15 new upkeeps and never fix the main gameplay loop."

The game overall is also quite a slog in general, it heavily encourages never being risky or going out of your way so its understandable as to why. The only thing that is fun about the game is the side-quests and story, but even those are disappointing and half-baked half of the time. Each of the super-villans stories are incredibly similar and there is not much difference between how each of them plays. Its basically feels like your first super minion and not really a *Super Villain*. Each Villain gets small bonuses to various things and a *unique* minion unit. They try to make them unique through art style, but you need both drastic mechanical difference and serous style changes in order for a faction to feel unique. Both of these are minor making the whole thing feel hollow. When it comes to side-missions that's honestly the only point where the game shines a tad bit. The small comedic stories are at least somewhat enjoyable until you get to the cap and can't play them anymore. Then all you have is stealing world wonders like despicable me which i think in any villain game is incredibly enjoyable. Some of them give small bonuses to your lair which is also somewhat nice. Probably not worth going through the actual game to get, and half of why they are nice is that i have to go through a terrible game to get to them. Overall they are incredibly cheap, short stories, but that really says how you can elevate something with trash.

What makes a dungeon game fun? is it the endless tedium of managing a dungeon? Is it the tower defense elements? Or maybe the siege of other dungeons?. when it comes to this game they have a half baked tower defense mechanic and a overly complicated management mechanic. If that sounds down your alley than this games for you bud. not really for me.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEvil Genius 2 was never going to be a world reforming hit. It was made for a very specific kind of person.

The kind of person who could manually click "Brainwash Soldier" somewhere between 3 to 4 hundred times, kept there entirely because the feel of of it is in the right place.

And I am one of those people.

Evil Genius 2 has a lot of problems, and the game is threatening to collapse in on itself at any second (though it never did in my case), but it captures a very particular feel that very few games do. Note I said feel , not necessary in effect. As a base builder, it's weirdly bare bones and it suprisingly easy to contain everything you need to win hard mode on a single floor of your base, and by the same token, very easy to 'finish' your base before your doomsday weapon is even at level 2. Which means the rest of the game is a lot of click on menues while the game runs at twice speed and you quietly wish there was a better form of automation so that you didn't have to manually handle prisoners or heat.

EG2 is far more streamlined than EG1 was, and not necessarily for the better. Agents very rarely teleport into your base and the two that do are predictable and loudly annouce themselves. Gold is magically transported to and spent from your vault, so the logistics of money aren't a concern any longer. Bodies are destroyed immediately so there's honestly very little for Investigators to catch. Missions are also no longer influenced by minion type, and are straight pass/fail now, so you can't modify rewards or speed.

The sum total of these changes is a game where it's very easy to build a bottleneck for your enemies and turn it into something out of trench warfare, but the enemies keep scaling with progression into the game so there's not honestly much reason to manage heat. There's no XCOM-ian failure state if the whole world 'goes red', and enemy levels Cap at 9 (which you can see as early as the first chapter) and if you can 'take them' then the game has no other surprises.

If that sounds like a biblical hell to you, it probably will be. But the upside to it all is that EG2 if a far more casual world-ender than it's predecessor. I was captivated by some surprisingly good writing and legitimately interesting developments as the game went on, I'll admit: No game I've played has ever asked "What if M. decided go rogue", and it was a question I definitely wanted to see answered.

But that's probably not going to be enough for the normal buyer. People who played EG1 are probably going to be appalled by the let's-call-it-frankly-casualization of the much more sim-heavy original into something a lot poppier and faster. People who want a tightly built game are going to be off-put by the fact that a lot of their toys are irrelevant in the face of a fully stacked muscle minion with AKs that can not be turned off by the very agents said toys were put there to stop. People looking for a world conquering strategy game are going to be put to sleep by how little consequences there are to the actual world conquering.

And then there's me, brainwashing my 235th soldier and shockingly taken in by characterization and feel. That's the main draw of the game, that's it biggest hook. That's not going to be enough for most people.

But it was exactly enough to get me to complete it. Sometimes, you run into something that was made for you, flaws be damned.

----------

Also, I have no idea what the hell the people talking about the game being 'mobile style' are talking about. I'm assuming they misread what the Gold Missions actually do: They feed you a constant stream of currency for the whole duration of their run. This is so that you don't end up accidentally bankrupting yourself as often as you did in EG1. The game has a lot of faults, a trully stunning number of them, but "Being mobile like" isn't one of them.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEvil Genius is one of my favorite base-builders of yesteryear, and Evil Genius 2 makes everything better about that game. From different anti-heroes to play as to several bases to build out, every aspect has been added onto to make a comprehensively better sequel. Personally, I most enjoy the myriad of side missions which add to your base's abilities to spot and deter agents of good, equip your minions, and add a new crime boss to your payroll. To be fair, there are issues and bugs with this game which can be annoying - most notably the hardest difficulty being a slog of constant battles with agents and training - but frequent updates and patches help to soften these issues.

I whole-heartedly recommend this game for anyone who is a fan of the original or just wants a new base-builder to add to their library.\t\t\t\t\t
