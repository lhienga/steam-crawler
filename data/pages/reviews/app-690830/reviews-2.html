\r\n\t\t\t\t\tV1. Great city builder with an economic focus and minor combat. What makes this one stand out is the free form building where there is no grid or path construction. Instead roads appear automatically based on routes. As a result you can really build beautiful settlements that have a lived in appearance.

+ Living Settlement. Loads of games talk about having the so called "lived in" feel. This one actually does a good job. Paths automatically build where your citizens are consistently walking. All movement of materials actually has to be moved from place to place. Marketplaces open your resources to your citizens creating realistic logistic requirements. When a citizens work shift ends they go to the markets to get their goods and return home. Each citizen learns and gets better at their jobs(all work experience is kept even if you change their workplace. Almost everything is simulated in the game world. Currency being the main thing that is not.
+ Building. When building your structures you can place them anywhere in any orientation. Plenty of decoration objects and modular major buildings lets you achieve a truly custom layout. Yes, you do have to consider travel distances to optimise economy, but not having to build to a grid lets you focus on visuals at the same time.
+ Play styles. If your one for optimising and making the most out of your layouts. The economic aspect should keep you playing for a while as extensive planning would be required to get maximum efficiency. At the same time the game is pretty easy going so you can focus purely on visual layouts as well if you want to.
+ Easy going. Despite having a descent amount of logistics to deal with. You don't really feel overwhelmed at any point because the game doesn't have the requirement of maximum efficiency like other similar games.
+Graphics. A consistent simplified style is used. Settlements come together beautifully. The terrain and water are the two things that look off. They are too smooth and featureless along with low texture variation.

- Tutorial. There isn't much to the game, but some of the symbols and modular construction aren't explained so your going to end up doing some trial and error to figure it out.
- UI. Sometimes text can be hard to read. The white text can blend with the paper-like appearance of the panels. Making the text boxes have a darker backdrop would solve the problem.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGreat little city-builder. Full of charm, you build your little workshops and people build their own houses wherever you designate residential land. People won't die of starvation while walking down the street. I see people calling this a "Banished clone", but this game actually has personality instead of just being a grid-based mess of brown and olive green. Reminds me more of the old Settlers games(if this had Settlers branding, it'd be the best entry in the series since the DS remake lol), or Black & White 2 without the giant bipedal cow. Either way, it captures the imagination. Winter isn't a thing, there are "bad weather" events that lower crop yields, villagers can leave if unhappy(I had one leave in the early game becuase of too many tax levies), money can be scarce before you learn the economics, but the game seems less inclined to Dwarf Fortress-esque societal collapse than most others in the genre. There are three "Estates"(Labor, Clergy and Kingdom[militia]), you can build [markets/monasteries/forts] to improve your Splendor rating with them, but splendor for one estate will result in a penalty for the others. The estates will court you with resource trades from time to time, which are all wonderfully optional. All sorts of cute, wholesome little events occur while you play. Like randomly getting hugged once an hour.

Monasteries have all sorts of buildings and little jobs, adds a lot to the game. There's a rudimentary militia system where you can send troops on little expeditions for rewards. There's a minute chance that a soldier might die, but I've had two die in about 50 missions so I guess if you play your cards right you can avoid that? haha. Apparently visible on-map combat isn't going to be added and I can't tell if there's any progression from the Militian class(civilians and monks/nuns can upgrade, giving them access to new jobs but increasing their needs), but it's something. Building is fun and it's nice to see your little town develop on its own alongside your additions. This game is slower-paced than something like Black & White so I can understand skirmish mode not being an option. Might be fun to explore in a sequel though. Everything about this game leaves me thinking "I like that, I would like more of that", which is nice.

I wishlisted this years ago, it was $31.99 CAD then. Very rarely goes on sale, and when it does it's like 5% off. I got it on sale for, like, $27 or something, and feel as though I got my money's worth for sure. Not sure I'd pay $38.99 CAD for it(blame the price of everything else having doubled since 2022 lol), but for $30 or under it's well worth a look. If it ever hits $20, it'll be a no-brainer.\t\t\t\t\t

Vanilla buildings feel meh, and mods improve how they appear with new options for decorations. For instance the rustic church vanilla options look like the villagers just threw together whatever they had on hand. But I am unable to get the mods to work, even when accounting for dependencies. And inevitably, the game just ends up breaking. I played for 4-5 hours the first time before realizing I could not produce textiles (a needed sub-building for production was not available). And now I cannot even gather berries. The berry gathering hut just does not unlock. I've uninstalled most mods and down to a few I really hoped would work, but short of uninstalling and trying each one, or adding additional mods one at time to figure which one doesn't work, and knowing that it could still break right away or after hours of dedicated building. That is not time I can dedicate. I love the game, love the artwork. But this is a game I'm going to have to come back to.\t\t\t\t\t
