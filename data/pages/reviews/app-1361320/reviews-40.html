\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat to say about this fourth game...? Appropriately for a game about puzzle boxes, this feels like a late-series Hellraiser sequel - the kind that started off as an unrelated spec script but became part of that series through the addition of Pinhead and the Lemarchand Box. That's the best way I can describe the feeling of playing The Room 4.

That's not to suggest that the gameplay feels significantly altered or compromised in any way - certainly not! It's a quality game and its puzzles were a joy to solve. Its story, however, doesn't quite feel like a sequel to the previous entities and more like a story set in the same universe, related to the others through the puzzle boxes, the special lens, the Null, and certain other references. Another comparison could be that this is what Silent Hill 4 was in relation to its previous games, appropriate since that was also the fourth game in a series and was subtitled "The Room:" the first Silent Hill game to bear a subtitle, just as this is the first game in its series to bear a subtitle.

A consequence of the multiple endings in the previous game meant that, either the developers needed to choose one ending to be the correct one, or that this game needed to start with a clean slate, which is the direction chosen here. The unnamed character through whom we experienced the first three games is absent, and we play as another nameless puzzle box expert with a special lens and knowledge of the Null element. The stories in these games have never been anything too grand, just a couple worldbuilding details that lend the series its characteristic atmosphere; what details are included seemed to lean into Lovecraftian themes of forbidden knowledge and cosmic horror, while mixing it with a Myst-like sense of worlds connected through puzzles (the submarine puzzle in this game really gave me rocketship vibes from the original Myst).

The Room 4 adds a story about the married couple Edward and Abigail (perhaps a King Diamond reference?), and I can't say it fits particularly well. The story is parsed out through cursed books the player discovers in each new room, but the drama of an obsessive husband and worried wife left me skimming the pages, just in case they included needed clues (which they don't). One can play this game and ignore the story, which is precisely what I began doing when I realized that opening the books is not required to progress. Perhaps I'll appreciate the characters' drama more on a second playthrough, but it feels as if this was someone's short story that they crammed into this game rather than release in another medium. Maybe I missed something that would have made all the drama worthwhile; maybe Edward threw a fit in one of the cursed books and proclaimed, "You're tearing me apart, Abigail!"

Gameplay-wise, it's in some ways a step down from The Room Three, but not significantly so. Rather than travel to multiple unique locations, including some like the Forge or Clock Tower which had side rooms that opened up as one played, now we're back to the first game's concept of a single puzzle box in an attic. That's not to say that the series has regressed: the mechanic introduced in the previous game of traveling into small spaces using the lens is expanded upon to become a core component of the game. It might appear to be one sole puzzle box, but there are several rooms inside the doll house that each offer unique puzzles and challenges, and the mechanism of traveling to and from the different rooms allows the doll house's potential to be fully exploited. It's a great concept and it's executed wonderfully. So, while we go from the island of Grey Holm in the last game to a single doll house in an attic, the doll house incorporates a Metroidvania-style progression by using the multiple locations together, whereas Grey Holm had multiple locations that were isolated from each other.

If I had one complaint about the gameplay, it would be that the final puzzle was underwhelming, though it may have been more meaningful to someone who enjoyed the game's story, as it involves the aforementioned characters of Edward and Abigail, albeit in a superficial way. This is a very minor complaint - the gameplay leading up to the climax is replete with several excellent puzzles. Though there's nothing here that compares to the optional puzzles in The Room Three's hub world. It's also a significantly easier game than the previous one. I played with hints off (as you should), and didn't get stuck at any time. On that note, all these games have opening tutorials that hold the player's hand and teach him or her the game's mechanics. This was fine for the first two titles, but by the fourth game in the series, it breaks immersion and slows down the opening moments. Still, an argument could be made that a player new to this series would be able to begin with this game, so the tutorial may actually be needed, though I'd rather have had an option to turn it off in the main menu.

I've greatly enjoyed playing these games. I don't plan to purchase a VR headset, so I won't be playing the VR-only title, but I would very much like to see a fifth or sixth regular game, even if their stories amount to nothing. I don't play these games for their stories - it's all about the puzzles. I had a dream last night that I was manipulating thighs and drumsticks to solve chicken-based puzzles. I'll likely replay these four games and keep an eye out for similar titles, in order to pacify my newfound puzzle addiction.

In conclusion, The Room 4 is a very fun game. Perhaps you'll appreciate the story more than I did, but even if you're also lukewarm about it, you can enjoy the game for its lovely interconnected puzzle rooms.\t\t\t\t\t
