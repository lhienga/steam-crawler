\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI love these series!

Each game is getting better than its precursor, and adds a little bit to the gameplay and lore. Now, to be completely honest, the plot/lore on the first game is pretty much next to non-existent, so do not expect the series to rely on the script, which is merely a device to give the player a reason to explore the rooms. And mind you, I am an advocate of good story-telling in computer games, even puzzlers.

But the atmosphere, the feeling, the graphics, the textures, the satisfying sound effects, the intricate mechanisms of the various objects, oh my! Bring to mind the opening credits of "Game of Thrones", and imagine you gan play with all those clockwork trinkets that pop out of the Westeros map. Every little interaction and animation is indeed satisfying.

And most of the themes of the rooms, along with the nature of most of the items, are so catchy: Trains, submarines, fountains in gazebos, Japanese dragons, libraries/desks with hidden compartments, you name it.

So, any cons?
Well, there were a couple of times that the cusror got "stuck" with the image of an inventory object, even though said object was supposed to be back in the inventory. As a result, the cusror would not interact with the enviroment as it should. The solution is to exit to main menu, and restore the game (hit "continue"). So, not much harm done.
Another couple of times I did not know how to progress, not because of I couldn't solve a puzzle, but because I didn't realize that an invertory object was supposed to be furhter manipulated in my hands, or an environment object could be further manipulated via the looking glass mode (looking at you, dragons!). Both times the hint system couldn't help me.
