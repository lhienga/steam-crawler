\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've actually played this a little more than 6 hours because you can play multiplayer locally AND online! So, I would play with my husband on his game and two other players online.

I love it so much I decided to get my own copy.

It's very easy to pick up and play whenever. My friends and I like to play it every now and then. We all seem to prefer our little jobs - I often delegate myself to making tracks and refilling the bucket, another friend likes to chop trees or mine rock. However, some of the challenges in-game force you to switch around a little. There's also the golden bolt which is handy for upgrades and another challenge (for one of my friends to do - I'm busy trying to keep the train from burning! We need more wood!! Lay more tracks down!!!) It can get a little hectic but that's what makes the game so much fun. Always moving, keep chopping, don't stop laying those tracks.

The music is a delight. I find myself humming the first tune a lot but all the other tunes are great as well - very fitting for each biome.

Each biome provides a different challenge as well and with the save points it's easy to continue whenever without losing too much progress...unless you're trying to complete a challenge and you crash the train and lose all progress...*sigh*

Overall, fantastic little game. Great with your friends and pretty much anyone can play. Only downside could be the size and detail - I do lose sight of the bucket sometimes because there is a lot on screen and everything is moving (albeit not so fast). Have a giant monitor and you should be okay!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe repetitiveness and lack of sense of progress made me stop playing this game. In my opinion, the number of hours of gaming that made me find it repetitive to the point of stop playing is incompatible with the price (even considering I got a good offer).
This game is fun in the beginning. It made me laugh and have a lot of fun playing cooperatively with 3 other friends. Finding out what each wagon does is interesting and brings a sense of discovery. Learning how to deal with different biomes is also nice. The art is pleasing, the overall visual design is consistent and clear, and the controls make sense. Although there is no tutorial and explanations in-game are lacking, I do not feel like this is a major problem, as the mechanics are simple and testing stuff is a fun way to learn.
I stopped finding the game entertaining after the 6 hour mark. It felt like repeating the same tasks over and over and just trying to work faster in order to progress. Playing felt more like a chore than actually overcoming obstacles in a clever way (what I initially expected). I feel like there is no incentive to keep playing, as the increase in difficulty in latter stages seem to be related on how fast and efficient you can work, while avoiding the natural traps and obstacles in the landscape. Yes, there is some strategy in picking the correct wagons and buying a new train, but it is not very difficult to find out the optimal strategy. Although there are different difficulty levels and harder difficulties make resources more scarce and maps more closed, I did not think they make a huge difference in gameplay difficulty.
I feel the game lacks a bigger sense of progression. From what I understood, progressing meant basically buying a new train. I think buying trains should not make the biomes progress, but buying them should offer different advantages in each biome (which would progress naturally), so that it makes the buy more a strategic. Also, I think the game should require more strategic decisions from the player in the long run, like different wagons working better with some environments, and being more emphatic in increasing difficulty so that the player to better reconcile the need of surviving the current stage with need of making latter stages beatable.\t\t\t\t\t
