\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's definitely a good game - especially for those who like sorcery, swashbuckling medieval stuff in their games. The question is, are the graphics up to VR standards? I would have to say no - not if you are at all sensitive to graphics in VR - i.e. if low-res graphics make you feel queasy. You would then need mods.
I've tried a number of mods, and whilst many concerned with graphics do help a lot with the pukey feeling, most have failed at some point - often in dark scenes. I'm still trying to find one (or a collection of mods) that make me feel fine throughout.
If it weren't for that, it would be difficult to stop playing, despite the regular feeling of same-old, same-old that comes after the first few tasks/quests. Some of the quests can also get a bit confusing for one reason or another - for this I have obtained two guides available on the internet - one the official strategy guide and the other is the IGN Wiki Guide PDF. I recommend getting these, but it will take you quite a while to read through them, so probably best to use them as reference books when you need or want to know more about a particular game method or quest. Reddit is also a very good place for advice and mod lists and guides.
With the help of these references, the game can be turned into something very enjoyable (pukiness excepted).\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEveryone says you have to mod this game, and that the game sucks without them. I ended up spending more time on setting up the mods and tweaking settings than actually playing the game, as i'm sure many others had. Even though I used a curated mod pack, it made the game crash frequently, and made it more buggier. All those mods really bloat the game, and made it take forever to load up. Lots of the mods are gimmicky and superfluous. Many of the gameplay mods made things more complicated than they needed to be, and some mods had no directions that could be found online on how to use them. Visual mods made the games aesthetic and art style inconsistent.

I went back to vanilla, and ended up loving the game. Crashes are rare, bugs less frequent, the game loads up in 2 seconds, which is great when you need to travel the map a lot when selling a large haul of treasure, or you need to reload after failed pickpocket attempts. The games visuals are good enough outdoors, and look outstanding inside dungeons. Everything works. Try the game in stock form and decide for yourself.

Aside from that, Skyrim is a game that works great in VR. It's easily one of the most immersive experiences that can be had. You put the headset on, and the hours just fly by.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThey did literally the bare minimum to port this to VR.
In classic Bethesda fashion, they charge full price for an 11 year old game because the Fans keep it going with mods. Which is exactly what you need in order to enjoy Skyrim VR.
"I think standard Skyrim VR is fine as it is" You say? Good for you sport. By the way, know that sled you've been playing on? Did you know you can take it to the top of a snowy hill and slide it down? You don't have to just sit on it in your back yard and pretend. It's going to blow your mind when you learn how much fun sledding can be.

Back to Skyrim VR.
If you're willing to learn how to use mods, Skyrim VR can be transformed into how it should have been on release.
For example. Like role playing? Like building your character's face for 20 minutes? Well, sucks to be you because once you're done, you're a pair of floating hands with no body.
"Then why did I just spend 20 minutes building a face?" Because it was easier to keep it in than to remove it. Character building in VR may as well be Race, Sex, Name, and then done for all the impact it has.
You could argue cosmetics does nothing in regular Skyrim but it's fun to walk around in 3rd person and pretend your character lives in Skyrim. But in VR that won't happen.

Also, why do you want VR Skyrim?
"Well, to live in and interact with the world of course!"
Okay, but you can't. Your virtual disembodied hands can't physically touch anything. Your sword phases through solid matter and enemies with no collision.

But with mods, although you'll not be able to see your own face, (at least not with the mods that I use) but you'll have a set of arms. You can walk into a bar and pick up a mug of ale, pretend to get drunk and throw that mug at the barkeep's head. If your companion gets stuck in the door, AGAIN! you can grab her by the hand and drag her aside, give her a smack on the ass and go about your day.
I killed a man and threw his body into the river.
One mod I love, lets you throw weapons and call them back to you like Thor's hammer. Which is instantly more fun than skull F***ing Nazim...
oh, you don't know who that is? You mustn't get to the Cloud District very often.

I could go on. Or I could show you this.
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3kFj4DgcJeI&list=LL&index

Compare that to video of Unmodded VR Skyrim. You choose what you want to play.
