\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhen it comes to immersive gameplay - Skyrim is and has been towards the top for years. Since its release, Skyrim has been a staple of every single console in existence, from Xbox 360 to Switch, PC to One X.

However -

When you couple the normal 2d screen immersion of Skyrim with the immersion of VR, you lose out on a lot of what makes Skyrim great.

VR makes Skyrim feel exhaustively outdated. The ugly textures, the floating hands, the janky bow and arrow gameplay, odd aiming, and the lack of an immersive melee combat system - especially when looked at through the scope of what has been achieved in other VR combat games - turns what used to be a fun nostalgic experience to a frustrating and at times downright unplayable one instead.

There are those that will review this game from the standpoint of modding. This is - in my opinion - a bit of an unfair position to come from. In order to make the game more enjoyable, visually worth it, and enjoy true gameplay mechanics that should have been in the game from the start - it's about an hour or two of modding unless you have a friend or a community that has a modpack you don't have to hunt down. Yes, at that point - the game becomes more playable, enjoyable and slightly better.

The game, by itself, before modding - is so very not worth it to me. Get this one on sale. A large sale.

Or, go play Blade and Sorcery for better combat, and play games that were MADE for VR for your stories (Lone Echo is a very good starting point there). Both games are VERY much worth their full price tag.\t\t\t\t\t

Part 1: A Tale as old as 2011...
So I was just done with my half hour session of savescumming to kill some guy and become the arch-mage of the College of Winterhold and I had a meeting booked with Nocturnal to see about my managerial position at the Thieves Guild.
Moments before this meeting is scheduled to begin, a colleague Karliah turns around and just walks out of the cave, never to be seen again. "Fine" I thought, pushing this meeting back indefinitely so that I could attend to other business, "I suppose I'll help the Empire to fix the Stormcloak issue so that they can get back to slaughtering those disgusting high elves".
Upon arriving at the Empire's capitol in Skyrim and speaking with the relevant authorities, I was asked to clear out an old fort in the nearby mountains named Hraggstad which had apparently been occupied by bandits. Fortunately I can recall having visited there previously and making quite a mess of the place and so there should not be any bandits left there to fight, assuming nobody else has moved in. I was of course incorrect in this assumption as upon arriving at the fort I found it to have already been retaken by Imperial soldiers, despite my "divine intuition" telling me to kill the bandits in the area while pointing at an empty spot on the floor (casting detect life could highlight no subterranean bandits).
Utterly confused by this I thought I would speak with the Greybeards in High Hrothgar and maybe address the situation with the dragon Alduin. The spokesman for the Greybeards informed me that the arrangements to proceed with our plans towards this end would need to involve a temporary ceasefire of the civil war that I had only recently attempted to meddle in.
As such I walked back to Solitude only to find that the leader of the Imperial garrison refused to talk to me about any subject until I could come back with results regarding the retaking of Fort Hraggstad. Seemingly at something of a dead end I was forced to take a path that I would normally never dare tread and temporarily ally with the Ice-Dweller Stormcloaks with the intention of betraying them for the Imperials in order to have them ignore my initial Hraggstad task and escalate the civil war to my own benefit. Executing this plan flawlessly, stealing some ugly crown from a ruin as the Stormcloaks asked and attempting to instead hand it over to the Imperials, I was given no option to do so. For whatever reason however the leader of the Imperials was now willing to hear me out about the ceasefire so I suppose that's progress.
At the Greybeards' meeting hall were numerous factions gathered around a stone table to hash out the details of the ceasefire, to begin with the members of the meeting all stood around and refused to take their seats despite me waiting in mine for several days, in order to convince them I simply had to assert dominance and sprint into someone repeatedly so that I could edge them towards the correct part of the room that they were intended to stand in.
As a seat of authority in the meeting I immediately force the representative for the Aldmeri Dominion to sit outside rather than be present so that she will hopefully succumb to the sub-zero temperatures and thin mountain air if not maybe a courteous ice troll passerby. After this point I zoned out and allowed them to drone on for a bit, knowing that very soon over half of the table's occupants will be dead anyway.
Following the meeting the ceasefire was agreed on and I was tasked with shouting at a fire breathing dragon in order to convince it to land in a wooden palace and be caught by a wooden contraption. After calling this dragon however it decided instead to appear instantly overhead and then fly off into the horizon before getting wedged between two trees, and then thirty seconds later it would appear again and repeat this behaviour forever.
Part 2: ...But in VR
Skyrim VR is still Skyrim, it will still crash frequently and at random which looks even weirder in VR, you'll still see slow unskippable dialogue interrupted and forcibly played again from the beginning of the conversation, you'll still select a dialogue option only for the game to choose the incorrect one 4 choices down for you instead every time until you leave and reload the area.
Out of the box I would say that Skyrim VR is a half-step above unplayable, however with tools like Wabbajack you can install 100GB+ of mods in a few clicks, 32GB of RAM and hours of install time, with mods like VRIK, HIGGS, Planck, and Spell Wheel VR the experience becomes pretty good. You can put weapons on your back, pick up and throw things, slap NPCs and have them react, a whole bunch of stuff so long as you're willing to sit in the mod configuration menu and tweak everything to not suck and then remember to save afterwards. But your melee combat will still be arm flailing and smacking your desk with your expensive controller, and crossbows will still auto reload and consider your off-hand to be occupied even if you're firing one-handed, I did not have anything comparable to deal with when playing a magic build so try that if you want the dual wield Time Crisis experience vs Draugr.
Skyrim VR is actually on an older build of the game than the regular Special Edition post-Anniversary Edition update, but midway through my playthrough a modder finally managed to implement support for .ESL plugin files which should greatly increase the potential for the number of active mods, expect modpack creators to slowly adapt, maybe even allowing for a less painful way to import Anniversary Edition content to the game if you ever wanted to try it out in VR.
Part 3: Conclusion
Everyone knows that Skyrim is a bit crap, but boy if this isn't the best way to play it. Especially when you no longer need to take up secondary and tertiary hobbies of curating your own modlists and rigorously testing them for bugs and incompatibilities.
Dawnguard is still a terrible DLC but there is a "Serana Dialogue Add-On" mod that replaces her entirely (new voice actress and everything) to make her the only good companion in the "vanilla" game (though she does quote Revolver Ocelot which I found off-putting), and the Dragonborn DLC still absolutely rules.
I'm kinda annoyed that Oblivion doesn't have a VR mode like this, maybe if they ever remaster it...\t\t\t\t\t
