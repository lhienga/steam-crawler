\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWell what can one say about SkyrimVR that has not already been said...

The base game is good for its time. But when you add enough mods, it becomes freakin' amazing for today. I have the luxury of a 3090 and a G2 headset and SVR suitably modded looks real enough to touch.

More mods expand the map. There are expansion mods for SVR that are as big as whole titles from other publishers. The community is amazing. You can play SVR as a survival game, a stealth/theft game, a hardcore combat game, or enable a couple of cheats and just stroll around sight seeing (and the sights are worth it -- some of the most beautiful scenery and architecture in gaming). There are many different character types, many different storylines, huge replay value.

SkyrimVR is not really a game, it's more of a hobby or a way of life. It's a real-feeling place that you visit, a vacation from the real world, like a waking dream that you can step into whenever you want. SkyrimVR is what VR promises: another world. It's fully open and fully modelled -- go off the paths, head into the bush, orienteer your way ad lib around an expansive, scenic landscape. Until you hit the map boundaries there will be no limits, no "back sides" to things -- it's all modelled all the way down.

I have a lot of VR titles and I've enjoyed most of them, from casual toony sims to fairly sophisticated A-list games, but the one I never get tired of, never get bored with, always come back to... is SkyrimVR. Give it a try if you seek immersion, gee-wow vistas, humblingly huge structures and spaces, atmosphere, occasional creeps and jump scares, elaborate combat, magical spells, a ridiculously complex lore, and quirky NPCs who are only too eager to tell you their life stories.

It's the title that would not die, and there are reasons for that. SkyrimVR is the best single reason to buy a headset, and one of the most compelling demos you could ever show to a friend, to explain to them what VR can do.\t\t\t\t\t
