Thankfully, some of these issues can be fixed by modding.

Unless it's your very first time playing the game, Skyrim is widely considered at its best when stuffed to the gills with mods. This isn't to say that playing Skyrim isn't good or isn't fun without mods, but after you play your first 20 or 30 hours of vanilla Skyrim you'll probably be thinking "huh I wish the two handed combat felt more impactful" or "if only the alchemy perks were more applicable to the way I play" or even just "I want my anime waifu as my companion, dang it". Modding those things into Skyrim Special Edition (which I'll refer to as "SE") is a cakewalk. SE is (at the time of writing) the most recently updated version of Skyrim available on PC. It includes all of the DLC from the original and, as such, it has the most updated mods out of all the PC versions. Because of this, if you hop over to Nexus Mods and open the homepage for Skyrim SE, you'll be greeted by countless mods that can be installed and set up with little to no effort. Certainly not all are worth your attention, but the sheer number of SE mods available means you can find a working mod to satisfy almost any change you'd like to make. That story is quite a bit different for VR.

To start, there's no good way to browse mods made specifically for VR because Skyrim VR and SE mods are all lumped into the SE category on Nexus Mods. Technically speaking, this makes sense; behind the scenes VR is actually just SE with some tweaks by Bethesda that make it function as a VR game. Because SE and VR share the same core game files behind this VR coat of paint, mods made for SE are â€” in theory at least â€” compatible with Skyrim VR. The reality though is that many SE mods aren't compatible with VR at all and not every SE mod that works on VR will work on the first try. This is in part because VR is based on a slightly older version of the game files than SE and hasn't been updated in some time. Certain content present in SE is missing completely from VR while some has been updated in SE but not in VR. Mods made for SE typically expect to be installed in the latest version of SE, but since the version used in VR is older you'll often have the game hang, crash, or generally misbehave the first time they are installed forcing you to either remove the mod or go dig up a compatibility patch.

Mercifully, not *every* mod will wreak this havoc on your game and some of the most popular mods are compatible with VR right out of the box. I've personally had the most trouble with mods related to graphical upgrades to the game (improved lighting, ENB, re-worked water, etc.), but some functionally important mods like "Unofficial Skyrim Special Edition Patch" or "Ordinator" require their additional compatibility patch installed first to smooth over the problem spots caused by the mismatches between SE and VR. If you have experience modding Skyrim, whether that's through a tool like Vortex or by hand, you can no doubt get many of your favorite mods to work in VR with some coercion. If you have never modded Skyrim before and you want to try your hand at it, I would again point you to SE first as it is the more straightforward and forgiving option.


Conclusion:

If all you want is to play vanilla, middle of the road, Skyrim-as-Todd-Howard-intended-but-this-time-in-VR, go right ahead and snag this game when it's in the $10-$15 range. Like all versions of Skyrim this is a moderately jank experience by default, but it does an adequate job of taking the highs and lows of plain Skyrim and putting them faithfully into VR. Similarly, if you have never played Skyrim before and are looking to dip your toe in, I would suggest you try out Skyrim Special Edition first since the addition of VR is really more of a novelty than a necessity.

If instead you are looking for the same Skyrim experience you know and love from previous editions of the game, preferably modded somewhere between a little and too much, and you are willing to put in a great deal each of time, tears, and sweat to get there, this is the way to go.
