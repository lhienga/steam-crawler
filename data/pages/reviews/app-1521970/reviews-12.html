\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI do not usually write reviews but I feel like I need to because it is criminal that this game is getting mixed reviews so I need to add to the positive ones.

The general gameplay system of Goblin Stone is similar to Darkest Dungeon but without a lot of the things that make that game incredibly frustrating and grindy to play. Not to say that Darkest Dungeon is bad, it is made that way by design, but if you liked Darkest Dungeon but never ended up finishing it because you just didn't want to grind as much as the game requires you to, this game will scratch that itch. This game is much more forgiving (Not easy necessarily) healing is plentiful enough that while damage doesn't feel great, it doesn't feel like you're going to get absolutely destroyed because of one mistake. While goblin death is permanent, I'll get into why that isn't a big deal later in the review.

The art-style is cute and charming, the combat system is simple to understand but has a deep underlying complexity to it, it has enough rogue-like elements to make every run feel just different enough even with the exact same team to keep things fresh, and the meta-progression is unique compared to other games like this.

There are fun synergies between all of the classes, so far at the point that I am at, I have 5 classes, and all of them have felt great to use and useful in conjunction with each other. Some goblins get powerful abilities if the enemy is under some sort of crowd control effect, and some goblins are good at applying said crowd control effect, so there's a real strategy in putting goblins in your team in a particular turn order.

Goblins cannot level up, so the only way to get more powerful goblins is through the breeding system. It's actually pretty simple though, so don't be intimidated. There's only 3 stats to worry about and while the trait system is important, the base stats are much more important. Some classes want a lot of one stat, and the other doesn't matter, some just want their main combat stat and some health. You will get some goblins with incredibly skewed stats and all that should tell you is that they will be good to breed into a particular class. The game wants you to cycle through many goblins to continue progressing, as need a currency you can only get by permanently retiring goblins to the old peoples home to continue upgrading your base and unlocking new abilities for goblins. So just go crazy, make some goblins kids every day, and see what you get.

The loop to this game is: make some new goblin children every day and see what class would be the best for them, take them out on adventure, acquire loot for your base upgrades, see what you can upgrade at the base, make more children, go on another adventure, retire some of the goblins from your previous team to get souls for base upgrades or ability upgrades, make more children, add the new blood to your old team, rinse and repeat. If a goblin dies, while it is sad (Especially if it was a good one), you still get the soul you would have gotten for retiring them, so their life wasn't wasted. Ultimately even though you lost a good goblin, you can just make more, and the new kids might even be better than the one that just died in combat.

There ARE some bugs, but this game is finished and only needs a small patch or two to iron out the kinks. Do not let the bugs scare you away from a great experience, this is a fresh take on the genre and if you enjoyed Darkest Dungeon, you WILL enjoy this game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tUPDATE: As the save issue was fixed, I put quite a few more hours into the game. While the previously mentioned QoL issues are still present, It is nice to see that they are being taken under consideration. I want to mention a few additional findings while playing through the game.

1) There is an infinite item glitch that I stumbled upon and was able to replicate about 50% of the time. This can lead to infinite gold & or resources. Without explaining it fully here, it involves the larder & how overflow items that are recovered after a quest are handled.

2) Some gems seem to be worth more when broken down into smaller gems. If you start with a large green gem for example, you can break it down into its smallest unit and come out +50% in value when you go to resell. If it were consistent across all gems, it would seem intentional. The fact that is is not makes me think otherwise.

3) The game does not know how to auto complete stacked items. For example: If you have a stack of 17 stone and stack of 5 stone, and you drag and drop the stack of 5 stone onto the 17 stone, the game will tell you that stone only stacks to 20. In most modern games the stack would peel off 3 stone from the 5 stone stack, maximize the first stack, and leave you with a stack of 20 stone and 2 stone. This gets tedious when having to manually spit several stacks of resources into specific piles in order to save room in the larder. This is especially frustrating when you have a full inventory and want to combined items to free up room on a quest.

4) Wood seems like a vastly overused material. The amount of wood that is required compared to the other 5 resources seems disproportional versus the amount of any of the other 5 resources you need relative to the rest.


At this point the game is definitely in a much more playable state. I am going to switch my review to a 'would recommend' but honestly I wish there was a middle option. I feel that if you enjoy similar games like Darkest Dungeon, you may want to pick this up as it scratches an itch. But on the other hand, if you enjoy similar games like Darkest Dungeon, you may not enjoy this game as it is lacking some more polish.

If the previous QoL issues and the ones mentioned above are eventually resolved, this could shape up to be a really solid title.
