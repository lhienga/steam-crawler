\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAfter playing this game for a few hours, I am forced to question whether or not the developers have actually played their own game.

The main issues are:

1. This game is completely unforgiving. Each and every single move you make is properly logged, right down to each move you make in the fights, so If you make a mistake, the game will force you to regret it. This is exacerbated by the fact that you can't get your hands on resources without completing missions, missions that will force you to take casualties if you are not careful. Also, there is no way to effectively replenish losses in the early game. So if you make a single mistake (I.e. forget to equip one of the goblins with a weapon.) You can very easily be forced to start a completely new game.

2. The grind is real. There is a clear and defining change in difficulty with each mission you unlock/play. However, you can't upgrade/improve your goblins with the same relative ease. The only items you can equip on your goblin are weapons, and those are very difficult to improve/find in the game. There is a way of improving the general quality of your goblins through breeding lines. However, you are forced to play a mission after each attempt to breed, so you will have to play several missions before you are capable of raising a whole new squad of useful goblins.

3. RNGesus plays a big part. There are only a few moves that each particular class of goblin can make so teamwork and the synergy of each party is important. However, the problem is exacerbated by how the combat mechanics work. During battle, goblins are only given a choice between a few randomly selected abilities of the entirety of the abilities that the goblin actually possesses. I.e. There is no guarantee that you can actually use the combination that you want to use.

4. The turn based setup. While there is a merit to their way of playing a turn based game by making a character wait a certain amount of turns before they can move again. However, the game makes this happen by forcing both teams to face each other standing in a line, with the order being determined by how many turns it takes before they are allowed to make a move again. But the problem with that is that the vast majority of moves involve attacking the person who is standing at the front of the enemy team. So you constantly have to be on the lookout for how many turns it takes for your goblins to start moving again and you have to balance out the cost of the moves you make in order to actively prevent your own goblins from being stacked out.

All in all. This game gives you very little diversity and very little opportunity to make stronger teams of goblins, essentially making the game very grind heavy and tedious. The game has to be much more forgiving and the player has to be given more means of modifying and increasing the capability of the goblins before it actually becomes playable.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe best part of this game is the graphics, unfortunately. There are a lot of design decisions in this game that summarily make it not that fun... the most fun, I guess, is the suspense that something cool might unlock. For me, 13.7 hours later, it didn't happen. I doubt I'll keep going.

The story started off very interesting, but by the second map, I started to really hate it. Instead of being the underdog against the bad humans, it's all about orc supremacy and being their little errand runners non-stop, which actually started in the first map. It's not interesting enough to want to see what happens in the third map.

For the design decisions that I have an issue with:

The controls are annoying - finish a fight, try to loot, it talks at you like Darkest Dungeon (and repeats a lot) but removes your controls, wait, then you try to loot again and sometimes you have to do it twice to make it go. Also, clicking the mouse doesn't loot if you are too far away, it stops moving as soon as you let go of the key, but holding it down won't loot either you have to click it again. Once I found hitting the space key works, I just spam that until it does it.

When completing combat, the combat loot randomly gets dumped somewhere. I especially enjoy it when it puts it behind some of the fancy shrubberies that are in front of the goblins, then I have to fight my camera or hit space a whole lot everywhere. Oh, there is also sometimes random loot that you can't see (there might be a shiny treasure pixel or something) which reinforces my space spamming.

The stages have a ton of temporary buffs, most of which are weak, and the stages are short, which make most of the stage pointless.

The stages are also filled with an odd walking simulator mechanic, I guess in order to see the pretty graphics somebody did, but it's annoying after a while. Maybe I'm just impatient.

Ambushing (approaching an enemy from behind) is random AF. Half the time nothing happens, the other half only part of them are surprised.

Why does bleed to a crapton of damage, but poison takes a very large amount of stacks in order to do anything? If anything, I would think it the other way around (small amount of poison kills, but death by 1000 cuts)

The buildings are extremely uninteresting. You can dig out all this depth, but the only buildings that you can duplicate are the item holding ones and the retiree ones, which are barely helpful. Half my spaces are literally empty.

I keep hoping for a way to add permanent enchantments on my weapons but I have to pray to RNGESUS for the merchant to show up with decent ones - it is rare.

Soul currency is beyond weird. You do not get a soul per goblin death like you may expect, you get X amount of souls depending on if they've seem combat, how many adventures they been on, their age, and if they get to retire or not. That's right, you get more souls if they retire. No souls if they haven't seen combat. What the f...

The breeding doesn't make a lot of sense beyond a basic level. The max stats seem to be tied to the story, so there is no real point to the breeding system - you just do the story then rescue a goblin or hire a squatter then breed that one.\t\t\t\t\t

I'm not yet certain if there is true meta-progression between full runs of the game, but there's lots of things to progress at your base by upgrading rooms, unlocking classes, buying weapons and breeding better goblins and such, so you can feel the overall progression slowly as you send your teams out on their missions.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA wonderfully thematic piece where art and style carry the simple but likewise thematic gameplay.

Many reviews have a fair point, but also one that likely misses the point of a goblin-based game: Longetivity of characters.

In Goblin Stone, the theme isn't an individual hero or long-living ones even. It's the idea of the tribe- the idea that an individual goblin can almost never be truly 'special' or 'legendary', but that as a whole the little greenskins pull together to succeed in feats that others would never expect them capable of- even with lots of death and doom along the way.

I enjoy that level of theme, but it does take the attention off of the little guys we do spend a lot of the game staring at. So I can understand why some people have a harder time engaging when other games like XCOM fulfill the RNG/Hero fantasy urges of us people way better.

That being said, if you are a fan of goblins and want to play a good, medium-low difficulty roguelite about a tribe of goblins trying to make it in a wonderfully created world? I really recommend this.

Overall: 7/10
For enjoyers of Goblins and the idea of flipping the traditional script on fantasy adventures: 8/10

Solid game, loving my purchase. Keep up the good work, Devs!\t\t\t\t\t
