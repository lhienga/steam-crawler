\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a narrative, atmospheric game where you explore your own family's bizarre and mysterious past and worry about your own future by exploring your family's ridiculously labyrinthine home. Nothing is quite as it seems, and everything is connected. Overall, the game is immersive, suspenseful, even creepy, but not terrifying. Don't expect to play this as a game--it is more walking-simulator than game--but DO expect to be captivated by the story of Edith Finch.

Pros:
Graphics: This game contains gorgeously done art. The backgrounds are lush, dramatic, intense--even mesmerizing, sometimes. I had a hard time leaving the forest at the beginning--I was sure there was something more to see around a corner. It's not even just about the beauty of the game, though--music and images combine to build up an atmosphere so intense that it truly does feel like you are there.

Plot flow: Suspense is the right term here, bordering on creepy. Pacing is excellent--I felt almost compelled to continue even when I was feeling a little dread. I had to know what had happened. Getting into the core mechanic, each room is a story and each of them takes their own approach. The different types of stories engaged me in different ways, each relatively unique. None of them felt stale or dull. Part of this is the evolving game mechanics--your basic character can walk and squint at things, but you get a little variety in each sub-story

Gameplay: I played with the mouse and keyboard (WASD). These worked aok, and little else was needed. No quicktime events or anything time-essential like that, so even if you screw something up or go a wrong way, you can come back and try again.

Cons:
Gameplay: okay. controls were a little finicky, and no instructions given. Early on, I did end up letting go of the door knob and walking away almost as much as I ended up opening the door, but I got better eventually.

Content: (trigger warning: death, child death) Look, Gregory's storyline is always going to be upsetting as hell--I wish there was a way to select 'skip the baby death story' at the beginning of the game. However, my true distress in the game is from the (several) times you must essentially kill yourself as your past family members in order to advance the plot. Calvin's was.... really absurd to the point of funny, Sam and Walter's stories were tragic, Molly's story was creepy. But Lewis's story requires you to literally put your head in the chopping block . ohmahgawd. eesh

Plot: Despite being the whole point of the game--a narrative story game--the plot points are essentially unresolved by game's end. It feels like nothing is explained and we are basically back in square one. Part of this fits just fine with the narrative being told--something about self-destruction, imagination, grief, and the family history being chains holding every subsequent family member down, doesn't NEED to be resolved with a nice flourish. But sometimes it feels like NOTHING is answered. Perhaps it's the absurdist elements of the narrative that line up side by side with the realistic depiction above. The family is weighted down with its own past, which has been rife with extensive tragedy. Okay. But then Milton literally walks through a door into nothing? Walter gets hit by a train in a dead-end tunnel where no train could possibly be going that fast? all that is left of barbara is her ear because she literally got eaten by monsters?.

So I feel left with this tension: does the house actually like... eat people? does it transcend space and time? Do people disappear within its walls? Or is the family so deep in its own grieving that people, like Lewis, simply give in to their imaginations and allow that to take over? One seems realistic, the other as unrealistic as possible. Probably the answer is supposed to be "both of these are true" but the way in which the story resolves doesn't feel like it addresses such a compromise at all. It feels like the ending ties into the realism interpretation of the house--aka everybody is deeply depressed. But like.... there are people who are literally never seen again. Hrmph.


Overall:
I 100% recommend that you play this game. It is a true delight, exquisitely done, darkly beautiful, and it left me wanting more.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is full of ghosts.

That's not a spoiler, it's something you learn the first time you open the in-game menu.

I've seen bits and pieces talking about What Remains of Edith Finch that imply it's beautiful, melancholy, and inspiring. I'm not arguing those points - the game reminds of both Gone Home and perhaps even moreso, Tacoma - but it misses a core aspect of WRoEF:

This game is weird.

That doesn't make it bad. It's part and parcel with the whole story, part of its charm. But if you're expecting a sober, somber, serious tale with a happily ever after, this isn't the game for it.

What Remains of Edith Finch is, to put it bluntly, kind of bonkers. It's about death and dealing with death, it's about imagination, it's about legacies. It's full of whimsy and gallows humor, often at the same time. It is full of Dead Kids. There's sharks, drowning, schlock horror comic books and bad decisions involving sledgehammers and brick walls. There's a house that's alive while all of its occupants are dead. There's a man who loaded a house onto a boat to escape a curse, synchronized swimming bath toys, and dragons.

The thing about this game is that it never fully defies possibility or belief, if you look at it right. Many of the stories are fantastical, but only 'unbelievable' if the narrator is reliable, which is never a given.

There's a lot more, but I've probably spoiled something already.

The game is beautiful, funny, and tear-jerking in its ways, with great attention to detail (at most points) and strong voice acting and music composition. Every part of the House has life breathed into it, so to speak.

The cons are clunky gameplay at some turns - this is first and foremost a 'walking simulator'/living story. It's also easy to get disoriented or turned around at some points. Sometimes this plays into the idea, though, that a real person is struggling to navigate the house and the challenges it presents.

If you enjoy story-centric games such as Gone Home and Tacoma, and even Superliminal to some extent, and don't mind a little clunkiness (and absurdity) then this game is for you.\t\t\t\t\t
