\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is not terribly long, but it really crams a lot of story into its playtime. More of an interactive story than a proper "game," What Remains of Edith Finch does a really amazing job keeping you interested in the story by changing up the play: in one vignette, you are a cat jumping from branch to branch; in another, you must fly a kite; in a third, you must walk through a maze with one hand, while maintaining a repetitive action with the other. What each vignette wants you to do is tied inextricably with the story of that character. This is a very innovative and engaging way to tell stories!

The story, overall, is both heart-wrenchingly sad and extremely hopeful. The shortest way I can summarize the storytelling in this game is "How the members of the Finch family (across four generations) cope with tragedy." (Spoiler alert: not very well.) I was moved to play the game through a second time after finishing it the first time, because I knew I would pick up on other details, having made it to the end of the narrative. What Remains does an amazing job making creepy melancholy an attractive game environment.

The graphics are good, if a little dark in places. The sound is lovely: lots of appropriate ambient sound and most of the music is unobtrusive, unless it's a storytelling device, in which case it's well-seated. Everything feels a little fantastical while also feeling incredibly mundane and real. This suits the game narrative perfectly.

Truly, I cannot recommend this game highly enough! The only reservations I have would be for someone who has difficulty with death. There's a lot of it. Seriously (spoilers for characters): ||everyone dies, including a baby, and you play all of their final moments||. But if you're comfortable with that dynamic, Edith Finch delivers an experience that will stay with you long after you finish playing.\t\t\t\t\t
